## The Draft

She is standing in the temple of the moon god at Ur, and she is angry.

Not because the moon god has displeased her. Because a usurper has thrown her out.

Her name is Enheduanna. She is the daughter of Sargon of Akkad, and Sargon has done something no one before him had done at this scale: he has taken a world of rival city-states and tried to make them answer to one center.

That is why his daughter is in this temple in the first place.

Ur is in the Sumerian south, in what is now southern Iraq. Its god is Nanna, the moon god. Its language, its priesthood, its memory, its rituals: all Sumerian. Sargon is Akkadian, from the north. Different language, different political base, different claim to rule. Installing his daughter as high priestess at Ur is not a family favor. It is empire learning one of its oldest tricks: if you want to rule people you do not know, you do not only send soldiers. You send legitimacy.

Enheduanna is not just a symbol. That would make her smaller than she was. She writes hymns to Inanna that are good enough to survive four thousand years. In the "Exaltation of Inanna," probably the oldest substantial work we can attach to a named author, though the attribution still gets argued, she writes in a voice that is wounded and direct:

*You have made me fly like a swallow from the window.*

*My good name is destroyed.*

*I am Enheduanna, let me speak to you.*

The appeal works. The usurper is defeated. She returns to the temple. Her anger survives in clay.

That is the doorway into empire: a woman with a voice of her own, placed by a father whose whole political problem was that most of the people he ruled were not his people.

Sargon himself is harder to see. His origin story is propaganda polished by later generations: humble birth, a mother who hides him in a basket on a river, rescue, service to a king, then the impossible climb. You can hear the familiar shape. Moses will have it. Romulus has a version of it. Heroes often arrive by water when later storytellers need power to look chosen instead of seized.

What we can say is that around the twenty-fourth century BCE, Sargon conquered the Sumerian city-states and pushed beyond them. Kish. Uruk. Ur. Then west toward the Mediterranean, north toward Anatolia, east toward the Zagros. The details are difficult and often come through royal boasting, so we should keep the edges soft. [VERIFY: campaign extent phrasing and chronology.] But the central fact is clear enough: more cities, more languages, more gods, more distance, under one ruler than the world had seen before at this scale.

For the men doing the marching, none of that was a map.

Picture a soldier in Sargon's army: a farmer's son from the Akkadian plain, carrying bread, beer, and a spear with a copper-alloy head, walking farther from home than anyone in his family had ever gone. The villages sound wrong. The gods have unfamiliar names. The city wall ahead of him belongs to people who did nothing to him personally. He is there because a king he may never stand near has decided that this city now belongs inside the same system as his own.

That is empire from below: distance entering the body through the feet.

The old city-state world knew war. One city fought another, took tribute, took hostages, maybe seized land, and went home. Sargon attempted something else. Not raid and return. Rule. The conquered city is not just defeated; it is administered. Its grain, labor, soldiers, gods, scribes, and prestige must somehow be made useful to the center.

Empire is the machine for ruling people you do not know.

That sounds simple until you ask what it requires.

Messages have to travel and be trusted. A cylinder seal, rolled across wet clay, becomes a signature and a security system. Weights and measures have to mean the same thing in cities that do not trust one another. Governors have to be loyal from hundreds of miles away. Armies have to be fed before they start feeding themselves off the land you just conquered. Local gods have to be honored or absorbed or carefully not insulted. Local elites have to be frightened enough to obey and rewarded enough not to rebel every spring.

The list can get dull if we let it. The lived version is not dull at all.

In a Sumerian village under Akkadian rule, the house has not changed. Same packed-earth floor. Same grain jars. Same sheep. Same temple facing the same direction. Same god in the same local shrine.

What has changed is the name of the man who takes the grain.

Before, the grain went to your city and your city's god, through a system your grandfather understood because he had grown up inside it. Now an Akkadian governor stands in the marketplace. The records may be kept in Akkadian. The orders may come from a king five hundred miles away. The script is familiar, but the words are not. The tax is familiar, but the direction has changed. The largest thing you belong to no longer ends at a wall you can walk around in a morning.

You are inside Akkad.

You did not choose this. You were not asked.

And because empire runs on the same ledgers the city invented, the moral cost deepens. The tablet can remember your ration and forget your face. It can count your labor without caring whether you offered it willingly. Sargon did not invent extraction. But empire gives extraction longer arms.

It also gives dependency longer roads.

Bronze matters here because bronze needs tin, and tin does not sit conveniently beside the cities that want bronze. The supply routes ran through regions no Akkadian king could fully control. Some goods came from astonishing distances. Mesopotamian texts speak of Meluhha, usually identified with the Indus world, and a seal names a man, Shu-ilishu, as an interpreter of the Meluhhan language. A professional interpreter for a language from the far side of the known world, working somewhere inside this early connected system. The script of that Indus world has never been cracked. We will meet that silence again. [VERIFY: Shu-ilishu seal wording and Meluhha identification.]

This is one of the first hints that the Bronze Age will become larger than any one kingdom's imagination. Even now, before the great international age, the roads are already longer than the rulers.

Sargon's empire survives him. That alone is remarkable. He hands it to sons, then to a grandson raised inside the institution Sargon invented. That grandson is Naram-Sin.

Naram-Sin inherits a thing that already feels less like an experiment and more like reality. He expands it. He boasts. He crushes revolts. He learns the dangerous lesson every successful empire teaches its heirs: if the impossible worked once, perhaps the next impossibility is only waiting for someone bold enough.

Then he makes himself a god.

Not favored by the gods. Not chosen by the gods. A god, during his own lifetime, in his own inscriptions. Present tense.

For a Sumerian priesthood whose whole world rested on the difference between gods and men, this was not a small innovation. Later Sumerian tradition remembered Naram-Sin as the arrogant king whose impiety brought disaster. The "Curse of Akkad" turns collapse into moral drama: the king overreaches, the gods withdraw, enemies descend, the empire falls.

As history, that is too neat. As human behavior, it is perfect. When a great system breaks, people go looking for the sin that explains it. Usually the real causes are less satisfying: drought, rebellion, overextension, succession trouble, hostile neighbors, local resentment, supply strain, administrative limits. The Akkadian collapse around the late third millennium BCE probably involved several of these, in proportions we cannot fully recover. The 4.2-kiloyear climate event is part of the debate, but not a magic key. [VERIFY: current 4.2ka framing and Akkadian collapse chronology.]

The important thing is what collapse did not erase.

Akkad fell. Empire did not become unthinkable. That is the dangerous afterlife of Sargon. Once someone has proved the thing can be done, every later ruler standing in the wreckage can tell himself the same lie ambitious people always tell themselves.

It can be done. We'll do it right this time.

Within a century, southern Mesopotamia tries again.

The Third Dynasty of Ur, Ur III, is less famous than Akkad and in some ways more revealing. It is a bureaucratic empire. Tablets everywhere. Rations, work gangs, fields, animals, deliveries, textile workers, flour, oil, wool. In one provincial archive, the numbers for women in state textile production become immense. [VERIFY: Girsu textile worker figures and period assignment.] The empire knew what it owed the woman at the loom down to the ration. Whether it knew anything else about her is not a question the tablets were built to ask.

That is the ledger again: astonishing memory, narrow sympathy.

Ur III falls in a way we can watch almost from inside the paperwork. Amorite groups press in from the west. A wall meant to keep them out does not solve the problem, because walls are much better at stopping armies than migrations. A grain official breaks away and interrupts the food supply. The last king, Ibbi-Sin, complains that grain promised to Ur is not arriving. Then Elam attacks from the east. Around 2004 BCE, Ur is sacked, the moon god's temple is looted, and Ibbi-Sin is carried away.

The empire of Ur did not fall only when an army arrived. It fell, functionally, when the grain stopped moving.

What survives is grief in literary form: the *Lament for the Destruction of Ur*. The goddess leaves the city. The streets fill with the dead. The temple is abandoned. The poem is not a battle report. It is a city trying to understand its own death.

And here the Sumerians had already given themselves a terrible explanation for civilization. In the myth of Inanna and Enki, Inanna carries home the *me*, the powers or offices of civilization: kingship, priesthood, craft, writing, law. But the same divine package also includes darker gifts: deceit, conflict, the plundering of cities, lamentation. [VERIFY: specific *me* list items and translation.] The people who built the first cities looked at civilization and saw the splendor and the wound in one bundle. They did not separate the receipt from the price.

So when Ur dies, the lament is not an accident of literature. It is one of civilization's own tools being used for one of civilization's own disasters.

The city itself continues. Ur is not erased from the earth. But Sumerian as a living spoken language fades after this period, surviving like Latin later will survive Rome: learned, copied, revered, no longer the language of childhood. Enheduanna's language becomes a school language. A sacred language. A dead language with a long afterlife.

The living voice dies. The tablets remain.

Egypt, meanwhile, is the control case a thousand miles west. It too builds a centralized state. It too extracts labor and food. It too makes kings divine. But the Nile gives Egypt a different political grammar: one long river, a flood that can be made to look like cosmic order, a geography easier to imagine as one kingdom. By Sargon's day, the Great Pyramid is already centuries old. Egypt was old. Mesopotamia was inventing.

And the people who built those pyramids were not the faceless slave swarm of popular imagination. At Giza, the workers' town at Heit el-Ghurab gives us bread ovens, barracks, cattle bones, beer and ration evidence, and named work gangs such as "Friends of Khufu" or similar crew names in the broader pyramid-building evidence. [VERIFY: exact crew-name wording and site association.] Not freedom in any modern sense. Not a pleasant summer job. But organized labor, skilled logistics, food, hierarchy, pride, compulsion, all mixed together in the usual uncomfortable human way.

Egypt's Old Kingdom eventually fractures too, but the institution of pharaoh survives the fracture. Mesopotamia's pattern is more jagged: city, empire, collapse, city again, empire again, collapse again. Same broad human problem, different river, different memory.

By the end of this part, the city is no longer the largest political idea available. The world has learned a new shape.

A ruler can claim people he has never met.

A soldier can die outside a wall whose name he learned last month.

A villager can owe grain to a center beyond his horizon.

A priestess can become the hinge between conquered gods and imperial power.

And a scribe, centuries later, can look back on the ruin and write grief so carefully that we can still hear it.

The next world will not be one empire. It will be something more intricate and, in some ways, more dangerous: merchants, letters, law, debt, tin roads, palace households, and people whose private complaints survive because clay is patient.

Empire has taught distance how to rule.

Now distance is going to learn how to trade.
