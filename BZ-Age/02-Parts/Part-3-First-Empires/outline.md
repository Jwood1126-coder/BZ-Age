# Part 3 — The First Empires

**Status:** Skeleton. Less developed than Parts 1–2.
**Working length:** Provisional 3–4 hours.

---

## The core problem this part solves

The shift from city-state to multi-ethnic empire is one of the most consequential transitions in political history, and the Akkadian Empire was the first attempt. Why is empire hard? What does it take to govern people who don't know you, who don't share your language or gods? Sargon's experiment failed, but the idea didn't — it became the template for everything that followed, including the empires we know. This part is about the birth of an institution that has shaped the entire subsequent history of the species.

---

## Opening hook (concept)

Sargon's daughter. Enheduanna — the world's first author whose name we know, the en-priestess of the moon god at Ur, a poet whose hymns survive. She is also a political instrument: her father has installed her at Ur to bind the Sumerian south to his Akkadian north. Open on her — the first known author, the first known propagandist, the first known woman whose voice we can hear from antiquity — and through her, the experiment her father was attempting.

## Narrative arc (sketch)

- **Opening:** Enheduanna.
- **Rising:** Sargon's rise. The conquest. The institutional innovations — Akkadian as administrative language, governors, a standing army.
- **Turn:** the strains. Multi-ethnic governance is harder than it looks. Revolts. Climate stress.
- **Payoff:** the collapse of the Akkadian Empire. The legend that outlives the empire by two thousand years.
- **Exit:** the Old Kingdom of Egypt as parallel case — same problem, different solution — and then the long Intermediate Period that takes us toward Part 4.

## State of the World snapshot (placement and sketch — required, Rule 10)

**Placement:** Middle of part. Contrast against Part 2 — what does empire feel like, from below?

**Vantage:** A Sumerian villager (no name needed) in a previously independent city under Akkadian rule, around 2270 BCE.

**Sensory rings:**
- *Body and surroundings:* the same room a great-grandparent might have lived in. The same fields, the same crops.
- *What's different:* an Akkadian governor's official passes through the marketplace. The taxes are now collected in the name of a king five hundred miles away. The language of the official documents is no longer the language of your home.
- *Wider world:* you now belong to something called "Akkad." Until recently the largest political unit you would have known was your own city.

**Mental world:** the gods of your city are still the gods of your city. But there is a king somewhere who claims the favor of all the gods at once, including yours. The category of "great king" is new and confusing.

**Negative space:**
- Still no horses ridden, still no chariots in combat (light chariots are coming in the next century or two).
- Still no iron. Still no alphabet. Still no coins.
- Still no concept of citizenship in any modern sense.
- The empire itself is new — there has never been one before this. Within a generation of older people, the very idea of multi-city, multi-ethnic political unity didn't exist.

**Contrast to Part 2:** the city-state world has been overlaid by something larger that the city-state world had no category for. This is the political imagination expanding, painfully.

**Length:** Brief — 2–3 minutes.

## Turning Points (required, Rule 11)

### The invention of the multi-ethnic empire
- **The change:** Sargon binds together cities of different languages, gods, and ethnic identities under a single administration. There has never been a political unit like this before.
- **The personal moment:** the first Akkadian governor installed at Ur. He is not Sumerian. He does not speak Sumerian as a native language. He is the king's man, sent to rule a city that belongs to a culture he is not from. The institutional improvisation he is carrying out — what works, what doesn't, who he negotiates with — is being invented in real time.
- **The archetype callback:** the conquered Sumerian villager we meet in this part's State of the World snapshot is downstream of the Sumerian barge-puller from Part 2. The world he inhabits — same fields, same gods, but now a foreign administrator in the market — is the world that the Akkadian Empire created.
- **The community change:** bilingualism appears as a survival skill. Documents are issued in multiple languages. Trade routes lengthen because they are now protected by a single political entity.
- **The civilizational consequence:** every later empire is a variation on Sargon's experiment. Persian, Macedonian, Roman, Mongol, British. The institutional form was invented here. The fact that it could be invented at all is the turning point.
- **The honest cost:** the conquered cities lose their political autonomy. Some of their elites are absorbed into the imperial administration; many are simply displaced. The extraction is real. The institutional innovation belongs to Akkad; the cost is paid by the cities Akkad conquered.
- **Throughline:** the deep weirdness of early statehood (now: of early empire). Also fragility of complexity — the empire's later collapse will be the first case study in multi-ethnic political failure.

### Akkadian as the first international lingua franca
- **The change:** Akkadian, the language of the conqueror, becomes the language administrators across multiple cultures use to communicate with each other. Sumerian survives as a scholarly language, but Akkadian becomes the operating language of power.
- **The personal moment:** a Sumerian scribe in the second generation of Akkadian rule who has had to learn the conqueror's language to retain his job. His grandchildren will use Akkadian without thinking about it.
- **The civilizational consequence:** Akkadian's status as a shared administrative language survives the Akkadian Empire's fall by more than a thousand years. By the time we reach Part 5, Egyptian and Hittite courts will write to each other in Akkadian — neither side's native language, but the language everyone has learned to use. The category "international lingua franca" is established here.
- **Throughline:** the first globalization — the seed is planted in this part.

### The deification of the living king
- **The change:** Naram-Sin, Sargon's grandson, declares himself a god during his lifetime. Not the favored of the gods. *A god.* The first ruler we know of to do this.
- **The personal moment:** the temple of Naram-Sin — built for him, by him, during his reign. The priests serving him as a god while he is sitting in the palace alive.
- **The community change:** the boundary between human rule and divine status is breached. Subsequent kings will copy this innovation, and it spreads.
- **The civilizational consequence:** every later self-deified ruler — Alexander, the Roman emperors, the Chinese emperors with their Mandate of Heaven, the divine-right monarchs of medieval Europe — descends from a tradition Naram-Sin opens. The category itself is created here.
- **The honest cost:** the curse-of-Akkad literature, composed after the empire's fall, blamed the collapse on Naram-Sin's impiety. Whether this is a contemporary view or a retrospective theological gloss is debated. Either way, it's one of the world's first "hubris destroys empire" narratives.
- **Throughline:** the deep weirdness of early statehood. Also the invention of historical memory itself.

## Contrarian-honest beats

- Sargon was probably not who his own propaganda says he was. The legend has eaten the man.
- The Gutians-blamed-for-the-collapse story is suspect. Empires fall for internal reasons; barbarian invaders are often a convenient scapegoat.
- The climate-collapse explanation is real but oversold in some recent pop history. Multi-causal.

## Anticipated questions & lesser-known facts

- **Was Sargon the first emperor in any meaningful sense?** Yes — he was the first ruler we know of who created a political unit binding together multiple cities of different ethnic and linguistic identity under a single administration. Earlier rulers had hegemonies; Sargon had something closer to an empire.
- **How did anyone hold an empire together without modern communications?** Through governors, hostages, and selective brutality. Akkadian governors were installed in conquered cities, often the king's own sons or trusted military officers. The children of subordinate rulers were kept at the imperial court — formally as honored guests, functionally as hostages.
- **Did the conquered Sumerians stop being Sumerian?** No. They kept their language for centuries after, alongside the imposed Akkadian. Bilingualism was the survival strategy. Sumerian became the prestige scholarly language even after it stopped being spoken — much like Latin in medieval Europe.
- **Why did the empire fall?** Honestly: a mix of causes. Possibly the 4.2-kiloyear climate event (drought), possibly internal succession crises, possibly overextension, possibly the Gutians (though this last is suspect). The honest answer is multi-causal and partly unknown.
- **Lesser-known:** Enheduanna's hymns to the goddess Inanna are arguably the foundation of the literary tradition that produced the biblical Psalms. The lineage is real, not metaphorical — Mesopotamian liturgical poetry directly influenced Hebrew religious literature through cultural transmission across more than a millennium.
- **Lesser-known:** Naram-Sin, Sargon's grandson, was the first Mesopotamian king to claim divinity in his own lifetime — to have himself worshiped as a god while still alive. This was a major innovation that later kings copied. The deification of living rulers is older than Alexander, older than the Roman emperors. Naram-Sin invented it.
- **Lesser-known:** the curse-of-Akkad literature, composed after the empire's fall, blamed the collapse on Naram-Sin's impiety — specifically his sacking of a major temple. This is one of the world's first "hubris destroys empire" narratives, predating Greek tragedy by 1,500 years.
