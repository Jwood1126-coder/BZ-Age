## The Draft

---

She is standing in the temple of the moon god at Ur, and she is angry.

Not because the moon god has displeased her. Because a usurper has thrown her out.

Her name is Enheduanna. She is the daughter of Sargon of Akkad — the man who built the first empire in human history — and she has been installed at Ur as the en-priestess, the high priestess of the moon god Nanna, as a deliberate act of political engineering: her father's way of binding the Sumerian south to his Akkadian north by placing someone of Akkadian blood in the most sacred religious role in the city. She has been there for years, doing the work, composing hymns to the goddess Inanna that are so remarkable that we still have them four thousand years later. She may be the first author in human history whose name we know. She is certainly among the earliest.

And now Lugal-ane, a rebel, has taken control of Ur, thrown her out of the temple, forced her into something she describes in her own words as "a hostile house." She is writing to Inanna. She is asking the goddess to restore her. The language she uses is not the formal elevated language of the official hymn — it is personal, urgent, the voice of someone who has been wronged by history and is appealing to the highest court she can access.

She writes: *You have made me fly like a swallow from the window.*

She writes: *My good name is destroyed.*

She writes: *I am Enheduanna, let me speak to you.*

The appeal works. Eventually the usurper is defeated. She goes back to the temple. She writes more hymns. She lives out her life as the most powerful woman in the most sacred role in one of the most important cities of the ancient world, in the empire her father built.

I am starting here — with her, with her anger, with her voice — because she holds in a single person everything this part is about. She is the first author. She is the first woman whose full voice reaches us from antiquity — not a character in someone else's story, not an accounting entry, not a name on a seal. A person who wrote, and whose writing survived, and whose writing tells us what she felt.

She is also a political instrument. Deliberately placed. Deliberately selected for her bloodline. Her role in Ur is not primarily religious, however fully she inhabits it. Her role is colonial administration through theological legitimacy.

She is the empire's project and its conscience at the same time.

---

So who was Sargon?

The honest answer is: we don't fully know, and what we know has been mostly composed by the people who came after him to serve the purposes of the people who came after him.

Sargon's own propaganda — preserved in later texts — tells us this: he was of humble birth, the son of a gardener, found as an infant in a basket floating on a river and rescued by a water-carrier. He rose through service to the king of Kish, eventually the city's cup-bearer, and then — through the favor of the goddess Ishtar, or through political maneuvering of considerable skill, or both, which is how these things usually work — became king himself.

You may have noticed that this origin story has a certain familiar shape. The infant in the basket on the water. Found and raised by a servant. Rose to greatness against all apparent odds.

Moses. And Romulus. And Perseus. And several others. The "foundling hero" birth narrative appears across cultures with such regularity that it has a name in folklore studies — the monomyth of the hero-of-humble-origin. Whether Sargon was actually of humble birth or whether the story was invented or embellished by later propagandists who needed the empire to have a suitably dramatic founding figure, we cannot say with confidence.

What we can say is this: Sargon did something that had never been done before. Around 2334 BCE, he began a series of military campaigns that brought the Sumerian city-states of the south under his control. One by one. City by city. Not through absorption — he was not Sumerian, his language was not Sumerian, his gods were not fully Sumerian — but through conquest and administration. He did not stop at the edge of the Sumerian world. He pushed further. His armies reached the Mediterranean in the west, the Taurus mountains in the north, possibly as far as the Gulf to the south.

By the end of his reign, Sargon of Akkad controlled more territory, more cities, more people of more different languages and cultures than any political entity in human history had ever controlled before.

He had invented the empire.

---

Now. What does an empire actually require?

This is the question I want to spend real time on, because "empire" can become a word that feels obvious when it is not obvious at all. The Sumerian city-states had been governing for centuries. They had law courts, tax systems, bureaucracies, standing armies of a sort. The temple-state model was sophisticated. What was the problem with just being a very powerful city-state?

The problem was that the other cities were not yours.

When your city-state is at war with the neighboring city-state, and you win, and you extract tribute and hostages and perhaps some territory and then go home — that is one category of thing. It is what the Sumerian city-states had been doing to each other for centuries. You win. You extract. You leave them to their own temple, their own king, their own scribal school.

What Sargon attempted was different. He attempted to make the other cities part of the same political structure as his own. Same ultimate ruler, same administrative language, same system of taxation and obligation, same flow of military and agricultural resources toward the same center. Not conquest-and-departure. Conquest-and-administration.

This requires things the city-state model had never needed to produce. You need governors who can function in cities that are not their home city, administering people who do not speak their language, whose gods are not their gods. You need a common language of administration — Sargon chose Akkadian, his own language, which was already widely spoken in northern Mesopotamia. You need a loyalty structure that reaches across linguistic and cultural lines, which means you need to either import loyal administrators or train local ones, with all the control problems that implies. You need supply lines. You need communication systems. You need some way of establishing your legitimacy to people who did not grow up inside your cosmology.

The last problem is where Enheduanna comes in.

Enheduanna is not just a propagandist, or not just a propagandist. She is a genuine solution to a genuine political problem. The Sumerian south had its own priests, its own theological traditions, its own claims to religious legitimacy that pre-dated Sargon by a thousand years. Installing your daughter as the en-priestess of the moon god at Ur — the most sacred position in one of the most sacred Sumerian cities — is a way of saying: the Akkadian ruler is not replacing your gods. The Akkadian ruler is endorsed by your gods. The legitimacy flows through.

It is also a way of keeping a trusted person in a strategic location. An en-priestess had real power. Real access. Real visibility. She was, in effect, the imperial administrator with the best possible cover story.

---

**[State of the World snapshot — Sumerian village under Akkadian rule, approximately 2270 BCE]**

The house has not changed.

That is the first thing to understand. The packed-earth floor is the same floor. The grain jars are the same jars. The sheep in the enclosure beside the house are the descendants of the sheep that were there before. Your tools are the same tools your grandfather used. The temple at the edge of the village square still faces the same direction it always faced, and the god who lives in it is still the god who has always lived in it.

What has changed is the name of the man who takes the grain.

Before, the grain went to the city, to the city's temple, under the authority of the city's king, whose legitimacy you understood because it was the legitimacy you had grown up with. The grain went to Ur, and Ur was your city, and Ur's king was the mortal husband of Ur's goddess, and that was the system.

Now there is an Akkadian governor in the marketplace. He was not appointed by your king. He was appointed by a king five hundred miles away, a king who speaks a language that sounds like Sumerian but isn't quite, a king whose goddess is not your goddess although apparently she and your goddess have reached some kind of arrangement. The grain still goes to the city. But the accounting records are now being kept by people who write your language as a second language, carefully, the way you write something you learned as an adult rather than something you absorbed as a child.

You are part of something called Akkad. You did not choose this. You were not asked.

The things your grandfather could not have imagined: that there would ever be a political authority beyond your city. That anyone would ever claim the right to send administrators to your city from outside your culture. That a king could be king of your city without being from your city. That the largest political unit you belonged to would not end at your city's walls.

The things your grandson will not be able to imagine: that it was ever any other way.

What is not in your life, in this world: chariots, still. Iron, still. Coins, still. An alphabet, still. Horses ridden in combat, still. The political imagination of empire, even now — you are *inside* it but you don't have a name for it. There is no word in your vocabulary for what Sargon has done, because nothing like it has existed before.

What is different from five hundred years ago, from the world of Part 2: the political unit has grown by an order of magnitude. The administrative language has been imposed from outside. The legitimacy claim now operates at a scale and across cultural distances that the early Sumerian temple-state never had to manage.

What is the same: the ground under your feet, the grain in your jars, the gods in your temples, the names of the people who matter to you.

Empire, in its first form, is most visible from inside as: everything is the same, and the person who takes the grain has changed.

---

Naram-Sin did something that requires more careful attention than it usually gets.

Sargon's grandson was, by the evidence of his own propaganda, an extraordinary military commander and an ambitious innovator. He pushed the empire's boundaries further than his grandfather had, including into regions nobody had thought to push into before. He built. He crushed revolts with what the inscriptions describe as pitiless efficiency.

And then he made himself a god.

Not the favored of the gods — that was standard language for Mesopotamian kings going back to the beginnings. Favored by the gods, protected by the gods, divinely appointed — that was the standard package. Naram-Sin went further. He added a divine determinative before his name in inscriptions. He was Naram-Sin, god of Akkad. Present tense. Not posthumously deified. Not symbolically divine. A god, during his own lifetime, serving as king.

We don't have a record of what the Sumerian priesthood thought of this. We can guess. The theological tradition in which they worked had a fairly clear line between human and divine, and Naram-Sin was erasing it while he was still breathing.

What we do have is the curse-of-Akkad text, composed after the empire's fall, which places the blame for that fall squarely on Naram-Sin's impiety. According to this text — and this is one of the world's first "empire falls for hubris" narratives, roughly 1,500 years before Greek tragedy developed the same idea — Naram-Sin sacked a major temple. The gods were enraged. The Gutians, a people from the mountains, swept down and destroyed the empire as divine punishment.

Is this historically accurate? Almost certainly not as a complete explanation. It is theologically satisfying. It is narratively powerful. It places the catastrophe inside a moral framework that makes sense of senseless things.

The catastrophe, by the way, was real. The Akkadian Empire collapsed in the period of roughly 2200 to 2100 BCE. The Gutians were real — they appear in the sources, and they caused genuine disruption. But empire collapse tends to work like most complex-system failures: multiple causes converging, some of them visible in advance, some not, and the one cause that gets remembered is usually the most dramatic one rather than the most consequential.

The 4.2-kiloyear event is the most recent contender for "most consequential cause." This was a significant drought that hit large parts of the Near East in approximately 2200 BCE — we know this from tree-ring data, from sediment cores, from the pollen record. If you were an agricultural empire dependent on grain harvests from a region that suddenly experienced a prolonged drought, your administrative system — which was already strained by the difficulty of governing people who didn't share your language or traditions — would crack, and then break.

We don't know how much of the Akkadian collapse was drought, and how much was internal succession crisis, and how much was external pressure, and how much was the inherent limits of the administrative model Sargon had invented. The honest answer is: all of these, in proportions we can't fully determine.

What we do know is that the Akkadian Empire fell, and that its legend survived it by two thousand years.

---

Here is the thing about the Akkadian Empire that I think matters most:

It failed. And the idea survived.

Every subsequent ruler in Mesopotamia and beyond who aspired to control more than one city — more than one culture, more than one language — was working within a template that Sargon's experiment had created and that Sargon's failure had proven was achievable, even if it was hard to sustain. The Persian Empire, the Macedonian Empire, the Roman Empire — all of them were variations on a form that was invented here, in the flat alluvial plain of southern Iraq, by a man whose actual birth story we will never fully know.

Empires are hard. They are always hard. They require administrative solutions to problems that don't have clean solutions — how do you govern people who don't share your gods, your language, your sense of what legitimacy means? How do you extract resources from distant regions without the extraction triggering revolts that cost more than the resources are worth? How do you maintain a standing army large enough to project force over vast distances while also paying for the administration that makes the force projection meaningful?

Sargon's successors ran into all of these problems. So did every empire that came after them. The problems are intrinsic to the form.

And yet empires kept being built, because the people who built them could look at what Sargon had done and say: it is possible. It is achievable. We failed last time; we will do it differently this time.

The history of empire, which is a very long history indeed, begins here. With a man we know mostly from his propaganda, and his daughter, who wrote her name in the clay and whose anger and faith survived everything that came after her.

---

After the Akkadian Empire fell, the Sumerian world attempted a revival. The Third Dynasty of Ur — Ur III, as it's called — created something like a bureaucratic renaissance: meticulous record-keeping, a systematized tax structure, administrative innovation that may have been, in sheer administrative density, more sophisticated than anything Sargon had built. We have hundreds of thousands of Ur III tablets. The empire was, in some ways, better organized than its Akkadian predecessor.

It also fell. Faster.

And after it fell, the world fragmented back into the city-state pattern — not quite the same city-state pattern as before, because the political imagination had been expanded and could not be unexpanded, but a period of smaller units, competing polities, regional powers, which is the landscape we enter in Part 4.

What comes out of all of this is not just political history. What comes out of it is a pattern that will repeat. Complexity builds. An innovative administrative form appears and seems to solve problems that nothing had solved before. The form works, for a while. And then the thing that made it fragile from the beginning — the distance, the cultural diversity, the extraction economy, the climate stress, the internal succession crisis — asserts itself, and the experiment ends.

We have seen this once. We will see it again.

The first empire failed. The idea of empire persisted. That gap — between the failure of the form and the persistence of the idea — is where the political history of the next four thousand years lives.

---

Egypt.

I should have said more about Egypt before now, and I'm going to say more in a minute, but I want to place it here because the comparison matters.

While Sargon was building in Mesopotamia, Egypt's Old Kingdom pharaohs were doing something structurally similar — building a centralized, administratively sophisticated state that exercised control over a vast territory — but doing it differently in almost every specific. Egypt had the Nile. The Nile is a flood-managed monoculture engine: predictable, centrally controlled, producing enormous surplus that flows naturally to whoever controls the flood management and irrigation distribution. Mesopotamian rivers are less predictable, less manageable, prone to catastrophic floods and droughts that even sophisticated engineering can only partially mitigate.

Egypt's centralization was possible partly because of geography. Mesopotamia's centralization required more institutional improvisation because the geography pushed back harder.

And when Egypt's Old Kingdom collapsed — which it did, in the First Intermediate Period, around 2160 BCE — it collapsed differently from the way Akkad collapsed. Egypt fragmented into regional powers but retained its cultural continuity. The pharaoh as an institution survived even when the specific pharaoh's power collapsed. The language, the religion, the administrative forms — all of this was preserved through the fragmentation.

Mesopotamia was messier. The Akkadian empire left institutions but also left trauma. The Sumerian literary tradition that we have — which is preserved in the scribal schools of the later Ur III period and the early Old Babylonian period — contains lament texts about the destructions, about the gods abandoning cities, about the particular grief of a city losing its divine protector.

The lament genre is one of the oldest literary forms we have. And it is, specifically, a form for processing civilizational catastrophe. The Sumerians developed it because they experienced civilizational catastrophe. Multiple times.

This, too, is a pattern that will recur.

---

Exit.

The Akkadian Empire's fall didn't end empire. It ended this empire.

What the period from roughly 2000 to 1800 BCE looks like, as we approach the transition to Part 4, is not chaos exactly, but something more like regrouping. The Sumerian revival and collapse of Ur III. The fragmentation into Isin, Larsa, Eshnunna, the dozens of competing city-states that jockey for position across the Mesopotamian plain. To the north and west, the Old Assyrian trading networks beginning to establish themselves. To the northwest, the various Amorite groups establishing kingdoms — including, eventually, a dynasty in a minor city called Babylon that will produce a king named Hammurabi.

And in Anatolia — the high plateau to the northwest of Mesopotamia, the land that will later be called Turkey — something new is building. A people called the Hattians, then the Hittites after Indo-European migrants blended with them, are developing what will eventually become a major empire.

The world is getting complicated.

And in that complication, for the first time, we can hear individual voices. Not just the scribal tradition's accounting and hymns. Individual people writing to each other about real things that happened to them.

Part 4 is where we meet them.
