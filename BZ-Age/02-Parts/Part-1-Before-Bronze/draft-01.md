## The Draft

---

Your feet hurt.

That's the first thing. That's always the first thing.

Dawn is barely a suggestion in the east — a lighter shade of black at the edge of the sky — and you are already awake because you were never quite asleep, because when you sleep it's on a mat on packed earth and something always hurts. Your back. Your hip. A tooth that's been wrong for weeks. The cold in the hours before dawn, which cuts through the wool and the smoke in ways you've stopped really noticing but which have been slowly ground-walking you toward something that will end your life far earlier than it would have ended if you had made different choices three generations ago. Choices that were not, strictly speaking, yours.

You are approximately forty years old. You are old.

The fire is low. Someone needs to feed it. Outside, a sheep has been wrong all night — that low intermittent sound you hear even in the brief periods when you sleep. Somewhere in the darkness near the storehouse, there is the particular smell of grain that has been stored just long enough that you need to decide today whether it's still good. The whole single room of the structure you live in — packed-earth floor, mudbrick walls, smoke-blackened timber overhead — holds about a dozen smells simultaneously, none of them what anyone from five thousand years in the future would call pleasant. This is your home.

The year is approximately 5000 BCE. The world — meaning the world as you understand it, the world that is real to you, the world that matters — is about forty kilometers in every direction. Beyond that are other places, which have names that are also rumors. The largest number of people you have ever seen gathered in one place at one time was maybe two hundred, at a seasonal gathering that had something to do with the new moon and the beginning of the planting season, and it felt like a city.

There are no cities.

No — stop. I want you to actually sit with that.

There are no cities. Anywhere. On the planet Earth, in the year 5000 BCE, there is no settlement that deserves that name — no concentration of people large enough and permanent enough and functionally differentiated enough to be called a city. The largest human settlements in the world right now hold a few thousand people at most, and they are extraordinary exceptions. Most people live the way you do: in villages of thirty to a hundred souls, surrounded by their fields, with a horizon that functions as the edge of the knowable world.

Nothing has been written down. Not here. Not anywhere. There is no writing. Everything that humans know — every story, every technique, every memory, every cosmology, every lie — exists in human minds, passed mouth to ear, generation to generation, with all the distortion and loss that implies. There is no record. When people die, what they knew dies with them unless they told it to someone who lived.

No bronze. No metal of any working significance, not for you, not here, not yet. Your tools are stone — obsidian where it's available, flint, bone, wood. Better than nothing. Incomparably worse than what's coming. You don't know what's coming.

No wheel. Or — to be more precise — the wheel exists somewhere, far away, in the sense that clever people have been reinventing it independently for centuries without the concept quite locking in and spreading. But you haven't seen one. You carry things on your back and your head and your animals' backs. There is no vehicle in your world that rolls.

No horse. Not as a riding or working animal, anyway. The horses in your world — if you've seen them, and you might not have — are wild, and they are prey. Sheep, goats, cattle, pigs, dogs. These are your animals. They are also your wealth, your protein, your wool, your traction, and in bad years, your buffer against starvation.

No state. No king. No palace, no temple-complex, no standing army, no professional priesthood, no professional anything. There are people who are better at certain things than others — the woman in your village who knows plants the way most people know faces, the man three households over who seems to understand what the weather is going to do two days before anyone else does. There are people with more authority than others. But the apparatus of the state — the institution that can compel you, tax you, conscript your children, build roads, conduct diplomacy, field armies — does not exist. The human political imagination has not yet produced it.

No money. No market in anything like the modern sense. What moves between people here is gift and obligation and barter and debt, mostly debt, the invisible web of who owes whom what favor, which binds a village together just as surely as anything visible. But there is no coin, no accounting system, no general-purpose medium of exchange. You trade in things.

That is the world. That is the baseline.

And I want you to hold this world in your head — feel it, smell it, feel the cold floor under your feet at dawn — because you're going to need it. We're going to spend the next several hours watching that world change. And the change is easier to feel if you can feel where it started.

This is what we're doing here.

---

My name doesn't matter. What matters is that I've spent more time than is probably healthy thinking about the Bronze Age — which is not a period most people think about at all, which is, honestly, part of the appeal. Ask most people to name a Bronze Age figure and they'll give you Achilles, or Agamemnon, and they're not entirely wrong, but they're drawing from the story that the Bronze Age's *survivors* told about the Bronze Age, which is not the same thing as the Bronze Age itself. Ask most people what the Bronze Age was, and you'll get something like "before iron, there was bronze, and then iron replaced it, and history moved on." Which is true in the way that "a house fire started, burned for a while, then stopped" is true — accurate as far as it goes, and missing almost everything.

The Bronze Age ran, depending on where you are and who's counting, from roughly 3500 BCE to somewhere around 1200 BCE. That is twenty-three hundred years. To give you a sense of scale: the entire history of Western civilization, from the fall of Rome to right now, is about sixteen hundred years. The Bronze Age is longer than that. It contains more history than you probably have room for in your head right now, and that's fine — we're going to build the room.

What happened during the Bronze Age, at its peak, was something genuinely strange: for the first time in human history, something resembling a globalized world existed. Trade networks that spanned continents. Letters between kings who had never met, addressed as "my brother," conducting diplomacy in a shared diplomatic language. Merchants who traveled from the Anatolian plateau to the heart of modern Iraq and back, carrying goods that had been manufactured in places they had never been. A palace economy that tracked the movements of thousands of workers, millions of rations, entire herds — in writing. In writing. With a bureaucracy.

And then, in the space of about fifty years in the second half of the twelfth century BCE, almost all of it collapsed.

The palace economies of the eastern Mediterranean — Egypt, the Hittites, Mycenaean Greece, Ugarit, Assur — all of them went silent or broke or contracted in ways that the modern world has no precise analogy for. Cities that had stood for a thousand years were abandoned. Trade routes that had moved copper and tin and olive oil and purple cloth across the known world shut down. The art of writing, in some places, was forgotten entirely. The Bronze Age ended not with a transition but with a catastrophe.

That catastrophe — how it happened, why it happened, what it felt like from the inside, and what survived it — is ultimately what this series is about. But we can't understand the catastrophe without understanding what was lost. And we can't understand what was lost without going back much further than the catastrophe, to the world before any of it existed, to the foundational choices that humans made — or stumbled into — that made the Bronze Age possible.

So we start here. In the dark. In the smoke. In the forty-year-old body with the bad hip and the tooth that needs attention. In the world before.

---

Let's talk about the most consequential mistake humans ever made.

I call it a mistake because that's the honest read of the bioarchaeological evidence. Whether it actually *was* a mistake — whether any collective act that no individual chose, that unfolded over thousands of years, that enabled every complexity we've discussed and are about to discuss, can meaningfully be called a mistake — is a question I'm going to leave open. But in the strict sense of "did this make individual human lives worse," the evidence is uncomfortable, and we're going to look at it directly.

The agricultural revolution. The shift from foraging to farming. The moment — or rather, the long drawn-out process — by which humans went from following the food to making the food stay in one place.

This is where the story we're telling actually begins, which is not quite where you might expect. The Bronze Age nominally starts around 3500 BCE with the first cities in Sumer. But the Bronze Age is built on the Neolithic, and the Neolithic is built on the agricultural revolution, and if you don't understand what happened in those earlier transitions, the later story floats. It becomes an interesting sequence of events rather than a process you can feel. And the process is what we're after.

Here's the thing about the agricultural revolution that every survey course in Western civilization gets wrong: it was not a discovery. It was not an invention. There was no morning when someone woke up, looked at the grain growing wild in the field, and thought: *I should plant that*. The process of plant and animal domestication was a mutual adaptation that unfolded over hundreds and in some cases thousands of years, happening independently in multiple places around the world, driven not by individual genius but by the slow statistical pressure of people doing slightly different things and seeing slightly different results and passing those results on to their children.

In the Fertile Crescent — the arc of land running from modern Israel and Jordan through Lebanon, Syria, southern Turkey, down through Iraq and into Iran — wild grasses that were ancestral to wheat and barley grew in dense stands on the hillsides. Forager groups moved through this landscape seasonally, harvesting the wild grain when it was ripe, moving on. At some point — we can't quite pinpoint it, and that's fine, the discipline here is to let that be fine — some groups began coming back to the same stands, began harvesting more deliberately, began possibly spreading the seeds of the most productive plants.

And then their grandchildren were farmers.

Not all at once. Not everywhere. The transition from foraging to farming took millennia even in the regions where it happened fastest, and it happened differently in different places — independently in China, independently in Mesoamerica, independently in the New Guinea highlands, independently in sub-Saharan Africa and the Andes. The agricultural revolution is not a single event. It's a thing that humans, apparently, have a tendency to do when the conditions are right, over and over, in different places, in different ways.

But in the Fertile Crescent, the evidence for what this transition did to the people who made it is detailed enough that we can look at it directly. And what we find, when we look directly at it, is not comfortable.

The bones tell the story.

Bioarchaeologists — the people who study ancient human remains — have been comparing forager populations to early farming populations for decades now, and the trend is consistent enough across enough sites that it's worth stating plainly: by the measures that matter for individual human wellbeing, the early farmers were worse off than the foragers who came before them. They were shorter — a centimeter or two on average, sometimes more. Their bones show more signs of chronic stress, more evidence of nutritional deficiency. Their dental health was markedly worse — farming diets tend toward grain, which ferments in your mouth, which is bad for teeth; forager diets tend toward diversity, which is better for teeth. They died younger. They showed more signs of arthritis — specifically the kind associated with repetitive labor, because farming is relentless repetitive labor in a way that foraging, even strenuous foraging, tends not to be.

And they were sick more often. Because they lived in permanent settlements with animals, which is an excellent way to catch diseases. Because population density creates disease vectors that dispersed foraging populations avoid. Because you can't just move when the water source becomes contaminated. You stay, because you have a field.

The modern analogy that comes to mind — and I want to flag that this is an analogy, it doesn't map perfectly, but it's useful — is the Industrial Revolution. Eighteenth and nineteenth century industrialization produced enormous aggregate wealth. It produced new kinds of cities, new kinds of goods, eventually better lives for the people who came a few generations later. And the people who actually lived through the transition — who moved from cottage industries and subsistence farming to factory work in new industrial cities — by most measurable standards, for several generations, had worse lives than the people who came immediately before them. The aggregate wealth was real. The individual cost was also real. History does this, sometimes. Progress — if we even want to use that word — can be bad for the people who live through it.

The agricultural revolution was worse. The time scale was longer. The costs were paid by more generations.

So why did it happen?

The frustrating honest answer is: because populations win evolutionary competitions even when individual members pay the cost. A forager band of thirty people and a farming village of a hundred and fifty people occupy the same valley. The farming village can field more workers. They clear more land. They eat more total calories. They have more children — not because individual farmers are healthier, but because sedentary groups with stored food can support more pregnancies and more infants than mobile groups that have to carry everything they own. The farming village expands. The forager band, over generations, either adopts farming practices itself or gets pushed out or absorbed.

The transition, once it started, was a ratchet. Once population density in a region rose high enough that the land could no longer support forager lifestyles at that density, you could not go back. The option was gone. The farmers had outcompeted the foragers not because their lives were better but because their *population growth rate* was higher, and population growth rates beat individual wellbeing in competition. The choice — if it ever was a choice — was made, in some meaningful sense, by demographic pressures, not by people.

This is one of the deepest and most uncomfortable things I know about human history. The foundational transition of the last twelve thousand years — the thing that made cities and writing and states and empires and the Bronze Age and eventually everything you and I live in possible — was, at the level of individual lived experience, mostly not good. The individual deal was bad. The aggregate deal, eventually, after many generations, was transformative. And no one chose it. It just happened.

Sit with that.

---

I want to talk about a place called Göbekli Tepe, because it breaks the story in a way that turns out to be more interesting than the unbroken version.

Göbekli Tepe is an archaeological site in southeastern Turkey, excavated by Klaus Schmidt starting in the mid-1990s and now continued by Schmidt's successors. It consists of a series of stone enclosures, arranged in a rough ring, each containing large T-shaped stone pillars up to five or six meters tall and weighing ten to twenty tons each, decorated with bas-relief carvings of animals — foxes, vultures, scorpions, aurochs, gazelles, cranes. The carvings are detailed, skillful, clearly intentional. The whole complex was, at some point, deliberately buried — carefully backfilled — and then more enclosures were built on top, or nearby, and those were eventually buried too. The site was a construction project that went on for centuries.

Here is the thing about Göbekli Tepe that should be making you revise a mental model right now: it is older than agriculture. Not a little older. About a thousand years older, in this region. The people who built Göbekli Tepe were not farmers. They were foragers. Hunter-gatherers. They moved with the seasons. They had no permanent villages, no stored grain, no domesticated animals except possibly dogs.

They built this.

We do not know why. This is important. We do not know. We have hypotheses — Schmidt himself thought it was a ritual center, a place where the animal world was invoked and the dead were commemorated, and that hypothesis has some support but also critics. Some have argued that the labor involved in building Göbekli Tepe was so large that it *required* the invention of agriculture to feed the workers — making Göbekli Tepe not a product of civilization but a *cause* of it, the ritual motivation that preceded and possibly drove the economic motivation. This is a genuinely contested hypothesis, and I am not going to tell you it's settled, because it isn't.

What I can tell you is what Göbekli Tepe does to the standard story.

The standard story goes: agriculture → surplus → permanent settlements → specialization → division of labor → monumental architecture → complexity → eventually, eventually, cities. In that story, the economic comes first. Humans get reliable food, they stop moving, they accumulate, they specialize, they build things. The ritual and the monumental are downstream of the economic.

Göbekli Tepe says: no. Or at least: not here, not in this order, not necessarily. Whatever motivated the people who built it — the dead, the animals, the sky, some now-inaccessible cosmology that we can only see the edges of in the carvings — it was strong enough to organize enormous collective effort before any of the economic preconditions the standard story requires were in place. These were mobile foragers. They organized, they quarried, they carved, they dragged ten-ton stones, they arranged them with clear intention, for purposes we cannot read.

We do not know what they thought they were doing.

I find this more interesting, not less, than having a tidy explanation. One of the habits I want this series to build — one of the disciplines I want to try to hold onto through however many hours we end up spending together — is the habit of letting genuine mystery be genuine mystery. Ancient people did things we cannot explain. Their minds are not transparent to us. They had reasons we cannot fully reconstruct. When we reach for a tidy explanation — when we say "oh, they built this because of grain storage logistics" or "they built this because of social competition among elites" — we are sometimes right, and we are sometimes projecting, and it is not always possible to know which we're doing. Better to say: we see what they built, we can infer something about the effort it required, we cannot yet explain what they were thinking. And that gap in our knowledge is real, and it's part of the story.

Göbekli Tepe sits at the beginning of this series as a warning. Even before we have states, before we have cities, before we have writing or bronze or any of the apparatus of the world we're about to explore, humans were doing things that do not fit easily into the modern frame. The deep weirdness starts this early. Keep that in mind.

---

Back to the Neolithic farmer.

By 5000 BCE — which is where we placed ourselves at the beginning — the agricultural revolution in the Fertile Crescent has been going on for several thousand years. The first farming villages appeared somewhere around 9000 BCE. So we're already well into the world that agriculture made. The foragers who preceded the farmers are, in most of the region, a memory. The permanent village is the basic unit of human life.

What does that life actually look like?

We know more than you might expect, because archaeologists have been digging up Neolithic villages for a century and a half, and the material record is rich in certain ways even where the documentary record is nonexistent. I want to spend some time here, because this is where the ground-level discipline has to begin — the commitment we're making for this whole series, to spend real time in real lives rather than in the abstract movements of civilizations. Civilizations don't experience anything. People do.

Çatalhöyük is the site I keep coming back to, because it's strange enough to be interesting and well-excavated enough to actually tell us things. It's in south-central Turkey — Anatolia — and it was continuously occupied from roughly 7500 BCE to about 5700 BCE. At its height, it held perhaps eight thousand people, which makes it genuinely unusual for its period — most Neolithic settlements were much smaller. In a meaningful sense, Çatalhöyük is almost a proto-city, though the people there hadn't invented the idea of a city yet and it shows in the layout, which has no streets, no central plaza, no organized public space at all. Houses were built directly against each other, sharing walls, with entry and exit through holes in the roof. You lived inside your house; you crossed your neighbor's roof to get there. The settlement was, essentially, a continuous mass of adjacent rooms, entered from above.

Inside those rooms, the material record is detailed. Plaster floors, replastered again and again — Ian Hodder's team at Çatalhöyük has counted up to fifty layers of replastered floor in some buildings, meaning the same house was maintained, renewed, lived in, for generations. Obsidian tools — flint knappers knew what they were doing; the obsidian is sourced and the tools are good. Awls for leather, needles for wool. Grindstones everywhere, because grain is the dietary center of this world and grain needs grinding, and grinding is relentless physical labor, almost entirely performed by women based on the upper-body skeletal changes the work produces.

And, in the walls and under the floors: the dead.

At Çatalhöyük, people buried their dead under the floor of the house they lived in. Children, mostly, given the childhood mortality rates, but adults too. The bones were sometimes retrieved after burial and rearranged, the skulls especially — some skulls were plastered and painted, treated as objects in ways that suggest the ancestor was still somehow present in the household. We don't know what they believed. We can see what they did.

The wall paintings are there too — stylized animals, hunting scenes, vultures with human-headed figures below them (which may have something to do with excarnation rituals, the practice of leaving bodies to be defleshed by birds before burial, which is attested elsewhere in the region). Red ochre on the walls. Geometric patterns. The visual world of Çatalhöyük is not primitive in the dismissive sense — these people had an aesthetic, a cosmology, a set of practices around life and death and the animal world that was coherent to them. It's just not fully coherent to us.

What they didn't have was metal. Or writing. Or the wheel. Or states. Or money.

And in 5000 BCE — the moment we started in — what they're beginning to have, in some places, in some forms, is something new: the faintest early whisper of something that will change everything.

---

The story of metallurgy is usually told as discovery: someone found some unusual rocks, heated them, got something shiny out of them, and everything changed. This is not quite right. It is, like the agricultural revolution, more gradual, more ambiguous, and more expensive to the actual humans involved than the tidy version suggests.

Native copper — copper that occurs naturally in metallic form, not bound in ore, just lying there being coppery — has been worked by humans since at least 8000 BCE in some parts of the world. It can be hammered cold into simple shapes. This is not smelting; it's more like stonework with a softer material. Anatolian and Near Eastern sites show hammered copper beads and pins from very early on. But copper in this form is rare and soft and doesn't do most of the things you need tools to do.

Smelting — the process of heating copper ore hot enough to extract the metal — appears sometime around 5000 BCE in the Fertile Crescent and Anatolia, give or take several centuries depending on who's counting and what sites they're working from. This is a real technological leap. It requires recognizing that certain kinds of rock, when heated enough and in the right way, yield a usable material. It requires building fires hot enough — around 1083 degrees Celsius for copper, which is hotter than you can achieve with a wood fire alone, you need bellows or draft arrangements of some kind. It requires a chain of knowledge about ore sources and fuel and technique that does not accumulate overnight.

How did people figure it out? We don't know. One hypothesis is that it happened in or near pottery kilns, where temperatures could be pushed high enough and where mineral-rich materials might have been introduced by accident. Another is that it developed from the practice of firing malachite — a green copper carbonate ore — for use as pigment, and that someone noticed the metallic behavior of the product under heat. These are plausible. They are not proved.

What we do know is that copper metallurgy, once it appears, spreads. The techniques move, presumably with metalworkers — people whose skill is rare and valuable, who can demand premium treatment from communities that want access to metal tools and ornaments. The trade in metal objects and in metal itself begins early. And it creates, from the very start, the pattern that will define the Bronze Age until it kills it: the materials for metalworking are not evenly distributed across the landscape. Copper ores are where they are. Tin ores are where they are. If you need both, and you're in a place that has neither, you need trade networks. You need supply chains. You need a world connected by commerce. The metallurgical constraint — the fundamental inequality of access to the materials of the Bronze Age — is built into the technology from its first moment.

Now: copper. Copper is too soft. This is the practical problem. You can make copper pins, copper ornaments, copper awls, copper axes that will hold an edge better than stone. But copper bends. Under the stress of real agricultural or military use, a copper tool is disappointing. The Bronze Age is not named for copper.

At some point — and the evidence here is genuinely murky enough that I'm not going to pretend it isn't — someone discovered that adding a small amount of another metal to molten copper produced something dramatically better. The alloy is harder, holds an edge better, flows more cleanly into molds. The alloy is bronze. The question is: which additive?

Here is where the story gets genuinely strange, and strange in a way that matters for understanding the people involved.

The classic answer is tin. Copper plus tin, in roughly a nine-to-one ratio, produces true bronze — the material that gives the age its name. And this is correct. True bronze with tin appears in the Near East by sometime around 3000 BCE, possibly earlier in some regions.

But the first "bronzes" — the earliest copper alloys showing up in the record before true tin-bronze becomes widespread — often used arsenic instead. Arsenical bronze. It works. It's harder than copper. It can be produced in regions where tin is unavailable but arsenic-bearing ores occur, which in the Near East means quite a lot of the available copper ore naturally contains small amounts of arsenic anyway, so some arsenical bronzes may not even have been intentionally produced.

The problem with arsenical bronze — the problem the metalworkers who produced it were experiencing in real time, in their bodies, without any framework for understanding what was happening to them — is that arsenic is a poison. Working with arsenical ores in an enclosed space, inhaling the fumes, day after day, year after year, deposits arsenic in the body. Arsenical poisoning at occupational levels causes peripheral neuropathy — damage to the nerves of the hands and feet. It causes lameness. It causes what looks, in the skeletal record, like chronic degenerative change.

There is a theory, not fully settled but intriguing, that the mythological smiths of multiple ancient traditions — Hephaestus in Greek mythology, Goibhniu in Irish, Weyland the Smith in Germanic — are described as lame. And that this lameness might be a cultural memory of what metalworking did to actual metalworkers. That the god of the forge walks with a limp because the smiths who worked with arsenical bronze walked with a limp.

We cannot prove this. The connection between mythological lame smiths and arsenical bronze toxicity is speculation, and I'll label it as speculation, even though I find it beautiful in the specific way that plausible connections between deep history and surviving myth tend to be beautiful. What we can say with more confidence is that arsenical bronze metallurgy was, among other things, occupational poison. The first humans to make the material that would define an age were, in many cases, being slowly killed by their craft.

This is a recurring shape in the series. The turning points — the great changes that remake the world — tend to carry a cost. Sometimes the cost falls on people we can identify. Sometimes it falls on people whose names we will never know, whose bones we find in archaeological contexts, whose faces we cannot reconstruct. The first metalworkers are in the second category. They made a world they couldn't see the edges of, at a price they couldn't understand, in service of something — the social prestige of metal, the practical advantage of better tools, maybe the sheer pleasure of working a material that does something stone won't do — that they could not have named in the terms we'd use.

You hold the first bronze blade. You have never felt anything like this. It's heavier than you expected and lighter than a good stone tool and it cuts — you drag it across a knot of rough cordage and it cuts, cleanly, without the chipping and pushing and careful angling that stone always requires. It just cuts. The world has changed in a way you can feel in your hand.

You do not know that you are dying.

You will be dead in fifteen years. The thing killing you is invisible and tasteless and has no name in your vocabulary. The limping will get worse before the end. Your hands will stop working reliably about a year before you die. You will be famous in your village for what you make — the tools, the ornaments, the ceremonial objects that move through the gift networks of the community and mark the moments that matter — and you will have a better diet and a more interesting life and a kind of respect that most people in your village will not have, and you will be dead at fifty-five.

Your children will learn your craft. They will also die young.

Two generations after you, someone in a place you never heard of will figure out that tin — traded in from much farther away, expensive, requiring a supply chain you cannot imagine — produces a better result than arsenic and without the particular way your hands feel wrong. The world will shift again. The tin will need to come from somewhere. Trade routes will need to cross distances that your village, with its forty-kilometer horizon, cannot conceive of.

The metallurgical constraint is built in from the beginning. Tin is not where copper is. Copper is not where people want bronze. The world will have to connect to solve this problem, and the world connecting will produce, eventually, the most extraordinary moment we're going to discuss — and, eventually, its end.

But we are not there yet.

---

It's 3200 BCE. You are two thousand years past the Neolithic village we started in, and I want to mark what has changed, because the pace of change in this period is not obvious and it's easy to underestimate.

The village is still the basic unit of human life for most people. But the villages are different. There is metal now — not everywhere, not for everyone, but it exists, and it moves. There is trade — longer-distance than before, more specialized, carrying objects and materials that a single region cannot produce on its own. There is social stratification, visible in the graves.

The Varna necropolis in modern Bulgaria is one of the earliest large cemeteries in the world that shows what stratification looks like when it shows up in material form. It dates to around 4500 BCE — so late Neolithic, before bronze, but already well into the copper-working period. And what the graves at Varna show is that, by 4500 BCE, some people were being buried with objects of extraordinary value — gold, lots of it, more gold in some graves than archaeologists had found in the entire rest of the prehistoric record combined up to that point — while most graves were ordinary.

One grave in particular, Grave 43, contained gold objects weighing more than six kilograms in total. A penis sheath of gold. Bracelets of gold. An axe of solid gold that was almost certainly ceremonial — it would not hold up in actual use. And in the same cemetery, many graves with almost nothing. The stratification is already visible. The idea that some people are different from other people — fundamentally, materially, visibly, in ways that survive death and can be seen by the living — is already in the world.

We don't know the name of the person in Grave 43. We know what they were buried with. We know they had a lot.

And we know that the accumulation of surplus — the grain that didn't get eaten this season, the cattle that weren't slaughtered last winter, the metal that wasn't traded away yet — is the material precondition for everything that follows. You can't have cities without surplus. You can't have specialists without surplus to support them. You can't have states without surplus to extract. The surplus isn't everything. But without it, nothing else is possible.

The Neolithic world, across two thousand years, has been building up a head of steam. The agricultural revolution has produced populations dense enough to support specialization. The specialization has produced metalworkers and potters and traders. The trade has produced the beginning of the long-distance networks that will become the Bronze Age's circulatory system. The stratification has produced elites with the interest and the resources to sponsor everything that follows.

In southern Mesopotamia — the flat alluvial plain between the Tigris and Euphrates, the region the Greeks will call Babylonia, the region whose ancient inhabitants we call Sumer — the head of steam is about to vent.

Something extraordinary is about to happen.

It's going to be loud.
