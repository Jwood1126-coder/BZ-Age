# Part 7 — The Collapse

**Status:** Skeleton.
**Working length:** Provisional 3–5 hours. The dramatic heart of the series.

---

## Core problem this part solves

The collapse needs to be told as a system failing, not as a mystery to be solved. Multiple causes converging. The Sea Peoples as part of the story but not the whole. The Carlin move here is to stay with the human experience of it — what it would have felt like to be in one of those cities as the fires started. Do not turn it into a whodunit. Render it.

---

## Beats to develop

- **The Ugarit letter in the kiln** — the warning sent but never delivered, found at the moment of the city's destruction. Open here.
- **The cascade.** City after city burns. The timeline.
- **Hattusa's end.** The Hittite Empire vanishes from history.
- **The Mycenaean palaces destroyed.** The Linear B tablets baked into preservation by the fires.
- **Egypt's survival, barely.** Ramses III's victory inscriptions over the Sea Peoples (read past the propaganda).
- **The multi-causal model** — climate, internal revolt, supply chain breakdown, succession crises, migration, possibly disease. All interacting.

---

## Ground-level material (required content)

This is one of the parts where ground-level material is the heart of the story. What it would have felt like to be in Hattusa in its last days. What it would have felt like to be in Ugarit when the warning letter was being inscribed. The survivors picking through the rubble. The slow loss of skills as the last scribe in a village dies and there's nobody to replace him. The collapse from inside, not from the historian's distance.

## State of the World snapshot (seed — Rule 10)

**Placement:** Middle of part, after the cascade has begun but before the final destruction beats. Brief but devastating — what is still here, what is already gone, what is about to go.

**Vantage:** A merchant in a coastal Levantine city around 1180 BCE who has just received news from a port two days' sail away. Or a Hittite courier who has reached the capital and found it empty.

**Sensory rings:** the smoke of the previous city's destruction is visible from this one. The familiar trade routes have gone silent. Ships expected weeks ago have not arrived. The palace functionaries are still in their offices but have no one to write to anymore — the courts they corresponded with last year are ash.

**What is gone:** the long-distance trade network. The grain shipments. The reliable expectation that foreign ports will be there next month. The diplomatic system. The sense that the world is connected and known.

**What remains:** local agriculture. Local crafts. The bronze-working knowledge in your craftsmen's heads. The cuneiform tablets in the archive — for whatever they will be worth now.

**What is about to go:** much of the literacy, because there will be no one to administer. Much of the long-distance metalwork, because the tin will stop arriving. Much of the population of this city, because cities cannot feed themselves without their hinterland and their network.

**The Carlin move here:** "Imagine that everything you depend on — every supply chain, every diplomatic relationship, every assumption that goods will arrive next month — is failing at once, and you can see it failing, and there is nothing you can do to stop it."

**Length:** 3–4 minutes. This snapshot is part of the climactic emotional work of the series.

## Turning Points (seeds — Rule 11)

### The collapse itself — the inverse turning point
- **The change:** the cascade of destructions across the eastern Mediterranean and Near East. Hattusa burned and abandoned. Ugarit destroyed. The Mycenaean palaces destroyed. Most of the Late Bronze Age palace civilization ends in a few decades.
- **The personal moment:** the scribe at Ugarit inscribing the warning letter that will be found, baked in its kiln, by the fire that destroys the city. He is writing to ask for help. The help will not come. The letter will not be delivered. The fire that destroys the city will preserve the tablet by accident, and three thousand years later we will read it.
- **The archetype callback:** the dockworker at Ugarit (Part 5 snapshot) is no longer at the docks. The merchant's wife archetype (Parts 4 and 5) is watching her commercial world die. The Mycenaean palace administrator (Part 5) is one of the last people of his profession in his entire region.
- **The civilizational consequence:** an entire age ends. The cosmopolitan system, the palace economies, the chariot armies, the international diplomatic system, much of the literacy. Not all at once and not everywhere, but enough that the post-collapse world is structurally different from the pre-collapse world.
- **The honest framing:** this is the inverse of a turning point. What was possible *shrinks*. Render the loss honestly without either melodrama (this was not "the death of civilization") or false consolation (the loss was real, and many people died in it).
- **Throughline:** fragility of complexity at full payoff. The metallurgical constraint (tin stops arriving). The first globalization broken. Literacy as a losable technology — losing it now.

### The chariot becomes obsolete
- **The change:** massed infantry with new weapons (slashing swords, javelins, possibly some iron-rich kit) renders the elite chariot armies obsolete. The weapon system that defined Late Bronze Age warfare for centuries stops working.
- **The personal moment:** the chariot driver who survives a battle his side should have won. He doesn't know yet that the change is permanent. He may never know.
- **The civilizational consequence:** the whole expensive ecosystem of chariot maintenance — palace stables, training programs, specialized smithies — has nothing left to do. The supporting economy collapses with it.
- **Throughline:** fragility of complexity — a weapon system that was the right answer to the previous war.

Contrarian beat: the collapse may have been good for many of the people inside the systems that fell — defer the full development of this to Part 8.

## Anticipated questions & lesser-known facts (seeds)

- **Was it the Sea Peoples?** Mostly no — they were one symptom among many causes. Climate stress, internal revolt, supply-chain breakdown, succession crises, possibly disease, all interacting. The Sea Peoples are the most dramatic component but probably not the most consequential.
- **Why did Egypt survive when others did not?** Barely. And as a shadow. Geography helped (the Nile is defensible, the deserts buffered some threats). Egypt was less embedded in the tin trade than the others, so the supply-chain failure hit less directly. But the New Kingdom that survived was a fraction of what came before; "survival" is the wrong word for what happened to Egypt.
- **How fast was the collapse?** Decades, not years. The cities did not all burn in a single season. The Hittite Empire's collapse, the Mycenaean palatial destruction, the Levantine city-burnings — these happened over roughly half a century, with variations in timing. From inside, it would have looked like a long, terrible time, not a single catastrophe.
- **Lesser-known:** the warning letter found in the kiln at Ugarit is real and devastating. The king reports that enemy ships have already burned villages along the coast; he asks for help; the letter was being inscribed when the city was attacked and never delivered. It was found in the kiln where it had been being baked — preserved by the destruction it warned about.
