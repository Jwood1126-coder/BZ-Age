## The Draft

---

Somewhere along the Tigris River, in the land that is now Iraq, in the year 401 BCE, a Greek soldier named Xenophon looked up at a wall and could not explain it.

The wall was a hundred feet high — the height of a ten-story building, made of brick, in a world without cranes. Twenty-five feet thick. Baked brick on a foundation of dressed stone. The circuit of the city it enclosed — walking at a normal pace — would take you most of a morning to complete. And the city was empty. Completely empty. No smoke from cooking fires. No sound from the markets. No sound at all, really, except the wind moving through gaps in the collapsed sections, and the pigeons.

Xenophon wrote it all down. He was that kind of man — an Athenian intellectual, a student of Socrates, precise and curious and observant even under pressure, and he was under considerable pressure. He was part of an army of ten thousand Greek mercenaries who had spent the past few months in a situation that could charitably be described as extremely bad. They'd been hired by a Persian prince to help him seize the throne from his brother. The prince had led them deep into the Persian Empire, where the decisive battle had gone well right up until the moment the prince himself was killed in it. Then the Greek generals who were supposed to lead the march home had been invited to a diplomatic meeting by the Persians and murdered.

Ten thousand Greeks. Stranded. Middle of the Persian Empire. Generals dead. Enemies in every direction.

Under the circumstances, stopping to examine ruins was almost beside the point. And yet.

The wall was a hundred feet high.

He asked the locals who built it. The Medes, someone said. An old people. The Persians destroyed it, long ago. Xenophon wrote the answer into his account of the march: *the name of the city was Larissa, and it was inhabited, in the old days, by the Medes.*

A day's march further north, there was another ruin. Larger. A stone base the locals said was polished and inlaid with shells. Brick walls above that, fifty feet thick and a hundred feet high. Another enormous empty city, another circuit of walls the soldiers spent half a day walking around. Xenophon asked again. He got roughly the same answer: the Medes built it. The Persians destroyed it. Or the gods did — Zeus sent a thunderbolt and the walls crumbled. The story shifted depending on who was telling it. What stayed constant was this: nobody knew.

Xenophon was looking at Nimrud and Nineveh.

He didn't know that, because the names had been forgotten. The cities he was walking past were the former capitals of the Assyrian Empire — a Mesopotamian people whose state, in the centuries just before Xenophon's time, had controlled more territory than any state before it. It had reached from the Persian highlands in the east to the shores of Egypt in the west. It had built some of the most impressive monuments the ancient world had ever produced. Nineveh — the city the Hebrew Bible calls "that great city," a city so large, the Book of Jonah says, that crossing it took three days.

It had been destroyed — catastrophically, finally, burned by a coalition of enemies — two hundred and eleven years before Xenophon walked past it.

Two hundred and eleven years.

That is not a very long time. It is less than the distance between George Washington and today. It is three or four long human memories laid end to end. Short enough that the empire's administrative records, its libraries, its temples, its traditions — some of it should have survived in some form, passed down, preserved, maintained. Short enough that the ruins should have had names.

But the names were gone. The memory was gone. What was left was a local story about the Medes and a thunderbolt, and the wind in the empty streets.

That is what happens when a civilization disappears.

This series is about the civilization that disappeared before that one.

The Assyrians who built Nineveh were not the beginning. They were inheritors — of cities, of writing, of law, of the idea of empire itself, all of it stretching back two thousand years before their empire existed. The world Xenophon was walking through was built on the ruins of the Bronze Age: from the first cities in the valley of the Tigris and Euphrates, somewhere around 3500 BCE, to a collapse around 1200 BCE that brought down the most connected world human beings had ever made — and brought it down within a single human lifetime.

It fell apart in ways nobody fully understood at the time, and in ways historians are still arguing about now.

Xenophon had no idea it had existed. The people who had forgotten Nineveh in two hundred years had long since forgotten the Bronze Age in eight hundred. By the time the classical Greeks were building the Parthenon and writing philosophy, that older world survived only in distorted myths, told by people who didn't know they were remembering it.

We have ways of getting at it that Xenophon didn't have.

Archaeology. Decipherment. Decades of scholars learning to read scripts that had been unreadable for millennia. Tens of thousands of clay tablets, pulled from the rubble of cities that had been gone since before Rome was founded. The Bronze Age ran on clay. And clay, fired by the same catastrophes that destroyed the civilizations, survives.

The Bronze Age is recoverable. Imperfectly. Partially. With enormous gaps and considerable uncertainty. But recoverable.

And what's waiting under the rubble, when you go down and recover it, is this: before Greece, before Rome, there was a connected world. Kings and scribes. Traders and dockworkers. Farmers, diplomats, slaves, sailors. It built the first cities. It invented writing. It wired the eastern Mediterranean together into something you could almost call global. And then most of it vanished. How that world worked, why it broke, and what survived in memory after the names were gone — that is this series.

I want to tell it a particular way. Not as a parade of kings — the kings are here, but they're outnumbered, the way they were in life. I want to know what that world felt like from inside a body standing in it: what people ate, what they wore, what they were afraid of, what they did on a good evening, what a city smelled like the first time you walked into one. I want to ask the simple questions most histories are too dignified to answer — why did anyone start farming in the first place? why does a city need writing? why is an hour sixty minutes? — because the simple questions are where the good answers live. And when the evidence runs out, I'll say so, out loud, every time. The gaps are part of this story. Some of the best parts of this story are the gaps.

We're going to go from a world with no cities in it at all, to the first city. From the first city to the first empire. From empire to a golden age so connected you could almost mistake it for the modern world — and then we're going to watch the whole thing come apart in a single human lifetime, and follow what was left down through the wreckage, into legend, and finally into the ground, where it waited three thousand years for somebody to dig it back up and learn, letter by letter, to read it again.

It begins long before any of those things existed. Before the wall Xenophon couldn't explain. Before anyone, anywhere, had ever seen a city, or a king, or a written word.

It begins in the dark, in a small house that smells of smoke and sheep, with a person waking up on a packed-earth floor.
