# Series Cold Open / Prologue
## FIRST DRAFT — v0.1
**Date:** May 27, 2026
**Status:** First draft. Series-level prologue, before Part 1.
**Estimated audio length:** ~8–12 minutes
**Function:** Establish the modern-disaster frame before entering the ancient world. Make the listener feel the Bronze Age collapse through lived analogy before they know what the Bronze Age is.

---

## The Draft

---

Think about 2020. Pick any month you want, but I'll pick March, because that's when it stopped being something you could explain away.

You're inside. The city outside your window is either quiet in a way it has never been quiet, or it isn't — but the feeling is the same either way. Something has happened to the world that wasn't supposed to be able to happen. The system that was supposed to catch this kind of thing — the surveillance, the protocols, the agencies with their acronyms, the supply chains that had been getting more efficient for sixty years — had caught it too late, or had seen it coming and hadn't moved fast enough, and now the hospitals were counting ventilators, and the flights were being cancelled, and the borders were closing, and you were standing in a grocery store with a half-empty shelf in front of you where the flour used to be, and you were understanding, for maybe the first time in your adult life, what it feels like to have the infrastructure fail.

That was one thing. One pathogen. And it cracked the world open.

Now I want you to imagine you didn't get to recover from it. The vaccine doesn't come. Or it comes but not fast enough, not everywhere. The variant is worse. And six months into the original crisis — while the hospitals are still overcrowded, while the supply chains are still scrambled, while the shipping containers are piling up in the wrong ports and the truck drivers are sick and the factories are at half-capacity — the rain stops.

Not in your city. In the places where your food comes from.

A drought. Not a bad year — you've had bad years. This is different. This is three bad years, then four, then five, the kind of drought where you stop hoping it will end before you've figured out what comes after hoping. The rivers that irrigate the grain belt drop. The reservoirs get low and then lower. The harvest reports start coming in at seventy percent of normal, and then sixty, and then people stop publishing the harvest reports in the way they used to because the numbers are doing something that numbers aren't supposed to do in a functioning civilization.

And while that's happening — while the pandemic is still burning, while the food prices are spiking, while the news has this quality of emergency that never quite resolves into something normal again — the politics breaks. Not one country. Multiple countries, more or less at once. Governments that held together through the first year, the second year, start coming apart under the weight of the third. The institutions designed to handle crises turn out to work only when the people running them agree on the basic terms of the problem. When they don't, the institutions grind.

And then the migrations. Millions of people moving because the place they were living in can no longer support them — because the water is gone, or the government is gone, or both. They're moving through borders that are already stressed. Some of them are desperate. Some of them are organized. The desperate ones flood the coastal cities. The organized ones start taking what they need.

Now we're living in a disaster movie. Except it isn't one disaster. It's four, all feeding back into each other, each one making the others harder to survive. The pandemic makes the supply chain fragile. The supply chain crisis makes the drought worse to manage. The drought makes the politics impossible. The political collapse makes everything else harder to address. Round and round, each loop tightening, and the whole structure — the vast, sophisticated, interconnected structure that took generations to build — is running on the last of its reserves, trying to hold together, not knowing whether it will.

That's the frame. That's the feeling I want you to hold.

Because something like that happened once before.

Not like it in every detail — I'll tell you where the analogy holds and where it breaks down. But something like it, in its structure, in the way the failures wove together and amplified each other, in the gap between how bad things looked from the outside and how bad they actually were for the people living inside them.

And it happened to the most sophisticated, most interconnected, most ambitious world that human beings had ever built.

We call it the Bronze Age.

---

The Bronze Age is not really an age. That's the first thing to know. It's a label that historians use for a period in human history — roughly from 3000 BCE to 1200 BCE, though the dates get slippery depending on where you are on the planet — when the dominant technology for tools and weapons was bronze: the alloy of copper and tin that is harder than copper alone and keeps a better edge than stone.

The name is a little misleading because it makes the Bronze Age sound like a single thing, like a flat era defined by one material, when what it actually was is four thousand years of human ingenuity, human ambition, human catastrophe, and human survival playing out across a world that gets more complicated — more connected, more sophisticated, more fragile — with every passing century.

By the end of it, in the second millennium BCE, the world looks something like this:

There are great empires. Multiple great empires. Egypt in the Nile Valley and the Levant. The Hittites on the Anatolian plateau, what is now Turkey. The Mycenaean civilization in Greece. Kingdoms in Mesopotamia, in Cyprus, in the Levant. All of them in diplomatic contact with each other — sending letters and gifts and ambassadors and daughters given as wives. All of them trading: tin from Afghanistan, copper from Cyprus, gold from Nubia, grain from Egypt, lapis lazuli from the Hindu Kush. The ships are moving constantly.

And then, in about fifty years — faster in some places, slower in others — almost all of it comes down.

The palaces burn. Not one palace, not a small regional crisis. Dozens of cities destroyed or abandoned within a single human lifetime. Ugarit on the Syrian coast, one of the great trading cities of the ancient world, gone. The Hittite Empire, gone — not conquered, not absorbed, but gone, its capital abandoned, its name vanishing so completely from history that by the time Greek civilization is producing philosophy and drama and democracy, nobody knows the Hittites existed. The Mycenaean palace civilization in Greece, gone. The writing system that recorded its language — a syllabic script called Linear B, used for centuries — goes with it, and the Greek world enters three hundred years of functional illiteracy, three centuries in which nobody writes anything down, because the institution that trained scribes and the institution that needed scribes had both burned.

The cosmopolitan world of the Bronze Age — the world with the trade routes and the diplomatic letters and the marriages between royal houses — becomes a memory. And not even a clear memory: it becomes a mythologized, distorted half-memory that will take three thousand years and the invention of modern archaeology to decode.

That is the story this series tells. All of it — not just the collapse, but the long building toward it: the cities, the empires, the long-distance trade, the writing, the diplomacy, the extraordinary human effort that it took to build a cosmopolitan world when the fastest form of communication was a man on a donkey. And then the collapse itself, and the long, strange aftermath, and what survived and what didn't and why.

---

I want to be honest with you about what this kind of history requires. It requires sitting with uncertainty in a way that popular history often doesn't ask you to do. The sources for the Bronze Age are extraordinary — we have tens of thousands of clay tablets, we have excavated cities, we have shipwrecks with their cargo intact, we have the tombs, we have the letters — and the sources are also fragmentary, contested, and frequently silent on exactly the questions we most want answered. We don't always know why things happened. We don't always know who the people were. We don't always know what they believed or felt or intended. The tablets break off. The fires were more thorough than we'd like.

In the places where we genuinely don't know, I'm going to say we don't know. The uncertainty is not a failure of the story — it's part of the story. It's part of what it means to reach back four thousand years and try to find the people there.

Because they were there. That's the thing I keep coming back to, the thing that makes this material irresistible to me. The people who lived in the Bronze Age were not an abstraction. They were people who had bad days and good meals and complicated families and things they worried about at night. They sent letters — actual letters, written in cuneiform on clay tablets — that complain about the price of goods and the quality of the tin and the fact that their business partner hasn't sent the shipment he promised. They wrote love poems. They wrote administrative records so meticulous that we can tell, from the records of a single palace storehouse, how many jars of olive oil arrived in a particular month thirty-two hundred years ago.

They were here. And then they weren't.

And the world they built, and the world that came out of the wreckage of what they built — that world is the ancestor of the world you're in right now. The alphabet you read with. The legal traditions you live under. The religious ideas that have shaped your civilization whether you're religious or not. The memory of a soldier trying to get home. All of it comes through the Bronze Age and through the collapse that ended it.

This is where we came from.

Let's go find them.

## Draft notes for revision

**Voice check:** The 2020 cold open works — it builds the frame without the "not a... not a... but a..." AI-rhetorical structure. The disaster accumulation is sequential and feels lived-in rather than listed. The pivot to "something like that happened once before" is earned. The closing "Let's go find them" is right — Carlin-style invitation, not lecture.

**What the cold open does:**
1. Establishes the multi-causal collapse model before naming the Bronze Age
2. Makes fragility of complexity (spine throughline) visceral before the series starts
3. Signals the contrarian-honest posture ("I'll tell you where the analogy holds and where it breaks down")
4. Introduces the literacy-as-losable-technology throughline (Linear B → three centuries of illiteracy)
5. Signals the humanizing posture — "they sent letters that complain about the price of goods"
6. Sets listener expectation for uncertainty acknowledgment

**Missing:**
- No specific modern disaster scenario detail (could add one more concrete beat — the half-empty flour shelf is the best, could add one more like it)
- The transition into Part 1 could be sharper — "let's go find them" is right in spirit but may want a bridge line to the Neolithic body that opens Part 1

**Placement:**
- This runs before Part 1. It is the cold open for the entire series, not for Episode 1 specifically.
- Episode 1 (Part 1) would then open with "Your feet hurt" — the Neolithic body cold open — treating the prologue as already heard.

**Length check:** At ~1,200 words, this is 8-12 minutes of audio. Right for a series prologue. Could be trimmed to 6-8 minutes if production wants shorter.
