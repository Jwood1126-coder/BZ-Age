## The Draft

There is a kind of disaster that begins in sunshine.

You want the disaster story to start with darkness. A black sky. A failed harvest so obvious that even the proudest king has to understand it. Enemy sails on the horizon. Something clean enough that everyone inside the story can say: this is the moment.

That is not usually how systems fail.

They fail through ordinary days that get slightly worse. A ship is late, but ships are late. A harvest is short, but harvests are sometimes short. A governor does not answer, but governors are busy, roads are dangerous, messengers get sick, pirates happen. The trouble looks like a string of inconveniences until it has become the weather everyone lives inside.

Then, much too late, the inconvenience has a shape.

You are in the end of something.

So begin in the sunshine, because that is where this part belongs. Around 1259 BCE, the Late Bronze Age world still looks as if it works. Egypt, the river kingdom that remembers itself; Hatti, the mountain empire of the Hittites; Babylon and Assyria in Mesopotamia; the Mycenaean palace Greeks across the Aegean; Cyprus with its copper; Ugarit on the Syrian coast, where the wires cross. Letters move. Brides move. Grain moves. Tin moves. Everybody complains, which is one of the signs the system is alive.

The clearest symbol of that working world is the peace after Kadesh.

Ramses II of Egypt and Hattusili III of Hatti had fought each other to exhaustion. Both sides claimed glory, because royal inscriptions were not exactly neutral press briefings, and then both sides did the more useful thing: they made peace. We have the treaty in Egyptian, carved on temple walls, and in Hittite, from the archives at Hattusa. Mutual non-aggression. Extradition. Defensive alliance. The gods of both nations called as witnesses.

And after the treaty came marriages, letters, gifts, and the careful maintenance of one of the most important relationships in the world. Hittite princesses went south to Egypt. Egyptian princesses did not go north. Egypt imported royal brides and kept its daughters home, and the pharaohs do not seem to have felt this required much explanation. Rank has always been good at sounding like nature.

In those same decades, Ramses' son Khaemwaset was restoring monuments that were already ancient to him: Old Kingdom pyramids and sun-temples, a thousand years old and more, as far from him as the Vikings are from us. The golden age was tending the graves of an older golden age, with no way of knowing that within about a century its own world would need the same mercy.

That is the bright surface.

Under it sits the wrong lesson.

Last time, I asked you to keep one name: Mitanni. The most forgotten of the great kingdoms. Here is why it matters.

Mitanni had once been one of the pillars of the international order: a great kingdom across northern Mesopotamia and Syria, tied to Egypt by royal marriage and diplomacy. Its kings wrote to Pharaoh as "brother." Its princesses entered the Egyptian royal house. It sat at the table with the powers that mattered.

Then it disappeared.

A Hittite king, Suppiluliuma, broke Mitanni's power in war. Its ruler was murdered. Part of the kingdom became a Hittite client zone; part was swallowed by Assyria. By the time Egypt and Hatti made peace after Kadesh, Mitanni was no longer really a state on the map. A great power had been deleted.

And the world kept going.

That is the important part. The trade routes bent around the damage. Assyria moved into the empty seat. The letters continued. The tin still came. The harvests still came. Foreign brides still arrived. For everyone watching from the chancery rooms of the Near East, the lesson looked obvious: the system is strong. It can lose a kingdom and close over the gap.

It was the wrong lesson.

Mitanni's fall was survivable because everything around Mitanni still worked. Routes can reroute when there are safe routes left. Grain can cover a shortage when some other region has grain to spare. A great power can fall, if the surrounding world remains solid enough to absorb the shock.

What nobody had seen was a different kind of test: not one pillar failing, but the floor under all the pillars tilting at once.

And Suppiluliuma, the king who broke Mitanni, gives us one of the first hints that connection has a price.

Late in his reign, a letter came from Egypt. It came from a royal widow, probably the widow of Tutankhamun, though the identification remains argued [VERIFY]. The Hittite records do not give her name; they preserve her as the *dahamunzu*, the queen. Her message was astonishing: my husband is dead, I have no son, send me one of your sons and he will be my husband and king of Egypt.

You have to feel how wrong that sounded. Egypt did not ask foreigners for kings. Egypt barely admitted foreign kings were equals. Suppiluliuma suspected a trap and sent someone to check. The answer came back wounded and proud: if I had a son, would I humiliate myself by writing like this?

So he sent the prince. The prince's name was Zannanza. Somewhere on the road to Egypt, he died. The Hittites said he was murdered. They may have been right [VERIFY].

Suppiluliuma retaliated against Egyptian territory in Syria and marched prisoners home. The prisoners brought plague. It killed him. It killed his heir. It kept killing for years, long enough that a later Hittite king, Mursili II, left plague prayers that are among the strangest royal documents we have: not triumph, but fear; not a king boasting, but a king asking the gods what sin had made his land die.

The roads that moved marriage alliances moved armies. The roads that moved tin moved prisoners. The roads that moved prisoners moved disease. Connection had a price that no scribe had a column for.

Within about eighty years of the Kadesh treaty, the Hittite Empire would be gone. The Mycenaean palace world would burn or empty. Ugarit would be ash. Egypt would survive, but not as the empire it had been. The cosmopolitan world would become a memory, and not even a clear one.

What happened?

Not one thing.

That matters. The old simple version is tempting: the Sea Peoples came, the Bronze Age ended. A newer simple version is also tempting: drought came, the Bronze Age ended. Simple stories are satisfying because they let you imagine one lever. Pull the lever, explain the world.

This collapse does not give us one lever.

Start with climate, because it sits under everything else. Tree rings, pollen, sediment, lake levels: the landscape kept records even when people did not. Those records point to a prolonged drying trend across much of the eastern Mediterranean beginning in the later thirteenth century BCE, uneven but serious, with some of the hardest years in the early twelfth [VERIFY]. A year of poor rain is hardship. Five years is crisis. Decades, with just enough decent years to keep hope alive but not enough to rebuild the buffer, is something else.

Put it in one household.

The seed grain sits in a jar. It is next year's field, but it is also food. Every farming family knows the rule: you do not eat the seed grain. You eat less. You sell an animal. You borrow. You pray. The jar stays sealed because eating the jar is eating next year.

Now make the harvest short three years in a row. The palace still wants its share. The tax tablet has no line for rainfall. Somewhere in the third year, someone is looking at the jar.

Multiply that by the Anatolian plateau, the Syrian steppe, the Greek uplands. Multiply it by people who did not own the fields they worked: Hittite transportees, captives, debt-bound laborers, the people who appear in royal records beside cattle and silver. A food crisis never lands evenly. The ranked list gets shorter from the bottom first.

In the archives, drought appears as paperwork. The Hittites ask Egypt for grain. Merneptah, Ramses II's successor, says in an inscription that he sent grain north "to keep alive the land of Hatti" [VERIFY]. A Hittite letter to Ugarit orders a ship and crew for a grain shipment and says the matter is life or death [VERIFY].

One of the two great powers of the age is buying food from the other to stay alive.

That does not mean climate caused everything. It means climate removed slack.

Slack is what lets a system absorb trouble. A late tin shipment. A bad harvest. A revolt in a vassal kingdom. A raid on the coast. A palace succession fight. A route that has to be rerouted. The Late Bronze Age had survived all of those before. The new thing was synchronization: more things going wrong at the same time, across too much of the map, with fewer untouched places left to help.

This is where the Sea Peoples enter, but carefully.

Egyptian inscriptions name groups attacking Egypt and the Levant: Peleset, Tjekker, Sherden, Sheklesh, Lukka, and others. "Sea Peoples" is our label, not theirs. Some were probably raiders. Some were probably displaced people. Some may have been both, depending on the year and the offer. The Peleset are almost certainly the Philistines, later famous from the Hebrew Bible [VERIFY]. Many of the origins are murkier. The old image of one huge migration-invasion rolling over civilization does not hold. The destructions do not line up that cleanly.

But the raids were real. The fear was real. And for a world already short on grain, bronze, men, and trust, fear is not a side issue. Fear reroutes ships. Fear raises insurance, even when nobody calls it insurance. Fear makes a merchant decide not to sail, a village decide not to send men, a king decide to keep grain home.

Could they see it coming?

They saw things. They saw late ships, poor harvests, raiders, unanswered letters. But each of those had happened before. The hard thing to see was the system: grain and tin and treaties and ports and palace labor all tied together so tightly that trouble in one place could become shortage somewhere else. They did not have the outside view. We do, partly. Tablets, tree rings, burn layers, shipwrecks, settlement surveys. We can see the board they were standing on.

They had to play from inside it.

So when we go looking for the moment someone noticed, we do not find a prophet.

We find an accountant.

The grain inventory is wrong again.

A Hittite official has counted three times. Same answer. The stores are short. Not empty. Empty would be simple. Short. Short enough to worry about, not short enough to stop the machinery.

Tribute still comes from the subordinate kingdoms, but less of it. Letters explain why. The harvest was poor here too. Roads are unsafe there. A shipment is delayed. A coastal town sent one alarming message and then went quiet, which might mean the trouble passed and might mean the town is no longer there to write.

Outside, grain from Egypt arrives. It should feel like surplus. It feels like survival.

In another courtyard, a bronze-smith waits for tin that should have come by now. Bronze is not just copper; it is copper plus tin, and tin is the awkward ingredient, the far traveler. If the tin does not come, the workshop can repair old tools and melt scrap, but it cannot make the same world new again.

From a distance, the palace still looks like the peak. Tablets are still being written. Seals are still being pressed. Letters still open with the proper courtesies. The whole apparatus is still moving.

It is just moving with less underneath it.

Across the water, the Mycenaean palace Greeks are starting to build fear into stone. At Tiryns and Mycenae, walls thicken. The blocks are so large that later Greeks will credit them to Cyclopes. That is a good myth and bad labor history. Human beings moved those stones: farmers dragged from fields, men hauling limestone under orders from administrators who could not name the deadline but could feel it coming.

At Pylos, the evidence is different. Among the palace's last tablets are emergency requisitions for rowers, the *o-ka* set, the kind of precision term that hides a human panic inside catalog language. Same clay, same desks, same scribal habits that once counted figs and wool. Now the palace is counting men to put at oars.

The instrument has not changed. The urgency has.

Merchants probably felt the change before kings did. Kings learn a route is dead when the tin fails to arrive. Merchants learn it earlier: when a partner stops answering, when a caravan will not risk the road, when the man who always took the wool suddenly wants impossible terms. A connected world disconnects through thousands of sensible decisions. No decree. No single day. Just risk becoming too expensive, one route at a time.

And then the states begin to say it out loud.

A senior official from Cyprus, Eshuwara, writes to Ugarit about enemy ships: some twenty have been sighted; they have done harm; where they went, nobody knows [VERIFY]. The form is still diplomatic. The titles are still correct. The courtesies still stand in line. But the content is a confession that the sea between them is no longer controlled.

The system is using its own language to report its own failure.

Meanwhile, the last Great King of Hatti bears the same name as the conqueror who erased Mitanni: Suppiluliuma. The first Suppiluliuma had taught the world the wrong lesson, that a kingdom could vanish and the system could absorb it. The last one fought to keep the sea lanes open, even claiming victories off Cyprus [VERIFY]. He may have won battles. It was not enough.

That is the shape of these years. Not one blow. A narrowing. The harvest shortens. The grain ships become necessary. The tin is late. The coast is dangerous. The merchants hesitate. The walls rise. The rowers are counted. The letters sound normal until you hear what they are saying.

The last generation born fully inside the cosmopolitan world died without knowing it had been the last. Their children inherited a world slightly worse. Their grandchildren inherited one worse than that. By the time the great-grandchildren were born, the old world was wreckage.

And even then, it was not the whole planet. Far away at Anyang, Shang kings were casting bronze on a scale Mediterranean smiths would have understood as magnificent and strange. Around 1200 BCE, Fu Hao, royal consort and military leader, was buried with bronzes, jades, weapons, and chariots [VERIFY]. On the other side of the landmass, the Bronze Age was not ending. It was at full noon.

What was ending was one region's version of it: the seaborne, treaty-bound, palace-fed, tin-hungry world whose wires have been tightening all hour.

And then came the fires.
