# Part 6 — The Crisis Builds

**Status:** Skeleton. Significantly less developed than 1–5.
**Working length:** Provisional 2.5–4 hours.

---

## Core problem this part solves

The collapse around 1200 BCE is not a single event. It is the climax of a long, building crisis. This part holds the slow tightening — the moment where everything still looks cosmopolitan on the surface but the fault lines are visible to anyone who knows where to look.

The Kadesh treaty and its peace. The first appearances of the Sea Peoples in the Egyptian record. The Hittite grain shortages requiring Egyptian imports. The Mycenaean palaces beginning to fortify. The dread should build.

---

## Beats to develop

- **Kadesh, 1274 BCE** — the largest chariot battle in history, both sides claim victory, both sides eventually sign the first peace treaty we have from both sides. Sustained focus.
- **The peace and its meaning.** The treaty's language. The royal marriages that followed.
- **The first Sea Peoples references** — small mentions in Egyptian sources, possibly raiders, possibly something else.
- **Climate signals:** the proxy record beginning to show drought. Tree rings. Sediment cores.
- **Hittite grain dependency on Egyptian imports** — they were already running on thin margins.
- **Mycenaean fortification** — Tiryns and Mycenae walling up, indicating perceived threat.
- **Internal stress signals** — succession crises, religious disputes, regional revolts visible in the archives.

## State of the World snapshot (seed — Rule 10)

**Placement:** Brief, after the climate-signal beats. The cosmopolitan world is still standing — but something is wrong.

**Vantage:** Same kind of person as the Part 5 dockworker, or possibly a Hittite official inspecting a grain shipment from Egypt around 1230 BCE. The world looks the same as Part 5 — but the granaries are emptier, the harvests are worse, the foreign merchants speak more nervously.

**Negative space:** all the absences of Part 5 still apply. The chariot armies are still the apex weapon system. Bronze is still the metal of everything. But the supply lines that make all of this work are under strain in ways the people on the ground can feel without yet understanding.

**Contrast to Part 5:** the world looks like Part 5 from the outside. From the inside it has begun to tighten. The snapshot serves as foreshadowing — and as a marker the listener will return to when Part 7 shows what came next.

**Length:** Very brief — 2 minutes.

## Turning Points (seeds — Rule 11)

### The supply chain begins to fail (seed)
- **The change:** the trade networks that made the cosmopolitan age possible begin to falter. Tin arrives less reliably. Grain shipments are delayed. The Hittite request to Egypt for emergency grain is a marker — a great power cannot feed itself from its own resources.
- **The personal moment:** the Hittite official inspecting an Egyptian grain shipment that has arrived late and short. He has done this job for years; the shipments used to arrive on time. They don't anymore. He doesn't yet know why.
- **The archetype callback:** the merchant's wife archetype — the Old Assyrian commercial-class household figure from Part 4. By Part 6 the institutional form she lived inside is straining. Her counterpart in this period is watching her family's business get harder.
- **The civilizational consequence (the deferred payoff):** the whole cosmopolitan system depends on supply chains functioning. They are no longer fully functioning. The cascade begins here, even if no one yet sees it.
- **Throughline:** fragility of complexity. The metallurgical constraint. The first globalization (now stressed).

### The Kadesh peace treaty (briefly — it's a marker, not a true turning point)
- **The change:** Egypt and the Hittites sign the oldest peace treaty we have from both sides. The treaty includes mutual extradition, anti-rebellion clauses, divine witnesses on both sides.
- **Why it's here:** not because the treaty itself reshaped the world — it didn't — but because it's the *zenith* of the diplomatic system whose collapse is the larger story. The structural form of international peace-making was perfected just before the system collapsed.
- **Throughline:** the first globalization at its formal apex. (The treaty's afterlife — a copy hangs in the UN headquarters — is a closing detail for the beat.)

## Anticipated questions & lesser-known facts (seeds)

- **Why did the great powers not see it coming?** Some of them did. Hittite grain shortages required emergency Egyptian imports. Mycenaean palaces were being fortified. Egyptian inscriptions mention the Sea Peoples decades before the worst attacks. The system saw individual threats; it failed to see the systemic pattern.
- **What were the Sea Peoples?** Honestly: we don't fully know. The Egyptian records name several groups (Peleset, Tjekker, Sherden, Lukka, Sheklesh, etc.) but the descriptions are propagandistic and the modern identifications are contested. The Peleset are usually identified with the Philistines; some of the others have proposed Aegean or western Anatolian origins. Treat with appropriate uncertainty.
- **Lesser-known:** the Kadesh peace treaty (1259 BCE) is the oldest peace treaty we have from both sides. Both the Egyptian and Hittite versions survive, and they say substantially the same things — including mutual extradition clauses, anti-rebellion clauses, and divine witnesses on both sides. A copy of the treaty hangs in the UN headquarters.
