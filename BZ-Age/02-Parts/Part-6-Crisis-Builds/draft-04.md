## The Draft

---

There is a kind of disaster that begins in sunshine.

You want the disaster story to start with darkness, with obvious portents, with something in the air that lets the people inside it know — or should let them know — what's coming. The sky turns the wrong color. The harvest fails visibly and dramatically. The enemies appear on the horizon.

What actually happens, with complex systems failing, is different. It starts on a perfectly normal day, or a series of perfectly normal days, and the days are slightly less good than the previous batch of days, and this seems unremarkable because some years are slightly worse than others, and in the previous generation the years were also sometimes slightly worse, and things recovered. And then the slightly-less-good runs longer than it used to, and then certain things that were reliable become less reliable, and then certain things that were expected begin to not arrive, and at some point — much later than it should have been, because the system has built-in mechanisms that absorb stress and delay the signal — you realize that you are not in a temporary disruption.

You are in the end of something.

This part lives inside those slightly-less-good years, in several different locations, in a world coming apart from the inside, before it knows it's coming apart.

---

The Kadesh peace treaty, 1259 BCE, is the last moment I can point to and say: the system is working.

Not perfectly — the system was never perfect, the empires were never stable in any deep sense, the complaints never stopped, the grain shipments were never always on time. But in 1259 BCE, the Egyptian and Hittite empires signed a formal peace treaty, and both sides kept it. We have both versions — the Egyptian hieroglyphic text carved on the walls of Karnak, the massive temple complex at Thebes in what is now Luxor, and the Hittite cuneiform version from the archives at Hattusa, the Hittite capital built on a rocky plateau in what is now central Turkey — and they say substantially the same things. Mutual non-aggression. Mutual extradition. Defensive alliance against internal and external threats. The gods of both nations as witnesses.

Ramses II and Hattusili III — two of the most powerful rulers in the world — had fought at Kadesh, failed to defeat each other, claimed victory anyway, and then, fifteen years later, decided that peace was more useful than another campaign. Following the treaty came royal marriages: Hittite princesses traveling south to Egypt — never the reverse; Egypt took foreign wives and, in the whole record of the age, never sent a daughter out. Letters back and forth. The careful maintenance of a diplomatic relationship between two empires that shared a border and needed each other's restraint to remain stable.

In those same decades, Ramses' fourth son was spending his life on the past. His name was Khaemwaset, high priest of Ptah at Memphis, and the monuments around his city — the pyramids and sun-temples of the Old Kingdom — were by his lifetime a thousand years old and more: as far from him as the Vikings are from us. Their cults had lapsed; the sand was taking them. Khaemwaset restored them — cleared them, repaired them, and carved onto their stones fresh inscriptions naming the ancient kings who had built them, so that the names would not be lost. Modern writers like to call him the first Egyptologist. The label is an anachronism, but the instinct it names is real, and the image is worth keeping: the golden age at its peak, tending the graves of a golden age before it — with no way of knowing that the world doing the tending was itself about a century from needing the same mercy.

How we can read the Hittite side of any of this — how an empire that vanished so completely that the Hebrew Bible's stray mentions of "Hittites" were sometimes dismissed as scribal errors walked back onto the historical stage after more than three thousand years — is a story I am saving, deliberately, for the very end. For now, all you need is the fact: we have their archives. Tens of thousands of tablets. Their half of the diplomatic record of the cosmopolitan world.

That's 1259. That's the peak.

---

But before we walk into the failing years, I owe you a name. Last time, going around the table of the great powers, I told you to hold one: Mitanni. The most forgotten of the great kingdoms. Here is why they matter.

Mitanni was a founding member of the club. At its height around 1500 BCE it stretched across northern Mesopotamia from the Mediterranean watershed toward the Zagros; its kings married daughters into the Egyptian royal house for three generations; its ruler Tushratta wrote some of the most vivid letters in the Amarna archive — the gold-is-like-dust complaints, the anxious questions about his daughter in Pharaoh's palace. A great power among great powers, addressed as "brother" by Egypt for over a century.

And then it was erased — in stages, across two generations: knocked from the ranks of the great powers well before Kadesh was ever fought, and scrubbed from the map entirely right around the time the treaty was being sealed.

The fall came in stages, the way these things do. A Hittite king — Suppiluliuma, the great conqueror of his age — broke Mitanni's power in war, around the 1340s BCE. Tushratta, the letter-writer, was murdered in the aftermath; the sources point to a conspiracy that included his own son. The Hittites installed a client on the throne and turned the western half of the kingdom into a buffer. And the eastern half was swallowed, over the following decades, by a former vassal that had been waiting a long time for exactly this opportunity: Assyria. By the 1250s BCE, the name Mitanni no longer corresponded to anything on the map. A great power — one of the four or five pillars of the international order — had been deleted. Its royal line ended. Its court language and its archive tradition guttered out. We don't even securely know where its capital city was. Archaeologists are still looking.

The system *absorbed* it.

The trade routes rearranged themselves. The diplomatic correspondence carried on — Assyria simply took Mitanni's seat at the table, and after some sniffing (there's a letter where the Hittite king is plainly offended that the Assyrian upstart presumes to call him "brother"), the club adjusted. Foreign princesses kept arriving at the great courts. The tin kept moving. The harvest came in. For everyone watching — and every chancery in the Near East was watching — the lesson was unmistakable: the system is robust. A great power can fail, completely, and the world it belonged to closes over the gap like water.

It was the wrong lesson.

Mitanni's fall was survivable because everything around it still worked. The routes could reroute because there were intact kingdoms to reroute through. The grain could cover the gap because the rain was still falling somewhere. What nobody had seen — what nobody had any way to see — was the difference between a system surviving the failure of one part and a system in which all the parts fail together. The cosmopolitan world walked out of the Mitanni episode more confident than it walked in. It had tested its own resilience and passed. Sixty years later, the real test arrived, and it was not that test.

The man who erased Mitanni got a letter, late in his reign, that nothing in the diplomatic tradition had prepared him for.

It came from Egypt, and it came from a woman. Her husband had died. She was almost certainly Ankhesenamun, the young widow of Tutankhamun — the identification is argued, like almost everything about this episode — but the letter itself is preserved, quoted in the Hittite court's own records of the king's deeds: *My husband is dead. I have no son. They say you have many sons. Give me one of yours, and he will be my husband, and he will be king of Egypt.*

Suppiluliuma did not believe it. Egypt did not ask foreigners for kings. Egypt, as we just saw, did not even export its daughters. Now its throne was being offered to a Hittite prince, in a letter, from a widow. He sent an envoy south to find out whether it was a trap, and the reply that came back still has its wounded tone intact after thirty-three centuries: *Why do you say "they are deceiving me"? If I had a son, would I have written to a foreign country in a way that humiliates me and my country? He will be my husband, and king of Egypt.*

He sent the son. The prince's name was Zannanza, and somewhere on the way to Egypt he died — how, we don't know; the Hittites said murder, and given what a Hittite on the throne of Egypt would have meant, murder is not a stretch. Suppiluliuma responded the way great kings respond: he attacked Egyptian territory in Syria, won, and marched the prisoners home to Hatti.

The prisoners brought the plague with them.

It killed Suppiluliuma. It killed his heir. And it kept killing, year after year, into the reign of a younger son, Mursili II, who left us some of the strangest and most human documents of the entire Bronze Age: the plague prayers. Not victory inscriptions — petitions, drafted in the chancery's careful forms, addressed to the assembled gods of Hatti. The land is dying, they say; the plague has been in the land for twenty years; the plowmen who worked the god's fields are dead, the grinding-women who made the god's bread are dead. And then the king does something almost no ruler in the ancient record does: he goes looking for the guilt, and finds it at home. The archives are searched. The oath his father broke — the attack on Egypt, the war that marched the prisoners home — is named by his own son, out loud, as the sin behind the dying: *It is so. We have done it.* He offers the gods restitution and asks them to lift the plague. Whether they did, the tablets don't say.

The shape of it belongs to this hour. A royal marriage — the mechanism the system trusted above every other — failed once, and the failure killed two kings, burned a treaty, and seeded twenty years of epidemic. The roads that moved the tin moved the plague. Connection had a price that never appeared on a tablet.

Within about eight decades of the treaty — by the 1180s BCE — the Hittite Empire will not exist. Its capital will have been abandoned and burned. The Mycenaean palaces will be destroyed. Ugarit will be ash. Egypt will have survived, barely, and will never recover to anything like its New Kingdom power. The cosmopolitan world of the Amarna letters and the Uluburun ship will be a memory, and not even a clear memory — it will be a mythologized half-memory that will take three thousand years to decode.

What happened in those decades?

A lot of things happened at once. Not one cause. Not the Sea Peoples, as the conventional story used to have it. Multiple stressors, converging, in a system that had been built for surplus and became, gradually and then suddenly, a system operating at deficit.

Eric Cline, whose 1177 B.C.: The Year Civilization Collapsed (2014) is the most widely read modern account of the collapse, calls it "a perfect storm of calamities" — each individually survivable, together fatal. Cline's multi-causal model has been influential and is probably the right general frame, even if specialists debate exactly how to weight the different factors. His core argument: the Late Bronze Age system was "an overly interdependent system that had no way to absorb multiple shocks at once." The interconnectedness that had been its strength for three centuries was also what made it brittle.

---

The climate.

We have proxy records now that didn't exist a generation ago — and the way to think about them is that the landscape itself kept diaries. A tree cannot lie about a bad year; it writes the year into its own body as a ring too thin, and when that wood survives — in a lake bed, in a beam, in a bog — the diary is still legible thirty-two centuries later. Tree rings from across the eastern Mediterranean. Pollen records, showing what was growing where and when. Sediment cores from the sea bed, showing what the rivers were carrying — the particles that indicate erosion, drought, vegetation collapse. Lake levels, measurable from ancient watermarks.

What they show, consistently, is that the eastern Mediterranean experienced a prolonged drought event beginning sometime around 1250 BCE and lasting for decades. Not everywhere equally. Not every year. But the trend line shows drier, warmer conditions across the region in the last quarter of the thirteenth century BCE, with some of the worst years in the first quarter of the twelfth century.

Brandon Drake, in a 2012 study in the Journal of Archaeological Science, analyzed sea surface temperature records, pollen cores, and isotopic data from across the eastern Mediterranean. His finding: sea surface temperatures cooled rapidly during this period, reducing moisture flux into the atmosphere and cutting precipitation over land. The drought was not local. It was regional, and it arrived precisely when the palace economies were least able to absorb it.

A drought that lasts a year is a hardship. Agricultural systems have buffers — stored grain, alternative crops, emergency redistribution. A drought that lasts five years is a crisis. A drought that lasts twenty years, punctuated by occasional adequate years that allow hope but not recovery, eats through the reserves, eats through the patience, eats through the assumption that next year will be normal.

The palace economies of the Late Bronze Age were calibrated for normal conditions plus a buffer. They were not calibrated for twenty years of below-normal agricultural output across an entire region simultaneously.

A farmer in his forties has been measuring the same facts his whole life. This is imagination, but it is imagination on rails, because subsistence arithmetic only runs one way. The seed grain for next year sits in a jar, and it is also food, and every farming family in history has known the rule about that jar: you do not eat it. You eat less, you eat worse, you eat things you would rather not, and the jar stays sealed, because eating your seed grain is eating next year. Now run three short harvests in a row through that household. The buffer animals are sold or eaten by the second year. The obligations to the palace do not shrink with the harvest — the assessor's tablet doesn't have a column for rainfall. Somewhere in the third year, the family is looking at the jar. That calculation — eat next year, or starve this one — was running, household by household, across the Anatolian plateau and the Greek uplands and the Syrian steppe, for years, while the kings' letters went back and forth about ships and grain. The empire's crisis is the household's crisis, multiplied by a hundred thousand kitchens, with the palace's share taken off the top.

The climate was one factor. Some popular accounts have made it the single explanation, the tidy cause that lets everything else off the hook. The climate was real. The climate was consequential. It did not, by itself, explain everything.

---

The grain letters.

Among the Hittite archives from the period just before the collapse, there are letters that ask Egypt for grain. Not as tribute, not as a gift — as a purchase, an emergency import, because the Hittites cannot grow enough food to feed their population and their armies.

The Hittite Empire — one of the two great empires of the Late Bronze Age, a military power that had fought Egypt to a standstill at Kadesh — was, in the decades before its collapse, dependent on Egyptian grain imports to function.

The Egyptians said so themselves, in public. The pharaoh Merneptah — Ramses II's successor — recorded in an official inscription that he had sent shiploads of grain north "to keep alive the land of Hatti." The empire that fought Egypt to a draw at Kadesh, two generations back, is now named, in Egyptian state propaganda, as the empire Egypt is feeding. And the urgency wasn't rhetorical: in a letter from these years, the Hittite court orders its vassal city Ugarit to provide a ship and crew, immediately, to move a grain shipment up the coast — and adds a line that needs no commentary across three thousand years: *it is a matter of life or death.*

This is the metallurgical constraint applied to food. The same logic that made bronze dependent on a supply chain for tin — the same logic that made the whole Bronze Age system vulnerable to disruption in the network — applies to the agricultural base. The Anatolian plateau, where the Hittite heartland was, is not the Nile Delta. It is not guaranteed surplus. It requires good years. When the good years stop coming, the empire is eating into its reserves, and there are limits to reserves.

The grain letters are evidence that the Hittite Empire was already operating close to the edge. That it needed the international system to function. That the international system's failure would mean the Hittite Empire's failure.

And feeding "the population" of an empire like Hatti meant feeding people I haven't told you about yet.

The Hittite war machine did not just take territory; it took people. The royal annals count them alongside the cattle and the silver — the Hittite term is usually translated "transportees": populations of conquered regions, marched back to the Anatolian heartland and resettled where the crown needed labor. Temple estates. Crown land. The households of soldiers. One king's annals claim fifteen and a half thousand transportees from a single campaign in the west, brought home for the royal estates alone, with what his soldiers took for themselves described as *beyond counting*. The numbers are royal propaganda — "he claimed," not "there were" — but the practice is solid, regulated, written into Hittite law, which gives the transportee a defined legal status. Some real fraction of the people working the fields that were now coming in twenty percent short had been carried to those fields as plunder.

And an empire's food crisis does not land evenly. A ration list is a ranked thing, whether anyone says so out loud or not — when the warehouse is short, somebody decides whose measure gets smaller, and you know roughly where in the table that lands first. No tablet records that decision. But the decision is real, and you can guess where it falls hardest: on the people who have been at the bottom of these ledgers since the weaving halls of Uruk.

Add to this: succession crises at the Hittite court. Internal revolts among the subordinate kingdoms. The Mycenaean palaces beginning to increase their fortifications in ways that suggest they perceive threats in their regional environment. The first references in Egyptian sources to the Sea Peoples.

---

The Sea Peoples.

The Egyptian sources refer to groups of people attacking Egypt and the Egyptian-controlled Levant. These groups are named — Peleset, Tjekker, Sherden, Lukka, Sheklesh, others — and the Egyptian inscriptions describe them as marauders, invaders, destroyers of civilization. The famous inscription of Ramses III at Medinet Habu names the groups and claims a great victory. The battle itself belongs to the next part.

The Peleset are almost certainly the Philistines of the Hebrew Bible, and the modern identification is reasonably secure. Where they came from is less clear — probably somewhere in the Aegean, possibly Mycenaean refugees, possibly people from Anatolia or Cyprus displaced by the broader disruptions of the period.

The other groups are murkier. "Sea Peoples" is a modern category, not an ancient one. The Egyptians didn't use this term — they described specific groups in specific contexts. Modern scholars have grouped them together because they appear in the sources around the same time and apparently in similar roles. Whether they were a coordinated coalition, or a series of separate groups, or opportunistic raiders taking advantage of the chaos created by other stressors, or refugees displaced by the drought and the collapse of their own polities — these are genuinely debated questions.

I find this one hard to land on. The honest answer is probably "some of all of the above," in different proportions in different places at different moments — but "some of all of the above" is the kind of answer that doesn't pack down into a documentary line, so it gets simplified into one of the alternatives, and the alternative that gets chosen tends to depend on what story the teller wants to tell. The framing I have come to find most plausible is the refugee-raider hybrid: that the same disruptions which were producing displaced people in the eastern Mediterranean were also producing the opportunistic warbands that hit the coasts, and that the same individuals may have been both at different points in their lives. But I am not certain, and the people who study this for a living are not certain either, and I'd rather say that out loud than pretend otherwise.

The old model in which the Sea Peoples swept out of somewhere and destroyed the Bronze Age civilizations in a single great migration-invasion doesn't hold. The timing doesn't work — the destructions at different sites happened at different times over decades, not in a single coordinated assault. Some sites that were destroyed weren't destroyed by Sea Peoples — they were destroyed by internal revolts, or by processes whose cause we still don't fully understand, or by economic collapse that preceded any physical destruction.

The Sea Peoples were real. They were part of the story. They were not the whole story.

Cline puts their role precisely: "I believe they're as much a symptom of the collapse as they were a cause." The evidence he cites is what happened far inland, out of any sea raider's reach — Kassite Babylonia fell within decades, Elam buckled, and even Assyria, which survived, contracted hard. Whatever was squeezing those kingdoms, it wasn't a naval invasion.

Here is the question I always want to ask at this point, and it is the question you end up asking of every collapse, including the ones people worry about now: could they see it coming?

Not *did* they — *could* they. Did the world give them enough signal, early enough, that a sufficiently clear-eyed person could have stood up in a court at Hattusa or Mycenae or Ugarit, around 1230 BCE, and said: this is not a run of bad years. This is the end of the conditions that make our world possible.

And the honest answer, I think, is: almost certainly not — and not because they were less intelligent than we are. They saw *things*. They saw the harvests come in short. They saw the raiders. They saw shipments slip and letters go unanswered. Every one of those things had happened before, within living memory, and the world had recovered, because that is what the world had always done. The thing they could not see was the system — the whole interlocking arrangement of grain and tin and treaties and rowers — because no one alive had ever seen it from the outside. There was no word for it. You cannot diagnose the failure of a thing you do not know you are standing inside of.

And there is a crueler layer underneath that one: the system itself was hiding the evidence. I told you this disaster begins in sunshine. This is why — a well-buffered system looks fine until it is almost finished.

So when we go looking for the moment somebody noticed — and people must have noticed *something* — we don't find a prophet. We find an accountant.

**[State of the World snapshot — a Hittite official's warehouse, approximately 1230 BCE]**

The grain inventory is wrong again.

He has done this accounting three times in the past month because he doesn't trust the first two results, and the third result is the same as the first two. The stores are twenty percent below what they should be at this time of year. The harvest was the third poor harvest in a row. Not catastrophic — catastrophic would be obvious — but below expectation, and below expectation three years running.

The palace is still receiving tribute from the subordinate kingdoms. Some of it. Less than it used to, because some of the subordinate kingdoms are also having poor harvests, and a kingdom with a poor harvest cannot ship as much grain as one that had a good year. He has sent letters explaining this. He has received letters explaining this from other officials. The letters stack up. The shortfalls accumulate.

Outside the warehouse, in the courtyard, a shipment from Egypt has arrived. This is unusual — it should not be unusual, Egyptian grain arrives every year as part of the treaty relationship, but until recently the Egyptian grain was supplementary, was an additional margin of safety. This year, and possibly last year, the Egyptian grain is not supplementary. It is necessary.

The world this official inhabits: the palace of the Great King, to whom he is responsible. The administrative network that runs from the palace out to the subordinate kingdoms and back in as tribute flows. The accounting tablets that track, in meticulous cuneiform, quantities of grain and oil and metal and livestock that are the material expression of imperial power. The cuneiform itself is unchanged from the form Lamassi would have recognized nearly six centuries earlier — the same signs, the same conventions, the same press of stylus into clay. The technology has been functioning for so long that no one in the palace administration thinks of it as technology anymore. It is simply the way records are kept. The letters arriving from the coasts — the coastal governors' reports, which have become, over the past few years, increasingly alarming in specific ways.

What the coastal reports say: there are raiders. Not unprecedented raiders — there have always been raiders — but raiders operating at greater scale, in more organized groups, hitting multiple sites in a pattern that suggests something more than opportunistic piracy. Some of the subordinate kingdoms on the coast have sent emergency messages. Some of those messages have not been followed up with subsequent messages, which may mean the situation resolved, or may mean something else.

The official does not know how to categorize what he is seeing. He has no framework for "system collapse." He has a framework for "bad year" and "worse year" and "this year's problems need to be solved before next year's problems arrive." He is trying to solve the current problem with the current tools.

The current tools are not adequate to the current problem, but he doesn't know that yet.

What was at the harbor at Ugarit, in that dusk light we were standing in not long ago, is still there. But ships expected last month have not arrived. The tin shipment that was supposed to supply the bronze-smiths' workshop has been delayed, which has never happened before, or has only happened once or twice before, but that was during the disruptions, and those disruptions resolved.

The world still looks like the cosmopolitan world we just left. From a certain distance, in a certain light, it still looks like the peak.

It isn't.

---

The Mycenaean palaces are fortifying.

Tiryns — the Mycenaean palace complex on the plain of Argos — was, by the late thirteenth century BCE, surrounded by walls that are genuinely extraordinary. Cyclopean masonry: enormous limestone blocks, some of them weighing several tons, fitted together without mortar, forming walls up to eight meters thick. The palaces had always had defensive works. These walls are different. These walls are serious.

Stand for a moment with the men building them, because somebody built them. A work gang on the slope below Tiryns, in the heat, moving a block the weight of two oxen with rollers and levers and rope and too few people, under a foreman who answers to the palace. These are farmers, mostly — conscripted labor owed to the palace the way grain is owed, drawn away from their own fields in the season the fields need them. The wall they are building is not like the walls their grandfathers knew. Later Greeks, standing where these men stood, will decide that no human beings could have raised these stones, and will assign the work to the Cyclopes — to giants. It was not giants. It was frightened administrators and tired men, working against a deadline nobody could name out loud.

The same is true at Mycenae itself. At the palace of Pylos on the southwestern Peloponnese, there is less evidence of fortification, which will turn out to be relevant. And among the palace's last records: emergency requisitions for rowers. Oarsmen for ships, requested urgently, from the coastal regions that were the palace's territory. Someone was expecting a naval attack — and the someone writing it down was the administrator we met in his archive room at the peak. Same desk. Same clay. Same handwriting tradition. The desk had always counted rowers — owed by the coastal towns, annual, routine as the figs and the linen. These tablets count rowers begged for, urgently, by name. The instrument hasn't changed at all. The urgency has.

What were the Mycenaean palaces afraid of? Probably each other, partly, because the Mycenaean world had its own internal politics and its own inter-palace dynamics. And external threats, partly, from the disruptions that were already visible in the coastal areas of the eastern Mediterranean and that the Mycenaean trade network would have been receiving information about.

The fortification effort itself is evidence. You don't build those walls if you're not afraid. The palaces of the Cosmopolitan Age don't look like fortresses — they look like administrative centers, which is what they were. Fortress-palaces are what you build when the administrative center has to become a military one, and the architects weren't planning for that.

---

Who noticed first? Not the kings — the kings noticed late, because the system's job was to keep kings comfortable. The people built to notice were the ones whose livelihoods *were* the connections: the merchants. The caravan operators, the ship captains, the agents in foreign ports — the heirs of the trade Lamassi's family ran, people for whom "the system" was never an abstraction but a list of names. The partner in this port. The customs man at that crossing. The buyer who takes the wool every autumn. A king learns a route is dead when the tin doesn't arrive. The merchant learned it months earlier — when the partner stopped answering, when the risk-sharing arrangements merchants had been writing for centuries got too expensive to write, when the men who knew the road stopped being willing to take it at any price. Trade is a nervous system, and pain travels it fast. By the time the palaces were writing each other about enemy ships, the commercial networks had been rerouting, thinning, and going quiet for years. Nobody was charting that. It was just thousands of individually sensible people, one at a time, declining to risk what their fathers had risked routinely — which is how a connected world disconnects. Not with a decree. With ten thousand small refusals.

When the states finally said it out loud, it sounded like this.

From the final years of the cosmopolitan correspondence, there is a letter — found in the archives at Ugarit, sent by a senior official of Alashiya, which is Cyprus, a man named Eshuwara. He is writing about enemy ships. Some twenty of them, he reports, have been sighted; they have done evil things; and where they have gone now, nobody knows. He writes it in the formal diplomatic register, the courtesies intact, the titles in their proper order — because the diplomatic system is the only channel that exists for one state to tell another state anything at all.

What gets me about that letter is not the drama of the moment but the texture of the situation. A governor on Cyprus is using the international system's language, its scribes, its couriers — to report, in effect, that the international system has lost control of the sea between them. He is using a system to report the beginning of that system's failure. The form and the content are working against each other.

And the letter survives because it reached Ugarit — a city that, within a few years of filing it, would be drafting pleas of its own about ships nobody could name. We will stand in that room in the next part.

And on the other end of that correspondence, the last Great King of Hatti was doing something no Hittite king had ever had to do. His name — by one of those ironies history doesn't bother to apologize for — was Suppiluliuma, the same name as the conqueror who had erased Mitanni a century and a half before. The first Suppiluliuma had made the empire untouchable. The second was fighting *naval battles* — the first naval battles in recorded history, off the coast of Cyprus — with an Anatolian land empire's improvised fleet, trying to keep the sea lanes open because the sea lanes were now where the grain came from. He won them, for whatever it was worth. He commissioned inscriptions about his victories, in the old confident imperial style, while his administrators counted shortfalls in the warehouses and his coastal vassals' letters went unanswered. The form held. The content was gone.

The accounting tablets are still being updated. The letters are still being sent. The grain inventories are still being taken. The diplomatic correspondence is still using the formal address, the "my brother" and the "your humble servant." The system is still using the forms it was built on, while the problems it's being asked to handle have stopped being the problems the forms were built for.

---

The last generation of people who lived fully inside the cosmopolitan world — who were born into it, worked in it, had no personal memory of anything else — died around 1230 or 1220 BCE. They died in their beds or in the fields or from illness, in the ordinary way that people have always died, without knowing that what was ending was not just their lives but a world.

Their children grew up in a world that was slightly worse than what their parents had known. Their grandchildren grew up in a world that was notably worse. Their great-grandchildren — born around 1175 or 1170 BCE — would be born into the wreckage.

That is not a fast collapse. That is a long, grinding deterioration. That is multiple bad harvests and missing supply shipments and coastal raids and emergency messages and rowers requisitioned and walls built higher, accumulating over two or three generations, until the thing that had been the cosmopolitan world was no longer the cosmopolitan world.

And none of it was the whole world. In these same decades, at Anyang on the Yellow River, the kings of Shang China were casting ritual bronzes by the ton, in piece-molds, by methods no Mediterranean smith had ever seen. When a royal consort named Fu Hao died around 1200 BCE — a woman who had led armies in the field; the king's own diviners recorded her campaigns on the oracle bones — she went into the ground with fifteen hundred objects: bronzes, jades, weapons, and chariots — the steppe's invention, so newly arrived in China that her grandparents would never have seen one, carried east from the same grasslands that had sent it west toward Kadesh. On the other side of the landmass, the Bronze Age was at full noon. What was ending was not the Bronze Age. It was one region's version of it — the interlocked, seaborne, treaty-bound version whose wiring we have spent this hour watching fray. From Anyang, none of what follows was even a rumor.

And then came the fires.
