## The Draft

---

There is a kind of disaster that begins in sunshine.

You want the disaster story to start with darkness, with obvious portents, with something in the air that lets the people inside it know — or should let them know — what's coming. The sky turns the wrong color. The harvest fails visibly and dramatically. The enemies appear on the horizon.

What actually happens, with complex systems failing, is different. It starts on a perfectly normal day, or a series of perfectly normal days, and the days are slightly less good than the previous batch of days, and this seems unremarkable because some years are slightly worse than others, and in the previous generation the years were also sometimes slightly worse, and things recovered. And then the slightly-less-good runs longer than it used to, and then certain things that were reliable become less reliable, and then certain things that were expected begin to not arrive, and at some point — much later than it should have been, because the system has built-in mechanisms that absorb stress and delay the signal — you realize that you are not in a temporary disruption.

You are in the end of something.

This part lives inside those slightly-less-good years, in several different locations, in a world coming apart from the inside, before it knows it's coming apart.

---

The Kadesh peace treaty, 1259 BCE, is the last moment I can point to and say: the system is working.

Not perfectly — the system was never perfect, the empires were never stable in any deep sense, the complaints never stopped, the grain shipments were never always on time. But in 1259 BCE, the Egyptian and Hittite empires signed a formal peace treaty, and both sides kept it. We have both versions — the Egyptian hieroglyphic text carved on the walls of Karnak, the massive temple complex at Thebes in what is now Luxor, and the Hittite cuneiform version from the archives at Hattusa, the Hittite capital built on a rocky plateau in what is now central Turkey — and they say substantially the same things. Mutual non-aggression. Mutual extradition. Defensive alliance against internal and external threats. The gods of both nations as witnesses.

Ramses II and Hattusili III — two of the most powerful rulers in the world — had fought at Kadesh, failed to defeat each other, claimed victory anyway, and then, sixteen years later, decided that peace was more useful than another campaign. Following the treaty came royal marriages: Egyptian princesses to Hatti, Hittite princesses to Egypt. Letters back and forth. The careful maintenance of a diplomatic relationship between two empires that shared a border and needed each other's restraint to remain stable.

Worth pausing on how we have the Hittite side of any of this — because until the early twentieth century, the world had effectively lost the Hittites. There were a few references to "Hittites" in the Hebrew Bible — King David's loyal commander Uriah is called one, Solomon is said to have taken Hittite wives — but the references were thin enough that biblical scholars sometimes dismissed them as scribal errors or as names for unrelated peoples. The Hittites, in any serious sense, were forgotten. In 1906, the German archaeologist Hugo Winckler began excavating a mound called Boğazköy on the central Anatolian plateau, about a hundred and fifty kilometers east of modern Ankara. What he uncovered was a massive walled city, and inside the citadel, archives — tens of thousands of clay tablets in cuneiform, most of them in a language nobody had ever read. When the Czech linguist Bedřich Hrozný cracked the language in 1915, he established two things at once: it was Indo-European, more closely related to English than to anything spoken in the modern Middle East; and Hattusa, the lost capital of an empire that had matched Egypt for power, was the city Winckler had been digging. The Hittite Empire walked back onto the historical stage after thirty-two centuries of being gone. The Hittite version of the Kadesh treaty came out of that archive. So did the Cyprus letter we'll meet shortly. So did half of the diplomatic record of the cosmopolitan world.

That's 1259. That's the peak.

By 1200 BCE — fifty-nine years later — the Hittite Empire will not exist. Its capital, Hattusa — built on a dramatic rocky plateau in what is now central Turkey, about 150 kilometers east of modern Ankara — will have been abandoned and partially burned. The Mycenaean palaces will be destroyed. Ugarit will be ash. Egypt will have survived, barely, and will never recover to anything like its New Kingdom power. The cosmopolitan world of the Amarna letters and the Uluburun ship will be a memory, and not even a clear memory — it will be a mythologized half-memory that will take three thousand years to decode.

What happened in those fifty-nine years?

A lot of things happened at once. Not one cause. Not the Sea Peoples, as the conventional story used to have it. Multiple stressors, converging, in a system that had been built for surplus and became, gradually and then suddenly, a system operating at deficit.

Eric Cline, whose 1177 B.C.: The Year Civilization Collapsed (2014) is the most widely read modern account of the collapse, calls it "a perfect storm of calamities" — each individually survivable, together fatal. Cline's multi-causal model has been influential and is probably the right general frame, even if specialists debate exactly how to weight the different factors. His core argument: the Late Bronze Age system was "an overly interdependent system that had no way to absorb multiple shocks at once." The interconnectedness that had been its strength for three centuries was also what made it brittle.

---

The climate.

We have proxy records now that didn't exist a generation ago. Tree rings, extracted from ancient wood preserved in lake beds and archaeological sites, showing the growth patterns of trees across the eastern Mediterranean in the late second millennium BCE. Pollen records, showing what was growing where and when. Sediment cores from the eastern Mediterranean sea bed, showing what the rivers were carrying — the particles that indicate erosion, drought, vegetation collapse. Lake levels, measurable from ancient watermarks.

What they show, consistently, is that the eastern Mediterranean experienced a prolonged drought event beginning sometime around 1250 BCE and lasting for decades. Not everywhere equally. Not every year. But the trend line shows drier, warmer conditions across the region in the last quarter of the thirteenth century BCE, with some of the worst years in the first quarter of the twelfth century.

Brandon Drake, in a 2012 study in the Journal of Archaeological Science, analyzed sea surface temperature records, pollen cores, and isotopic data from across the eastern Mediterranean. His finding: sea surface temperatures cooled rapidly during this period, reducing moisture flux into the atmosphere and cutting precipitation over land. The drought was not local. It was regional, and it arrived precisely when the palace economies were least able to absorb it.

A drought that lasts a year is a hardship. Agricultural systems have buffers — stored grain, alternative crops, emergency redistribution. A drought that lasts five years is a crisis. A drought that lasts twenty years, punctuated by occasional adequate years that allow hope but not recovery, eats through the reserves, eats through the patience, eats through the assumption that next year will be normal.

The palace economies of the Late Bronze Age were calibrated for normal conditions plus a buffer. They were not calibrated for twenty years of below-normal agricultural output across an entire region simultaneously.

The climate was one factor. Some popular accounts have made it the single explanation, the tidy cause that lets everything else off the hook. The climate was real. The climate was consequential. It did not, by itself, explain everything.

---

The grain letters.

Among the Hittite archives from the period just before the collapse, there are letters that ask Egypt for grain. Not as tribute, not as a gift — as a purchase, an emergency import, because the Hittites cannot grow enough food to feed their population and their armies.

The Hittite Empire — one of the two great empires of the Late Bronze Age, a military power that had fought Egypt to a standstill at Kadesh — was, in the decades before its collapse, dependent on Egyptian grain imports to function.

This is the metallurgical constraint applied to food. The same logic that made bronze dependent on a supply chain for tin — the same logic that made the whole Bronze Age system vulnerable to disruption in the network — applies to the agricultural base. The Anatolian plateau, where the Hittite heartland was, is not the Nile Delta. It is not guaranteed surplus. It requires good years. When the good years stop coming, the empire is eating into its reserves, and there are limits to reserves.

The grain letters are evidence that the Hittite Empire was already operating close to the edge. That it needed the international system to function. That the international system's failure would mean the Hittite Empire's failure.

Add to this: succession crises at the Hittite court. Internal revolts among the subordinate kingdoms. The Mycenaean palaces beginning to increase their fortifications in ways that suggest they perceive threats in their regional environment. The first references in Egyptian sources to the Sea Peoples.

---

The Sea Peoples.

The Egyptian sources refer to groups of people attacking Egypt and the Egyptian-controlled Levant. These groups are named — Peleset, Tjekker, Sherden, Lukka, Sheklesh, others — and the Egyptian inscriptions describe them as marauders, invaders, destroyers of civilization. The famous inscription of Ramses III at Medinet Habu describes a great coalition of Sea Peoples attacking by land and sea, describes Egypt's heroic victory over them, depicts the battle in extensive relief sculpture.

The Peleset are almost certainly the Philistines of the Hebrew Bible, and the modern identification is reasonably secure. Where they came from is less clear — probably somewhere in the Aegean, possibly Mycenaean refugees, possibly people from Anatolia or Cyprus displaced by the broader disruptions of the period.

The other groups are murkier. "Sea Peoples" is a modern category, not an ancient one. The Egyptians didn't use this term — they described specific groups in specific contexts. Modern scholars have grouped them together because they appear in the sources around the same time and apparently in similar roles. Whether they were a coordinated coalition, or a series of separate groups, or opportunistic raiders taking advantage of the chaos created by other stressors, or refugees displaced by the drought and the collapse of their own polities — these are genuinely debated questions.

I find this one hard to land on. The honest answer is probably "some of all of the above," in different proportions in different places at different moments — but "some of all of the above" is the kind of answer that doesn't pack down into a documentary line, so it gets simplified into one of the alternatives, and the alternative that gets chosen tends to depend on what story the teller wants to tell. The framing I have come to find most plausible is the refugee-raider hybrid: that the same disruptions which were producing displaced people in the eastern Mediterranean were also producing the opportunistic warbands that hit the coasts, and that the same individuals may have been both at different points in their lives. But I am not certain, and the people who study this for a living are not certain either, and I'd rather say that out loud than pretend otherwise.

The old model in which the Sea Peoples swept out of somewhere and destroyed the Bronze Age civilizations in a single great migration-invasion doesn't hold. The timing doesn't work — the destructions at different sites happened at different times over decades, not in a single coordinated assault. Some sites that were destroyed weren't destroyed by Sea Peoples — they were destroyed by internal revolts, or by processes whose cause we still don't fully understand, or by economic collapse that preceded any physical destruction.

The Sea Peoples were real. They were part of the story. They were not the whole story.

Cline puts their role precisely: "I believe they're as much a symptom of the collapse as they were a cause." The evidence he cites is the collapse of inland empires — Kassite Babylonia, Assyria, Elam — that the Sea Peoples never touched. Whatever destroyed those empires, it wasn't a naval invasion.

**[State of the World snapshot — a Hittite official's warehouse, approximately 1230 BCE]**

The grain inventory is wrong again.

He has done this accounting three times in the past month because he doesn't trust the first two results, and the third result is the same as the first two. The stores are twenty percent below what they should be at this time of year. The harvest was the third poor harvest in a row. Not catastrophic — catastrophic would be obvious — but below expectation, and below expectation three years running.

The palace is still receiving tribute from the subordinate kingdoms. Some of it. Less than it used to, because some of the subordinate kingdoms are also having poor harvests, and a kingdom with a poor harvest cannot ship as much grain as one that had a good year. He has sent letters explaining this. He has received letters explaining this from other officials. The letters stack up. The shortfalls accumulate.

Outside the warehouse, in the courtyard, a shipment from Egypt has arrived. This is unusual — it should not be unusual, Egyptian grain arrives every year as part of the treaty relationship, but until recently the Egyptian grain was supplementary, was an additional margin of safety. This year, and possibly last year, the Egyptian grain is not supplementary. It is necessary.

The world this official inhabits: the palace of the Great King, to whom he is responsible. The administrative network that runs from the palace out to the subordinate kingdoms and back in as tribute flows. The accounting tablets that track, in meticulous cuneiform, quantities of grain and oil and metal and livestock that are the material expression of imperial power. The cuneiform itself is unchanged from the form Lamassi would have recognized two centuries earlier — the same signs, the same conventions, the same press of stylus into clay. The technology has been functioning for so long that no one in the palace administration thinks of it as technology anymore. It is simply the way records are kept. The letters arriving from the coasts — the coastal governors' reports, which have become, over the past few years, increasingly alarming in specific ways.

What the coastal reports say: there are raiders. Not unprecedented raiders — there have always been raiders — but raiders operating at greater scale, in more organized groups, hitting multiple sites in a pattern that suggests something more than opportunistic piracy. Some of the subordinate kingdoms on the coast have sent emergency messages. Some of those messages have not been followed up with subsequent messages, which may mean the situation resolved, or may mean something else.

The official does not know how to categorize what he is seeing. He has no framework for "system collapse." He has a framework for "bad year" and "worse year" and "this year's problems need to be solved before next year's problems arrive." He is trying to solve the current problem with the current tools.

The current tools are not adequate to the current problem, but he doesn't know that yet.

What was at the harbor at Ugarit, in that dusk light we were standing in not long ago, is still there. But ships expected last month have not arrived. The tin shipment that was supposed to supply the bronze-smiths' workshop has been delayed, which has never happened before, or has only happened once or twice before, but that was during the disruptions, and those disruptions resolved.

The world still looks like the cosmopolitan world we just left. From a certain distance, in a certain light, it still looks like the peak.

It isn't.

---

The Mycenaean palaces are fortifying.

Tiryns — the Mycenaean palace complex on the plain of Argos — was, by the late thirteenth century BCE, surrounded by walls that are genuinely extraordinary. Cyclopean masonry: enormous limestone blocks, some of them weighing several tons, fitted together without mortar, forming walls up to eight meters thick. The palaces had always had defensive works. These walls are different. These walls are serious.

The same is true at Mycenae itself. At the palace of Pylos on the southwestern Peloponnese, there is less evidence of fortification, which will turn out to be relevant — Pylos will be destroyed thoroughly, and the Linear B tablets baked in the destruction fire are the most complete palace archive we have from Mycenaean Greece, and they include, among their last records, emergency requisitions for rowers. Oarsmen for ships, requested urgently, from the coastal regions that were the palace's territory. Someone was expecting a naval attack.

What were the Mycenaean palaces afraid of? Probably each other, partly, because the Mycenaean world had its own internal politics and its own inter-palace dynamics. And external threats, partly, from the disruptions that were already visible in the coastal areas of the eastern Mediterranean and that the Mycenaean trade network would have been receiving information about.

The fortification effort itself is evidence. You don't build those walls if you're not afraid. The palaces of the Cosmopolitan Age don't look like fortresses — they look like administrative centers, which is what they were. Fortress-palaces are what you build when the administrative center has to become a military one, and the architects weren't planning for that.

---

From the Hittite archives at Hattusa, there is a letter from the king of Alashiya — Cyprus — to the Hittite king. The letter survives in the Hittite diplomatic corpus translated by Gary Beckman; Cline dates it to approximately 1200 BCE or slightly before — right at the edge of the crisis.

The letter says, roughly: my cities have been burned. The enemy attacked from the sea. He has done very harmful things. My father, you must know that seven of my own ships are gone. Whatever there was of silver and gold was carried off. The enemy has been doing evil things, and the matter is urgent. The king of Alashiya is asking for help.

The help was not going to come. The Hittites were already in crisis. The ships that might have helped were already not where they were supposed to be. The letter — like the Ugarit warning letter we'll encounter shortly — was written in a moment of emergency and received, if it was received, by a recipient who had no capacity to respond adequately.

What gets me about that letter is not the drama of the moment but the texture of the situation. The king of Cyprus is writing in diplomatic language — the formal language of the international system — because the international system is the framework he has for communicating with other powers. He is using a system to report the beginning of that system's failure. The form and the content are working against each other.

The accounting tablets are still being updated. The letters are still being sent. The grain inventories are still being taken. The diplomatic correspondence is still using the formal address, the "my brother" and the "your humble servant." The system is still using the forms it was built on, while the problems it's being asked to handle have stopped being the problems the forms were built for.

---

The last generation of people who lived fully inside the cosmopolitan world — who were born into it, worked in it, had no personal memory of anything else — died around 1230 or 1220 BCE. They died in their beds or in the fields or from illness, in the ordinary way that people have always died, without knowing that what was ending was not just their lives but a world.

Their children grew up in a world that was slightly worse than what their parents had known. Their grandchildren grew up in a world that was notably worse. Their great-grandchildren — born around 1175 or 1170 BCE — would be born into the wreckage.

That is not a fast collapse. That is a long, grinding deterioration. That is multiple bad harvests and missing supply shipments and coastal raids and emergency messages and rowers requisitioned and walls built higher, accumulating over two or three generations, until the thing that had been the cosmopolitan world was no longer the cosmopolitan world.

And then came the fires.
