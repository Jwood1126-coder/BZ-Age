## The Draft

---

Let me read you a cargo manifest.

This is not a metaphor. I am going to read you the actual cargo of an actual ship.

The ship sank off the southern coast of what is now Turkey, near a place called Uluburun, sometime around 1300 BCE. It was about fifteen meters long. We don't know where it was going. We don't know who owned it. We don't know what its crew looked like or what they ate for breakfast or what language they spoke — probably more than one language, given who was on it.

What we know is what it was carrying when it went down.

Ten tons of copper ingots, shaped in the distinctive ox-hide form of the Late Bronze Age Mediterranean, each one weighing about twenty-five kilograms. The copper came from Cyprus — the name of the metal, copper, comes from the Latin name for that island.

One ton of tin ingots. The tin almost certainly came from somewhere far to the east — possibly what is now Afghanistan, possibly further. The exact sourcing of Late Bronze Age tin is still being worked out.

A ton of terebinth resin in Canaanite jars — the resin used in incense and in preserving wine.

Ebony logs. The ebony is sub-Saharan African.

Hippopotamus ivory. Also African.

Elephant ivory. Possibly African, possibly Syrian.

Blue glass ingots from Egypt or the Syro-Palestinian coast.

Ostrich eggs from North Africa.

Amber beads. Baltic amber. From the region that is now Poland or the Baltic states, two thousand miles away.

Gold jewelry. Faience. Copper tools. Tin tools. A gold scarab that may have belonged to the Egyptian queen Nefertiti. Mycenaean pottery. Canaanite pottery. Cypriot pottery. A Near Eastern lamp. A Canaanite merchant's cylinder seal set. Personal belongings consistent with a crew of Canaanite and possibly Mycenaean origin.

Weapons from at least half a dozen different military traditions.

A book of words inscribed on ivory writing boards in the kind of frames that scribes used as notebooks — the wooden boards and the ivory hinges survive; the writing, on what was probably waxed surfaces, does not.

This is one ship. This is the cargo of one ship that sank three thousand three hundred years ago.

Item by item, the manifest describes a world that should be impossible. Sub-Saharan Africa, the Baltic coast, the mountains of Afghanistan, the Nile Delta, the Aegean — all of it in transit in a single vessel the size of a modest pleasure boat, in a world with no compass, no powered transport, no satellite navigation, no international banking system, no global governance framework.

And yet here it is.

How did the world get from a Neolithic farmer whose horizon was forty kilometers away to a merchant vessel carrying goods from every corner of the known world, all in one hold?

That is the question that the last four hours of this series have been building toward. And this is the part where we try to answer it.

---

The Late Bronze Age cosmopolitan system.

Let me give you the cast of players, briefly, because you need to know who is on stage before you can understand what they're doing together.

**Egypt.** In its New Kingdom phase — from roughly 1550 to 1070 BCE, Egypt is at the height of its imperial power. The New Kingdom pharaohs: Thutmose III, who pushed Egyptian influence further into the Levant than it had ever been; Akhenaten, who we'll spend real time on; Tutankhamun, who is famous mostly for being dead and well-preserved; Ramses II, who fought the largest chariot battle in history and then told everyone he'd won it. Egypt controls the Nile Delta, the Levant as far north as the Lebanese coast, and maintains influence as far as Nubia to the south. Egypt produces gold. More gold than anywhere else in the Bronze Age world. Gold is Egypt's political currency.

**The Hittites.** Based on the high Anatolian plateau, the Hittite Great Kingdom is the power that keeps Egypt from expanding further north. By around 1400 BCE, the Hittites control most of Anatolia, have absorbed or subordinated dozens of smaller kingdoms, and are in active diplomatic and occasional military conflict with Egypt over the control of the Levant — the coastal region that is roughly modern Syria and Lebanon, which is strategically valuable because it's the land bridge between the two empires.

**Mitanni.** A kingdom in northern Mesopotamia, now mostly in what is northern Syria and Iraq, speaking a language that is probably Indo-European (possibly related to the Vedic Sanskrit tradition, though this is debated) and ruling over a population that was largely Hurrian. At its height, around 1500 BCE, Mitanni was one of the great powers. By the time of the Amarna correspondence we'll discuss, it is beginning to decline.

**Kassite Babylonia.** The Kassite dynasty that took power after the Hittite raid on Babylon has been ruling for centuries. They are stable, administratively sophisticated, and fully integrated into the international system. They produce and export horses and lapis lazuli, among other things.

**Assyria.** At this point still subordinate to Mitanni, but growing. By the end of this period, Assyria will be one of the major powers, and it is Assyria that will eventually outlast most of the Late Bronze Age order.

**Mycenaean Greece.** The palace civilization of mainland Greece. They write in a script we call Linear B, which is an adaptation of an earlier Minoan writing system for the Greek language. The palaces — Mycenae, Tiryns, Pylos, Thebes — are sophisticated bureaucratic organizations. They are recognized as a power by the Hittites, who call them Ahhiyawa. They are, in a very real sense, the Bronze Age Greeks. They are nothing like the Greeks of the classical era. They are more like the other palace civilizations of the Bronze Age than like Pericles or Socrates.

**Ugarit.** Not a great power but a crucial node — a cosmopolitan city-state on the Syrian coast that functions as the crossroads of the international system. Six or more languages were in daily use in Ugarit. Its archive is one of the most important documents in any period of Bronze Age history.

And **Cyprus** — Alashiya in the ancient sources — which sits in the Mediterranean producing copper and serving as a transshipment hub.

These are the players. They know each other. They write to each other. They marry each other's daughters. They occasionally try to kill each other.

Together they constituted something that the world had not seen before and would not see again for two thousand years: an international system.

---

The Amarna letters.

In 1887 CE, an Egyptian woman digging in the ruins of what had once been a city called Akhetaten — the capital that Pharaoh Akhenaten built during his reign and that was abandoned after his death — found some clay tablets. She didn't know what they were. She sold them. Eventually they reached scholars. And what they turned out to be was the diplomatic correspondence of the Late Bronze Age great powers.

Three hundred and eighty-two tablets. Letters from the kings of Mitanni, Assyria, Babylon, the Hittites, and the rulers of smaller states in the Levant. Letters to Pharaoh. Letters from Pharaoh. The diplomatic archive of a superpower, written in the diplomatic language of the age — Akkadian — by scribes who were not native Akkadian speakers, received by scribes who were not native Akkadian speakers either, because Akkadian was the shared language that nobody spoke natively and everybody used.

Let me tell you what the letters say.

The great kings address each other as "my brother." This is not familial affection. It is diplomatic protocol. It establishes a formal equality between great-power rulers. You are my peer. You are not my subordinate. You are my brother. The language of kinship — brotherhood, in particular — was the language the Bronze Age international system used to designate peer recognition. The smaller rulers, the subordinate kings of the Levantine city-states, addressed Pharaoh as "my sun god, my lord" — the language of hierarchy. Brotherhood was for equals.

What do the brothers write to each other about?

Gold. Constantly. Gold. The Babylonian king writes to Pharaoh complaining that the gold shipment arrived lighter than agreed. The Mitanni king writes asking for more gold, specifically for a palace project, specifically in the quantity of "large quantities of gold." Pharaoh's gold is the lubricant of the international system — it flows outward from Egypt to maintain the relationships that keep the system functioning.

Marriage. The great kings exchange daughters. Not their sons — that would imply inferiority on one side or the other, and nobody wanted to imply that. But daughters: Egyptian princesses to Babylonian courts, Mitanni princesses to Egypt. The Mitanni king Tushratta sends his daughter Tadukhipa to Amenhotep III. When Amenhotep III dies, she presumably becomes part of Akhenaten's household. The letters about this are among the most human documents in the archive — the father anxious about his daughter's welfare, asking after her repeatedly, asking to be told how she is doing in Egypt, never quite sure if the answers he receives are true.

And complaints. The letters are full of complaints. About gifts that didn't arrive, or arrived in inadequate quantity, or arrived damaged. About messages that weren't answered promptly. About the behavior of envoys who were treated badly at the court they visited. About the behavior of subordinate rulers who were getting above themselves.

The Babylonian king, in one letter, complains at length that his envoy was kept waiting in the Egyptian sun for so long that he became ill. This is presented as a diplomatic incident of some seriousness. Pharaoh's response — if we have it — probably involved explanation and mollification.

This is the great-power diplomacy of the Late Bronze Age. Brothers, gold, daughters, complaints.

It is recognizably the diplomacy of any period in which rival powers need to manage their relationships without constant warfare. The specific forms are Bronze Age. The underlying dynamics are legible.

---

Akhenaten.

I'm going to tell you who Akhenaten probably was before I tell you who we have been tempted to think he was, because the second version is more attractive and less true.

Akhenaten — born Amenhotep IV, son of Amenhotep III — became pharaoh around 1353 BCE. He had strong opinions about the nature of the divine, and the opinions were unusual. He promoted the worship of a single deity, the Aten — the solar disc, the light itself — above all other gods, and in the course of his reign he did things to Egyptian religious life that his successors would spend decades trying to reverse.

Who he probably was: a pharaoh with a distinctive theological vision, probably genuine religious feeling, and catastrophically bad political judgment. He destabilized the temple economy of Egypt — which was, in this period, an enormous economic force — by suppressing the cults of other gods and diverting their resources. He moved the capital from Thebes to a newly built city called Akhetaten on a stretch of river that had no strategic or administrative advantage other than not being Thebes. He ran his foreign policy badly — the Amarna letters from his reign show subordinate Levantine rulers pleading desperately for military assistance against the Hittites and their clients, and not getting it, probably because Akhenaten was too focused on his religious program to manage the empire effectively.

Who he has been romanticized as: the first monotheist, a proto-enlightened figure who saw through the polytheistic traditions of his age toward a more rational or spiritually advanced vision of the divine. The distant ancestor of Abrahamic monotheism. A man ahead of his time, crushed by the conservative religious establishment.

The romantic version has a long history. Sigmund Freud wrote a whole book arguing that Moses was an Akhenaten follower. The lineage sounds elegant.

The problem is that the Egyptian sources after his reign treat Akhenaten as a criminal, not a visionary. The deliberate erasure of his name from inscriptions. The dismantling of his capital. The reversal of everything he did, within a generation, by successors who did not want to be associated with him. The Amarna heresy, as Egyptologists call it, was seen by its Egyptian contemporaries and successors not as a noble experiment but as a disaster.

The monotheism connection is also less clean than the romantic version suggests. The Aten was not, in Akhenaten's theology, the only god in the universe. The Aten was the god of Egypt under Akhenaten. The gods of foreign peoples were not denied; they were acknowledged as foreign powers. This is henotheism — the elevation of one god among many — not monotheism in the Abrahamic sense.

Render him honestly. He is interesting enough without the gloss. A pharaoh who tried to radically transform the religious and economic framework of his civilization, who failed, and whose failure was so thorough that his own name was erased for three thousand years. The name Akhenaten was not recovered until the modern archaeological period. His mummy has probably been found — there is a candidate — but is still the subject of active dispute.

His lesson for this series is specific: the great powers of the Late Bronze Age could afford internal disruption of this scale, barely. Egypt survived Akhenaten. It emerged into the reign of Ramses II and the Battle of Kadesh and the diplomatic triumph of the Kadesh treaty. But the disruption was real, and the accumulated disruptions — each one of which could be absorbed alone — are part of what makes the system eventually vulnerable.

**[State of the World snapshot — Ugarit harbor, approximately 1350 BCE]**

Evening is better here. The harbor quiets as the sun goes down — less shouting, the ship captains have made their decisions about loading and departure, the day's frantic commerce has resolved itself into dinner and negotiation. The lamps come on in the windows of the houses that line the harbor-side streets.

The goods stacked along the wharves are wrong.

Not wrong in the sense of illegal or problematic — wrong in the sense that they don't belong to each other, don't come from the same world, shouldn't be sitting side by side. Copper ingots from Cyprus, fresh from the smelters. Amphorae of olive oil from somewhere in the Aegean, painted in a style you'd only see in the Mycenaean workshops. A consignment of lapis lazuli from the east — from Afghanistan, though that word doesn't mean anything to anyone here; the eastern trade routes are known by their nodes, not their endpoints. Wool textiles bundled for transport, the weaving pattern you recognize as Egyptian even before you look at the markings on the bundles.

The people are wrong in the same way.

Four languages in the first twenty steps from the wharf to the warehouses. Ugaritic, which is yours. Akkadian, which is the language you speak when the other person doesn't speak Ugaritic. Something that sounds like it might be one of the Aegean Greek dialects — you can pick out some words, trade words, numbers, names of goods — and something else, from the three sailors unloading a small boat, that you don't recognize at all.

Inside the nearest tavern: a Cypriot copper merchant you have done business with twice before. An Egyptian bureaucrat who arrived last week, probably here to assess the tribute flows. Two men from the Hittite trade mission who have been here long enough to learn where the good fish is.

The world this person knows:
The harbor, which is the world. The smell of the sea and the copper and the oil and the rope and the wood of the ships. The sound of a dozen dialects. The knowledge that the goods on these wharves have come from places you could not walk to in a year, from people you will never meet, through networks of ships and caravans and exchange relationships that have built up over centuries into something that feels, to the people inside it, like permanence.

The great kings — Egypt, Hatti, Babylonia, Mitanni — write to each other about gold and daughters and complaints. They are the structure. The structure is why there is a harbor at all, why the ships arrive, why the Cypriot copper merchant can make a living, why there is a tavern worth going to.

What is not in this world: iron used at scale. The curious listener has been asking about iron for several episodes, and the answer is about to be delivered, so hold on.

What is also not in this world: any sense that it will end. The people on this harbor do not know they are living at the peak. They think this is normal. They think it will continue.

They have forty or fifty more years.

---

But what about iron?

You have been waiting for this. If you have been listening for any length of time to this series, you have probably wondered: they had bronze, and then they had iron, so when did iron appear? Why did they keep using bronze when iron is better?

The answer to this question is one of the most important single lessons in the whole series about how technology and systems interact, and I'm going to take the time to do it properly.

First: they had iron.

Meteoric iron — iron that fell from the sky in meteorites — had been worked into objects since at least 3000 BCE. You could hammer meteoric iron into small objects: beads, pendants, the occasional blade. Meteoric iron was extremely rare and therefore extremely valuable. In the Hittite records, there are references to iron being worth roughly forty times its weight in silver. It was a luxury good, a royal gift, a thing kept in palace treasuries.

Tutankhamun's tomb contained a dagger with a blade made from meteoric iron. It's still there, still iron, still recognizable. It was a remarkable object when it was made, and the people who made it knew it was remarkable.

So iron was known. Iron was valued. Iron was worked in limited ways.

Why wasn't iron used at scale, the way bronze was?

Here is the answer: because bronze was better.

Not forever. Not in the long run. But in 1300 BCE, the iron being produced — which had to be smelted from terrestrial ore, a much harder process than working meteoric iron — was actually inferior to bronze for most military and agricultural purposes. Smelted iron is soft when it comes out of the process. To make it hard — to make what we would call steel — you need to add carbon and then work and quench the metal in specific ways. This technology — the carburization and quenching of iron to make hardened steel — was not yet reliable or widespread. The iron weapons being produced in this period were likely inferior to good bronze weapons.

Moreover, the trade network worked. Tin was arriving. Copper was arriving. The craftsmen who knew how to make excellent bronze weapons had been making them for centuries. The quality was good and the supply chain was functioning.

Bronze won, in 1300 BCE, not because it was inherently superior to iron but because the bronze system was working and the iron technology wasn't yet reliable enough to replace it.

And here is the payoff — the thing I've been building toward since the first part of this series where we talked about the metallurgical constraint:

Iron won, eventually, not because iron became better than bronze.

Iron won because bronze broke.

The trade network — the network that shipped tin from the far east and copper from Cyprus and combined them into bronze in workshops across the Bronze Age world — was the thing that made the Bronze Age possible and also the thing that made it fragile. Tin was scarce. Tin came from far away. Tin required functioning trade routes to arrive.

When the trade routes stopped functioning — when the Bronze Age collapse we're approaching disrupted the system so severely that the tin stopped arriving — the bronze-working tradition began to falter. And the alternative, the substitute, the metal you could produce from ore that exists almost everywhere rather than from an exotic ore that required a functioning international supply chain — that metal was iron.

The Iron Age is not the story of human ingenuity producing a better metal.

The Iron Age is the story of a supply-chain collapse forcing humans to use the metal they could get instead of the metal they preferred.

Sit with what that says about technological progress.

The chariot at its peak.

Kadesh, 1274 BCE. The Battle of Kadesh between the forces of Ramses II of Egypt and Muwatalli II of the Hittites. Our best estimate is that between ten and fifteen thousand chariots were engaged on both sides combined. Whatever the actual number — and Egyptian inscriptions inflate the Egyptian count and the Hittite count both — it was the largest chariot battle in human history.

Ramses II was nearly killed. He claimed victory in inscriptions so prominent and so thoroughly plastered across Egyptian monuments that they survive in detail to this day. The Hittites also claimed victory, and their version is equally propaganda-inflated, and the reality is probably that both sides fought to something close to a draw and then chose to call it a win.

Sixteen years later, they signed the first peace treaty in history that survives from both sides. Both versions — Egyptian hieroglyphics and Hittite cuneiform — say substantially the same things. Mutual non-aggression. Mutual extradition of refugees and criminals. Divine witnesses on both sides — the gods of Egypt and the gods of Hatti are named as guarantors of the agreement. A copy of this treaty hangs in the United Nations headquarters in New York, in the second-floor corridor outside the Security Council chamber.

The Bronze Age invented the international diplomatic system. Including, apparently, the architecture that would still be housing it three thousand years later.

But here is what the Kadesh treaty also represents: the apex and the edge. The chariot armies that fought at Kadesh were the most impressive military force the Bronze Age could produce. Within a generation, massed infantry with new weapons — the slashing swords, the javelins, the changed tactics — would make the chariot arm obsolete. The palace economies that could produce and maintain chariot armies were, within three generations of Kadesh, going to collapse.

The peak of the system and the beginning of its end are closer together than they appear.

---

**Exit: what's wrong with the picture**

Stand back from the harbor at Ugarit, from the diplomatic correspondence, from the great powers and their gold and their daughters and their complaints. Stand back and look at the whole thing.

What you see is remarkable. What you see is a level of interconnection, sophistication, and organized complexity that the world had not achieved before and would not achieve again for two thousand years. The ships arrive. The letters cross the known world. The kings call each other brother. The trade network moves copper from Cyprus and tin from Afghanistan and grain from Egypt and wool from Assyria and wool from Mycenae. The palace administrators keep their records. The dockworkers unload. The scribes translate.

What you should also see, if you've been paying attention, is how thin the margin is.

The trade network depends on tin arriving from somewhere very far away through routes that nobody fully controls. The palace economies depend on surplus grain from agricultural regions that depend on adequate rainfall, and the climate is not reliable. The chariot armies depend on a palace economy that can sustain the stables and the craftsmen and the training programs, and palace economies are sustained by extracted surplus, and extracted surplus has limits. The diplomatic system depends on all the great powers remaining great powers, which depends on all of their internal systems functioning, which depends on all of their agricultural systems functioning, which depends on the climate.

The whole magnificent system is, at its peak, a structure built on dependencies that run all the way down to the rain.

The rain is about to change.
