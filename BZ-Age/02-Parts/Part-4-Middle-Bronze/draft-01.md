## The Draft

---

The letter is about textiles.

More specifically, it is about the quality of the textiles she has been sending him, which she considers excellent, and which he apparently keeps complaining about, which she considers insulting. He is in Anatolia — what will later be Turkey — running the trading operations that have kept the household solvent for years. She is in Assur, on the Tigris river, running the household, the children, the weaving operation, and the supply chain. She is running everything except the part he is physically present for.

And she is tired of his complaints about the textiles.

The letter was written approximately four thousand years ago. We have it. We found it in the ruins of Kanesh, a trading colony in central Anatolia, in a room that was the business archive of an Old Assyrian merchant family. Thousands of similar letters were buried in that building. When archaeologists excavated them, starting in the 1920s, they expected to find records of commerce and administration. What they found instead was: people.

Real people. Arguing about the quality of textiles, and missing each other, and worrying about their children, and complaining about the dishonesty of a business partner, and asking for specific kinds of cloth that were popular this season in Anatolia and wouldn't sell in Assur, and occasionally — in language that you recognize as love, even in translation across four thousand years — telling each other to please come home.

This is where the series changes register.

Not because the Bronze Age suddenly becomes modern, or simple, or easy to understand. The world of these merchant families is deeply strange in ways I want to make sure we don't paper over. But the people in these letters are the first people in this series whose voices are, in the most direct way, fully accessible. Not kings proclaiming their victories. Not temple accountants counting rations. Individual human beings, with private lives and private feelings, whose private correspondence was buried in a ruin and survived.

Let me introduce you to a woman whose name we don't know, who lived in Assur around 1800 BCE, who was furious about textiles and missing her husband and keeping the family business alive.

---

The world she lived in.

The Old Assyrian trade network is one of the most remarkable commercial institutions in human history, and it has received approximately one-tenth the attention it deserves in popular history, probably because it doesn't have pyramids. Let me try to fix that.

Assur — the city, not yet the empire — sits on the Tigris river in what is now northern Iraq. It was, in this period, a city-state of middling size and considerable commercial sophistication. Its primary export was not a natural resource but an institutional innovation: the capacity to organize, finance, execute, and manage long-distance trade across distances that no previous commercial system had reliably covered.

The mechanics worked like this. Merchant houses in Assur would collect silver — the medium of exchange for elite commercial transactions, weighed against standard weights — and use it to purchase goods for export: primarily textiles produced in Assur and its region, and tin, which came from somewhere further east (exactly where is still debated — possibly Afghani­stan, possibly further east still; the precise sourcing of Bronze Age tin is one of the more interesting open questions in the field). These goods would be loaded onto donkey caravans — the only reliable overland transport in a world without wheeled vehicles adequate for rough terrain, without roads in any modern sense, without bridges across most rivers.

The caravans traveled from Assur to Kanesh, a journey of roughly 700 miles across the Anatolian plateau, through passes in the mountains that were dangerous in winter and uncomfortable in summer, past bandits and local rulers who taxed the trade for the privilege of passing through, crossing rivers on foot with loaded animals, sleeping in inns that were marginally better than sleeping on the ground. The journey took about six weeks in good conditions.

At Kanesh, the Assyrian merchants had established a karum — a trading colony, a self-governing merchant quarter operating under Assyrian commercial law even though it was sitting inside an Anatolian city. The karum was not a conquest. The Anatolian rulers allowed it, regulated it, taxed it, and occasionally had disputes with it. The Assyrian merchants operating in the karum were guests, essentially, with their own internal governance and their own dispute resolution mechanisms that operated in parallel to the local legal system.

The goods were sold. The proceeds — silver, mostly, sometimes gold, sometimes copper from the Anatolian mines — were loaded onto the return caravans. The silver went back to Assur and was used to finance the next round.

The whole system ran on credit. Letters of credit. Financial instruments that allowed merchants to trade on the basis of expected future income rather than current holdings. Contracts that were legally binding and could be enforced by the Assur city authorities. Dispute resolution procedures for when partners cheated each other or goods were lost or a debtor defaulted.

Stateless capitalism, in the sense that the commercial system operated semi-independently of any political authority, under its own norms and enforced by its own institutions.

Four thousand years ago.

---

The woman's letter begins to explain why the first globalization throughline is not an abstraction.

She is in the weaving room. She is, or she is supervising, the production of fine woolen textiles of a specific weight and quality and sometimes specific pattern that the Anatolian market will pay a premium for. She knows the Anatolian market because her husband writes to her about it, and because she has been doing this for years, and because the business depends on getting the product right. The wool has to come from somewhere. The dye has to come from somewhere. The looms require maintenance. The women working the looms — employees, dependents, possibly slaves; the texts include all three — require management.

She is running a textile manufacturing operation embedded in a transnational commercial supply chain while also raising children and managing household finances and maintaining relationships with the business partners who handle the Assur end of the operation when her husband is away.

She also writes letters.

Not dictates them — there is genuine scholarly debate about how many of the women in these archives were directly literate versus how many dictated to scribes. The evidence increasingly points toward at least some of the women having direct writing competence. The letters have distinct voices, distinct rhetorical styles, distinct personalities. Whether or not all of them wrote by their own hand, they composed by their own minds.

This matters because for most of the Mesopotamian scribal tradition up to this point, the literate were a professional class of men, trained in years of grueling school, serving institutions. The Old Assyrian merchant class represents something different: commercial literacy, family literacy, women's literacy, all outside the official scribal-school system. Reading and writing as practical tools for people whose livelihood depended on communication at a distance.

The world is becoming literate in new ways. Not widely — still vanishingly rare by modern standards. But the shape of who can read and why and for what purposes is beginning to shift.

**[State of the World snapshot — Assur, approximately 1800 BCE]**

The weaving room is cold in the morning. She starts the fire before the household wakes up. The wool on the loom is from the last delivery — good quality this time, better than the batch before, which had to be beaten twice to remove the lanolin adequately. The children are somewhere behind her, still sleeping. The youngest has had a cough for a week and she is keeping a close eye on it, which means she is not sleeping well, which means she is tired.

The letter she needs to write is one she has been composing in her head for three days.

Her husband is at Kanesh. He sent a letter last month — arrived with the caravan, as letters always do, because the caravan is also the postal service — and the letter had several things in it that required careful responses. The textile quality complaint. A question about a debt owed to a partner whose family she knows better than he does. A mention, almost in passing, of an opportunity he has heard about for a new kind of trade arrangement with a local Anatolian ruler that she is not sure she trusts.

She will address all of these. She will compose the letter in her head first, then either write it herself or dictate it to a scribe, and it will be pressed into clay and sealed and given to the next caravan going to Kanesh, which is leaving in three days.

The world of this woman:
The market of Assur, where she buys wool and sells finished textiles. The household, which is an economic unit, not just a domestic one. The network of other merchant-family women who trade information about prices, about merchants, about whose credit is good and whose isn't. The letters from Kanesh, which arrive every six weeks or so if the roads are clear. The accounting tablets that track what is owed and what is owned and what has been shipped and what has been received.

What is in her world that was not in the Neolithic farmer's world: letters. The postal service that is a donkey caravan. Credit. Abstract financial instruments. The knowledge that there is a city five hundred miles away that she can reliably communicate with and trade with and that her family's livelihood depends on.

What is not in her world that will be in later worlds: coined money (still silver by weight). Iron in any useful quantity. The alphabet (she writes in cuneiform, if she writes at all herself, and cuneiform requires years to learn fully). The chariot as a military force (it is arriving, in the north, on the steppe, but it hasn't reached her world in its decisive form yet). The great empires of the Late Bronze Age (she lives between the Akkadian collapse and the rise of what will become the Hittite and Babylonian empires; the political map around her is fragmented and fluid).

What is the same as the Neolithic farmer's world: she is tired. The youngest child has a cough. She is worried about something she can't fully control.

---

The chariot arrives.

It arrives from the steppe. This is the first thing to understand about the chariot — it was invented outside the civilized centers, by the nomadic peoples of the Eurasian grasslands, and it moved south and west into Mesopotamia and Anatolia and Egypt through cultural transmission and probably through conquest. The people who invented the thing that would define Bronze Age elite warfare for three centuries were not the people who wrote the most records and built the most impressive temples. They were the people that the record-keepers regarded as barbarians.

The chariot, in its decisive form, is a light vehicle with spoked wheels — the spoke being the engineering breakthrough that makes it viable, because a solid wooden wheel is too heavy to be pulled fast enough over rough ground by horses of the size available in this period. Spoked wheels are light. Light wheels allow the vehicle to move fast. Fast movement with a trained bowman on the platform — that is the weapon system.

The horse problem is worth pausing on. In this period, the horses available are smaller than the horses of later antiquity, and considerably smaller than modern horses bred for size and strength. They are good for pulling light vehicles at speed. They are not good for carrying armored soldiers in combat, which is why riding horses in battle — as opposed to riding them for transport and communication — is not the dominant military technology of the Bronze Age. The cavalry-dominated battlefield is a later development. The Bronze Age battlefield is dominated by the chariot, which is the way you get the horse's speed into combat without requiring a horse large enough to carry a person in armor.

What the chariot does to the battlefield, when it arrives, is change the calculus of elite warfare. The chariot crew — typically a driver and an archer — can move faster than any infantry. They can strike and withdraw. They can cover distance on the battlefield that infantry can't. And they can project force over a range — the effective range of a Bronze Age composite bow from a moving platform — that changes how armies need to be organized and how battles need to be fought.

Every serious military power in the Near East, over the course of the next century or so, either acquires chariots or starts losing battles to those that have them.

The chariot does something else too. It is expensive. Enormously expensive. The horses require breeding programs and specialized training. The vehicles require sophisticated woodworking. The harnesses require specialized leatherwork. The archers require years of dedicated practice. The logistics of a chariot army — the fodder, the veterinary care, the maintenance of hundreds of intricate wooden and leather mechanisms — require an entire institutional apparatus.

The chariot is, in this sense, a technology that can only be sustained by a palace economy. A city large enough and rich enough to maintain the stables and the craftsmen and the training programs. The chariot doesn't just change warfare. It deepens the connection between military power and centralized economic extraction.

The weapon that protects the palace is the weapon the palace economy is uniquely positioned to produce and maintain.

Sit with what that means for what happens later when the palace economy breaks.

---

Hammurabi.

His law code is probably the most famous document from the ancient Near East that isn't the Bible. It is chiseled on a diorite stele — a black stone pillar about seven feet tall, currently in the Louvre — and it depicts Hammurabi receiving the law from the god Shamash and then lists about 280 legal provisions covering everything from wages and prices to medical malpractice, from inheritance disputes to what happens if a builder constructs a house that collapses and kills the owner's son.

Here is what the law code is not: the first law code. Ur-Nammu's code — from the Ur III period — is older. Lipit-Ishtar's code is older. Hammurabi was working within a tradition of legal codification that was already centuries old. What he produced was not a legal revolution. What he produced was the most monumental version of an existing tradition.

And here is what I mean by "monumental": he had it carved on stone. A single piece of stone, large enough to see from across a room, inscribed in a script legible to any competent scribe in the region, and erected in a public place — not in an archive, not in a palace record room, but where it could be seen. The code wasn't just a record. It was a declaration. *Look. This is what I have done. This is the order I have brought.*

The declaration outlasted the king. And the king outlasted his empire. And the empire outlasted most of what existed around it when it fell. And the stele was found, intact — moved in antiquity from Babylon to Susa by an Elamite king who carried it off as a trophy of conquest and never got around to adding his own inscription, so it survived in the Elamite archives — by French archaeologists in 1901 CE.

Hammurabi has been famous ever since. Most of the older law codes are not. This is partly because of the document's monumentality and partly because of the accident of the Elamite king's trophy-collecting and partly because French archaeologists knew what they had found.

History is, among other things, a game played by survivors.

Let me tell you what Hammurabi's law code actually does.

Some of it is not what you'd call progressive, by modern standards. The code makes distinctions between free persons, freedmen, and slaves that govern everything from compensation for injury to punishments for crime. The compensation for killing a free man's daughter is higher than for killing a slave. The punishments for a slave who strikes a free person are severe. The code is a document of the society that produced it, not a charter of human equality.

But some of it is — and this is what makes it interesting — genuinely innovative within its context. There are provisions for medical malpractice: a surgeon who kills a free man during surgery will have his hand cut off. This is brutal as a punishment and also remarkable as a legal concept: the idea that a professional can be held accountable for the quality of their professional work, that the practice of medicine carries legal obligations to the patient, is here in 1750 BCE. The specific penalty is not the point. The concept is the point.

There are provisions for commercial liability: if a merchant employs an agent who loses the goods, the liability is shared between them in specified ways. If a builder constructs a house that collapses, he is responsible for the consequences. If a tavern keeper extends credit to a customer and the customer can't pay, the procedures for resolution are specified.

These are not primitive intuitions about fairness. They are sophisticated legal categories. The difference between intentional and negligent harm. The doctrine of respondeat superior. Liability for professional negligence. The Bronze Age was thinking about legal problems that common law wouldn't fully work out for another three thousand years.

---

The world assembles.

This is the other thing Part 4 needs to do: set up the players for what's coming.

The Hittites are rising in Anatolia. They began as a relatively small Indo-European-speaking kingdom in the center of the Anatolian plateau, absorbed older Hattian culture, and by around 1650 BCE were capable of projecting military power as far as Mesopotamia. Around 1595 BCE, the Hittite king Mursili I led an extraordinary raid that reached all the way to Babylon, sacked the city, and departed — an audacious military achievement that was also an administrative dead end, because Mursili had no intention of administering Babylon from Anatolia. He took the goods and went home.

But the Kassite dynasty, which moved into the Babylonian power vacuum left by the raid, would rule for the next four centuries — the longest single dynasty in Mesopotamian history. The Kassites are interesting partly for what they preserved: they maintained the Babylonian scribal and administrative tradition through a long period that could have disrupted it, and they produced a dynasty stable enough to be involved in the Late Bronze Age diplomatic correspondence that is Part 5's central document.

Egypt is going through its Second Intermediate Period — a period of fragmentation and the rule of the Hyksos, a foreign dynasty of probable Levantine origin — and is about to emerge into the New Kingdom, the period of Egypt's greatest imperial reach. The expulsion of the Hyksos and the beginning of the New Kingdom under Ahmose I is the event that starts the clock on the Late Bronze Age cosmopolitan system.

Mycenaean Greece is coalescing. The shaft graves at Mycenae — the ones that Schliemann found in the 19th century and incorrectly attributed to Agamemnon — date to around 1600 BCE. Whatever the Mycenaean kingdom was in this early period, it was accumulating wealth and demonstrating connections to the wider Bronze Age world through the goods found in those graves: amber from the Baltic, ivory from Africa, bronze weapons of Aegean and Near Eastern types.

The players are assembling. The system is taking shape.

What we are watching is the world building the apparatus that, in about three hundred years, will be the most sophisticated and interconnected it has ever been, and then will fall apart in ways nobody fully sees coming.

The merchant's wife in Assur does not know this. She is writing a letter about textiles. Her husband does not know this. He is trying to sell wool cloth to Anatolians who have opinions about the weight and weave.

The system is building around them, and they are inside it, and they are human, and they are worried about their children and their bills.

That's enough. That's all it ever is.
