# Cold Opens — Menus, Verdicts, and the Taxonomy

**Written:** July 2, 2026 (Fable), per Commission G. The series's ten opens already exist and
several are locked-in strengths — so this file does three things: (1) render the honest verdict
on each current open, (2) supply divergent alternatives where a real contest exists, (3) hand back
the taxonomy so future opens can be built to spec. Alternatives are labeled by strategy;
★ = favorite. `[VERIFY]` on all new specifics.

**The headline: only one part needs a new cold open (P6), and one is worth a hybrid upgrade (P5).
Everything else: keep, and know why.**

## Verdicts and menus, part by part

### Prologue — Xenophon at the wall. LOCKED (D-008). No menu.

### P1 — "Your feet hurt." KEEP — the series' best open.
The alternatives all fire later in the part anyway (Harlan's sickle, the Martian's wager, Schmidt
walking up the hill), which is itself the evidence the open is right: it's the only beat that
*must* come first — the baseline body every later part measures against. Menu (insurance only):
object-open on the flint sickle experiment; reversal-open on the Martian's odds board;
discovery-open on Schmidt 1994. None recommended.

### P2 — the bored student. KEEP.
He anchors the scribe-spine (cast bible), and comedy is the correct door into the most
exposition-heavy part. Strongest rival, for the record: **the beveled-rim bowl** (object-mystery —
the ugliest famous object in archaeology, made by the million, identical, discarded; what kind of
world needs a disposable standardized bowl?). Verdict: the bowl slots as an internal beat
(09-why-here, Part III) and would be wasted as an open.

### P3 — Enheduanna thrown out of the temple. KEEP.
An angry named woman, the first author in history, opening the empire part by being its collateral
— that's the part's whole argument in one body. Strongest rival: the Great Death Pit attendant
raising the cup (single-body, harrowing) — correctly kept as the mid-part gut-punch instead; an
open that dark forecloses the part's range. Second rival: discovery-open on Woolley lifting the
Standard of Ur. Keep as is.

### P4 — "The letter is about textiles." KEEP.
Arguably the purest execution of the series' first-sentence rule. One augmentation worth
considering (not a replacement): a half-beat on the **clay envelope** itself — the letter sealed
inside a letter, the address written on the outside, her seal rolled across it `[VERIFY: standard
Old Assyrian practice]` — mail existing in full before paper. It deepens the object without
touching the voice.

### P5 — the Uluburun manifest. UPGRADE CANDIDATE ★ — the hybrid.
The list is hypnotic on audio (Ebony logs. Ostrich eggs. Amber beads.) but it starts at
maximum abstraction: cargo with no body attached. The upgrade: open thirty seconds earlier, with
**the sponge diver** — 1982, a Turkish sponge diver off Uluburun comes up talking about "metal
biscuits with ears" lying on the seabed `[VERIFY: the famous phrase from the discovery account —
şeytan minderi? confirm wording and the diver's name]` — *then* the list detonates as the answer
to what he saw. Strategy: someone-later-finds-it → object cascade. Why it wins: the list gets a
discoverer's eyes to land in, the "biscuits with ears" image is unforgettable, and the series'
reception spine gets a free modern chord. Cost: ~80 words. Menu also considered: the royal
courier's road-day (body); "the ancient world was isolated villages" detonation (misconception) —
both weaker: the first duplicates P4's caravan energy, the second is a lecture in a mask.

### P6 — the systems open ("disaster begins in sunshine"). REPLACE ★ — the one real gap.
The current open is the only *idea*-open in the series — correct thesis, wrong delivery (and the
fleet found its restatement mid-part; the spec already compresses that). The thesis should be
worn by a body. Menu:
1. ★ **The reversal, embodied:** second person, 1259 BCE, the best year the Bronze Age ever had —
   you stand in a harbor/granary/treaty city and *nothing you can see is wrong*. The narrator
   holds the dread; the body holds the sunshine. (Keeps the existing thesis; deletes the lecture.
   Most of the prose exists in the part already and can migrate forward.)
2. **Kadesh dawn** (single moment): the largest chariot battle in history about to begin, from a
   chariot-runner's feet `[VERIFY: scale claims; total chariots ~5,000–6,000 is the usual
   figure]` — then the pull-back: this is the world at maximum power, and it's about to sign the
   first peace treaty we have. Strong, but it burns the part's biggest set-piece in minute one.
3. **The treaty object:** two copies of the Kadesh treaty survive, one in Akkadian at Hattusa, one
   carved at Karnak — each side keeping the other's word `[VERIFY: silver original tablets,
   described but lost]`. Object-open; elegant, cooler than 1.
4. **Khaemwaset among the pyramids** (reception scene as open): the golden age curating its
   1,300-year-old past on the eve of losing everything. Beautiful, but better placed mid-part
   (spine build sheet) where its dread can echo the open rather than be it.
Verdict: 1, with 2's imagery arriving at its natural place later.

### P7 — the legend of the kiln, then the correction. KEEP — protect hard.
The legend-detonation open is the series' honesty posture at maximum stakes and the fleet
verified its execution. (The spec guards its downstream consistency — the P9 roll-call and clay
coda no longer resurrect what this open retires.)

### P8 — the grandfather. LOCKED — the series' summit. No menu. The only work here is the spec's

### P9 — "Homer did not know what he was remembering." KEEP.
Every alternative (Smith undressing, Nabonidus digging, a reception open) cannibalizes one of the
part's own peaks. The one-line diagnosis: this open is the *thesis of the whole part* stated as a
human limit — exactly what P6's open should learn from.

## The taxonomy — what makes a cold open work *for this series*

Eight rules, extracted from the ones that work (and the one that doesn't):

1. **Open on a body or a mystery — never an idea.** Nine of ten opens obey this; the tenth (P6)
   is the weak one. An idea can be the open's *engine*, but a body must be its face.
2. **First sentence under ~10 words, concrete, no throat-clearing.** "Your feet hurt." "The letter
   is about textiles." "Homer did not know what he was remembering." The first sentence is a
   promise about the next hour's register.
3. **Withhold the who/when for at least a few breaths.** Delayed identification is the hook's
   barb: the wall before Xenophon's name; the angry woman before "Enheduanna."
4. **Scale enters through one particular, never through a survey.** A hundred feet of wall; ten
   tons of copper; one letter about textiles standing for 23,000 tablets.
5. **The open is a debt the part repays on air.** P1 returns to the dying body; P7 returns to the
   letter; P8's grandfather IS the part. An open the part never returns to is a borrowed hook.
6. **The open must plant at least one thing the part (or a later part) detonates.** The student
   plants the scribe-spine; the manifest plants interconnection-in-one-hull. If it plants nothing,
   it's decoration.
7. **If the open involves a legend or misconception, the correction is the drama, not the
   footnote** (P7 is the model — tell the legend *well*, then take it apart).
8. **Rotate strategies between adjacent parts.** Current sequence: mystery → body → scene-comedy →
   body-in-crisis → object-letter → object-cascade → [body-reversal, proposed] →
   legend-correction → family scene → misconception. No two adjacent alike; keep it that way when
   any open changes.

**The generator:** to build a new open, pick the part's single most load-bearing *object, body, or
lie*; write its first sentence in under ten words; decide what it plants and where the part repays
it; then check rules 3, 4, and 8.
