architecture critique (05) says the series' need is concentrated in P4–P5 (the engine outage),

## Part 3 — First Empires

1. ★ **The band in the death pit** (specced already — spec P3.6: Lyres of Ur, Game of Ur,
   wrestlers, beer straws). Story-work: daily-life fabric inside the part's darkest set-piece; the
   same graves that hold the ranks hold the music.
2. ★ **The pyramid-builders' bakery.** The Giza workers' town (Heit el-Ghurab): bakeries,
   breweries, fish processing, dormitories; graffiti of work-gang names ("Friends of Khufu")
   `[VERIFY: gang names on quarry marks]`. Story-work: detonates the slaves-built-the-pyramids
   misconception *without lecturing* — you show the bread ovens and the beer rations and let the
   listener do the math. Also the anti-aliens beat done by evidence. Verdict: strongest single
   addition available to P3's Egypt half.
3. **Sargon's 5,400.** "5,400 men eat bread daily before him" `[VERIFY: the inscription's figure
   and phrasing]` — the first standing army rendered as a catering problem. Story-work: empire =
   logistics; feeds the man-with-the-spear cast option. One paragraph.
4. **The Curse of Akkad chord** (spine build sheet, P3) — reception-as-sermon. Already designed;
   listed for completeness.
5. **Enheduanna's authorship, honestly.** Some scholars doubt the hymns are hers as author rather
   than as attributed voice `[VERIFY: the attribution debate — copies are Old Babylonian]`.
   Story-work: a hedge that *raises* stakes — either the first author, or the first person a
   civilization *wanted* to be its first author; both are astonishing. One breath, placed late in
   her beat, not at the cold open. Verdict: include — it's the contrarian-honest posture applied
   to the series' own darling.
6. Skip: Ebla archive (rich but a proper-noun tax the part can't afford); Old Kingdom court
   autobiographies (Weni etc.) — good material, wrong part-density.

## Part 4 — Middle Bronze (engine restoration: questions + one mortgage chord)

1. ★ **"How do you mail a letter four thousand years ago?"** — asked bare, then the machinery:
   the tablet, the clay envelope sealed and addressed, six weeks by donkey, the caravan schedule,
   and the fact that we can read the *envelopes* of letters whose insides never survived (and vice
   versa) `[VERIFY envelope survival framing]`. Story-work: elementary question #1 for the part;
   makes the miracle of the archive physical. Mostly rides existing prose — the part already has
   the pieces; the question-form is what's missing.
2. ★ **The smugglers.** Old Assyrian letters discuss evading Anatolian tolls and the contraband
   trade — including smuggled meteoric iron and textiles hidden from inspectors `[VERIFY: hidden
   textiles / "let them enter by the narrow track" letters]`. Story-work: nothing says "real
   economy" like tax evasion; the first documented smuggling in history, and it's the *respectable
   merchants* doing it. Humanizes and complicates in one move.
3. ★ **The schoolhouse humor beat.** Old Babylonian tablets preserve proverbs and jokes copied by
   students — including the Sumerian bar-joke shape ("a dog walks into a tavern...") whose punchline
   no one can translate with confidence `[VERIFY: the dog/tavern proverb and its untranslatability
   — a famous crux]`. Story-work: fills the humor gap; and the untranslatable punchline is a
   *better* beat than a translatable one — four thousand years, and the setup survives while the
   laugh doesn't. Pure series-voice material.
4. ★ **A birth in Assur** (the midwife's first appearance — cast bible addition). The incantations
   for difficult labor exist in the Old Babylonian corpus ("the boat in the water..." imagery)
   `[VERIFY: OB birth incantations]`; render one birth, one midwife's hands, the odds honestly.
   Story-work: fills the childbirth gap inside the part best equipped for it (private, domestic,
   letter-scale) and plants the cast figure whose P8 return says: the one profession the collapse
   couldn't touch.
5. **Mortgage chord (D-007), P4's one:** Assur's whole world runs on treaty-protected road
   privileges through other people's kingdoms `[VERIFY: karum treaty framework]` — one clause of
   dread: the roads are a *permission*, and permissions can be withdrawn.
6. Skip: Mari's palace (already present per the spine of the part); Shamshi-Adad's empire
   (proper-noun tax); the eye-for-an-eye deep-dive (the malpractice beat already carries law).

## Part 5 — Cosmopolitan Age (engine restoration: the question density lives here)

1. ★ **"How do you marry a princess you've never seen?"** — the Amarna marriage machinery asked as
   the elementary question it is: the portrait sent ahead? the dowry inventories, line by line;
   and the Babylonian king's astonishing complaint that no one has confirmed his sister is even
   alive at the Egyptian court — "you have not seen her, whether she lives or is dead" shape
   `[VERIFY: EA 1–5 exchange, Kadashman-Enlil I / Amenhotep III]`. Story-work: the part's warmest
   existing material (Tushratta's anxious-father letters, already in the draft) gets a question-
   engine in front of it instead of arriving as topic.
2. ★ **"What language do two kings who share none write in?"** — Akkadian, a Mesopotamian tongue,
   used by Egypt to talk to everyone including its own vassals; the pharaoh's foreign ministry
   works in a foreign language `[VERIFY framing]`. Story-work: elementary question that carries
   the lingua-franca material and sets up P7's "the letters stop" and P8's literacy loss. One
   plant available: the scribes who read Akkadian aloud to kings in three accents.
3. ★ **"Gold is like dirt in your country."** Tushratta's actual line-shape to Pharaoh — "in my
   brother's land, gold is as plentiful as dirt" `[VERIFY: EA 19 wording]` — as the door into the
   gift-economy's arithmetic (and its lopsidedness). Story-work: one quote does the work of the
   whole gift-exchange section; prune exposition around it.
4. **The traveling physician.** Court experts moved between kings — physicians and exorcists sent
   on request, later documented Babylon→Hattusa `[VERIFY: 13th-c. Babylonian doctors at Hittite
   court; for Amarna-era, check before claiming]`. Story-work: medicine gap + connectivity in one
   body. Only if the dating holds for this part; otherwise it belongs in P6.
5. **Mortgage chords (D-007), P5's two:** the tin's single mountain corridor (one clause, rides
   the existing tin material); the peak-morning at Pylos already carries "a long lifetime before
   the end" (spec P5.13) — that's the second; add nothing else.
6. Skip: Nefertiti scarab elaboration (the manifest already sells it); Hatshepsut/Punt (wrong
   century for this part's frame `[VERIFY]`; Punt is a P3/P4-era Egypt story if used at all).

## Part 6 — Crisis Builds (two missing stories, both major)

1. ★★ **The Zannanza affair — the best missing story in the series.** A widowed queen of Egypt —
   almost certainly Ankhesenamun, Tutankhamun's widow — writes to Suppiluliuma I: *"My husband is
   dead. I have no son. They say you have many sons. Give me one of yours, and he will be king of
   Egypt."* The Hittite king, suspicious, sends an envoy to check; the request is repeated,
   wounded: "Why do you say 'they are deceiving me'?" He sends prince Zannanza. The prince dies —
   probably murdered — at or before the border, and Suppiluliuma's retaliation war brings Egyptian
   captives to Hatti carrying the plague that will kill him and his heir `[VERIFY: the Deeds of
   Suppiluliuma + the "dahamunzu" letter; queen's identity is majority-Ankhesenamun but debated;
   plague transmission via captives is the Hittites' own claim]`. Story-work: a queen gambling a
   throne, a murdered prince, and a pandemic in one documented episode — it connects P5's marriage
   system to P6's crisis, shows the diplomatic machinery failing at the top, and opens the plague
   thread. Placement: after the Amarna material's afterglow, before the climate signals.
2. ★★ **The plague prayers of Mursili II.** A king begging his gods for twenty years of epidemic:
   "Hatti is dying... the plague has raged for twenty years" — and, astonishingly, *confessing his
   father's sins* (treaty-breaking, the Egyptian war) as the possible cause, offering restitution
   `[VERIFY: Plague Prayers corpus; twenty-years figure]`. Story-work: disease-as-lived (the
   missing topic) at state scale; and theologically it's the series' richest what-did-they-believe
   beat — a Bronze Age king doing *theodicy in public*, reasoning with heaven like a lawyer. Pairs
   with (1) as cause-and-consequence.
3. **Khaemwaset** (spine build sheet — the P6 reception scene). Listed for completeness.
4. **Mortgage chord already present** — the grain convoys ARE P6's dread; add none.
5. Skip: Assyria's rise detail (P6 has it at the right dose); Trojan War historicity (P8's Homer
   material owns the question from the memory side; raising it here spends it early).

## From the Fall of Civilizations transcript (July 2) — gap-fillers, triaged against our drafts

1. ★★ **The Sumerian King List — the missing plant for a payoff we already have.** "When kingship
   from heaven was lowered, the kingship was in Eridu" — then Alulim reigns 28,800 years, his
   successor 36,000; then "the flood swept over"; then the reigns shrink, dynasty by dynasty,
   toward human scale `[VERIFY: quote-shape and reign figures against a scholarly translation
   (ETCSL/Jacobsen), NOT the transcript]`. Nowhere in the series — and P9's Ashurbanipal beat
   already quotes the king boasting he can read inscriptions "from before the flood," a callback
   with no plant. This beat is the plant. Bonus wiring: the kingship-lowered-at-Eridu line lands
   on the same Eridu whose temple-stack the reception spine gives P2 — the city the listener
   already knows as the oldest holy place. Story-work: the first civilization imagining its OWN
   deep past, plus kingship-as-ideology (scholars read the List as propaganda legitimizing later
   claimants — keep Cooper's honest hedge, which is also the field's). Placement: P3, with the
   kingship material; alternatively P2 beside Gilgamesh.
2. ★ **The silt mechanism — "why is the mud rich?"** The rivers grind the Taurus and Zagros into
   flour — quartz and feldspar too fine to see, carried down and dropped on the plain — so the
   poorest place in the Bronze Age world is made of the mountains' wealth, pulverized and
   delivered `[VERIFY the geology shape]`. One short paragraph in P2's why-here; it converts
   "rich from the river sediment" (currently asserted) into an answered elementary question, and
   it quietly connects the mountain world (Göbekli's foothills, P4's Anatolian roads) to the mud.
3. ★ **kur/kalam — the worldview inside two words.** Sumerian *kalam*: the Land, the civilized
   plain. *Kur*: mountain — and also foreign, wild, hostile, and (in time) the netherworld
   `[VERIFY the semantic range]`. One breath, P2, near the farmer's small-world beat: the whole
   Mesopotamian map drawn inside a single vocabulary item. Pairs with P1's "horizon as the edge
   of everything."
4. **"The black-headed people."** The Sumerians' name for themselves `[VERIFY: ùĝ saĝ gíg-ga]` —
   one clause, P2, wherever the language-isolate sentence lives. A people whose origins we can't
   trace, who called themselves something as plain as hair.
5. **The Sumerian-problem expansion (one breath, not a movement).** Our P2 states the isolate
   fact in a subordinate clause; Cooper builds a whole mystery movement from it. Middle path: two
   or three sentences — no relatives living or dead; we don't know where they came from or when
   they arrived; (hedged, optional) some scholars hear an older, nameless language underneath
   their farm-words `[VERIFY: the substrate/"Proto-Euphratean" hypothesis is genuinely disputed —
   Rubio's critique; if used, both sides in one clause]`. The honest unknown is pure series voice.
6. **The rain in their universe.** In the Mesopotamian picture of the cosmos, an ocean sits above
   the sky, held back by the solid firmament, and rain is that ocean leaking through; fresh water
   and salt water are two primordial kinds `[VERIFY: strongest in Babylonian sources — frame as
   Mesopotamian, hedge the Sumerian-period specifics]`. One or two sentences inside P2's existing
   cosmology beat — the gods-with-agricultural-consequences material gains a sky.
7. **Date-palm shade gardening** — orchards as architecture: palms overhead, fragile greens in
   their shade `[VERIFY]`. One clause in P2's food fabric.
8. **CAUTION — do not import the tuning-tablets beat into P3.** Cooper plays reconstructed
   Sumerian music mid-episode. Our contract reserves "we can hear their music" for the Hurrian
   hymn (P5 plant → P7 detonation); the P3 Lyres beat shows the *instruments* only (the spec's
   existing caution). Production may still use reconstruction recordings under P3 — the *claim*
   is what's reserved.

**Second batch (July 2 — transcript continuation through ~1:05, the Uruk/Ur material):**

0. ★★ **The long walk down the rivers — the missing connective beat between P1 and P2** (writer-
   requested, July 2: "I liked how he explained how people first came to this area and why this
   place worked"). Our P1 ends in the rain-fed hill world; our P2 begins in the rainless mud —
   and the *descent* between them is never walked. The beat, told as generations noticing things
   (nobody migrates to a desert; they follow a river one lifetime at a time): farming villages
   spreading down the two river-courses from ~6500 BCE `[VERIFY: Ubaid-period spread]`; the
   date palms discovered — calories overhead, and *shade*, so gardens can grow underneath the
   canopy in country where the sun kills seedlings `[VERIFY shade-gardening]`; and then the
   discovery that changes everything — you don't have to farm the riverbank: dig a channel and
   the river follows you inland. Each step is an answered child-question (*why would anyone live
   in a desert? — they never decided to; why do the palms matter? — they're a roof; who dug the
   first canal? — someone whose name is ten thousand years gone, doing something no one had done
   before). Story-work: repairs the P1→P2 temporal seam (the fleet's own finding), delivers the
   writer's question-engine theme at the exact place the story needs context, and sets up "the
   people of this region would become very good at digging canals" as the quiet overture to the
   temple economy. Placement: P2's opening movement, before "the place it happened first";
   ~250–350 words. (Framing inspiration: Fall of Civilizations ep. 8 — credit tier 2 applies
   only if the *sequence* is borrowed as-is; the facts are common scholarship.)

9. ★★ **The Wrangel Island mammoths — the best time-vertigo fact available to the series.** When
   the first scribes were pressing reed into clay at Uruk, the last population of woolly mammoths
   on Earth was still alive on Wrangel Island in the Siberian Arctic `[VERIFY: Wrangel population
   survived to ~2000 BCE — meaning mammoths outlived not just the invention of writing but the
   building of the Great Pyramid]`. Absent from the series (grep-verified). This is Voice Bible
   move #6 (time vertigo in one clause) at maximum voltage. Placement: P2, at the invention of
   writing — or held for the student time-anchor beat. One sentence; protect it with silence
   (no bow after).
10. ★★ **The dark gifts of Inanna.** In the myth of Inanna and Enki, the goddess carries the *me*
   — the divine inventory of civilization — down to Sumer: the crafts of the carpenter, the
   scribe, the smith... and also, in the same list, deceit, the plundering of cities, and **the
   making of lamentations** `[VERIFY the list items against ETCSL translation]`. The Sumerians'
   own packing list for civilization included sacking cities and grieving for them. Story-work:
   this is the P2 slavery-thread's mythological twin ("The magnificence and the ledger are the
   same instrument" — they said it first, in myth), and "the making of lamentations" as a divine
   gift plants P3's lament-genre beat perfectly. Placement: P2 cosmology material or P3 beside
   the Lament for Ur. (Cooper pairs this with Walter Benjamin's civilization/barbarism line —
   the myth alone carries it in our voice; skip the theory citation on air.)
11. ★ **The Girsu numbers — Ur III bureaucracy made physical.** Fifteen thousand women in one
   city's textile industry; a single mill producing eleven hundred tons of flour a year with 134
   specialists and 858 workers, mostly women; the unskilled ration: twenty liters of barley a
   month, two of oil, two kilos of wool a year `[VERIFY figures AND period — these are likely
   Ur III-era records, so they belong in P3's "hundreds of thousands of tablets" beat, not in
   P2's earlier centuries]`. Story-work: concretizes P3's claim that Ur III out-administered
   Akkad — and feeds the woman-at-the-loom cast thread at its industrial peak.
12. **The pottery got worse.** Ubaid-period pots: painted, geometric, beautiful, made on slow
