World" — digested in `04-Working-Notes/miano-notes.md`. Headlines:

  5. Jericho (~3000)** — "I think Uruk will be toppled off its perch." Two consequences for us,
  both logged as recency flags (13): the prologue's "around 3500 BCE" runs late (F-01), and P2
  earns its one northern-honesty clause (F-02).
- **His city criteria** (Childe's traits, updated — ≥20 ha, ≥10,000 people, division of labor,
  surplus-funded government, classes, centralized power, monuments, record-keeping, trade) are a
  ready-made checklist the series can *dramatize* rather than recite — P2's why-here material
  already hits every one of them in narrative form.
- **His Trypillia verdict** ("would have rivaled Uruk... but they lack monumental architecture
  and record-keeping") is exactly dossier 09's argument, with his population figures now cited
  there. Independent convergence.
- **His Uruk population (20,000–40,000)** matches ours — reinforcing the do-not-adopt on
  Cooper's ~90k.
- **One new hedge he surfaces:** Uruk's monumental buildings are "more recently understood as
  public assembly halls with associated cultic functions" (F-03).

**His method, and why it matters to us:** Miano's stated test for pseudo-history is *method, not
conclusion* — "it's not the what that matters, it's the how" [moderate confidence, search-snippet
quote]. That's a sharper formulation of our own contrarian-honest posture than we had: a claim
that agrees with the consensus but was reached lazily is still pseudo-history. Two applications:
1. **Our anti-aliens beats should attack the *how*, never the *who*.** The pyramid-bakery beat
   (12, P3) and the Cyclopes-become-aliens mirror (08, P9) both already do this — show the bread
   ovens, show the method, never sneer at the believer. Miano's framing confirms the register.
2. **Add to the Operating Manual:** when the series states a consensus view, it should be able to
   gesture at *how it's known* (one clause: the isotopes, the tablet, the stratigraphy). We
   mostly do this; make it a stated rule.

**Northern Mesopotamia:** the agent couldn't source Miano's position, but flagged independently
that **Tell Brak and Hamoukar** genuinely complicate "cities began in the south" in current
scholarship. Logged in the glimpse bank (P2 honesty clause) and for the recency file (13). Our
P2 claim should survive intact if scoped as *the south is where the full package assembles* —
worth one hedging clause.

### Citation and credit policy for Fall of Civilizations material (the writer asked)

Three tiers, consistent with the Carlin homage decision (D-008):
1. **Primary-source facts** (the King List, the proverbs, the epic quotes, the recipes, the
   Girsu records) are nobody's property — but they must be **verified against scholarly
   translations** (ETCSL, Andrew George's Gilgamesh, the Yale culinary tablets literature) and
   the Source Map cites *those*, not the podcast. Never quote the supplied transcript verbatim:
   it is auto-captioned and demonstrably garbles ("ward" for Ur, "oak saga Giga" for ùĝ saĝ
   gíg-ga, likely "soul" for "salt" in the bread proverb).
2. **Cooper's editorial framings** — the star-birth metaphor, the Benjamin pairing, the Gulf
   Oasis narrative arc, della-Valle-as-cold-open — are his creative work. Rule: don't use them,
   or credit them. The only one we're adopting is the della Valle *idea* (P9 shovels-chain
   chord), and the launch-copy/show-notes credit line should name it: e.g., "the della Valle
   thread owes its inspiration to Paul Cooper's *Fall of Civilizations*." Same register as the
   Carlin acknowledgment; never in-script.
3. **Where we independently converged** (witness-first opens, marsh continuity, Ur III arc,
   honest-hedge register) — no credit owed; convergence is not borrowing. The influences doc
   itself is the provenance record if anyone ever asks.

### Where we've converged (validation, not theft)
- **Witness-first cold opens.** His Sumerians episode opens with Pietro della Valle stumbling on
  the ruins of Ur in 1625 [high confidence]. That is our prologue's exact posture (Xenophon), and
  his stated rationale — examine the stage before the drama — matches our State-of-the-World
  discipline. The reception spine (08) is this instinct made systematic.
- **The fall in the back third.** His episodes spend most of their runtime building the
  civilization before it burns [medium confidence, structural]. Our whole-series shape does the
  same at 9-hour scale (the collapse doesn't begin until P6). Within-part, P7 already obeys it.
- **The ending on an in-world voice.** His Sumerians episode reportedly closes on Enkidu's dying
  vision from Gilgamesh [medium-high confidence] — melancholy delivered through the
  civilization's own literature, not the narrator's summary. Our roll-call + Indus ending is the
  same family. Validation for bookends Option A staying in-world.
- **The single-object-as-system emblem.** He uses Uluburun exactly as our P5 does [medium-high
  confidence]. Our sponge-diver hybrid open (11, P5) would differentiate ours from his telling.
- **The Ur III material.** His Sumerians episode climaxes on Ibbi-Sin, the grain failure, and the
  Lament for Ur — checked against our P3 (July 2): **we already have all of it**, including the
  grain-stopped-moving framing and the lament-as-genre beat. No gap; if anything ours goes
  further (Sumerian-as-Latin, which his summaries don't mention).

### What he does that we don't — decisions for Jake
1. **Voice-acted primary sources.** Cooper uses a small repertory of actors to read the laments,
   letters, and inscriptions [high confidence via credits]. Listeners single this out as the
   emotional payload. Our one-voice rule (D-002) says the narrator holds the pen — but D-002
   governs *authorship*, not *casting*. Decision to log when Jake weighs in: quotes stay
   narrator-read with a register shift (Carlin's way), or brief voice-acted primary texts at the
   3–4 peak moments (Cooper's way). My recommendation: **narrator-read** — the series' intimacy
   is single-voice, and the roll-call would be diluted by a cast — but flag the alternative
   honestly because reviewers consistently name it Cooper's best weapon.
2. **Commissioned, civilization-specific music** — reconstructed Sumerian music for the Sumerians
   episode, a Byzantine choir for Byzantium [high confidence]. Production note, not script note:
   the P3 Lyres of Ur beat could *play a reconstruction of the actual instrument in the story*
   `[VERIFY: modern Lyre of Ur reconstruction recordings exist — e.g., the Lyre Ensemble]`. That
   is a coup Cooper's Sumerians episode reportedly reached for generically; we can do it with
   the specific excavated object the narration is describing.
3. **Episode-length elasticity** (1–3+ hours). No action — our part lengths are set by D-005/006.

### What we do that he doesn't (our edge — for positioning, launch copy, and morale)
Per the research, his Bronze Age Collapse episode runs a greatest-hits evidence set (Sea Peoples

### The warning shot worth internalizing
His engaged listeners caught a Medinet Habu misidentification and a bronze-ratio arithmetic error
at a specific timestamp [medium confidence]. **This is our audience.** They fact-check named
numbers hardest. Our defenses are already built — the fleet, the honest-header arithmetic, the
defensible-shape rule for figures (the coast-watcher "569" fix is exactly this class) — but the
lesson goes in the Operating Manual: *every specific number spoken aloud is a promise; if the
source can't cash it, say "hundreds," not "569."*
