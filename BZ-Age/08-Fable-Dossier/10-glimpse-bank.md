# The Glimpse Bank — "Meanwhile, Elsewhere"

**Written:** July 2, 2026 (Fable), per Commission C. Facts below were gathered and
confidence-rated by a web-research pass (July 2, sonnet agents; full report preserved in the
workflow transcript) — status flags are the researcher's, kept honest. The Greece thread is
separate and already built (P1 → P2 → P5 → P8); this bank covers the rest of the planet.

## Design rules (the dosage contract)

1. **At most one glimpse per part.** The series is Near-East-centric *on purpose* — the glimpses
   exist to make the main stage land harder, not to build a world tour.
2. **Every glimpse does named story-work** — contrast, foreshadow, puncture, or plant. A glimpse
   that's only "interesting" gets cut.
3. **60–120 seconds each**, entered and exited cleanly ("On the other side of the planet..." is
   the established door — vary it).
4. **P7 gets zero, deliberately.** The collapse part stays claustrophobic; the world narrows to
   the burning shoreline. The silence of the wider world is itself the effect — and P6's Fu Hao
   glimpse (below) has already told the listener the wheel turns elsewhere.

## The bank, mapped to parts

### P1 — the Green Sahara exodus ★ (new; joins the existing Great Lakes beat)
~5200–3900 BCE: the African Humid Period ends; the Sahara — grasslands, lakes, cattle herders,
rock art of swimmers — dries out over centuries, and its people move, many toward one permanent
river: the Nile [status: climate trend well-attested; "exodus toward Egypt" is one driver among
several — hedge as the researchers do]. **Story-work: triple.** (1) Climate as protagonist from
hour one — the plant for the P6–P7 drought material, at series scale; (2) Egypt's rise gets a
prehistory (the Nile Valley fills up partly because the alternative is dying); (3) the series'
first lesson that geography is a *timeline*, not a backdrop. Placement: beside the P1
domestication-spread material. Note: P1 already carries Great Lakes + Greece; if three glimpses
crowd the part, this one can open as a single clause and pay off in P2's why-here material.

### P2 — Caral, and the northern complication (both mostly built; deepeners only)
Caral/Norte Chico is already compressed into P2. Two deepeners available if wanted: the total
absence of ceramics (a monumental civilization without pots — strangeness that resists the
grain-narrative being universal), and the knotted-cord fragments [status: quipu-precursor
framing is speculative — flag hard or skip]. Also file here: **Tell Brak and Hamoukar** — the
northern-Mesopotamian sites complicating "cities began in the south" [status: genuinely live in
scholarship — Ur, McMahon; corroborated July 2 by the Miano transcript, which ranks Brak the
earliest known city — urban ~3800 BCE, 130 ha; see `04-Working-Notes/miano-notes.md`].
Recommendation upgraded to should-do: one honesty clause in P2's why-here material ("the
south is where the full package assembles; the north may have started the race") + recency
flags F-01/F-02 (13).
Trypillia is assigned to P2's why-here argument (09, Part I) — cross-reference, don't duplicate.

### P3 — Shu-ilishu, interpreter of Meluhha ★★ (the best glimpse in the bank)
Sargon's records boast of ships from Meluhha — the Indus — docking at Akkad's quays; ~20 Indus
seals have been found in Mesopotamian cities; and one Akkadian-period cylinder seal belongs to
**"Shu-ilishu, interpreter of the Meluhhan language"** [status: well-attested; the seal is
published and widely cited]. **Story-work: a body, not a fact.** A man in Akkad whose ordinary
job — standing on a dock turning one language into another — depended on a language that no
living human being can now read. His clients' script is the one the series *ends* on: the Indus
signs no Champollion has cracked. Plant it here with no fanfare; when P9's final beat arrives
("The Indus script remains undeciphered"), the listener has already met the last kind of person
who understood it. This is the glimpse bank's contribution to the series' final note, and it
costs ninety seconds.

### P4 — Sintashta: the chariot is being invented by people with no cities ★
~2000 BCE, in the grasslands east of the Urals: burials with two-wheeled vehicles, spoked wheels,
wheel-imprints pressed into the grave floor — the earliest true chariots [status: majority view;
a minority argues "carts" — hedge with one clause]. Horses of the modern domestic line had begun
spreading explosively from the steppe only a couple of centuries earlier [status: well-attested —
Librado 2021]. **Story-work:** the weapon that will define the Late Bronze battlefield — Kadesh's
five-to-six thousand chariots, the arm the great kings beggar themselves for — is being invented
*offstage*, by people with no cities, no writing, no kings the record can name. The series
currently has chariots arrive from nowhere; this is where they come from. Plants: Kadesh (P5/P6),
the Hittite horse-training manual if used (Kikkuli [VERIFY]), and the steppe as the standing
answer to "where does the new thing come from?" Placement: beside P4's caravan material — two
kinds of animal-powered network, one commercial, one military.

### P5 — Kerma: the empire Egypt feared (placement P4-or-P5; recommend P5's Egypt material)
South of Egypt, ~2500–1500 BCE: a real rival state — the great mudbrick Deffufa temple, royal
tumuli up to ninety meters across with hundreds of sacrificed retainers [status: well-attested —
Judd & Irish], an invasion that struck deep into Egypt in alliance with the Hyksos [status:
well-attested — Sobeknakht inscription], archers so defining that Egypt's name for Nubia was
Ta-Seti, "Land of the Bow." Conquered by Thutmose I ~1500 [well-attested]. **Story-work:**
punctures the Egypt-had-no-peers assumption the listener carries; gives Egypt's New Kingdom
militarism a cause (trauma has a geography); and the retainer burials rhyme darkly with Ur's
death pits (P3) without a word of commentary. Alternative in the same slot: the Nebra Sky Disc
(~1600 BCE, gold and tin from *Cornwall* — the tin thread touching the sky [status: majority
dating; a minority redating exists — hedge]) if the part wants wonder instead of geopolitics.

### P6 — Fu Hao: the world is not ending ★
~1200 BCE, Anyang, China: a queen — and a general; the oracle bones record her commanding
campaigns — is buried with 1,500 objects, bronzes cast in piece-molds by an industry the
Mediterranean has never heard of; chariots, arrived from the steppe within living memory, go
into pits nearby [status: all well-attested — the one intact Shang royal tomb, 1976]. Placement:
near the P6 close, after the dread has been armed. **Story-work: the collapse is regional, not
planetary.** While the eastern Mediterranean's interlocked system tips toward the cliff, on the
other side of the landmass the Bronze Age is at full noon — and the steppe that invented the
chariot has just delivered it to China, proof the world's connective tissue works even while one
region's version of it fails. This is the honesty beat that keeps P7's apocalypse scoped — and
the reason P7 can then go glimpse-silent without the listener mistaking the Mediterranean for
the world.

### P7 — none. (Rule 4. The design is the absence.)

### P8 — the Olmec begin ★
~1150 BCE, the Gulf Coast of Mexico: at San Lorenzo, colossal stone heads — ten tons and more,
hauled without wheels or draft animals — are being carved *right now*, as Greece forgets how to
write [status: well-attested; per-head dating varies]. **Story-work: the wheel turns.** In the
part about aftermath and silence, one glimpse says: somewhere else, a first civilization is just
beginning its own version of this whole story — cities it will build, a collapse it will
eventually have, ruins someone will someday stand in front of and misread. The reception spine
and the glimpse bank shake hands here. (Poverty Point is the alternate — but P1's hedge already
cites it; don't spend it twice.)

### P9 — none needed. The Indus ending is the series' final glimpse, and Shu-ilishu (P3) has
already made it personal.

## The generator (handed back)

To mint a new glimpse: (1) fix the part's date; (2) ask what the most evidentially solid,
sensorially strange thing on Earth is at that date *outside the Near East*; (3) name the
story-work in one word — contrast, foreshadow, puncture, plant — and if you can't, drop it;
(4) find the body (Shu-ilishu, Fu Hao, the Amesbury Archer — a glimpse with no body is a
factoid); (5) one entry door, one exit door, under 120 seconds; (6) `[VERIFY]` every specific
and check the contested-flags above before air.
