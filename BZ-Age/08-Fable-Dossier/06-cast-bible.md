# The Cast Bible — Recurring Human Anchors & Peak Emotional Beats

**Written:** July 2, 2026 (Fable), per Commission K. Builds on `04-Working-Notes/archetypes.md`
(which remains the philosophy document — not-literal-genealogy, vantage-figures-not-protagonists,
cull-the-weak). This file is the *engineering* document that was missing: the registry that stops
chords misfiring, the analysis of who's overworked and underworked, and the design of the peak
beats. The critique (05) showed why this matters: nearly every continuity error the fleet caught
was a cast chord struck from memory. Memory is what nine hours exceeds. This is the memory.

**The rule this file exists to enforce:** no chord is struck without checking the registry; every
new plant or chord is added to the registry the day it's written.

## Part I — The registry

Format per figure: canonical anchor (part · date · the TWO canonical callback cues — Voice Bible
rule 10 caps callbacks at two cues) · struck chords (location → what the return revealed) · open
chords (designed, not yet written) · hazards (known error modes, mostly fleet-caught).

### 1. The Neolithic farmer
- **Anchor:** P1 open · ~5000 BCE · cues: *the feet that hurt* · *the smoke-and-sheep dark*.
- **Struck:** P4 (the lineage chord — being re-formed by the spec to "a hundred grandmothers
  back"); P8 highlands snapshot implicitly rhymes with her world (stone house, goats, no tax
  collector).
- **Open:** P7 — a refugee arriving at a village that still lives her life (the collapse from the
  view of people it barely reaches); the finale roll-call (struck, keeps).
- **Hazards:** kin-labels across millennial gaps (fleet: "great-great-great-grandmother" spanning
  3,200 years). Formula: *"a hundred grandmothers back"* / *"three thousand years before this
  letter."*

### 2. The barge-puller (Uruk)
- **Anchor:** P2 snapshot · ~3000 BCE · cues: *the rope across the shoulder* · *the temple being
  wrongly large*.
- **Struck:** P5 (spec item 11 adds the designed shoulder-echo at the Ugarit harbor — the rope's
  ache across two thousand years).
- **Open:** P3 — one clause under Akkad (the same labor, now taxed by an empire); P7 — the worker
  in a city about to burn.
- **Hazards:** he is *newly arrived* the morning we meet him — he has no established tenement
  (fleet caught P8 calling back a "barge-puller's tenement" never rendered; spec generalizes it).

### 3. The scribe — the series' spine (see Part II)
- **Anchor:** P2 · the bored student, ~2000 BCE (honestly timestamped in draft-04) · cues: *the
  doodles on the homework* · *the beatings*.
- **Struck chain:** P2 student → P9 Ashurbanipal beat ("the scribal chain that began with a bored
  student's doodles runs, unbroken, through this room") → P9 Iron Age scribe closing snapshot
  (twenty-two signs, learned in weeks — the caste dissolved).
- **Open (the biggest unexploited connection in the series — see Part II):** the decipherers.
- **Hazards:** the *Mesopotamian* scribe (edubba, beatings, hundreds of signs) must never be
  conflated with the *Aegean* administrator (Linear B, ~87 signs, no literature) — the P8 cold
  open made exactly this error; the spec's biggest fix.

### 4. The conquered villager
- **Anchor:** P3 snapshot · ~2270 BCE · cues: *the village unchanged* · *the official passing
  through in a language that isn't yours*.
- **Struck:** P8 highlands snapshot is his designed contrarian payoff (no palace, no tax collector
  — "by some measures reduced, by measures that matter, freer").
- **Open:** P6 — one clause: the tax demands rising *because* the empire is failing (the extraction
  system at its most extractive exactly when it has least to give back).
- **Hazards:** none caught; the least error-prone figure because his returns are structural, not
  biographical.

### 5. The merchant's wife (Lamassi, Assur)
- **Anchor:** P4 open · ~1800 BCE · cues: *the angry letter about textiles* · *the husband absent
  in Anatolia*.
- **Struck:** P6 warehouse chord (cuneiform unchanged — being corrected to *six* centuries); P7:136
  "the descendant of the Old Assyrian woman we met in Assur" (correct descendant-formula — the
  model other callbacks should follow).
- **Open:** P5 — currently skipped, and the archetypes file's suggestion (link her to Mycenaean
  textile inventories) is a **class error**: Lamassi is a proprietor; the Pylos weaving women are
  rationed dependents. Do not merge them. The right P5 chord, if any: a trading woman in the Ugarit
  or Cypriot network — commerce, not the loom.
- **Hazards:** century arithmetic (caught twice); the class conflation above.

### 6. The woman at the loom — **NEW: formalize her**
She already exists — the series just hasn't admitted it. Uruk's weaving halls (P2), the Pylos
weaving women with their children on the ration tablets (P5), the fleet's own finding that the
"weaving halls of Uruk" formula recurs past its cap — all one figure: **the series' longest-serving
worker.** The bottom of every ledger from the first city to the last palace is disproportionately
a woman at a loom.
- **Anchor:** P2 · the weaving halls · cues: *the loom* · *the rations counted out*.
- **Design:** one chord per era, *varied instantiation* (formalizing her is also the fix for the
  formula-repetition problem — the callback names her situation, not the same phrase): P4 — the
  paradox chord (Lamassi's textiles are made by women like her: one woman's commerce is another's
  loom); P5 — Pylos (struck, keeps); P8 — the loom in the highlands village, weaving for a
  household, no ledger anywhere (freedom chord, rhymes with the conquered villager's).
- **Why she matters:** the critique's "ledger saturation" problem — she lets the below-thread land
  in a body instead of a document.

### 7. The smith — **NEW: formalize him**
Also already latent: P1's arsenic-poisoned metalworker is one of the series' best bodies ("You
don't know you're dying"), and P7 has the scrap-crucible smith. Connect them.
- **Anchor:** P1 · cues: *the hands that are failing* · *the better diet and the respect*.
- **Chain:** P1 arsenical bronze (struck) → P3/P4 open chord: tin-bronze at scale — his successor
  works a safer material that must cross the known world to reach him (the mortgage-audit chord for
  the middle parts: his craft now depends on a supply line he's never seen) → P7 the crucible of
  scrap (struck; forward lean to iron) → P8 iron (struck: the hillside).
- **Why he matters:** he carries the series' *material* throughline in a body, the way the scribe
  carries the informational one.

### 8. The dockworker (Ugarit)
- **Anchor:** P5 snapshot · **~1230 BCE after the spec's re-date** · cues: *the harbor at dusk* ·

### 9. The palace administrator (Pylos)

## Part II — The one big move this file proposes: the scribe-spine runs through the decipherers

The series already knows its strongest cast thread is the scribe. What it hasn't noticed is that
**the thread doesn't end at the Iron Age scribe — it ends in Part 9's decipherment run.**
Champollion teaching himself Coptic as a teenager; Smith at the museum table; Hrozný in the hotel
room; Ventris with his crossword grid — they are the scribe archetype's final descendants,
re-learning, letter by letter, what the chain forgot. The prologue's new arc paragraph already
promises exactly this ("...where it waited three thousand years for somebody to dig it back up and
learn, letter by letter, to read it again"). The cast delivers it.

**The execution is almost free:** one clause per decipherer tying him into the chain the listener
has followed for eight hours. Smith is the easiest and most devastating — he is *literally* an
edubba student's successor, an apprentice who taught himself cuneiform from the tablets: the bored
student's doodles and George Smith's undressing are the same figure, at the two ends of the chain.
This is the kind of connection the master prompt hired me for, and I'd stake the most on it of
anything in this file: it converts P9's decipherment run from "history of scholarship" into the
cast's homecoming — and it's the missing warrant for growing P9 (D-005).

## Part III — The peak emotional beats (the parking-lot moments)

Five, ranked. For each: where it lands, what it does to the listener, what must be planted.

### Peak 1 — The grandfather who could read (P8 cold open) — *the summit*
A man who spent his life as the counting of a kingdom; a grandson who will never learn; literacy
dying in one lifetime, rendered as a family scene. **Plants (all live):** P5 morning-at-the-peak +
"Remember the shelf"; P6 requisition chord; P7's withholding ("The next part opens at their
table"). **Guard:** the spec's Linear B rewrite is what stands between this beat and a knowledgeable
listener's disbelief; it is the single most protected site in the series. Nothing else in P8's
first ten minutes should carry ornament — the beat wants bare prose and room.

### Peak 2 — George Smith undresses (P9)
The first human in twenty-five centuries to read the flood story — and it's a story he already
knows. **What it does:** collapses the distance the whole series has been building; the listener
feels the chain reconnect. **Plants needed:** (a) the scribe-spine clause (Part II) so Smith is
*ours*; (b) verify the flood-tablet story itself is rendered earlier (Gilgamesh in P2/P3) so "a
story he already knew" is also a story *the listener* already knows from inside the series —
`[CHECK: does P2's Gilgamesh section reach Utnapishtim/the flood? If not, one sentence there
buys this payoff]`. **Current cost:** it's taken at a jog (critique Problem 2); give it scene-speed.

### Peak 3 — "They have forty or fifty more years." (P5, now exact at ~1230 BCE)
The dread engine's purest note. Already built; the re-date perfects it. **Design note:** this is
the template for the D-007 mortgage chords — one per hour in P4–P5, never two, never explained.

### Peak 4 — Ventris on the BBC (P9)
"Five hundred years older than Homer, but Greek nevertheless" — the moment the Mycenaeans and the
Greeks become the same people, on air, in an architect's voice. **Plants (live):** P8's Homer
distortion material; the Linear B thread. **Fix first:** the Not-archaic-Not-proto contradiction
(spec P9.1). **Growth:** thirty more seconds of the study, the graph paper, the crossword line —
the amateurism is the emotion.

### Peak 5 — The roll-call as curtain call (P9 close)
"And tired in the morning. Worried about their children." Already the right ending move. **The
cast bible upgrade:** the roll-call is the cast's curtain call — each figure named by their
canonical cues from the registry (the feet that hurt; the doodles; the rope; the letter about
textiles; the harbor at dusk; the day-tablets; the storage pit), in first-appearance order, which
is also chronological order — the whole series walked back through bodies in forty seconds.
Check each entry against the registry (the spec already fixes the one corrupted entry — the kiln
scribe).

## Part IV — Callback grammar (the rules, consolidated)

1. **Registry first.** No chord without checking Part I. New plants get logged here same-day.
2. **Two cues maximum**; second callback, one cue. If two cues can't trigger recognition, fix the
   plant, not the callback.
3. **Never call back the unrendered.** An unplanted callback spends listener trust at compound
   interest.
4. **Time-honest kinship.** Within a lifetime: same person (say so). One-to-three generations:
   descendant-formula (P7:136 is the model). Beyond that: the hundred-grandmothers formula or a
   bare span of years. Never five "greats" across three millennia.
5. **One chord per figure per part**, at most. A cast member who appears in every part is
   furniture.
6. **Chords reveal change.** Every return must show the *world* changed around the constant human
   — that's Hard Rule 11's whole point. A callback that shows nothing changed is a wasted note
   unless the unchangedness IS the point (the midwife; "tired in the morning").
