# Script Draft

## 1. Cold Open - The King's Daughter In The Enemy City

[OPENING. Begin intimate and political at the same time.]

The first empire in history does not begin, for our purposes, with a battle.

Battles are too easy.

You can always open an empire story with dust and shouting and bronze weapons and the terrible intimacy of men trying to kill one another at arm's length. That material is there. We will get to it.

But the more interesting opening is quieter.

A woman is installed in a city that her father needs to control.

Her name is Enheduanna.

And if the standard framing holds, she is the first author in world history whose name we know. Not the first person to compose. Not the first person to sing. Not the first person to pray. Human beings had been doing those things for longer than we can imagine. But the first named author whose work comes down to us in the written tradition. A priestess. A poet. A political instrument. The daughter of Sargon of Akkad. [SOURCE CHECK: enheduanna]

That combination is almost too good.

Because it lets you feel the entire problem of empire in one body.

Her father has conquered Sumerian cities from an Akkadian base to the north. He is trying to make multiple cities, languages, gods, and local loyalties belong to something larger than themselves. And one of the ways he does that is by placing his daughter in Ur as high priestess of the moon god.

Think about what that means.

This is not just religion.

This is not just family.

This is not just propaganda.

Those categories are modern filing cabinets. In this world, they are all tangled together.

Her body is dynastic policy.

Her ritual role is imperial administration.

Her poetry is theology and politics at once.

She stands between her father's new power and the older sacred order of the conquered south. She is there to bind worlds that do not naturally want to be bound.

And maybe that is the cleanest way to define the invention of empire:

Empire begins when power has to rule people who do not already belong to it.

The city-state had its own problem: how do you organize strangers at urban scale?

Empire adds a harder problem.

How do you organize strangers who have their own cities, their own gods, their own languages, their own memories, and their own reasons to hate you?

That is Sargon's problem.

And the fact that one answer is "send my daughter to become priestess in one of their sacred cities" tells you immediately that this is not a simple conquest story.

This is the birth of a new political imagination.

## 2. The Legend Eats The Man

[SHIFT. Introduce Sargon, but keep the source caution visible.]

Sargon is one of those historical figures whose legend is louder than the man.

That is dangerous for a storyteller, because legends are built to be told. They are clean. They are dramatic. They know where the camera should go.

The baby in the basket. The rise from obscurity. The cupbearer who becomes king. The outsider who overturns the old order. The conqueror whose name echoes for two thousand years after his bones are dust. [SOURCE CHECK: sargon-legend]

It is all almost suspiciously usable.

And that is the problem.

The real Sargon is partly hidden behind what later people needed Sargon to be.

Every empire wants an origin story. Every dynasty wants its founder to look inevitable in retrospect. And every later king who invokes Sargon is doing something with his memory. They are not preserving him in amber for our convenience. They are using him.

So we have to read him with two minds at once.

One mind says: this man matters enormously. He seems to have created something genuinely new, or at least new at a scale and durability we can see in the record. A political unit that bound together multiple cities and peoples under one administration. If the word "empire" means anything in this early context, Akkad has a serious claim to being the first. [SOURCE CHECK: empire-definition]

The other mind says: be careful. The legend is not the man. The propaganda is evidence, but not evidence in the way it wants to be. It tells us what later rulers admired, feared, copied, or needed to believe.

That tension is not a problem to solve.

It is the texture of the part.

Part 3 is about the moment when the city is no longer the biggest thing human politics can imagine.

And once that happens, memory itself becomes a battlefield.

## 3. What Empire Feels Like From Below

[STATE OF THE WORLD SNAPSHOT. Conquered villager, around 2270 BCE.]

So leave Sargon for a moment.

Leave the inscriptions. Leave the founder legend. Leave the royal family.

Go down to a village in the conquered south.

The room you wake in may not look dramatically different from the room your grandparents knew. That is one of the things that makes empire strange from below. The roof is still the roof. The field is still the field. The canal still needs maintenance. The animals still need tending. The grain still has to be cut, stored, moved, protected.

Your local gods have not vanished.

Your language has not vanished.

The dead of your family have not packed up and moved north because a king in Akkad has announced a new political reality.

But something has changed.

An official passes through the market or the storage area. Maybe he speaks with an accent. Maybe he is not from here at all. Maybe the document he carries uses a language or a style that marks him as belonging to the conqueror's world. [SOURCE CHECK: akkadian-administration]

The tax, tribute, or labor claim that used to be local now points upward to someone you will never meet.

That may be the most important emotional fact of early empire:

Power becomes distant.

In the city-state world, power can still be enormous, sacred, extractive, and terrifying. But it is nearby. The temple is there. The ruler is there. The rituals happen in places you can walk to.

In the imperial world, decisions can arrive from beyond the horizon.

You now belong to an abstraction before you have the mental furniture to make abstraction comfortable.

Akkad.

What is Akkad to you, if you are a Sumerian farmer or potter or laborer? It is not a nation. It is not citizenship. It is not identity in the modern sense. It is a claim. It is a demand. It is the name attached to officials, soldiers, tribute, language, and fear.

And if you are a scribe, it may be an opportunity.

That is one of the quiet ways empire works. It punishes some local elites, absorbs others, and creates new routes upward for anyone who can serve the new machine.

Learn the conqueror's language.

Learn the formats.

Learn who signs, who seals, who reports to whom.

The old city world has not disappeared. It has been overlaid.

That is the State of the World in Part 3.

Still no horses ridden in combat at scale.

Still no chariot warfare in the later glamorous sense.

Still no iron.

Still no alphabet.

Still no coins.

But one thing is new, and it is enormous:

There is now a king whose name matters in cities he did not grow up in, among people whose gods were not originally his gods, in languages that were not originally his household's speech.

The political horizon has expanded.

And for many people under it, it probably felt less like grandeur than like someone far away had learned how to reach into your granary.

## 4. The First Imperial Machine

[TURNING POINT. Person, community, civilization.]

The invention of the multi-ethnic empire is not just "Sargon conquered many cities."

Conquest is old.

Raiding is old.

Dominance is old.

What is new, or newly visible at this scale, is the attempt to make conquest administratively durable.

One city beating another city is a military event.

One city continuing to rule another city, extracting from it, appointing officials over it, integrating it into a wider system, using its labor and resources for projects beyond its local world, and making that arrangement survive the season after the army leaves - that is something else.

That is empire.

Imagine the first Akkadian governor arriving in a Sumerian city. Again, composite, source-dependent, but structurally right. He is the king's man. He is not simply visiting. He has to make this place function for a ruler who is not here.

He needs local knowledge.

He needs scribes.

He needs cooperation from some existing elites and obedience from others.

He needs force available in the background, because everyone understands that the new arrangement began in violence and can return to violence.

He needs the local gods handled carefully, because the city does not stop being sacred to itself merely because it has been conquered.

That is the personal moment: not a crown, not a battlefield, but an administrator standing in a city that does not belong to him and trying to make rule repeatable.

The community changes around that.

Bilingualism becomes useful. Some families attach themselves to the new order. Others lose power. Hostages, marriage alliances, military postings, garrisons, tribute flows, official correspondence - the details need sourcing, but the pattern is clear enough to frame cautiously. [SOURCE CHECK: akkadian-administration]

And then the civilizational consequence ripples outward.

Once empire exists, it becomes imaginable.

That is the real turning point.

The first version does not have to last forever to matter. The Akkadian Empire can fail, and it will. It can be unstable, improvised, brutal, overextended, dependent on a founder's charisma or a dynasty's military momentum. It can collapse under pressure from revolts, succession strain, climate stress, outside groups, or all of those at once. [SOURCE CHECK: 4.2k-event]

But the idea has entered the human toolkit.

A ruler can claim more than one city.

A language can become an administrative bridge between peoples.

A conquered elite can be absorbed.

A god from one city can be forced into conversation with the gods of another.

A king can try to stand above the whole structure and say, not just "I rule here," but "I rule the world that matters."

Every later empire is a variation on that sentence.

Persian.

Macedonian.

Roman.

Mongol.

Ottoman.

British.

Not the same. Never the same. But all of them inherit the problem Akkad first makes visible:

How do you make people obey a center they cannot see?

## 5. Enheduanna As Imperial Technology

[RETURN TO OPENING. Tie voice, gender, power, and memory together.]

Now return to Enheduanna.

Because after the machinery of empire, she looks even more interesting.

She is not a footnote. She is not "and also, there was a woman poet," which is the small way to use her.

She is one of the ways empire thinks.

Her appointment says that Sargon understood, or at least his regime understood, that conquest by itself was not enough. You had to enter the sacred architecture of the conquered world. You had to bind the old order to the new one. You had to make your dynasty appear not merely victorious, but cosmically placed.

And the surviving literary tradition attached to her gives us something even stranger: an imperial voice that is personal enough to feel like a person and political enough to feel like a state speaking through a body. [SOURCE CHECK: enheduanna]

This is exactly the sort of evidence the series has to handle carefully.

Do not flatten her into a modern heroine.

Do not turn her into a symbol of empowerment stripped of the violent imperial project that made her position possible.

Do not turn her into merely her father's daughter either.

Render the world.

She is powerful and constrained.

She is a woman whose name survives in a world where almost no names survive, and especially almost no women's names survive.

She is a religious figure and a political figure.

She is a poet whose work outlived the empire that used her.

That last point matters.

Sargon's empire will fall.

His administrative experiment will crack.

The cities he conquered will not remain frozen inside his victory.

But the memory of Akkad will last for thousands of years, partly because later people keep telling stories about it. Sargon becomes a model. Naram-Sin becomes a warning. Enheduanna becomes a voice.

Empire fails.

Memory persists.

And sometimes, memory is more durable than power.

## 6. Exit From This Movement

[HANDOFF TO NARAM-SIN, CLIMATE STRESS, COLLAPSE OF AKKAD.]

Part 3 is the story of an experiment that should feel both familiar and strange.

Familiar because empire is one of the great recurring facts of later history. We know what it means for power to rule at a distance, to impose language, to absorb elites, to extract from conquered people, to tell stories about itself.

Strange because here the machinery is being assembled without a manual.

The city-state world gave human beings one kind of scale.

Akkad tries to build another.

And it works.

For a while.

That phrase should hang over the whole part.

For a while, the king's name travels farther than any Mesopotamian king's name has traveled before.

For a while, officials make distant cities answer to a center.

For a while, Akkadian becomes more than a household language or regional language. It becomes the operating language of power, and eventually one of the deep roots of the international system we will meet in Part 5.

For a while, Sargon's family stands at the top of the largest political structure the world has yet seen.

And then the old problem returns in a new form.

Complexity has to be maintained.

Distance has to be paid for.

Conquest has to be renewed.

The harvest has to cooperate.

The governors have to remain loyal.

The conquered have to stay conquered.

The story empire tells about itself has to remain more convincing than the suffering empire creates.

And eventually, one of Sargon's successors will go further than favored by the gods.

He will call himself a god.

That is where the next movement begins.

## 7. The King Becomes A God

[CONTINUATION. Make Naram-Sin structural, not trivia.]

The next escalation is theological.

That sounds abstract, but in this world theology is never only theology.

Naram-Sin, Sargon's grandson, appears to do something extraordinary: he claims divinity during his own lifetime. Not merely favored by the gods. Not merely chosen. Divine. A living king crossing a boundary that earlier rulers had approached more carefully. [SOURCE CHECK: naram-sin]

This can sound like vanity if we flatten it.

A king gets too impressed with himself and decides to call himself a god.

That is the cartoon version.

The more interesting version is that empire creates a new scale problem, and divine kingship is one attempted solution.

If you rule one city, your authority can be anchored in that city's gods, rituals, traditions, walls, and memory.

If you rule many cities, with different gods and different memories, you need a higher claim.

You need a language of power that can stand above local loyalties.

Naram-Sin's divinity may be understood as arrogance, and later texts may frame it that way, but as a political technology it makes a dark kind of sense.

The king is no longer simply the most powerful man inside the system.

He becomes a sacred object inside the system.

Begin with the personal moment.

Imagine a priest or temple worker serving a cult of a living king. The man whose orders move soldiers and grain is not dead. He is not ancestral. He is alive somewhere, issuing commands, aging, eating, sleeping, fearing enemies perhaps, and yet the ritual system treats him as divine.

That is a strange psychological arrangement.

The community changes around it.

Officials can invoke the king at a higher register. Loyalty is not only political. Rebellion is not only treason. It can become cosmic disorder. The person of the ruler absorbs more meaning than any human body can safely hold.

The civilizational consequence is long.

Later rulers across history will make claims that rhyme with this: pharaohs, emperors, sons of heaven, divine-right kings, rulers whose bodies become symbols too large for ordinary accountability.

Again, not the same. Do not flatten all sacred monarchy into one thing.

But the pattern begins very early:

As political scale expands, rulers reach upward for metaphysical scale.

And that move creates its own danger.

Because if the divine king fails, the failure is not merely administrative.

It is theological.

## 8. The First Hubris Story

[MEMORY BEAT. Curse of Akkad with source caution.]

Later Mesopotamian tradition remembers the fall of Akkad through a moral story.

Naram-Sin offends the gods. He attacks or violates a sacred place. The gods withdraw favor. Disaster follows. The empire collapses. [SOURCE CHECK: naram-sin]

Handle this carefully.

The curse-of-Akkad tradition is not a neutral documentary explanation of why the empire fell. It is literature, theology, political memory, and retrospective meaning-making. It tells us less about the exact mechanics of collapse than about how later people wanted collapse to mean something.

But that is why it belongs in the script.

Human beings hate meaningless collapse.

If an empire falls because rainfall patterns shift, administrative systems overextend, local elites rebel, trade routes fail, succession gets messy, and outside groups press in at the wrong moment, that may be accurate. But it does not satisfy the moral imagination.

"The king sinned and the gods punished him" satisfies something deeper.

It gives catastrophe a plot.

That is one of the first great historical-memory moves: turn systemic failure into moral narrative.

We still do this.

We still want collapses to have villains.

We still want disasters to reveal character.

We still want history to punish arrogance in ways that feel narratively clean.

Sometimes arrogance matters.

Sometimes leaders really do break things.

But the danger is that moral clarity can become analytical blindness.

So the curse story should be told as a story.

Let it be powerful.

Then read past it.

That is the discipline this series has to teach the listener: ancient sources are not windows. They are voices. They are arguments. They are memories doing work.

## 9. How The First Empire Fails

[MULTI-CAUSAL COLLAPSE. Keep uncertainty audible.]

So why did Akkad fall?

The honest answer is not one sentence.

There may have been climate stress associated with the 4.2-kiloyear event: drought, aridification, agricultural disruption, movement of people, pressure on northern Mesopotamian systems. The dating, severity, and regional effects need careful source work. [SOURCE CHECK: 4.2k-event]

There were likely internal stresses: succession, overextension, the difficulty of maintaining garrisons and governors across distance, local resistance from conquered cities, elite competition.

There were outside groups, including the Gutians in later Mesopotamian memory, though whether they caused collapse or moved into a system already cracking is exactly the kind of question where ancient blame narratives can mislead. [SOURCE CHECK: gutians]

The empire may have been less a solid block than a network of control points, obligations, fear, negotiated loyalties, and fragile administrative routines. If so, it did not have to shatter like glass. It could fray.

That is the image.

Not one hammer blow.

Fraying.

A governor stops obeying.

A city revolts.

A harvest fails.

A road becomes unsafe.

A garrison is too expensive to maintain.

A new king lacks the old king's authority.

A local elite decides the center is weak.

An outside group becomes the name later scribes attach to the humiliation.

And eventually, what had looked like the first great expansion of political possibility becomes a lesson in fragility.

The first empire teaches the world that empire can exist.

Its fall teaches the world that empire is not self-maintaining.

## 10. What Survives Akkad

[EXIT TO PART 4.]

The empire fails.

But failure is not erasure.

This is one of the key handoffs from Part 3 to Part 4.

Akkadian power collapses, but Akkadian prestige survives.

Akkadian language outlives Akkadian empire.

The idea that a ruler can stand above multiple cities survives.

The administrative imagination survives.

The memory of Sargon survives so powerfully that later kings will want to borrow him.

The warning story of Naram-Sin survives too, because failed greatness is even more useful to memory than ordinary success.

So when we leave Part 3, we should not leave with the feeling that the first empire was a failed experiment and therefore irrelevant.

Most of history is failed experiments that leave tools behind.

Akkad leaves tools.

Language as power.

Dynasty as integration.

Divinity as imperial scale.

Historical memory as propaganda and warning.

Administration across difference.

And then, after the empire, the world becomes less unified but more connected in another way.

The next great story is not a conqueror.

It is a letter from a wife to a husband hundreds of miles away, because the world after Akkad is not smaller in every dimension.

Empire fails.

Commerce learns to travel.
