# The Hard Rules

These are structural commitments. Outlines and scripts that violate them are broken and need to be reworked.

**Rule 0 (implicit) overrides them all when necessary:** if a rule, applied rigidly, would kill a great story beat, the rule gives way. Story is primary.

---

## Rule 1 — Narrative First, Always

Outlines read as story beats, not bullet lists of facts. Each beat needs a hook — a tension, a question, a sensory image, a stake. If a section can't be told as a story, flag it and ask whether it belongs.

## Rule 2 — Ground Everything Before You Abstract

Before introducing any concept (palace economy, tribute system, chariot warfare, collapse cascade, cuneiform bureaucracy), pair it with a concrete grounding device: a modern analogy, a sensory anecdote, a comparison to something the listener already knows in their bones. Load-bearing, not decorative. Flag the weakest analogies for sanity-check — some illuminate, some mislead.

## Rule 3 — Translate Scale Relentlessly

Raw numbers without scale-translation are a failure state. Army sizes, populations, distances, timespans — all rendered into something the listener can feel.

> "The gap between the building of the Great Pyramid and Cleopatra is larger than the gap between Cleopatra and us."

Every time. Not just when convenient.

## Rule 4 — Ration Names Ruthlessly

No person, city, kingdom, or group enters the narrative without context that earns the introduction. The listener needs a reason to care **before** getting the name. If a section has more than a small handful of new proper nouns, cut, consolidate, or defer.

Bronze Age material is a graveyard for podcasts that forget this.

## Rule 5 — Hunt the Lesser-Known and the Strange

Default away from the famous-set-piece version of any event. Where the standard telling goes to Troy, ask what was happening in Ugarit. Where it goes to pharaohs, ask about the scribes, the tin traders, the women running estates while the men were at war.

When the obvious story must be told, find the angle that makes it feel new.

The pitch is not *"the Bronze Age you've heard of"* — it's *"the Bronze Age you didn't know was there."*

## Rule 6 — Sit with Uncertainty

The Bronze Age has thin, contested, fragmentary sources. Make it texture. "We don't know," "scholars argue," "the tablet breaks off here" are features, not bugs. Make ignorance dramatic.

## Rule 7 — No False Confidence, No Invented Detail

If sourcing is shaky, say so. If a vivid scene requires speculation to land, mark it as speculation. Where scholarly consensus has shifted recently, say so and when.

**Never invent sources, citations, or specific scholar quotes.** If you don't know who said something, say you don't know.

## Rule 8 — The View From Below Is Not Optional

Every part must spend substantial time at human scale — what daily life looked like, smelled like, felt like, for the people whose names didn't make it into the records.

Target: roughly a quarter to a third of each part lives at ground level. More in parts where the material strongly supports it (Part 4 with the Old Assyrian merchant wives, Part 7 with the collapse-from-inside view).

**Sub-discipline — render the world, don't redeem it.** The temptation is to romanticize peasants as noble, women as resilient, workers as dignified. That uses people as props for a different argument. The actual ground-level world was hard, often brutal, often boring, sometimes joyful, sometimes terrifying, and never simple. Tell it that way.

## Rule 9 — Anticipate Common Questions and Lesser-Known Facts

A curious listener whose obvious questions go unanswered loses trust. Predict what they would wonder. Answer when it arises.

**Paradigm case — iron.** A listener will think "but they had iron, right?" almost immediately. Answer it. The answer is one of the great stories in the period: iron was known throughout the Bronze Age (Tutankhamun's meteoric-iron dagger; Hittite texts; scattered smelting); bronze was actually better for most purposes; the trade network worked; path dependency was massive; iron only won when the bronze supply chain collapsed. The Iron Age was born from supply-chain failure, not technological triumph. This is a perfect Hard Rule 9 payday — analytical, contrarian, and a great story in its own right.

**Two registers:**
- **Anticipated questions** — things a reasonable listener would naturally wonder. Why didn't they have cavalry? How did they navigate without compasses? What language did diplomats use? Did they have money?
- **Lesser-known facts** — things that would surprise even a moderately informed listener. The Tutankhamun meteorite dagger. The Sumerian beer straws. The Babylonian king's complaint about the gold shipment being light. The Hittite recognition of Mycenaean Greece as a "brother" great power.

Both serve the same end: rewarding curiosity, building trust, making the material feel alive.

## Rule 10 — State of the World Snapshots

Each part includes at least one brief snapshot painting the world at that moment. Single-person vantage outward to the largest visible groupings. What people see, eat, make, believe. What's *not* there yet ("remember, these people didn't ride horses yet...").

Re-anchors the listener after stretches of narrative detail. Tracks change across parts by contrast — the snapshot in Part 5 next to the snapshot in Part 1 is itself an argument about what was new in the cosmopolitan age.

**Distinct from Rule 8.** Rule 8 is the *interior* view — one specific body doing specific work. Rule 10 is the *exterior* view — the wide angle, the establishing shot. The series needs both.

Brief by design — two to five minutes of audio. List-of-facts mode is the failure state; sensory texture is the goal. Full discipline in [`07-state-of-the-world.md`](./07-state-of-the-world.md).

## Rule 11 — Turning Points and Their Human Consequence

Specific discoveries, inventions, and moments that changed what was possible. Rendered as narrative beats with personal stakes, never as lists of firsts.

**Test for what counts:** *Did this change what was possible for ordinary lives, or change the course of civilization, or both?* If yes to either, it's a turning point candidate. If yes to both, it's load-bearing.

**Order of telling, always:** one specific person whose life changes → the community changing around them → the civilizational consequence rippling outward.

**Archetype callback is the structural payoff.** The series establishes recurring vantage figures — the Neolithic farmer, the Sumerian scribe, the Old Assyrian merchant's wife, the dockworker at Ugarit — whose later "descendants" (narrative, not literal) we revisit as the world around them changes. Carlin's most powerful continuity move.

**Distinct from Rule 10.** State of the World snapshots show what the world *is* at moments. Turning points show what *moved* the world from one moment to the next. The two braid together: the contrast between snapshots is partly explained by the turning points in between.

**What this is not:** great-man history (most turning points are anonymous); tech-list ("inventions" is the failure state — the unit is the *consequence*); progression narrative (change is not improvement; render honestly).

Full discipline in [`08-turning-points.md`](./08-turning-points.md), with the working archetype list.

---

## Order of operations when a rule conflicts with a beat

1. Is the beat actually a great story? (Be honest. Many "great" beats are not.)
2. Does the rule's violation cost more than the beat gains?
3. Is there a way to satisfy both? (Usually there is.)
4. If genuinely irreconcilable, story wins (Rule 0). But the rule violation gets logged so we can audit whether we're using Rule 0 as an escape hatch.
