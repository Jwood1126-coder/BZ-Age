# The North Star — Great Storytelling

**Great storytelling is the main goal.** Everything else serves it.

If you can tell a great story, people care. They remember. They engage. They tell their friends. They come back for the next episode. They stop the podcast in their car and sit in the parking lot to hear the end of the beat. That is what we are trying to produce.

Everything else in this repo — the hard rules, the throughlines, the comparative reaches, the contrarian-honest posture, the ground-level discipline — exists to serve great storytelling. None of it is the goal. All of it is craft in service of the goal.

## What this means in practice

- If a rule, applied rigidly, would kill a great story beat, **the rule has to give.** The rules are heuristics built to produce great storytelling in the typical case. They are not ends in themselves.
- When evaluating any choice, the question is not "does this follow the rules" but "does this make the story land harder."
- We tell stories first. The analytical work, the political points, the academic accuracy, the comparative insights — these are all things great storytelling can carry. They are not what we are trying to deliver in their own right.

## The so-different-yet-so-same insight

There's an insight the series will hopefully land — that people across the chasm of four thousand years were so different from us and yet so much the same. It's a real and powerful idea, and it can do real work in the listener.

But this insight is an **outcome** of great storytelling about ancient humans. It is not the goal of the storytelling. We don't tell stories to deliver this insight. We tell great stories, and that insight emerges because that's what great storytelling about ancient humans tends to produce.

The distinction matters. A series that aims at the insight directly becomes preachy. A series that aims at the story and lets the insight emerge becomes the kind of thing people don't forget.

Backed into. Not aimed at.

## The discipline of remembering this

Many history podcasts forget this. They become lectures with sound effects. The host wants to teach. The listener wants a story.

Carlin doesn't make this mistake. He's clearly fascinated by historical analysis, by structural arguments, by the intellectual puzzle of why things happened. But what comes out of his microphone is *story*. Anything that doesn't serve story gets cut, no matter how interesting it is on its own terms.

We do the same. When in doubt, story wins.
