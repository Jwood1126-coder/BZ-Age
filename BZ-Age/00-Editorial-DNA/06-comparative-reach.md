# Comparative Reach

The series constantly reaches outside the Bronze Age for comparison. **Not as decoration. As analytical tool.**

Carlin makes ancient material land by refusing to let the listener treat it as separate from everything they know. The Bronze Age can feel impossibly distant — most listeners have no scaffolding for it. The comparative reach is the scaffolding.

## The discipline

Flag whether each comparison is doing **real analytical work** or just **rhetorical work**. Both are allowed. The distinction must be clear.

- *Analytical work:* the comparison illuminates structure or causation. The Late Bronze Age diplomatic system next to the Concert of Europe shows that something recognizable as international relations is older than people assume — and the structural parallels (royal correspondence, formal etiquette, multi-polar tension) tell us something about how interconnected systems behave.
- *Rhetorical work:* the comparison makes the unfamiliar feel familiar without arguing a structural point. Calling the Uluburun shipwreck "the container ship of its day" is rhetorical — useful for scale, not for analysis.

Both have uses. Treating one as the other is the failure mode.

## Palette

### Ancient civilizations outside the focus period

- **Han China** — bureaucratic states, persistence of written tradition, frontier management
- **Classical Greece** — the polis as a different political form; how the Greek Dark Age birthed something Mycenaean palaces couldn't have
- **Rome** — empire's durability, tribute systems, the politics of conquest
- **The Maya** — collapse, monumental kingship, partially-recovered literate civilization
- **The Inca** — empire without writing (in the modern sense), tribute and labor extraction
- **Vedic India** — what was happening at the eastern edge of the Bronze Age world
- **Pre-Columbian North America** — complex societies without Old World urbanism

### Medieval and early modern

- **Feudal Europe** — low-amplitude post-collapse worlds (relevant to Parts 8–9)
- **The Mongol Empire** — sudden multi-ethnic empire
- **The Ottomans** — long-lived multi-ethnic empires and administrative innovations
- **Ming China** — centralized states under climate stress
- **Aztec and Inca at contact** — collapse-from-outside (different from multi-causal Bronze Age collapse)
- **Hanseatic League** — long-distance trade networks (especially for Old Assyrian comparison)
- **Renaissance Italian merchant houses** — for Old Assyrian merchant world

### Modern

- **World Wars** — sudden destruction of established orders
- **Cold War** — Late Bronze Age diplomatic system as multi-pole structure
- **Decolonization** — reorganization after imperial order falls
- **2008 financial crisis** — cascading systemic failure in interconnected systems
- **COVID supply shocks** — what happens when global supply chains break
- **Climate stress on fragile states** — climate-collapse model
- **Digital revolution and format obsolescence** — losability of literacy

### Figures for analytical comparison

- **Hammurabi** next to Justinian, Napoleon, the framers of the U.S. Constitution — codifying law
- **Akhenaten** next to Constantine, the Buddha, the early caliphs, Luther — religious revolutionaries and what they break
- **Ramses II's propaganda apparatus** next to Augustus, Louis XIV, Stalin's image-making — political construction of the ruler
- **Sargon of Akkad** next to other rise-from-obscurity founder-figures
- **The Sea Peoples** next to the Goths and Rome, the Mongol expansion, the Bantu migrations, modern climate-driven displacement — migration crises across history
- **Enheduanna** next to other early known women's voices — Sappho, the Trotula, Hildegard. The first known author.

## Comparisons to use sparingly

Some tempting comparisons mislead more than they illuminate.

- **Direct equation of Bronze Age divine kingship with modern theocracy.** The structures are too different. Useful as rhetorical hook, dangerous as analytical claim. Flag the limits.
- **Atlantis.** Tempting because of the memory-of-lost-civilizations frame, but so freighted with crank associations that invoking it usually costs more than it gains.
- **Direct comparison of the Bronze Age collapse to Rome's fall.** Structurally distinctive — multi-system, geographically dispersed, no clear successor. Rome's fall was slower, more localized, produced clear successors. Treat with care.

---

## How to think about comparison

A good comparison does one of three things:

1. **Translates scale.** "An army of 5,000 chariots is the equivalent in ancient warfare of..."
2. **Reveals structural parallel.** "The Late Bronze Age palace economy shares with the Soviet command economy a specific failure mode: when central planning collapses, the network of dependencies it created has no other way of organizing itself..."
3. **Provides emotional access.** "Imagine waking up tomorrow and discovering that the supply chains that bring you food have all broken at once — that's roughly the experience of the Hittite court in the years before the collapse."

If a comparison doesn't do any of these three things, it probably shouldn't be in the script.
