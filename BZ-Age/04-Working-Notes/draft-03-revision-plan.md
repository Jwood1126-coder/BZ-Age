## D. The elementary-questions discipline (Rule 9 extension)

Questions asked plainly in the narrator's voice — "the question a ten-year-old asks, and the answer most adults don't have" — placed where the listener would actually wonder them:

- P1: Why did people start farming if hunting was easier? (Harlan's three weeks)
- P1: Who was the first person to own something? (graves material, existing)
- P1→P2: Why didn't the Great Lakes — copper, water, right latitude — get cities? (plant in P1, detonate in P2)
- P2: Why do all the first civilizations sit on rivers? (barge logic: a donkey eats its load; a river doesn't)
- P2: Why is an hour sixty minutes? (base-60)
- P2: What did people use for money before coins? (barley, silver by weight; shekel)
- P3: What did "empire" change for a person who never saw the king? (existing villager beat)
- P3: If iron is everywhere, why bronze first? (asked, deliberately NOT answered — "hold the question")
- P4: Were there really bad businessmen 4,000 years ago? (Ea-nasir) / What did a loan look like before banks? (interest rates, capped by Hammurabi)
- P5: Iron question detonates.
- P6: Could they see it coming? (grain letters; "the grain inventory is wrong again")
- P7: What happened to the people the palaces owned, when the palaces burned? (slavery thread payoff)
- P8: Was the dark age dark for everyone? (existing contrarian beat)
- P9: How do you read a language no one has spoken for 3,000 years? (decipherment run) / Will our records last as long? (Domesday)
