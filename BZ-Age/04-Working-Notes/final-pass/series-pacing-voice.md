# Series Pacing & Voice Audit — Draft-05, All Ten Parts

**Auditor pass:** July 6, 2026 (draft-06 final-pass prep). Measured against the P0 register exemplar
(`02-Parts/Part-00-Prologue/cold-open-draft-05.md`) and the draft-05 contract (sections A–H) plus the
draft-06 brief. Read: script sections only, in series order. No draft file was modified.

**Headline:** The draft-05 register sweep worked. Every part is now recognisably one person telling a
story, the seams are overwhelmingly pivoted rather than sectional, and wit clears the 2–5/hour target
in nine of ten parts. The residual problems are almost all **cross-part** — the kind no single-part
pass could see, because each part audited only itself. The single highest-impact finding is
**time-ruler anchor reuse across parts**, which directly violates contract §E and is invisible from
inside any one part's revision log. Everything below is ranked by what a listener, hearing these in
pieces over days, would actually feel.

---

## 0. Calibrating the exemplar (P0)

The register standard, in measurable terms: short declarative punches and protected refrains ("The
wall was a hundred feet high." / "Two hundred and eleven years."); contractions throughout; deadpan
understatement ("could charitably be described as extremely bad"); genuine narrator reactions ("And
yet."); **one** time-ruler, spent deliberately (Washington's 211 years; Rome laid down twice); em-dash
asides present but not the default sentence shape; zero throat-clearing; zero "necessary but not
sufficient"-style academic connective tissue. That is the yardstick.

---

## 1. TIME-RULER CENSUS — the flagship finding

Contract §E: "do NOT reuse one anchor," build a *varied* toolkit, "one time-ruler per major span."
The per-part passes each honoured this *within their own part* and each avoided the two flagged
house anchors (full-Rome, Christianity's age). Nobody kept a **series-level ledger**, so the mid-range
anchors got spread across parts without anyone counting. Full census:

| Anchor | Parts that use it |
|---|---|
| **Grandmother / generation chain** | P2 ("seventy generations, grandmother to granddaughter"), P3 ("a hundred and sixty of them, standing in a line"), P4 ("A hundred grandmothers back"), P9 ("reach back a hundred grandmothers, mother to mother to mother") |
| **The Crusades / First Crusade** | P1 ("more distance than sits between us and the First Crusade"), P5 ("reckon back from us to the First Crusade"), P7 ("as far from the fall as we stand from the Crusades"), P8 ×2 (Lefkandi: "older than the Crusades are to us"; heirloom collar "older to her than the Crusades are to us") |
| **Columbus** | P3 ("the whole distance from Columbus's first crossing to right now"), P4 ("Columbus set sail about five centuries ago… lay that distance down six times"), P9 ("Columbus to now, and then some") |
| **Vikings** | P4 ("as far back from Lamassi as the Vikings are from us"), P6 ("as far from him as the Vikings are from us") |
| Rome (full run) | P0 only (flagship, correctly reserved) |
| Christianity's age | P2 only |

**The sharpest instances:**
- **P4 and P9 use nearly the identical grandmother anchor** — "A hundred grandmothers back" (P4 L111)
  vs "reach back a hundred grandmothers" (P9 L44). Same image, same number.
- **P4 and P6 use nearly the identical Viking anchor**, same "as far … as the Vikings are from us"
  frame.
- The Crusades anchor fires in **four** parts (P1/P5/P7/P8), twice inside P8 alone.

**Why it matters to a listener:** heard a few days apart, "a hundred grandmothers back" and "the
Vikings are from us" register as *the narrator's habit*, not as fresh grounding — precisely the
"variance is the fingerprint of a human" failure the contract warns about (§C). A binge listener feels
it hard; even a spaced listener feels the Viking/grandmother twins because the phrasing is verbatim.

**FIX (spec, not prose):** Build a one-page **series time-ruler ledger** and assign each major span a
*unique* anchor. Concretely: keep grandmother-chain in ONE part (recommend P2, where "seventy
generations" is freshest and does structural work) and re-anchor P3/P4/P9's grandmother instances onto
distinct tools (P3 already has the Columbus/Meghalayan options; P9's 3,000-year silence could take an
institutional-lifespan or a "older than any scripture still read" anchor). Collapse the Viking twin —
keep it in P4 (Lamassi/cuneiform is the better fit), re-anchor Khaemwaset in P6 (the pyramids-were-
already-ancient beat is *right there* and self-grounds). Thin the Crusades from four parts to two
(keep P7's fall→Iliad, which is load-bearing; keep one of P8's two). This is the highest-leverage
voice fix in the pass and it is entirely mechanical once the ledger exists.

**Ungrounded large spans left bare (acceptable, logged):** the several "three thousand years" gestures
in P5/P7/P9 were deliberately left un-ruled to avoid stat-pile-up; that judgment holds. **[VERIFY]**
the reworked Cleopatra formulation in P8 L169 ("Cleopatra lived nearer to us than to their beginning")
— the brief flags the *standard* formulation for verification; the repurposed version checks out
arithmetically (~2,050 yrs vs ~3,470) but is a novel construction, so confirm before air.

## 2. THE "STEP AROUND THE CRATER" BIT — a quip becoming a pattern

Contract §F: "if a listener can predict the next quip, it's a bit." The brief's own nugget-type-3
example ("we're stepping around the crater") has been adopted **verbatim in two parts** and its
sibling ("careers made/unmade/swallowed") in two:

- P5 (Thera): "a chronology war that has swallowed careers whole. **We'll step around the crater.**"
- P8 (highland archaeology): "Careers have been made and unmade on these ridges… I'm going to keep to
  the dirt and **step around the crater.**"
- P3 (Enheduanna authorship): "a fight that flares back up in the journals every few years" — same
  move, different clothes.
- P8 also: "about the most heavily mined ground in the entire field."

Individually each is a good acknowledged-deep-end nugget. As a set they are the same joke with the
same shape ("there's a scholarly war here / I'm not wading in") four times. By the second "step around
the crater" a listener who heard P5 will hear the bit coming in P8.

**FIX:** Keep ONE verbatim "step around the crater" (recommend P5/Thera, the cleaner instance). Recast
P8's highland-wars nugget into a different shape — same acknowledged-deep-end job, different gesture
(it can lean on the "careers made and unmade" half without the crater tag). P3's is already varied
enough to keep.

## 3. CROSS-PART JOKE/FACT DOUBLES

- **Egypt-never-sends-its-daughters** is delivered as a *fresh deadpan smile in both P5 and P6.**
  P5 L106: "Egypt married other men's daughters. Egypt did not send out its own. Not once, not for
  anyone." P6 L30: "The traffic ran one direction, and the pharaohs saw no reason to explain why…
  never once exported one of its own daughters." Same fact, same one-way-street deadpan, consecutive
  parts. Contract §A: a second appearance must be a callback doing new work; P6's is a re-landing of
  the punchline. **FIX:** In P6, demote to a one-clause callback that assumes the P5 laugh
  ("Egypt did what Egypt always did — took a bride, sent none back") rather than re-building the joke;
  or cut, since P6's marriage material can lean on the Zannanza reversal instead.
- **The "same ledger every time" / slavery throughline** recurs in P2, P4, P5, P6, P7. This one is the
  intended spine and mostly earns each hit as a callback doing new work, detonating well in P7 ("not
  even counted"). Not a defect — but note the *set phrase* "since the weaving halls of Uruk / since the
  first ration lists in Sumer" has calcified; vary the wording once or twice so the throughline reads
  as thought, not formula. Low priority.
- **The forensic-wink smile** ("the one fact the shipper never bothered to keep," P5) and the
  "everybody's second-best language" recognition (P5) are both strong and singular — flagged only to
  watch that "our-forensics-vs-their-casualness" doesn't become a third recurring wink in draft-06.

## 4. SEAM CENSUS

The draft-05 pass pivoted essentially every NEEDS-PIVOT `---` seam; I found **no egregious surviving
sectional hard cut** outside the licensed devices (cold opens, State-of-the-World snapshots, and
dated cold-open datelines like P5's "Kadesh, 1274 BCE."). The seams are healthy. Two texture notes:

- **The "camera-move" pivot is itself becoming a recurring device.** "Now widen out, past the
  schoolroom wall" and "Pull the camera back" (P2), "Step back from the village for a moment" (P3),
  "Stand back from the harbor" (P5), "Cross the water to the Greek mainland" (P6), "Stand back and look
  at the whole thing" (P5 Exit). These are far better than hard cuts and each is locally fine, but the
  *stand-back / pull-the-camera / cross-the-water* family is now the default transition solvent. Vary
  a few toward causal-associative pivots (the model is P6's "A drought doesn't reach the archives as
  weather. It reaches them as paperwork") rather than spatial camera directions. Low-medium.
- **P5 cast section** ("Here's who's still standing, then — the cast of the cosmopolitan age") is
  pivoted at entry but is structurally a survey (bolded roster entries) — the one place the "no
  survey-style entry" mandate (draft-06 ADOPTED #2) strains. Each entry does carry a role-handle and a
  hook, so it clears the bar, but it is the most list-like stretch in the series after P5's own cargo
  manifest. Watch in draft-06; consider letting one or two players enter *in motion* (Assyria and
  Cyprus especially) rather than as roster cards.

## 5. TIC FREQUENCY

- **"So." paragraph openers:** within the ≤3/part ration everywhere I counted; P6's self-report of 2
  is representative. No finding.
- **Rhetorical check-ins** ("if you've been paying attention," P5 Exit; "You may have noticed…," P3;
  "you already know why," P8): all within the 1–2/part ration. No finding.
- **Em-dash asides — series-wide texture flag.** P1 self-flagged ~21/1,000 words ("high, uniform end
  of the Voice-Bible band… a future rhythm pass could thin these"); P7 self-flagged ~19.6/1,000. This
  was never swept. Across the series the em-dash-parenthetical is the **default** sentence complicator
  — the go-to move for inserting an aside or a hedge. In audio, one em-dash aside per breath is
  characterful; a uniform ~20/1,000 becomes a detectable cadence, especially stacked (two dashes in one
  sentence recurs often, e.g. P8 L119, P9 L124). **FIX:** a mechanical variance pass converting a
  fraction of em-dash asides to full-stops, colons, or restructured sentences (P7 already did a small
  version of this: "dashes converted to colons/commas/period-breaks"). Target the double-dash sentences
  first. Medium — pervasive but low per-instance.
- **Fragment stacks:** deliberate and rationed (P1 snapshot "Bears in the hills. Wolves at night. …
  Leopards."; P5 cargo manifest is a licensed fragment list). No finding.

## 6. PARAGRAPH RHYTHM — flattest stretch per part

The setup→payoff→bow paragraph is the house shape and it is mostly well-varied after the B-passes.
Flattest surviving stretch in each part (where consecutive paragraphs each land on a tidy bow and the
ear starts to predict the cadence):

- **P1** — the bioarchaeology run (L81–91): "…which turns out to be better for teeth." / "…because
  farming is relentless…" / "You stay." / "…never had to meet." Four consecutive units each closing on
  a neat verdict. Content is strong; the metronome is the issue.
- **P2** — the demographic-disaster sequence (L178–196): "…was, among other things, a disease engine."
  / "…probably applied, in some form, from the beginning." / "…rations offered by the temple were not
  nothing." Repeated "we know this from X / we know it from Y" openings compound the flatness.
- **P3** — the "How do you…?" catechism (L76–84): five near-identical Q→one-line-A→"looks obvious now"
  units. The B-pass broke four of five closers, but the *question-openers* still clock. It's a good
  device slightly over-length; consider cutting one of the five.
- **P4** — cleanest part. Flattest is the law-code provisions run (L169–177), several parallel
  "There are provisions for X" paragraphs; already partly recast (D6), residual mild.
- **P5** — the cast roster (L68–82), for reasons in §4.
- **P6** — the climate section (L82–92): "A drought that lasts a year… five years… twenty years"
  escalation is earned, but the two "The palace economies… were calibrated for X. They were not
  calibrated for Y." paragraphs sit adjacent and echo.
- **P7** — the archetype roll-call (L128–136): scribe / dockworker / merchant's-wife / administrator,
  each a tidy "his/her world is X now" unit. Intentional litany; on the edge of metronomic.
- **P8** — the dark-age argument (L58–64): "The palace is gone. … They also had less bronze. … The
  Bronze Age collapse was not a good thing." Several short verdict-paragraphs in a row; see §7.
- **P9** — the closing snapshot's benediction run (L152–168): a series of sentence-fragment
  paragraphs ("His neighbor, who can read what the scribe writes." / "Their community, telling
  stories…") building to the roll-call. Deliberate and moving, but it is the longest sustained
  bow-cadence in the series; watch that it reads as earned crescendo, not litany-by-rote.

None of these is a blocker; they are the places a draft-06 read-aloud should break one or two cadences.

## 8. REGISTER DRIFT — ranking, closest-to-furthest from the P0 exemplar

Ranked on how consistently the prose stays in the "smartest person you know, three drinks deep,
never loses the thread" register versus lapsing toward essay/textbook/sermon. Each ranked part's
single most AI-ish surviving passage is quoted.

**1. P4 (closest).** Lamassi and Ea-nasir are the told-story register at its purest; the comedy of
recognition is doing the thesis's work. *Most AI-ish residue:* "Something that functions like
commercial self-governance — what some historians have called, with acknowledged anachronism,
'stateless capitalism.' The phrase fits the structure, though Assyriologists often resist it…" (L56) —
a genuine seminar paragraph, the one place P4 stops telling and starts surveying scholarship.

**2. P1.** The second-person Neolithic body is exemplar-grade; the smith-dying beat lands. *Most
AI-ish:* "Because populations win evolutionary competitions even when individual members pay the
cost." (L89) — a textbook thesis sentence opening a paragraph, before the concrete valley example
rescues it.

**3. P6.** "A kind of disaster that begins in sunshine" is exemplar-quality; the seed-grain jar and
the accountant are superb. *Most AI-ish:* "The palace economies of the Late Bronze Age were calibrated
for normal conditions plus a buffer. They were not calibrated for twenty years of below-normal
agricultural output across an entire region simultaneously." (L88) — systems-abstract diction
("calibrated," "below-normal agricultural output across an entire region simultaneously").

**4. P7.** Emotionally the strongest part; the Ugarit correction, the song-survives, the coast-watcher.
*Most AI-ish:* "A weapon system developed for one kind of enemy becomes ineffective against a
different kind of enemy." (L118) — a general textbook proposition standing alone before the specific
Drews material grounds it.

**5. P3.** Enheduanna, the King List, the cylinder-seal wit all sit in register. *Most AI-ish:* the
Liverani paragraph — "describes what made this ideologically new: Sargon was changing the image of
kingship itself — from a cultic and administrative official… to a war hero whose legitimacy rested on
conquest and universal dominion." (L58) — the register briefly becomes the reference work it cites.

**6. P2.** Mostly excellent, but its length (§9) means more expository stretches survive. *Most
AI-ish:* "The conditions were necessary but not sufficient. Most of the agricultural world did not
produce cities." (L34) — "necessary but not sufficient" is the single most academic connective in the
series, exactly the register the contract moves away from.

**7. P9.** The decipherment run is vivid (Smith, Ventris, della Valle); the Xenophon bookend closes
beautifully. Drift is at the close. *Most AI-ish:* the final-snapshot benediction cascade — "But a
world that contains, in its bones, the inheritance of everything that came before — the agricultural
revolution, the first cities, the empire idea, the international trade network, the scribal tradition,
the cosmopolitan moment, the collapse, the rebuilding — and is, right now, in this moment, building the
things that the next several thousand years of human civilization will be built on." (L158) — a long
abstract list-cascade in sermon register. Load-bearing as a finale, but the furthest single sentence
from the exemplar's spoken punch.

**8. P5.** Uneven: the Uluburun and Amarna material is strong, but the cast roster and the Akhenaten
scholarship survey pull toward reference-book. *Most AI-ish:* "This is henotheism — the elevation of
one god among many — not monotheism in the Abrahamic sense." (L134) — a definitional gloss, textbook
cadence.

**9. P8 (furthest).** The aftermath-argument parts naturally drift essayistic, and P8 drifts most:
the dark-age-reconsidered scorecard, the criteria-inventory, and the Israelite-theology section all
argue rather than tell across multi-paragraph stretches. *Most AI-ish:* "The Bronze Age collapse was
not a good thing. It was also not uniformly bad for everyone. That distinction matters, because the way
we tell history shapes what we think is possible. If 'complex and sophisticated' always equals 'better'
and 'simpler' always equals 'worse,' we have built an assumption into our historical thinking that the
evidence doesn't support." (L64) — this is the most sustained lapse into meta-historical essay in the
series: five sentences of argument-about-history with no body, no scene, no person. It states the
thesis the *scene* (the highland farmer, next section) is about to prove — so it can largely be cut and
handed to the snapshot to demonstrate. This is P8's core register problem and its clearest fix.

*(P0 is the exemplar itself and sits above the ranking.)*

**Pattern in the drift:** the furthest-from-register passages are almost all **thesis-statement
paragraphs that announce what a following scene then demonstrates** (P8 L64, P1 L89, P7 L118, P2 L34).
The house voice's own rule — demonstrate, don't announce — is the fix in every case: cut or shrink the
abstract topper, let the body carry it. This is a coherent, mechanical draft-06 pass.

## 9. STRUCTURAL / PACING — one real big move

**P2 is a runtime outlier that unbalances the series.** Estimated lengths (from the drafts' own
headers): P0 ~9 min, P1 ~40–45, **P2 ~74–82**, P3 ~50–55, P4 ~46–51, P5 ~49–54, P6 ~39–44, P7 ~42–47,
P8 ~33–36, P9 ~32–35. P2 is nearly **double** most parts and more than twice P8/P9. For a series heard
in pieces, an 80-minute part against 33-minute parts is a felt pacing lurch — and it front-loads the
series' densest hour before the listener has fully bought in.

The content is superb and I would not gut it. But two candidate moves:

- **Split P2** at a natural seam (the cleanest is after the Uruk portrait + demographic material,
  before "I've been skirting the deepest strangeness of this world" / the sacred-marriage and
  Gilgamesh material) into "The First City" and "The Idea of the City" — restoring a normal per-part
  runtime and giving the sacred-marriage/Gilgamesh/global-survey material room to breathe. (Episode-
  splitting is logged as a Phase-Three decision per the draft-06 brief, so flag rather than execute —
  but the *pacing* case for it is strong and belongs on the record now.)
- If a split is off the table for draft-06: **tighten the closing global survey** (P2 L274–320, the
  Egypt / Indus / "everywhere else" tour + "how connected is the connected world" essay). It does real
  work (plants the Indus ending, sets the one-among-worlds frame) but it partly rehearses the *same*
  survey job P5's cast section does, and it is the part of P2 that most reads as tour rather than
  story. A 15–20% trim there is the lowest-risk pacing gain in the series.

Nothing else in the macro structure reads as mis-paced. The plant/payoff economy is, after the
deposit-rule pass, genuinely amnesiac-proof at the sites I checked (P6 Mitanni, P7 shelf/hymn, P8
grandfather, P9 Hittite/Indus) — that machinery is in good shape and needs no further work.

## 10. Summary of fixes, ranked by listener impact

1. **Series time-ruler ledger** — de-duplicate the grandmother-chain (4 parts), Crusades (4), Columbus
   (3), Vikings (2 near-verbatim). Assign each major span a unique anchor. (§1) — highest impact,
   fully mechanical.
2. **Kill the "step around the crater" repeat** — keep one, recast P8's. (§2)
3. **De-double the Egypt-one-way-daughters smile** — P6 becomes a callback, not a re-landing. (§3)
4. **P8 register: cut the L64 meta-essay**, hand the thesis to the highland-farmer scene that follows;
   surface one dry smile in the dark-age middle. (§7, §8)
5. **Series em-dash variance pass** — thin the ~20/1,000 double-dash sentences. (§5)
6. **P2 pacing** — split, or trim the closing global survey ~15–20%. (§9)
7. **Trim/vary the announce-then-demonstrate thesis-toppers** (P1 L89, P2 L34, P7 L118, P8 L64) — the
   register-drift pattern, one mechanical pass. (§8)
8. **Vary the camera-move seam solvent** in one or two spots. (§4)
9. **[VERIFY]** the reworked Cleopatra formulation (P8 L169). (§1)

Every finding above is grounded in quoted script and would change what a listener hears. Nitpicks that
would not change the listen were left out.
