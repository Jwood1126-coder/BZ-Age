## 1. MISSING

**M1 — The long walk down the rivers: how anyone came to be in southern Mesopotamia at all. THE missing beat, and the writer asked for it.** The draft cuts from "back to where we left off. The Neolithic village" (L28) straight to "The place it happened first… was the flat alluvial plain of southern Mesopotamia" (L36). Between those two lines, a newcomer is never told the one thing a child would ask first: *why would anyone live there?* The plain is described as rainless, stone-less, timber-less, metal-less, "waterlogged in the wrong places, dry in others" (L40) — and then people are simply *in* it, building the largest city on Earth. The descent from P1's rain-fed hill world down into the rainless mud is the literal P1→P2 seam, and it is never walked. The beat menu flags this as the writer's own July-2 request ("I liked how he explained how people first came to this area and why this place worked") and specs it: farming villages spreading down the two river-courses one lifetime at a time (nobody migrates to a desert; they follow a river), the date palms as calories-plus-shade, and the discovery that cracks everything — you don't have to farm the riverbank; dig a channel and the river follows you inland. **NEW-PROSE-SITE:** ~250–300 words as P2's second movement, between L28 and L36. Each step is an answered child-question, which is the series' MAIN THEME landing at the exact spot the story needs context. Pay for it with the SHAPE-A connectivity trim.

**M2 — Why is the mud rich? (The silt mechanism.)** L40 asserts it flat: "The soil is rich — spectacularly rich, from the river sediment." That is a stated answer to an unasked question — the newcomer never learns the *mechanism*, which is one of the best small ironies in the whole story. **NEW-PROSE-SITE:** one short paragraph (~60 words), folded into M1's descent or the why-here material: the rivers grind the Taurus and Zagros mountains into flour — quartz and feldspar too fine to see — and drop it on the plain, so the poorest place in the Bronze Age world is *made of the mountains' wealth, pulverized and delivered*. Converts an assertion into an answered elementary question, and quietly wires the mountain world (P1's Göbekli foothills, P4's Anatolian roads) to the mud. `[VERIFY the geology shape]`

**M3 — The first canal is the turning point of the part, and it's told as an abstraction.** The single invention that "cracked open the door to everything else" (L46) — organizing hundreds of people to move water — is delivered entirely in the abstract: "somebody had to figure out how to organize dozens or hundreds of people for a purpose." No person, no moment, no hands (a Rule 11 violation: turning point told as exposition, not as the person whose life changed). This is the mechanism the *entire rise* pivots on. The M1 beat's natural landing — "who dug the first canal? — someone whose name is ten thousand years gone, doing something no one had done before" — would fix M3 and M1 in one move, which is another reason M1 earns its words.

**M4 — kur / kalam: the worldview inside two words.** The draft names the language isolate (L36 "a language unrelated to any other we know about") but never lets the listener stand *inside* the Sumerian mental map. **NEW-PROSE-SITE:** one breath near the language-isolate line or the farmer's small-world beat (L310): *kalam*, the Land — the civilized plain, home; *kur*, the mountain — and also foreign, wild, hostile, and in time the netherworld itself. The whole Mesopotamian map drawn inside a single vocabulary item, pairing with P1's "horizon as the edge of everything." `[VERIFY the semantic range]` Near-zero cost, high worldview payoff.

## 2. PRESUMPTIONS (each quoted)

**P1 — "the Babylonian astronomers who inherited that mathematics" (L124).** In the base-60 chain, "Babylonian" arrives as a known quantity — but we are standing in *Sumer*, in Uruk, and Babylon does not exist yet and won't for centuries. A newcomer has no way to know Babylonians are the later Akkadian-speaking heirs of the Sumerians, not the Sumerians themselves. The chain is a series highlight; it should not trip on an unglossed civilization. Fix: one apposition ("the Babylonian astronomers — the later heirs of this world —").

**P2 — "a civilization — the Mycenaeans — that will eventually have an entire literary tradition, the Trojan cycle, built on top of it" (L250).** Two cold proper nouns (Mycenaeans, Trojan cycle) dropped as if known, mid-sentence, in a part that never otherwise mentions Greece's Bronze Age. The clause *assumes* the listener knows the Trojan War and that it attaches to a real lost civilization. It's a forward-plant to P5/P8, but at plant time a base-layer listener hears "wait, who?" Fix: lean on the *idea* (a later people whose whole heroic literature was built on a civilization they'd completely forgotten) and let the names ride lighter.

**P3 — "the Akkadian-speaking world… a Semitic language" (L332).** "Semitic" is dropped unglossed in the exit — the load-bearing contrast that sets up Sargon and all of P3. A newcomer doesn't know the word names a language family (Akkadian, later Hebrew, Aramaic, Arabic). One clause of gloss earns the whole P2→P3 hinge.

**P4 — the cosmology name-load (L200–222): Inanna, Dumuzi, Anu, Enki, Tiamat, Marduk, Atrahasis, Enheduanna, plus Enuma Elish and Atrahasis Epic.** No single intro is broken — most carry an apposition — but the *density* across L200–222 is the highest in the part, and it lands in the most abstract movement (theology), where the listener has the least concrete handhold. In audio, heard while driving, this is where a newcomer is likeliest to lose the thread. Not a line-fix; a FOCUS/SHAPE issue (see §4, §5-D): thin the creation-myth tail, don't gloss harder.

**P5 — "The region the Greeks would call Babylonia" (L36).** Minor: "the Greeks" as a reference point before Greeks exist anywhere in the series' frame. Reads as texture, not a hole, but flagged for the record — the *Greek* framing of Mesopotamia is doing no work here and could go.

*Clean intros worth protecting as models: James C. Scott ("the political scientist who spent a career on this," L72); Denise Schmandt-Besserat (built as a scene, L106); Robert McCormick Adams ("spent decades walking the floodplain," L308); the edubba ("'the tablet house' in Sumerian," L118); the lugal ("the 'great man,'" L202).*

## 9. THESIS

P2 is **the RISE** — "the invention of the future," the first upstroke of the non-linear arc. It serves the thesis well on three of four pillars:
- **Non-linearity made physical:** Cahokia (L78) is the thesis's best single proof that history isn't a ladder — the same city-forming logic fires independently, a hemisphere away, thousands of years later, *and* the New World later runs its own rise-and-fall. The beat is there; it could point at the thesis one degree harder ("history ran the experiment twice and got the same answer," L78, is already most of the way).
- **Fragility planted:** the wall-compounding loop (L270) and "when they eventually fail — which they will — everything built on top of them will fail with them" (L300) seed the collapse. Good. (SHAPE-A keeps this and sheds only the mechanics.)
- **"A world, not the world":** L282 ("The Bronze Age world we're going to walk through is not the world. It is a world") sets up the cosmopolitan center of gravity and the "tiny, blazing exception" (L308). Strong.
- **The privileged present (pillar 4) — under-served, which is correct for a non-bookend but leaves one clause on the table.** The part *uses* the modern vantage everywhere (Schmandt-Besserat "figured this out almost by accident," L106; Adams "spent decades walking the floodplain," L308; skeletal age-at-death, L186) but never lets the listener feel it as the *gift* the thesis names. P2 needn't carry a bookend beat — but the Schmandt-Besserat scene (a woman in the 1960s reading five thousand years of accounting out of museum-drawer clay nobody understood) is a latent privileged-present beat that one clause of wonder would activate. Optional, low-cost.

**What the part could do for the thesis that it doesn't:** the memory/rediscovery spine is *planted* (Gilgamesh myth-over-memory, "the story got there first," L254; the Indus script → P9, L278) but the part's own act of recovery is never named as remarkable. The single biggest thesis-strengthener, though, is M1: the long walk makes the *rise* feel earned rather than assumed — you can't feel a civilization rise if you never saw anyone climb into the valley.
