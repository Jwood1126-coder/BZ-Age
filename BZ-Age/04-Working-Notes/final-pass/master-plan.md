**NEW PROSE A — the long walk down the rivers:**

> So how does anybody end up down there? You walk. Not you personally — your family, over
> something like forty generations `[VERIFY span]`, starting up in the hills where the wild
> grain grew and the rain came free. Follow the two rivers downhill, south and east, out of the
> rain. Every generation of drift — one village budding off another, a day's walk apart — the
> rain gets thinner and the land gets flatter and emptier, until there is no rain you can farm
> on at all, and no stone to build with, and no timber worth the name, and no metal in the
> ground anywhere. Everything a village is made of: absent. And still they kept going, because
> the two rivers were carrying the one thing that beats all of it, and dropping it at the far
> end by the ton.

**NEW PROSE B — the silt (follows A after whatever seam sentence the draft already offers; if
none, they run consecutively — A's last line hands off clean):**

> Twice a year, the rivers come down off the mountains carrying the mountains with them —
> rock ground to flour, riding the snowmelt `[VERIFY: flood seasonality]` — and where the land
> goes flat the water slows down and lets it go. That's what southern Iraq is. Not land the
> rivers water: land the rivers *made*, delivered one flood at a time, some of the youngest,
> deepest, most absurdly fertile soil on the planet `[VERIFY: delta formation timeline]`. Plant
> a seed in it and it comes back several times over what the hills ever gave `[VERIFY: yield
> ratios]`. There was no soil like it within walking distance of anywhere.

**NEW PROSE C — Wrangel (deep-time ruler):**

> And if you want one fact to hold onto for how deep in time we are: while all of this is
> happening, there are still mammoths alive. A last few thousand, marooned on an island off the
> Siberian coast, dwarfing down generation by generation. Woolly mammoths outlived the
> invention of the city. They outlived the invention of writing. By the time the last one died,
> Ur had already built and lost an empire `[VERIFY: Wrangel extinction ~1650 BCE vs Ur III]`.
> The Ice Age didn't end so much as leave the room slowly — and the first cities grew up while
> it was still finding the door.

**NEW PROSE D — kur and kalam:**

> The Sumerians had a word for their own country: *kalam* — the Land. Flat, watered, planted,
> ours. And a word for everywhere else: *kur* — which means mountain, and also means foreign
> country, and also, eventually, means the underworld `[VERIFY: semantic range]`. One word. The
> hills you'd have to climb to leave, the strangers who live in them, and the land of the dead:
> same word. That's the first map of the world we can actually read, and it isn't drawn in
> distances — it's drawn in belonging. Everything is either the Land, or it's the mountain.
> We've been drawing that map ever since. We've just kept changing what counts as the mountain.

**NEW PROSE E — anonymity as an invention:**

> Here's a thing no human being had ever felt before this place existed: being in a crowd of
> strangers. For the whole two hundred thousand years before this, every face you ever saw came
> with a name, a mother, a grudge, a debt. In Uruk, you could walk from the temple quarter to
> the river gate and pass more people in an hour than your great-grandmother met in her life —
> and owe most of them nothing, and know none of them, and never see most of them again.
> Anonymity had to be invented. It was invented here. And we still haven't decided whether it's
> freedom or loneliness — the city has been both ever since, and it was both on day one.

**NEW PROSE B — the thesis coda (the series' final movement, after "The past is immense..." —

> And yet.
>
> Put the two things side by side, because they belong together. Xenophon stood on Nineveh and
> got a shrug and a thunderbolt. The scribe with his twenty-two letters looked back at the
> palace world and got heroes, because legend was the best storage his age had. Every people in
> this story stood on ruins and had to guess what they were standing on — and every one of them
> guessed wrong.
>
> We stopped guessing. The drought that helped pull the whole system down — we found it,
> physically, in the mud at the bottom of lakes `[VERIFY: paleoclimate records already
> fleet-verified in P6/P7 context]`. A king's grief for his plague-dead country — we can read
> it, in a language no living person had ever heard spoken. And the same instruments keep going
> down: past the Bronze Age, past the first farms, past the species, down to the age of the
> planet and the age of the starlight arriving tonight — one continuous story, and we are the
> first people who can check.
>
> Rise. Plateau. Fall. Restart. You've now watched the whole shape turn once, start to finish —
> and nobody inside it could see it. Not the people in the rise, who thought it was the way of
> things. Not the people in the fall, who thought it was the end of the world. Not the people
> in the restart, who didn't know they were inside anything at all.
>
> We can see it. First ones, ever. That's the seat you've been sitting in this whole time — the
> one genuinely new thing in a story where everything else has happened before, and will
