# P1 — Before Bronze — FINAL-PASS FINDINGS

**Read against:** draft-06 brief (thesis + final-pass mandate), draft-05 brief (Told-Story contract A–H), P0 prologue exemplar.
**Script length audited:** 6,286 prose words between `## The Draft` and `## Revision notes` (the 8,502 in the assignment counts the ~2,200-word revision notes). This is the shortest part by a wide margin (siblings 10–14k). Length verdict is folded into MISSING, FOCUS, and SHAPE below.

**Headline verdict (length audit).** The brevity is *mostly* earned — P1 covers a period with very few discrete "events," and most of its movements are properly scened (Harlan's experiment, the storehouse, Göbekli, Çatalhöyük's rooms, the dying metalworker are all real scenes, not skims). It is **not** thin across the board, and it is **not** stealing P2 material (the long walk down the rivers is correctly absent; the "why this place" river/silt geography is appropriately banked for P2; the geography that *is* here is light). But it is thin in exactly one structurally load-bearing place — the closing runway to the first cities — which happens to be the part's titular job and the launch of the whole series' "rise." That, plus one missing thesis beat, is where the length problem actually lives. The fix adds ~1,000–1,500 words and earns them; forcing P1 to sibling length would pad it.

## 1. MISSING

**M1 — The on-ramp to cities (the part's own titular job) is a montage, not a movement.** The entire final section — the 2,000-year runway from farming villages to the doorstep of Uruk — is ~450 words (L193–209) and opens by *telling* the span: "By 3200 BCE — two thousand years on from the Neolithic village we started in — the head of steam has been building for a long time." The connective mechanism (how surplus compounds into stratification into specialization into the pressure that becomes a city) is asserted in one paragraph (L203) and one convergence sentence (L205). Varna gets a scene; the *process* does not. This is the beat the series' entire "rise" launches from, and it's the most rushed thing in the part. NEW-PROSE-SITE: a proper movement that lets the listener *feel* two thousand years of intensification build — the villages thickening, the surplus deepening, the graves diverging — so that "It's going to be loud" lands as the top of a wave the listener felt rise, not a montage caption. This is the single highest-impact addition available to P1.

**M2 — The privileged-present beat the prologue promised and P1 silently drops.** The prologue makes an explicit covenant: "We have ways of getting at it that Xenophon didn't have. Archaeology. Decipherment." P1 is the first episode to cash that in — and it *uses* the tools everywhere (bioarchaeology at L81, the wheat-DNA cradle at L63, lake-sediment drought records at L153, the skeletal childhood-stress record) but never once names the vantage as the gift the thesis says it is. The thesis's fourth pillar — "we can use scientific tools to understand our place… like never before" — has no home in P1, and P1 is the natural first home because P1 is where the science first does visible work (reading a 9,000-year-old skeleton's malnutrition; DNA fingerprinting the exact hills; drought in the mud). NEW-PROSE-SITE: one organic beat — probably hung on the bones at L81 or the Harlan/DNA convergence at L63 — where the narrator lets the listener feel that we can *see* this, and that no one who ever stood in these ruins before us could. Never preached; one clause of wonder, then back to the story.

**M3 — Where is the farmer standing?** The opening immersion (L13–47) is geographically nowhere. "The world… is about forty kilometers in every direction" (L27) — but which forty kilometers, on which planet's map? The prologue handed off a located body ("along the Tigris… the land that is now Iraq"); P1 drops the location and doesn't restore it until L63, and then it's about Harlan, not the farmer. A listener waking up in this body wants to know where the body is. See PRESUMPTIONS P1. NEW-PROSE-SITE: one light placing clause in the snapshot (the arc of hills and rivers we'll come to call the Fertile Crescent) — enough to pin the body without importing P2's geography lecture.

**M4 — "What they did on a good evening."** The prologue promised the warm side — "what they did on a good evening, what a city smelled like." P1's snapshot is relentlessly grim (feet hurt, old at 40, the cold "will end your life far sooner than it had to"). That grimness is correct discipline (Rule 8: render, don't redeem) — but the part has essentially *one* warm human beat, the seasonal gathering that "felt like an enormous city" (L27), and the comedy-of-recognition register (contract F: ancient people being exactly like us) barely touches the interior life. The thesis's "profoundly the same" half is carried by wit about tomatoes and rooftop commutes, not by any glimpse of these people laughing, feasting, or loving. Not a structural hole; a one-beat opportunity so the snapshot isn't pure misery-tourism.

## 2. PRESUMPTIONS (each quoted)

**P1 — "the northern arc of the Fertile Crescent" (L63).** First and only framing of the single most important geography in the part, dropped as if the listener already holds the crescent's shape and extent in their head. A true newcomer has no map. The term recurs implicitly ("In the Fertile Crescent, we can actually look…" L79) still undefined. This is the lead presumption and ties to M3.

**P2 — "Knossos — yes, that Knossos — four thousand years before anyone builds a palace there" (L77).** The "yes, that Knossos" is a designed nugget (Pixar rule) and fine as a nugget — but "a palace there" is *also* the only context a newcomer gets, and most newcomers don't know Knossos at all. The line does double duty (wink + sole gloss) and the gloss is thin. Acceptable, but flagged: for the base-layer listener, Knossos here means only "a palace got built later," which is close to nothing.

**P3 — "Jared Diamond, synthesizing the skeletal record in the 1980s" (L81), later "Jared Diamond called it, bluntly, 'a catastrophe…'" (L95).** Diamond is leaned on twice as a load-bearing authority with only a role-fragment ("synthesizing the skeletal record"). Contrast the model intros the part *does* give — James C. Scott ("a political scientist who'd spent a career on how modern states see the people they govern," L85) and Marshall Sahlins ("studying twentieth-century forager groups in the 1960s," L91) are both introduced cleanly. Diamond deserves the same three-word epithet the others got.

**P4 — "the Neolithic village we started in" (L193).** "Neolithic" appears here for the first time as a bare label, ~180 lines after the village it names. The concept is fully built in the listener's ear by now, but the *word* is never glossed. Minor; a single "New Stone Age" apposition anywhere earlier would close it.

**P5 — "the land the Greeks will call Babylonia, the land whose oldest name is Sumer" (L205).** "Babylonia" is dropped as known en route to introducing Sumer. It's glossing the payoff word (Sumer), so it mostly self-carries, but "Babylonia" itself is assumed. Very minor.

*Clean intros worth protecting as models: Scott (L85), Sahlins (L91), aurochs ("the wild ox," L39), obsidian (by function, L33), emmer ("the ancestor of bread wheat," L41), peripheral neuropathy ("damage to the nerves of the hands and feet," L173), the lame smith-gods (glossed as a group, L175).*

## 3. PACING MAP (top to bottom)

**Movement 1 — The snapshot (L13–47).** Excellent open; the prologue handoff is seamless and the rhythm is well-varied (short punches "You are old." against long sensory runs). **Drag risk:** three inventory paragraphs run consecutively — animals (L37–39), food (L41), predators (L43). In audio, back-to-back catalogs can flatten into a list even when each is good. The food paragraph carries its weight (the Mediterranean-table beat); the predator list is short and fine; but the stack of three wants one break of rhythm or one to be folded. **Verdict: strong, with a mild mid-section catalog flatness.**

**Movement 2 — Agricultural revolution (L51–97).** The strongest sustained stretch in the part; Harlan is a genuine scene, the storehouse lands, the bones sober. **One pacing swerve:** the Greece/Knossos paragraph (L77) drops in between "it happened independently" and "But in the Fertile Crescent we can look at what it did to people," delaying the payoff of the bad-deal argument to run a forward-reference errand. It's good material but it interrupts a rising argument. **Verdict: excellent; consider relocating the Greece beat so it doesn't stall the health payoff.**

**Movement 3 — Göbekli (L101–123).** Strong hook ("a hillside that shouldn't exist"), strong landing ("We don't know what they were doing"). **Minor decel:** the honest footnote (L119) is necessary (Rule 6/7) but academic and long, arriving right on the payoff. Acceptable. **Verdict: solid.**

**Movement 4 — Çatalhöyük / Jericho (L127–139).** The stone-on-stone sound beat (L131) is the best sensory moment in the part. Jericho is a second site bolted to the tail (L139), slightly compressing, but the Joshua nugget earns it. **Verdict: solid; Jericho a touch rushed.**

**Movement 5 — Metal (L143–189).** The longest movement and the **density peak** — this is where an audio listener's attention is most taxed. It carries native copper → the Great Lakes detour (a full geographic swerve to North America, L149–157) → smelting mechanics → alloys/arsenic chemistry → the dying metalworker. The smelting "how did they figure it out" hypotheses (L159–161) tip toward lecture. The dying-metalworker close (L179–189) is a strong scene that rescues the movement. **Verdict: rich but technical; the middle (smelting mechanics) is the one place the part risks lecture-drift.**

**Movement 6 — Varna / close (L193–209).** **The rush.** See M1. The most important structural beat in the part (the launch of the "rise") is the most compressed. Varna's gold grave is a scene, but the runway around it is a montage. **Verdict: rushed at exactly the moment it should build.**

The story needs *that* we don't know how, not both hypotheses in full. Recover ~100 words by compressing to a single "maybe near kilns, maybe from malachite — nobody's sure" line.

## 5. SHAPE (biggest first)

**S1 — Rebuild Movement 6 as a felt movement, not a caption.** The part's job is the runway to the first city; right now it sprints the runway. Take the ~200 words recoverable from FOCUS, add M1's new material, and let the two-thousand-year intensification *build* — villages thickening, surplus deepening, the graves diverging — so the series' "rise" launches on a wave the listener felt rise. Highest-leverage structural move in the part.

**S2 — Consolidate three forward-teases to P2 into one clean handoff.** P1 currently promises "wait till next part" three times: the storehouse ("That storehouse is going to build a world. Next time, we watch it happen," L71), the metal/connection horizon ("the world connecting will produce… the most extraordinary thing we're going to spend time with. And, eventually, its end," L189), and Varna/Sumer ("Something extraordinary is about to happen. It's going to be loud," L207–209). The last two are near-identical *gestures* ("something extraordinary is coming") landing within ~20 lines of each other — in audio that reads as a tic, and it dilutes the ending. Keep the storehouse tease (it's earned, protected, and pays a real deposit) and keep "It's going to be loud" as the final button; **soften or absorb L189's "most extraordinary thing" gesture** so the part doesn't cry "extraordinary" twice into the same doorway. (Deposit rule §H4: adjacent teases stay light — three of them isn't light.)

**S3 — Close the proto-urban loop.** The snapshot plants "proto-urban concentrations" (L29) and Çatalhöyük later *is* one (L127) — connect them explicitly. One backward-glancing clause at Çatalhöyük ("this is one of those proto-urban exceptions I told you existed") answers a begged question at zero cost and tightens the part's spine.

**S4 — Relocate the Greece/Knossos beat (L77).** It currently interrupts the rising bad-deal argument (see Pacing M2). It's a recurring-motif flag ("we'll keep glancing back") and can sit *after* the health payoff, or at the movement's seam, without losing its forward function.

**Confirmed NOT to move:** P1 is clean on the assignment's worry — no river-descent, no "why-this-place" silt geography has leaked in from P2. Keep it that way; the light geographic touch M3 asks for is a *placing clause*, not P2's geography movement.

## 8. SIMPLE QUESTIONS (the series MAIN THEME)

**Answered well:**
- "Why would anyone start farming at all?" (L59) — the part's spine; answered across storehouse + Göbekli + ratchet. This *is* the prologue's promised question, delivered. The engine of the series, running.
- "Why did it spread if it made people worse off?" (L87) — answered (populations outcompete even when individuals lose).
- "Who was the first person to *own* something?" (L197) — asked and answered via Varna.
- "Why did they build Göbekli?" — honestly answered "we don't know" (L121) — the *right* answer, dramatized as a gap (Rule 6).
- "How do we know the wild harvest was that rich?" — *shown*, not asserted, via Harlan's timed experiment (L63–67). This is the how-we-know done exactly right, and it's the model M2 should extend to the bones.

**Begged / skipped:**
- **S-a: "Where am I?"** — the opening body is placeless (M3, P1). A child hearing "your feet hurt" wants the *where* first.
- **S-b: "How do you know they were shorter, sicker, died younger?"** — asserted ("the bones tell it," L81) rather than dramatized. Harlan proves the method can be *shown*; the skeletal record is only *claimed*. Half-begged — and the missing half is exactly M2 (the privileged-present beat).
- **S-c: "Wait, there were almost-cities already? Where?"** — the snapshot's "proto-urban concentrations" (L29) raises it and drops it; Çatalhöyük answers it 100 lines later without the connection drawn (S3).

## 9. THESIS (rise, plateau, fall, restart, privileged present)

**P1's assigned job — the runway to the rise — is delivered, and its non-linearity service is outstanding.** Two beats plant the thesis's hardest idea (progress is not a line) better than anywhere else could:
1. The agricultural revolution as "a catastrophe from which we have never recovered" (L95) — the foundational act of civilization was, one body at a time, a *bad deal*. That is the thesis's non-linearity in its purest, most counterintuitive form, and P1 owns it.
2. The Old Copper Complex (L149–157) — a people who had the metal, the water, the latitude, worked metal as early as anyone on Earth, and then *walked away from it*: "Some of the first metalworkers on Earth looked at metallurgy, used it for thousands of years, and set it down." This is a micro-collapse, a micro-non-linearity, a full dress rehearsal for the series' central event — a world that had it and lost it. It prefigures the collapse arc inside the runway part. Superb thesis service; if anything it could be flagged *one degree more* as the pattern we'll watch recur.

**What P1 does NOT do for the thesis, that it should:**
- **The privileged present has no home here (M2)** — the fourth pillar of the thesis, promised in the prologue, first-actionable in P1, unrealized. Biggest thesis gap in the part.
- **The rise doesn't launch with felt force (M1/S1)** — because the on-ramp is a montage, the top of the wave ("It's going to be loud") is *told*, not built. The thesis's "rise" is the payoff P1 exists to set up, and the setup is the part's thinnest section.

Net: P1 over-delivers on non-linearity, correctly establishes the baseline, and under-delivers on both the privileged-present pillar and the felt launch of the rise.
