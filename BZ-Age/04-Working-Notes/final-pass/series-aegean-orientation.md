# Final-Pass Audit — Orientation & the Aegean Thread

**Auditor lens set:** Region-Role Map · Pressure-Based Introductions · The Aegean Thread · The "What Changed" Spine
**Scope:** the ten script bodies (`## The Draft` → `## Revision notes`), read in series order, against the draft-06 External-review intake (lenses 1–4) and the draft-05 register contract.
**Date of pass:** final pre-publication.
**Standing rule observed:** findings are grounded in short quote + location; fixes are specced as jobs, never written in the narrator's voice; ranked by impact on a listener hearing this once, in pieces, over days.

---

## HEADLINE JUDGMENT

Three of the four lenses are already in strong shape and mostly need protecting, not fixing:

- **Role-map:** the P5 cast roster (L66–88) is exactly the orientation instrument the intake asked for — one functional handle per power. The discipline holds across the series. The one real weakness is **Assyria's fragmented handle** and a missing P4→P5 callback.
- **Pressure discipline:** the register sweep clearly hammered the seams; almost everything now enters under a problem, a stake, or a curiosity. The **one sustained survey structure left is the P5 roster itself**, and within it **Egypt's entry buries its handle**.
- **Aegean thread:** one of the best-built arcs in the series — clean plant (P1 Knossos), a consistent "glance back" device, an embodied center (the Pylos scribe), and a decipherment payoff (Ventris). The single biggest missed beat is that **the Minoan labyrinth is never cashed** — the one famous Aegean myth left on the table.
- **Spine:** every part lands its transformation as *felt* **except P4**, which runs three parallel "what changed"s (commerce / the chariot / codified law) without subordinating two to one — the muddiest single-sentence part in the series.

Details, quotes, and specced jobs below, lens by lens.

## LENS 1 — REGION-ROLE MAP

### Player-by-player (first entry quoted, role judged)

**Mesopotamia / Sumer — GOOD.** First functional entry, P2 L36: *"The region whose dominant culture, in its first great period, we call Sumer — a people whose origins remain genuinely mysterious, who spoke a language unrelated to any other we know about."* The role hardens into the series' single most durable handle at P2 L70: *"the temple, strip away the theology for one irreverent second, is a granary with a god on top."* Sumer = the inventor / the ledger-keeper; the "same ledger every time" motif (P5 L150, P4 L82, P2 L194) carries it forward. Consistent and re-established at every return.

**Egypt — GOOD (role), but see Lens 2 for the cast-entry problem.** First entry P2 L276: *"a king named Narmer consolidated two kingdoms into one. His river is the aristocrat of the arrangement… he is a god."* Role hardens at P3 L214: *"Egypt was old. Mesopotamia was inventing."* and at P5 L68: *"And Egypt has gold. More of it than anywhere else in this world, and gold is how Egypt buys its friendships."* The self-remembering depth is dramatized well by Khaemwaset (P6 L32, *"the golden age at its peak, tending the graves of a golden age before it"*). Handle = the old, stable, gold-rich river kingdom that remembers itself. Strong.

**Hatti / Hittites — GOOD, with a slow build.** Seeded at P3 L230 (*"something new is building"*) and characterized at P4 L187 (*"assassinated in a palace conspiracy, which is a very Hittite ending"*). The functional handle lands at P5 L70: *"the Hittite Great Kingdom is the power that keeps Egypt from expanding north."* The mountain-empire role is consistent thereafter (P6/P7). The delay to P5 for the full handle is acceptable because P5 is where they take the stage.

**Mitanni — GOOD; the model.** First entry P5 L72 defines the role by its future function: *"Mitanni is the most forgotten of the great kingdoms. Hold the name in your head — because next time, they turn into the test the whole system grades itself on."* Re-established at P6 L40 with the textbook amnesiac-proof payoff: *"I owe you a name. Last time, going around the table of the great powers, I told you to hold one: Mitanni… Here is why they matter."* This is the plant/payoff shape every other returning player should be measured against.

**Levant / Ugarit — GOOD.** First entry P5 L80: *"**Ugarit.** Not a great power — a node. A cosmopolitan port on the Syrian coast where the long routes came together, six or more languages loose in its streets on an ordinary day."* Handle = the switchboard / crossroads. Re-established well when the warning letter arrives (P7). The broader Levant carries a clean functional role too — P5 L70, *"the Levant — the coastal land bridge between the two empires."*

**Cyprus / Alashiya — GOOD, and correctly amnesiac-proofed.** First entry P5 L82: *"And out in the water, **Cyprus** — Alashiya, in the letters — sitting on the copper and passing everything else along."* Handle = the copper island. The Alashiya=Cyprus equation is re-established every time the ancient name appears (P6 L186, *"a senior official of Alashiya, which is Cyprus"*). Model handling of a name that has two labels.

**Aegean (Minoans / Mycenaeans) — GOOD; see Lens 3 for the arc detail.** Minoans, P5 L58: *"Crete's palace people — the ones who built Knossos with its thirteen hundred rooms… ran the Mediterranean shipping lanes for centuries."* Mycenaeans, P5 L78: *"These are, in a real sense, the Bronze Age Greeks. They are nothing like the Greeks of the classical era."* The intake's exact handle ("the Greeks before the Greeks") is delivered verbatim in spirit. Consistent, embodied (Pylos scribe), paid off (Ventris).

**Babylonia / Kassites — GOOD.** Babylon built across P3 L188 (*"a minor town called Babylon"*) → P4 (Hammurabi) → the cultural-capital role. Kassites, P4 L191: *"they do the thing nobody expects from the people the records called barbarians: they keep it… by becoming more Babylonian than the Babylonians."* Re-established cleanly at P5 L74: *"Remember what I said about them holding the seat by preserving it. The bet paid."* Strong callback.

**Assyria — WEAKEST HANDLE. FLAG (medium-high).** Assyria shape-shifts across three first-entries that are never linked for the listener:
- Prologue: the terrifying *lost* superpower whose capitals Xenophon can't name (*"the capitals of the Assyrian Empire — the superpower of the age before his"*).
- P4 L38: the *merchant city* — *"Assur — the city, not yet the empire… Assur sold competence."*
- P5 L76: the *sleeper* — *"**Assyria.** Under Mitanni's thumb for now, and easy to miss. Which is the mistake — Assyria is the one that outlasts nearly all of them."*

The long arc actually *resolves* beautifully — the prologue's unnamed ruin becomes P9's Ashurbanipal library (P9 L62–72) and closes the Xenophon bookend. But the **P4→P5 handoff has no connective tissue**: a listener is given "Assur sold competence" (merchant city, ~46 min earlier) and then "Assyria… outlasts nearly all of them" (sleeper great power) with nothing linking them as the same place. **Spec:** at the P5 L76 cast entry, add a one-clause backward tag that names the merchant Assur we followed over the passes, so "Assur" and "Assyria" fuse into one continuous player rather than three unlabeled strangers. Job only — do not write the line.

**Elam — thin but acceptable.** Enters functionally (sacks Ur, P3 L192) and recurs (P4 L161 the stele-hauler; P5 buckling), but never gets a role-handle beyond *"the kingdom in what is now southwestern Iran."* Given the name-ration rule, this is a defensible spear-carrier. Low priority; leave.

**The outside world — GOOD.** Indus/Harappa carries the strongest outsider handle, P2 L278: *"The Harappan civilization is the road not taken… Remember that script: at the very end of this series, it's going to be the last thing on the table."* Shang China, P5 L88: *"one Bronze Age cosmopolitan world among at least two"* — the parallel-world reminder, re-hit at P6 (Fu Hao) and P8. Europe/steppe carries a role at P4 L129 (*"the people that the record-keepers regarded as barbarians"* invented the chariot). All consistent.

### Proper-noun density — where names outrun role clarity

**#1 — The Zannanza affair (P6 L54–64). FLAG (high).** In one beat the listener juggles: Suppiluliuma, Tushratta, Ankhesenamun, Tutankhamun, *dahamunzu*, Zannanza, Mursili II — seven names, most of which never recur (Zannanza, Ankhesenamun, Tutankhamun, Mursili). Heard once in the car, the map is lost even though the *shape* survives (widow's letter → prince sent → prince killed → plague). The emotional payload is genuine (Mursili's confession, P6 L64: *"It is so. We have done it."*), so this is not a cut — it is a name-load problem. **Spec:** carry the beat on role-handles and relationships (the widow / the conqueror-king / the son who inherited the plague) and let the proper names drop to the one or two that must be spoken; the *dahamunzu* nugget can stay because it is already framed as "the reason the identification stays arguable" (P6 L221 rationale), which does role-work.

**#2 — The Ur III collapse (P3 L186–204). FLAG (medium).** Even after the documented name-purge (Shulgi→"his son," Ishbi-Erra→"a grain-procurement officer"), the stretch still stacks Ibbi-Sin + the Amorites + the unnamed official + Elam + Ur-Nammu + the Gutians in quick succession. The through-line (*"The empire of Ur did not fall when an enemy army arrived. It fell, functionally, when the grain stopped moving"*, P3 L190) is strong enough to survive, but this is the second-densest name passage. Watch on the read-aloud; consider whether Ur-Nammu (founder, named 20 lines up) needs re-naming at the fall.

**#3 — The P5 cast roster (L66–88).** By design a dense block — seven players back-to-back. It is a *feature* here (the one place density is the point), justified by *"What they're doing together only starts to make sense once you can tell them apart"* (L66). Acceptable, but it is the single highest-density passage in the series; see Lens 2 for the Egypt-entry problem inside it.

## LENS 2 — PRESSURE-BASED INTRODUCTIONS

The register sweep did its job: the overwhelming majority of places, systems, routes, and archives now enter under pressure. A representative sample that PASSES, to show the discipline is real:

- P4 L36 (Old Assyrian network): *"You have probably never heard of the Old Assyrian trade network, and the reason you haven't is that it didn't leave any pyramids. It left paperwork."* — curiosity + stake.
- P4 L119 (the wheel): *"Before the chariot can arrive, we owe the wheel a few honest minutes — because the chariot is the wheel perfected."* — the next beat needs it.
- P3 L212 (Egypt as control case): *"was any of that inevitable?… there's a control case running the same centuries, a thousand miles west… Egypt."* — question-driven.
- P2 L90 (writing): *"The standard story of writing's invention is almost exactly backwards from the evidence, and getting it backwards costs you something important."* — the stake is the error.
- P8 L68 (Israelite highlands): *"That same principle gets a second test a long way from Greece."* — enters as evidence for a claim.
- Every major archive (Amarna via the woman who sold the tablets, P5 L92; Ashurbanipal via "the most powerful man in the world was a collector," P9 L62; Mari via Hammurabi's conquest, P4 L153) enters through a person or a mystery, not a heading.

### The residual survey structures

**#1 — The P5 cast roster (L66–88). Acceptable device, but flag as a conscious decision.** This is the series' one sustained "here is the cast" block. The roster *as a whole* is pressured (*"only starts to make sense once you can tell them apart"*), and most entries land on a functional stake by their final clause (Mitanni = "the test the whole system grades itself on"; Assyria = "the one that outlasts nearly all of them"; Hittites = "keeps Egypt from expanding north"; Ugarit = "not a great power — a node"). Under the strict intake rule ("no place enters survey-style"), this is nonetheless the biggest tension in the series, and it asks a car-listener to hold seven new powers in sequence. **Recommendation:** keep it — a dramatis-personae table is a defensible formal device and it is the payoff of the whole role-map lens — but treat it as a deliberate exception and make sure *every* entry earns a stake on its last breath (see Egypt, next).

**#2 — Egypt's entry inside the roster (P5 L68). FLAG (medium).** This is the one roster entry that opens survey-style — dates and territory first, role-handle last: *"**Egypt.** In its New Kingdom phase — roughly 1550 to 1070 BCE — Egypt is at the height of its imperial power, controlling the Nile Delta, the Levant as far north as the Lebanese coast, and Nubia to the south. Thutmose III pushed Egyptian influence further into Asia than it had ever reached."* The functional handle — *"gold is how Egypt buys its friendships"* — arrives only at the paragraph's end, and a throwaway name (Thutmose III, who does no further work) sits in the front. **Spec:** lead Egypt on its function in the *system* (the gold that lubricates every relationship; the power that buys rather than conquers its peers), the way Mitanni and Ugarit lead on function; demote the New-Kingdom date-range and the Thutmose III name to a subordinate clause or cut. Job only.

**#3 — The P2 camera-pullback survey (L274–282). Acceptable.** *"We've been inside Uruk's walls long enough. Pull the camera back. **Egypt.**… **The Indus Valley.**… **And everywhere else**…"* This is an earned formal device (the explicit thesis-frame *"The Bronze Age world we're going to walk through is not the world. It is a world"* pressures it), and each entry carries a comparison-stake (Egypt solving the same problem differently; Indus the road not taken; the rest a gap of accumulated infrastructure). Leave.

**Net:** the only specced fix under this lens is the Egypt roster-entry reframe. The roster itself is a keep-with-eyes-open.

## LENS 3 — THE AEGEAN THREAD ("Greece before Greece")

### Full inventory of touchpoints

- **P1 L77 (the plant):** farming crosses the Aegean ~7000 BCE; *"One of them is Knossos — yes, that Knossos — four thousand years before anyone builds a palace there… No marble. No temples. No Greek language… That peninsula is going to grow into something you know… we'll keep glancing back at it as we go."* — familiar name, made strange; installs the "glance back" device.
- **P2 L250 (myth-over-memory plant):** *"a civilization — the Mycenaeans — that will eventually have an entire literary tradition, the Trojan cycle, built on top of it without anyone in the later tradition having any clear memory of what the actual civilization was like."*
- **P2 L280:** *"the Greek mainland, where farmers are already living on the sites of Tiryns and Thebes — villages sitting on the ground of future legends."* — one breath, keeps the glance alive.
- **P3:** *no Aegean touchpoint.* (See "arc" note below.)
- **P4 L195:** the shaft graves — *"gold masks on their faces, bronze swords at their sides — and amber beads at their throats… all the way to the Aegean — Mycenae, the citadel a German businessman named Schliemann will tear open thirty-four centuries from now."* — the network reaches Greece; Schliemann teased.
- **P5 L58:** the Minoans fall — Knossos, Linear A, Thera, the slide to the Mycenaeans and Linear B.
- **P5 L78:** the Mycenaeans as the Greeks-before-the-Greeks; *"The lion that Hercules would later kill… the Nemean lion, is the cultural memory of an animal that was actually on the next hillside."* — the model familiar-becomes-strange move.
- **P5 L144–158:** the Pylos administrator — the thread's embodied center; *"the palace the later Greeks will remember — dimly, wrongly — as the home of wise old Nestor."* / *"Remember him. Remember the shelf."*
- **P6 L168–178:** the palaces fortifying — Tiryns' Cyclopean walls, the wall-builders, *"It was not giants."*; Pylos' o-ka tablets and rowers.
- **P7 L48–58, L130, L186:** Pylos burns and its archive is baked into a time capsule; Linear B *"goes into the ground with the last people who could read it"*; the Iliad teased (*"as far from the fall as we stand from the Crusades"*).
- **P8 L30–56, L50, L159–163:** the Greek Dark Age reconsidered; Lefkandi punches a hole in the darkness; the polis grows in the cleared ground.
- **P9 L14–28, L110–116:** Homer's memory-of-a-memory; Ventris proves the Mycenaeans spoke Greek — *"The palace civilization that Homer had dimly remembered and completely distorted was the Bronze Age predecessor of the civilization that produced Homer."*

### Judgment as a deliberate arc — STRONG

The "familiar shoreline that becomes strange" is genuinely executed. The familiar handholds are real and well-chosen (Knossos, Homer, Nestor, the Nemean lion, the Lion Gate); the strangeness lands precisely where the intake wants it — palace Greeks who counted sheep rather than fought like Achilles (P9 L26: *"the men the Iliad turned into swaggering, half-divine heroes were, in the daylight of the tablets, the people who counted the sheep and signed off on the linen"*), a script dying so completely its descendants reinvent writing from the Phoenicians (P8 L30), a whole civilization Homer distorts without knowing it existed (P9 L14: *"Homer did not know what he was remembering"*). The thread has a plant, an embodied center, and a decipherment payoff, and it never crowds out the Near East engine except — arguably, earned — in the finale.

### Gaps in the arc — where a LIGHT touch strengthens it

**#1 — The Minoan labyrinth is never cashed. FLAG (high for this lens; low cost).** The mainland's familiar myths are all paid off — the Nemean lion is decoded as *"cultural memory of an animal that was actually on the next hillside"* (P5 L78); Homer's heroes are decoded against the tablets (P9). But Crete's one universally-known myth — the labyrinth and the Minotaur — is never invoked, even though P1 leans on the listener's own labyrinth-association when it says *"Knossos — yes, that Knossos."* The Minoans get *"painted the bulls and the dolphins"* (P5 L58) and nothing more. This is the single richest unused familiar-handle in the Aegean. **Spec:** at the Minoan paragraph (P5 L58) or the Mycenaean beat (L78), add a one-breath decode of the labyrinth on the exact model of the Nemean-lion line — the maze the later Greeks remembered as the memory of a palace complex, the bull-cult behind the Minotaur — completing the "familiar becomes strange" pattern for Crete the way it is completed for the mainland. **Pays for itself by** compressing the "bulls and dolphins" clause it sits beside; costs the newcomer nothing (the myth is the free association P1 already assumed). Job only — do not write the decode.

**#2 — Thera is currently a chronology-war anecdote, not a felt catastrophe. FLAG (low–medium; hold the limit).** P5 L58: *"pin down the exact year the Thera volcano blew and you've walked straight into a chronology war that has swallowed careers whole. We'll step around the crater."* The eruption itself gets *"one of the biggest eruptions the planet has seen since the last Ice Age."* This is the single most cinematic event in the Aegean Bronze Age, and it is framed mostly around the scholarly fight rather than the event. There is a case for one embodied clause (a town buried in ash, the frescoes frozen) — **but this is exactly where the hard limit bites:** a full Thera set-piece would over-weight the Aegean. **Recommendation:** leave the compression as-is *unless* a single embodied half-clause can be found that costs no more than the Nemean-lion line; otherwise the current restraint is correct.

**#3 — The "we'll keep glancing back" promise runs dry in P3.** P1 promises *"we'll keep glancing back at it as we go,"* and the glances are frequent (P2, P4, P5+) except for a ~50-minute silence across P3 (Akkad/Egypt). This is a minor tension, not a fix — cramming Greece into the Akkad part would violate the engine rule. Note only; leave.

### Where MORE Aegean would be a MISTAKE (protect these)

- **P3 must stay Aegean-free.** The Akkad/empire spine is dense already; a Greece glance here would dilute the engine.
- **Troy must stay a tease, not a thread.** The Schliemann tease (P4 L195) and the "the war, if it happened, happened around the end of the thirteenth century BCE. Right at the edge of the collapse" clause (P9 L18) are the correct dose. A Troy/Hisarlik archaeology thread would turn the anchor into a protagonist.
- **Thera set-piece** — see #2; resist expansion.
- **P9's Aegean weight is earned, not drift.** The Homer cold open plus the Ventris beat make the finale Aegean-heavy, but Linear B's decipherment *is* the payoff of the whole thread and the cleanest illustration of P9's spine ("memory survives by changing form"); it is balanced by the Hittite-rediscovery beat (the Near East's equivalent payoff). Do not trim to "balance" the map — the theme, not the geography, governs here.

## LENS 4 — THE "WHAT CHANGED" SPINE

One-sentence transformation per part, then the felt-judgment and the dilution/residue flags.

**Prologue (frame, not a life-transformation).** *We are living at the one moment in history that can read the vanished world back — a civilization can disappear so completely its own ruins lose their names.* FELT — Xenophon at the unnamed wall carries it. This is the privileged-present vantage the draft-06 thesis wants audited at the bookends; it lands here and is paid off at P9's close.

**P1 — *Humans become tied to stored food; the storehouse invents ownership, sedentism, inequality, and the state-to-come — and it was, one body at a time, a bad deal.*** FELT, strongly. P1 L71: *"the moment a family has a storehouse full of grain, three things have quietly happened to them. They have stopped moving… something that can be measured… something that can be taken."* The bones evidence and *"a catastrophe from which we have never recovered"* dramatize the cost. **Dilution watch (low):** the middle cluster (Göbekli → Çatalhöyük → Jericho → Old Copper/Great Lakes → smelting → Varna) is a lot of sites for one sitting, and the transformation recedes during the Great Lakes detour — but the plant is held alive by the *"you've already smelled the answer once tonight"* pointer (L157). Acceptable.

**P2 — *Strangers learn to live inside systems no one designed; scale itself becomes the thing that has to be managed.*** FELT, strongly. The barge-puller snapshot (L160–172) is built on exactly this: *"The apparatus has to be invented. It is being invented around you, right now."* **Dilution flag (medium):** the sacred-marriage / *hieros gamos* block (L202–214) is the part's longest digression from "strangers inside systems" — it is superb scene color serving throughline #8 (the deep weirdness of statehood), but it is where a listener's grip on the part's transformation loosens most. Not a cut (it's a listener-favorite and genuine); **candidate for compression** if P2 (already the longest part at ~74–82 min) needs to shed weight.

**P3 — *Rule learns to cross the city wall — and discovers, immediately, that it cannot hold.*** FELT. The conquered-villager snapshot is the transformation in a body — P3 L118: *"Empire, in its first form, is most visible from inside as: everything is the same, and the person who takes the grain has changed."* **Research-residue flag (medium):** the 4.2-kiloyear-event passage (L160–162 — Weiss, Tell Leilan, *Science* 1993, *"synchronous dry-farming settlement abandonment"*) is the most journal-ish stretch in the part, delivered in spine tone. It lands on a good honest verdict (*"The dust is there. Something happened. The argument is about what to make of it"*), but the middle reads like a literature summary. **Scene-color note:** the Woolley death-pits + music/games block (L196–198) is non-spine but earns its seat as a ground-level rest-stop; keep.

**P4 — SPINE DIFFUSE. FLAG (highest single-part priority).** The part runs three parallel transformations without subordinating two to one:
1. *The world becomes commercial* — Lamassi, Ea-nasir, credit/interest, smuggling (the cold open and the emotional weight).
2. *The military revolution* — the wheel, Sintashta, the chariot (L119–143).
3. *Codified law* — Hammurabi's stele (L147–181).
The Lamassi frame (opens and closes on her) tries to make commerce the spine, but the chariot and Hammurabi sections are each long enough to read as their own parts, and the closing then reframes everything as *assembly* — L197: *"the world building the apparatus that, in about three hundred years, will be the most sophisticated and interconnected it has ever been."* A car-listener finishes P4 unsure what the single "what changed" was. **This is the muddiest single-sentence part in the series.** **Spec:** pick the governing transformation and demote the other two to service it. The cleanest choice is the *assembly* reading already latent in the closing — "the pieces of the coming cosmopolitan world snap into place" — under which commerce, the chariot, and law are the pieces; that frame should be seeded earlier (near the cold open) so the listener holds one spine through all three blocks rather than discovering the unifier only at the exit. Job only — do not write the seed.

**P5 — *The world becomes interdependent; no kingdom can make what it needs alone, and the system feels permanent to everyone inside it.*** FELT, strongly — this is the thesis-center and it is the best-dramatized spine in the series (Uluburun's impossible manifest; brothers/gold/daughters/complaints; the Pylos scribe; Ugarit at dusk; the iron reveal — *"Iron won because bronze broke"*, L216; *"a structure built on dependencies that run all the way down to the rain"*, L264). **Research-residue flag (medium):** the Akhenaten monotheism historiography (L126–138 — Breasted, *The Dawn of Conscience*; Redford; the henotheism-vs-monotheism debate) is the longest reception-passage delivered in spine tone. It earns a seat because it lands on the fragility spine (L140: *"the accumulated disruptions… are part of what makes the system eventually vulnerable"*), but the middle is a survey of how modern scholars have read Akhenaten more than a beat about interdependence. **Candidate for compression:** keep the political-reform reading and the fragility landing; thin the Breasted/Redford literature tour.

**P6 — *Interdependence becomes fragility — the very connections that made the world strong are what make it unable to survive shocks together.*** FELT, strongly. *"There is a kind of disaster that begins in sunshine"*; the Mitanni false-lesson; the seed-grain jar; Hatti begging Egypt for grain; the Cyprus letter (*"He is using a system to report the beginning of that system's failure"*, L188). **Dilution / name-density flag (see Lens 1 #1):** the Zannanza affair (L54–64) is the part's biggest name-load and its connection to the core climate/economic fragility is thematic (*"The roads that moved the tin moved the plague"*, L66) rather than mechanical. It earns its emotional seat (Mursili's confession) but should be de-named, not cut.

**P7 — *The connected world comes apart in a chain no one inside could see or stop — and the collapse both destroys and frees.*** FELT, strongly, and the two-sidedness (loss AND release, L158–168) is the sophisticated payload delivered well. Discipline is high here. **Residue note (low):** the Millek destruction-audit (L42–44) and the Drews attribution (L118) are historiography, but both are framed as detective work (*"just what did they destroy?"*) and serve the "not a single wave" transformation. Keep.

**P8 — *The end of palaces is both loss and freedom; simpler is not worse, and what grows in the cleared ground needs no palace's blessing.*** FELT, strongly — the grandfather-could-read detonation; *"There is no one to take the grain"* (L98); *"The world has also, for this farmer, gotten freer"* (L113); iron and the alphabet as democratized substitutions. Tightest-on-spine part in the series.

**P9 — *Memory survives by changing form — a civilization can be forgotten, distorted into myth, or preserved unbroken, and we are the first who can read all three back.*** FELT, strongly. The three modes are the explicit structure (Homer = distortion; Hittites = loss-then-recovery; Ashurbanipal = continuity), and the privileged-present vantage closes the prologue's frame. The Domesday / digital-dark-age beat (L124–126) lands the literacy-as-losable throughline on the listener's own world and earns its seat.

### Research residue delivered in spine tone — consolidated

Ranked by how much it dips the "smartest person, three drinks deep" register into "let me summarize the literature":
1. **P5 Akhenaten historiography (Breasted/Redford/monotheism debate, L126–138)** — longest reception passage in spine tone.
2. **P3 4.2ka drought (Weiss/Tell Leilan/*Science* 1993, L160–162)** — most journal-ish stretch in P3.
Both are defensible (both land on honest verdicts) and neither is a cut — they are compression/register targets.

## CROSS-LENS PRIORITIES (ranked by listener impact)

1. **P4's diffuse spine (Lens 4).** Three parallel "what changed"s; the listener finishes unsure what the part was about. Choose the governing transformation (the *assembly* reading is latent in the exit) and seed it early. Highest structural payoff.
2. **The Zannanza name-density (Lens 1 #1 / Lens 4).** Seven names in one P6 beat, heard once. Carry it on role-handles; drop the names that never recur. The beat's emotional shape is worth protecting.
3. **The Minoan labyrinth never cashed (Lens 3 #1).** The one famous Aegean myth left on the table; a one-breath decode on the Nemean-lion model completes Crete's familiar-becomes-strange at near-zero cost.
