# Hard Rule 9 — Anticipated Questions

## What this is

Listeners arrive with questions. A curious listener whose obvious questions go unanswered loses trust. The discipline is not just to answer questions as they arise but to **predict** them — to think like the listener encountering the material for the first time and notice what they would naturally wonder.

## The paradigm example — iron

A listener hearing about the Bronze Age will almost immediately think: but they had iron, right? Why didn't they use it? If the series goes long without addressing this, the curious listener is distracted and the inattentive listener has been allowed to remain ignorant of something genuinely interesting.

The answer is one of the best stories in the period:

- Iron was known throughout the Bronze Age. Meteoric iron — fallen-from-the-sky iron — was being worked into ceremonial objects since the fourth millennium BCE.
- Tutankhamun was buried with an iron dagger made from a meteorite. Recent analysis confirmed the meteoric origin.
- Hittite texts mention iron. Smelted iron from ore was known in scattered places by the Late Bronze Age.
- **Bronze beat iron because bronze was actually a better material** for most purposes given the metallurgy of the time. Cast bronze can be made into complex shapes; early iron was wrought and painstaking. Bronze takes a sharper edge and holds it. Bronze doesn't rust. The "iron is harder" advantage applies only to steel (deliberately carburized iron), and reliable steelmaking is much later.
- The trade network worked, so tin kept arriving. **Path dependency** on a massive scale — established supply chains, established craftsmen, established status meanings.
- **Iron only won when bronze broke.** The collapse around 1200 BCE cut the tin supply. Iron, which can be smelted from ore that exists almost everywhere, became the only option.
- **The Iron Age is born not from technological triumph but from supply chain failure.**

This connects directly to the fragility-of-complexity throughline. It's also a perfect modern parallel for anyone who lived through 2020 supply shocks — the worse option wins because the better one's supply chain has failed.

## Two registers

### Anticipated questions
Things a reasonable listener would naturally wonder.
- Why didn't they have cavalry?
- How did anyone navigate the Mediterranean without compasses?
- Did they have money?
- What language did diplomats use across cultures?
- What did they think had happened during the collapse?
- Could they read each other's writing?

### Lesser-known facts
Things even a moderately informed listener would be surprised by.
- The Tutankhamun meteorite dagger
- The Sumerian beer-drinking straws
- The Old Assyrian merchant women's letters
- The Babylonian king's complaint about the light gold shipment
- The Hittite recognition of Mycenaean Greece as a "brother" great power
- Hammurabi's medical malpractice provisions
- The Ugaritic alphabet predating Phoenician

Both serve the same end: **rewarding curiosity, building trust, making the material feel alive.**

## What this is not

Not trivia for trivia's sake. The facts have to be doing work — illuminating a throughline, grounding an abstraction, supporting a contrarian beat, or connecting to a modern parallel. If a fact is interesting but doesn't carry weight in the larger argument, it's a digression, not content.

## How this serves storytelling

Great storytellers anticipate the audience. They notice when a question is forming in the listener's mind and address it before it becomes a distraction. The discipline of anticipating questions is the discipline of being awake to the listener's experience. The discipline of lesser-known facts is the discipline of rewarding the listener for paying attention.

Both make stories work harder.
