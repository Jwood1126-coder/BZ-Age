# Standing Questions — The Productive Obsessions

Two organizing questions identified during planning. Not theses to defend; productive obsessions the series can be about.

## First — How does complexity die?

The spine question. Not "what caused the Bronze Age Collapse" but the larger version: **what does it mean for a sophisticated, interconnected, literate civilization to come apart, and what does the wreckage teach us about the systems that look invincible until they don't?**

Every part can pull toward this question, even the early parts — because every part is showing the construction of the thing that will eventually break.

## Second — What do we owe the people the records don't see?

The conscience question. Bronze Age sources are written by palace scribes for palace purposes. The series keeps returning to the question of who is **not** in the record and what we can do about it.

Methodological commitment. Moral and analytical stance.

## Why these two together

The first question gives the series its argument. The second gives it its texture. A Carlin-style series needs both.

The structural question without the humane one becomes cold systems-thinking.
The humane question without the structural one becomes sentiment.
Together they hold.

## Relationship to the north star

Note that these are not the same as the north star. The north star is **great storytelling**. These two questions are productive obsessions the series can return to in the service of great storytelling.

Great storytelling that explores these two questions tends to produce the so-different-yet-so-same feeling as an outcome. That's the chain: tell great stories that wrestle with these two questions, and the listener walks away feeling what the series wants them to feel.

Story first. Questions as the obsessions that shape the stories. Insight as the outcome.
