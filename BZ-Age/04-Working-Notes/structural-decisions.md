# Structural Decisions — Reasoning Preserved

Decisions that have been made about structure, with the reasoning that supported them. If a decision is reopened, refer here for what was originally argued.

## Chronological, not thematic

Decided early. The series moves forward through time. Themes braid through chronology; they don't interrupt it. A thematic anthology was considered (one episode on trade, one on warfare, one on writing) and rejected because it would have lost the dramatic arc — and because the chronological structure lets the throughlines build, peak, and pay off in ways an anthology could not.

## Nine parts plus coda — working estimate

The right number is somewhere between seven and eleven, and we won't know until the source map is built out. The nine-plus-coda working estimate is defensible but provisional. Every previous attempt at a long historical podcast series has run aground on overcommitting to a structure that the material would not support. Stay loose.

## The dissolution of "Part 6: View From Below"

Originally, the macro structure included a dedicated Part 6 that would step back from the great-powers narrative to do the throughline work on invisible people, the metallurgical constraint, the agricultural baseline, women, merchants, slaves, migrants. Also the Bronze Age outside the great centers.

This was the right intention executed in the wrong structure. The problem: **a dedicated part ghettoizes the perspective.** It tells the listener that the regular-people view is a special interlude, and that the rest of the time we are back to the kings and the diplomats.

Better solution: the view from below becomes a structural discipline running through every part (Hard Rule 8). The Indus Valley, the European Bronze Age, China — these get braided into Parts 2, 4, and the comparative threads rather than getting their own room.

This decision was load-bearing for the editorial DNA. It is what separates this series from "the Bronze Age as told from palaces, with footnotes about peasants."

## Parts 6 (Crisis) and 7 (Collapse) kept separate

Considered combining into a single "Crisis and Collapse" part. Rejected because the dramatic structure of building tension and the dramatic structure of rupture are different and need to breathe separately. If they are combined, the rupture is less shocking because the listener is already inside the collapse before it begins.

## Parts 2 (First Cities) and 3 (First Empires) kept separate

Considered combining. Rejected because Sargon's empire is a real analytical break, not just a chronological one. The shift from city-state to multi-ethnic empire is one of the most consequential transitions in human political history.

## Coda — undecided

May fold into Part 9. Defer until Part 9 is being developed.
