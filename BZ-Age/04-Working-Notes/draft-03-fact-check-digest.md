## Ugarit "kiln letter" (the famous enemy-ships letter and the Court V "oven tablets"): findspot, authenticity, and honest cold-open framing

VERDICT: The letter text is genuine and quotable, but the dramatic frame is wrong on three counts: the famous "enemy ships" letter is RS 20.238 (not RS 18.147), it was almost certainly not found in a kiln (the "oven" find is now read as tablets fallen from an upper storey), and specialists think it was probably a file copy of a letter actually dispatched — so use the words, retire the kiln-and-undelivered tableau or flag it explicitly as legend.

KEY FACTS:
- The famous letter is RS 20.238 (Ugaritica V no. 24, published by Nougayrol et al. 1968: 87–89), an Akkadian letter from the (unnamed, presumably Ammurapi) king of Ugarit to the king of Alashiya/Cyprus, whom he calls “my father”; Beckman's translation (used by Cline) reads: “My father, now the ships of the enemy have come. They have been setting fire to my cities and have done harm to the land. Doesn’t my father know that all of my infantry and [chariotry] are stationed in Khatte, and that all of my ships are stationed in the land of Lukka?... Now the seven ships of the enemy which have been coming have done harm to us.”
- The RS number in the prompt is a circulating misattribution: RS 18.147 is actually KTU 2.46, a different, Ugaritic-language letter from a foreign royal named Pgn to the king of Ugarit about provision of food and ships (Cunchillos, “The Correspondence of Ugarit,” Handbook of Ugaritic Studies 1999, confirms KTU 2.46 = RS 18.147; Halayqa, UF 42, describes its content); Wikipedia's Ammurapi article pastes the RS 20.238 translation under the label “RS 18.147,” and Cline's endnote 24 notes Bryce 2005: 333 also quotes the letter “with incorrect RS tablet number.”
- RS 20.238 was excavated in 1956 in the House of Rapanu (a scribe/adviser's private archive of 200+ tablets, published 1968), not in the palace kiln; the “oven” story comes from Schaeffer's separate 1954–55 find of 70+ tablets in Court V of the royal palace (the “Southern Archive,” RS 18.x series), reported in Ugaritica IV (1962: 31–37) and popularized by Astour (AJA 1965) and Sandars (The Sea Peoples, 1978/1985: 142–43).
- Specialist reexamination rejected the kiln: per Cline's endnote 29 the key references are Millard 1995: 119, Singer 1999: 705 (with earlier references), van Soldt 1999: 32, Yon 2006: 44, Van De Mieroop 2007: 245, and McGeough 2007/2011; Cline's summary is that the tablet group “was not found in a kiln after all, but rather was probably stored within a basket that had fallen from the second floor,” and in lectures he adds the collapsed basket left “a kiln-shaped lump” that the excavators misread as an oven.
- Cline's 1177 B.C. corrected the kiln story already in the 2014 FIRST edition (pp. 9–10 and 158–59 with endnotes 24–25 and 29: “scholars have now shown that the tablet was not found in a kiln and... was probably a copy of a letter that had been dispatched to Cyprus after all”); Google Books snippets of the 2021 revised edition (pp. 55, 101, etc.) show the same debunking wording retained, so the claim that he only “softened it in the revised edition” is wrong — the popular kiln version predates his book.
- Cline explicitly warns (both editions) that “we probably cannot use the famous letter... either to date the destruction or to identify the destroyers,” and that even if the ships are Sea Peoples they could belong to the earlier wave that hit Egypt in 1207 BC rather than the final attack; his endnote 24 adds that the letter's purpose (an actual plea for help vs. routine report) is itself debated.
- The genuine incoming “warning” letter is RS L 1 (Ugaritica V no. 23): “You have written to me: ‘Ships of the enemy have been seen at sea!’ Well, you must remain firm... Surround your cities with walls. Bring (your) infantry and chariotry into (them). Be on the lookout for the enemy and make yourself very strong!” — sender disputed (king of Alashiya or a Hittite/Carchemish authority), and per van Soldt 1999: 33 n. 40 this tablet was purchased on the antiquities market, so no findspot drama attaches to it; a related letter, RS 20.18 from Eshuwara, senior governor of Alashiya (House of Rapanu), mentions twenty enemy ships.
- Ugarit's violent destruction is dated c. 1190–1185 BCE (possibly slightly later): a letter to Ammurapi from the Egyptian chancellor Bay (RS 86.2230) gives a terminus after c. 1191 BCE since Bay was executed in year 5 of Siptah, and Kaniewski et al. have argued an eclipse tablet (KTU 1.78 = RS 12.061, eclipse of 21 January 1192 BCE) corroborates this; the fire that destroyed the city is what baked and preserved the clay archives.

USABLE FRAMING:
The letters are real, and they are as chilling as advertised: in the city's final years the king of Ugarit wrote to the king of Cyprus that enemy ships had come and burned his cities while his own army was away in Hittite territory and his fleet off in Lukka — "the seven ships of the enemy... have done harm to us" — and another correspondent told Ugarit to wall up its cities and stand firm because enemy ships had been seen at sea. What you should not say is that the warning was found still sitting in the kiln, unsent, on the day the city died: for decades that was the textbook story, but specialists who restudied the find (Millard, Singer, van Soldt, Yon — a correction Eric Cline has carried since the 2014 first edition of 1177 B.C.) concluded the tablets had fallen from an upper-storey archive, the "kiln" was a misread lump of collapsed debris, and the famous letter was probably Ugarit's file copy of a message that actually went out. The honest drama is, if anything, better: the king's plea survives only because the fire that destroyed Ugarit around 1190–1185 BCE baked his archives hard — the catastrophe he feared is the reason we can still read his words. If you want the kiln image, use it as a debunking beat: "the story you may have heard... isn't quite what happened."

CAUTIONS:
- Do not call the famous “enemy ships / cities burned / seven ships” letter RS 18.147 — that label (propagated by Wikipedia and an error in Bryce 2005) belongs to a different tablet (KTU 2.46, the Pgn letter about food and ships); the famous letter is RS 20.238, from the House of Rapanu (1956), not the palace.
- Do not say the letter was found in a kiln being baked, and do not build the episode on it being “undelivered” — both elements are rejected: the kiln was likely a misidentified deposit of tablets fallen from an upper floor, and RS 20.238 is generally read as a draft/archive copy of a letter that was probably dispatched (the original would have gone to Cyprus).
- Do not claim the letter dates to the literal last days of Ugarit or names the Sea Peoples — Cline and others stress it cannot securely date the destruction or identify the attackers, and could even relate to the earlier raiding wave of c. 1207 BCE; the raiders are never named in the Ugarit letters.
- Do not say Cline first softened the kiln story in the 2021 revised edition — the correction is already in the 2014 first edition (and in his public lectures); the revised edition simply retains it.
- Keep the ship counts straight: “seven ships” is RS 20.238; “twenty ships” figures in RS 20.18 (Eshuwara); the “ships seen at sea, be on the lookout” warning is RS L 1, whose provenance is compromised (reportedly bought on the antiquities market), so attach no findspot story to it.
- Hedge authorship and dates: the sender of RS 20.238 is an unnamed “king of Ugarit” presumed to be Ammurapi; destruction is “c. 1190–1185 BCE, possibly a bit later,” not a precise 1185; the 1192 BCE eclipse-tablet corroboration (Kaniewski et al.) is an argument, not settled fact.
- Minor scholarly mess worth knowing: even Cline's own book is inconsistent on the findspot (House of Rapanu in ch. 4 vs. “Southern Archive, Court V” in ch. 5), a relic of the old conflation of the 1954 Court V “oven” group with the 1956 Rapanu letter — a podcast should avoid adjudicating beyond “the tablet was not in a kiln.”

## Uruk c. 3200-3000 BCE: White Temple / Anu Ziggurat (Kullaba) vs. Inanna's Eanna precinct

VERDICT: The correction is right: the White Temple sits atop the Anu Ziggurat in the Kullaba/Anu district, and Inanna's cult home was the separate Eanna precinct — safe to use, with a light hedge that the "Anu" attribution of the White Temple is conventional rather than inscriptionally proven.

KEY FACTS:
- Uruk had two distinct sacred districts roughly 500 meters apart: the Eanna precinct dedicated to Inanna, and the high terrace traditionally called the Anu Ziggurat in the older Kullaba district — the White Temple stood on top of the latter, not in Eanna.
- The White Temple dates to the Late Uruk period (conventionally c. 3500-3000 BCE; art-history sources like Smarthistory give c. 3200-3000 BCE / Uruk III), measured about 17.5 x 22.3 m, had a tripartite plan, and was oriented with its corners to the cardinal points.
- The temple was whitewashed with gypsum plaster inside and out (hence the modern name) and sat on a terrace roughly 13 m (~40 ft) high, making it gleam and be visible across the flat plain even beyond the city walls.
- Access used the typical Mesopotamian 'bent-axis' approach: none of its three doors faced the ramp directly, and a worshipper had to turn 90 degrees inside to face the altar.
- Excavators found a foundation deposit of leopard and lion bones in the temple's eastern corner, and about 19 gypsum tablets with cylinder-seal impressions (temple accounting) on its floor.
- Eanna means 'House of Heaven' (Sumerian e2-anna); the precinct was dedicated to Inanna from the earliest Uruk period onward and was a walled complex of multiple monumental buildings (e.g., Temple D at ~2,596 m2), unlike the single high terrace of the Anu district.
- Eanna's buildings were decorated with cone mosaics: tens of thousands of ~10 cm baked-clay or gypsum cones with colored heads (red, white, black) pressed into mud plaster, forming zigzag and lozenge patterns on walls and massive columns.
- Eanna Level IV (c. 3300-3100 BCE) yielded thousands of proto-cuneiform tablets — the earliest known writing — recording administration such as beer rations and livestock counts.
- The Warka Vase, the famous alabaster vessel showing a procession of offerings culminating before Inanna (marked by her reed-bundle gateposts), was found by German excavators in 1933/34 in the Eanna precinct.

USABLE FRAMING:
Uruk's skyline had two religious hearts, about a third of a mile apart. One was the Eanna precinct — the 'House of Heaven' — a walled compound of monumental buildings belonging to the goddess Inanna, its columns and walls studded with tens of thousands of little colored clay cones forming glittering mosaic patterns, and its rubbish layers yielding the oldest written tablets on Earth. The other was an older, man-made mountain of a terrace in the Kullaba district, later associated with the sky god Anu, and on its summit stood the so-called White Temple — plastered in gypsum so it caught the sun and could be seen far across the plain. We should be honest that no Uruk-period inscription tells us which god owned the White Temple; the 'Anu' label is a convention, projected back from the fact that Anu's great sanctuary stood on that spot thousands of years later. But one thing the evidence makes clear: Inanna's house was Eanna — the White Temple was never hers.

CAUTIONS:
- Do not claim the White Temple is inscriptionally or definitively 'Anu's temple': the attribution is traditional/conventional, retrojected from Anu's later (1st-millennium/Seleucid-era) sanctuary on the same spot; Wikipedia's Anu ziggurat article states flatly that 'there is no evidence that Anu was actually worshipped in this structure' in the Uruk period.
- Avoid calling the Anu Ziggurat a true ziggurat without qualification — it is a high terrace ('proto-ziggurat'); the canonical stepped ziggurats begin with Ur-Nammu around 2100 BCE.
- Heights vary by source: ~13 m (40 ft) for the terrace is the safe figure; Wikipedia's 21 m and its 'world's tallest building until 2667 BC' claim are not well sourced — skip them.
- Dates are conventional, not precise: sources range from c. 3500-3000 BCE (Wikipedia) to c. 3200-3000 BCE (Smarthistory/AP Art History); say 'late fourth millennium BCE' to be safe.
- The story that Uruk formed from two merging villages (Unug/Eanna and Kullaba/Anu) is a scholarly reconstruction, not an established fact — hedge if used.
- Don't credit a specific named excavator for the Warka Vase without further checking (one source names Herzfeld, but the Warka digs of 1933/34 were the German Archaeological Institute expedition under Nöldeke); 'German excavators in 1933/34' is safe.
- The 'sacred marriage of Inanna and Dumuzi' reading of the Warka Vase top register is interpretation, not fact — attribute it as a common scholarly interpretation.
- Figures like 'over 5,000 proto-cuneiform tablets from Eanna' vary by source and counting method; 'thousands of tablets' is the safe phrasing.

## Kültepe/Kanesh tablet count, excavation leadership (Tahsin Özgüç and successors), and the role of Mogens Trolle Larsen

VERDICT: Safe to use in the draft's form: "more than 23,000 tablets" is well supported, the long-running excavation was Turkish-led (Özgüç 1948-2005, then Kulakoğlu), and Larsen is a Danish Assyriologist who studied and synthesized the tablets (Ancient Kanesh, 2015) but never directed the dig.

KEY FACTS:
- The corpus of Old Assyrian cuneiform tablets from Kültepe/Kanesh is routinely cited as 'more than 23,000'; Cambridge University Press's description of Larsen's book uses 'more than 23,000 elaborate clay tablets,' Wikipedia gives 'over 22,000' in one passage and '23,500' in another, and 2017 news reports on the dig cited roughly 23,500 with new tablets still emerging each season.
- Systematic excavations at Kültepe began in 1948 under the Turkish archaeologist Tahsin Özgüç (1916-2005) of Ankara University, who directed the dig for some 57 consecutive seasons until his death in 2005; his Turkish colleagues included his wife Nimet Özgüç and Kutlu Emre.
- Since 2006 the excavation has been directed by another Turkish archaeologist, Professor Fikri Kulakoğlu of Ankara University, so the site has been continuously Turkish-led for over 75 years.
- Before the Turkish excavations, the Czech scholar Bedřich Hrozný dug at the karum in 1925 and recovered about 1,000 tablets, and several thousand 'Cappadocian tablets' had already reached museums via the antiquities market from the 1880s onward.
- Mogens Trolle Larsen (b. 20 May 1937) is a Danish Assyriologist, emeritus professor at the University of Copenhagen and member of the Royal Danish Academy of Sciences, whose career has centered on reading and interpreting the Old Assyrian texts; his major works include The Old Assyrian City-State and its Colonies (1976) and Ancient Kanesh: A Merchant Colony in Bronze Age Anatolia (Cambridge University Press, 2015).
- Larsen was a philologist/historian working on the tablets, not an excavator; the digging was done by the Turkish teams, while Larsen and the international Old Assyrian text-publication community edited and analyzed the archives.
- The Old Assyrian trading-colony (karum) period at Kanesh spans two main archaeological levels: Level II, conventionally c. 1950-1836 BCE (Veenhof's middle-chronology version: 1974-1836), and Level Ib, c. 1798-1740 BCE (some reconstructions extend to c. 1719/1700), so the draft's 'c. 1950-1750 BCE' is a fair round approximation.
- The tablets are overwhelmingly commercial records (letters, contracts, accounts) from the private archives of Assyrian merchant houses in the lower-town karum, making Kanesh the best-documented commercial community of the Bronze Age.

USABLE FRAMING:
More than 23,000 cuneiform tablets — by some counts closer to 23,500, and the number still grows — have come out of the merchant quarter at Kültepe, ancient Kanesh, making it the richest single window we have onto Bronze Age commerce. The site has been dug by Turkish archaeologists for over seventy-five years: Tahsin Özgüç of Ankara University led the excavation almost every season from 1948 until his death in 2005, and his successor Fikri Kulakoğlu directs it today. Reading those tablets has been a separate, international scholarly enterprise, and one of its central figures is the Danish Assyriologist Mogens Trolle Larsen of the University of Copenhagen, who spent his career studying the merchant archives and distilled them for general readers in his 2015 book Ancient Kanesh. So the honest attribution is: the Turks dug the tablets up; scholars like Larsen taught us what they say. The colony itself flourished roughly between 1950 and 1750 BCE — the twentieth to eighteenth centuries — with a documented gap when the karum burned around 1836 BCE before being rebuilt.

CAUTIONS:
- Do not say Larsen excavated, dug, discovered, or unearthed the tablets — he is a philologist/historian who studied and published them; excavation credit belongs to the Turkish teams (Özgüç, then Kulakoğlu).
- Do not say the tablet count is an exact figure; sources range from 'over 22,000' to '23,500+' and the number rises with each season — 'more than 23,000' is safe, 'exactly 23,000' or a precise total is not.
- Do not say ALL tablets came from Turkish excavations: a few thousand reached museums via the antiquities market before 1925, and Bedřich Hrozný (a Czech, not a Turk) recovered about 1,000 in his 1925 season — the Turkish digs account for the large majority, not the entirety.
- Do not present 'c. 1950-1750 BCE' as exact: the absolute chronology is debated (Level II is c. 1950-1836 or 1974-1836 BCE depending on the system; Level Ib ends anywhere from c. 1740 to c. 1700), so hedge with 'roughly' or 'about the 20th-18th centuries BCE'.
- Do not call Tahsin Özgüç the 'discoverer' of Kanesh or its tablets — the site was known and tablets circulated decades before 1948; he led the systematic, long-term excavation.
- Avoid implying Larsen worked alone: tablet decipherment and publication is a large international effort (e.g., Klaas Veenhof, Cécile Michel, Gojko Barjamovic, the Old Assyrian Archives series); Larsen's Ancient Kanesh is a leading synthesis, not the sole scholarship.

## The fall of Mitanni: Hittite reduction under Suppiluliuma I, the Shattiwaza vassal treaty, Assyrian absorption of Hanigalbat, and Mitanni's erasure a century-plus before the c. 1200-1177 BCE collapse

VERDICT: The core framing is accurate and safe to use — Mitanni really was a charter member of the Amarna "great powers club" that was knocked out of great-power status by Suppiluliuma I (c. 1340s-1330s), kept as a Hittite buffer vassal via the Shattiwaza treaty, then swallowed by Assyria in stages (Adad-nirari I, then Shalmaneser I c. 1260s-1250s) while the international system demonstrably kept running — just phrase the time gap carefully (great-power Mitanni gone ~130-150 years before 1177; even the rump erased ~75-100 years before).

KEY FACTS:
- Mitanni was one of the original Amarna-era great powers: thirteen letters from King Tushratta (EA 17-29) survive in the Amarna archive, addressed to Amenhotep III, Akhenaten, and Queen Tiye using the fraternal 'my brother' protocol reserved for equal Great Kings, and EA 24 (the 'Mittani Letter') is the longest known text in the Hurrian language.
- Two Mitanni princesses married into the Egyptian royal house: Gilukhepa, daughter of Shuttarna II, arrived in Amenhotep III's regnal year 10 (c. 1378-1376 BCE) with 317 ladies of her household, an event Amenhotep commemorated on a special issue of large scarabs (examples in the British Museum); Tushratta's daughter Tadukhepa followed around year 36 and passed into Akhenaten's household after Amenhotep's death.
- In Amarna letter EA 23, Tushratta sent the cult statue of Shaushka/Ishtar of Nineveh to Egypt around Amenhotep III's year 36 — explicitly as a loan, with Tushratta quoting the goddess saying she wished to go to Egypt and return, and asking the pharaoh to send her back; whether this was to heal the ailing pharaoh (the traditional reading) or to bless the Tadukhepa wedding (Moran's argument) is debated.
- Tushratta's letters are the source of the famous line that in Egypt 'gold is as plentiful as dirt' (EA 19, the 'Love and Gold' letter) — a vivid, fully verified quote.
- The Hittite king Suppiluliuma I (r. c. 1344-1322 BCE, dates debated) sacked the Mitanni capital Washukkanni in his great Syrian campaign(s) around the 1340s-1330s BCE and stripped Mitanni of everything west of the Euphrates (Aleppo, Alalakh, Nuhashshe, eventually Carchemish), ending its great-power status.
- According to the preamble of the Suppiluliuma-Shattiwaza treaty (CTH 51/52, c. 1330s BCE), Tushratta was assassinated in a conspiracy led by one of his own sons; the killer is not named in the text.
- Tushratta's son Shattiwaza fled (after the usurper Shuttarna III tried to kill him), took refuge with Suppiluliuma, married the Hittite king's daughter, and was installed by a Hittite army under prince Piyassili of Carchemish as vassal king over a rump Mitanni east of the Euphrates — deliberately maintained as a buffer against rising Assyria.
- Assyria's rise was the direct flip side of Mitanni's fall: Ashur-uballit I (r. c. 1363-1328 BCE), whose kingdom had been under Mitannian domination, wrote to Akhenaten as a 'Great King' (EA 15-16), prompting Burna-Buriash II of Babylon to protest to Egypt that the Assyrians were 'my vassals' with no right to negotiate.
- Adad-nirari I of Assyria (r. c. 1305-1274 or c. 1295-1264 BCE, depending on chronology) defeated Shattuara I of Hanigalbat and made him a tributary, then crushed the revolt of his son Wasashatta (whose Hittite allies 'took his money but did not help,' per the Assyrian inscription), capturing Taidu and Washukkanni and boasting that he destroyed the city of Irridu and sowed salt (kudimmu) over it.
- Shalmaneser I (r. c. 1273-1244 or c. 1263-1234 BCE) defeated Shattuara II and his Hittite and Ahlamu allies around the 1260s-1250s BCE, claimed in his inscriptions to have blinded 14,400 captives in one eye, and annexed Hanigalbat outright as Assyrian provincial territory, installing his own officials — later Assyrian grand viziers even bore the title 'king of Hanigalbat.'
- The Late Bronze Age international system demonstrably kept functioning after Mitanni's removal: the Battle of Kadesh (1274 BCE), the Egyptian-Hittite peace treaty (c. 1259 BCE), and Ramesses II's marriage to a Hittite princess (c. 1246 BCE) all postdate Mitanni's reduction, and the general collapse came only c. 1200-1177 BCE.
- Hanigalbat is simply the Assyrian/Akkadian name for the Mitanni realm (Egyptians called it Naharin, Hittites also 'Hurri-land'), not a new successor entity; and the capital Washukkanni's site is still not certainly identified (Tell Fekheriye near Tell Halaf is the leading candidate).

USABLE FRAMING:
In the Amarna letters of the mid-14th century BCE, Mitanni is unambiguously one of the handful of 'Great Kings' — Tushratta calls the pharaoh 'brother,' sends him a daughter in marriage, lends him the statue of Ishtar of Nineveh, and famously begs for gold 'as plentiful as dirt' in Egypt. Within a generation, Suppiluliuma I of Hatti sacked the capital, Tushratta was murdered in a palace conspiracy led by one of his own sons, and the Hittites installed the refugee prince Shattiwaza as a vassal ruling a deliberately preserved rump state — a buffer against the rising power that Mitanni itself had once dominated: Assyria. Over the following decades Adad-nirari I and then Shalmaneser I dismantled that rump piece by piece, and by roughly the 1250s BCE the old Mitanni heartland was simply Assyrian provincial territory run by Assyrian officials. So a founding member of the great powers club was wiped off the map while the club kept meeting: the Battle of Kadesh, the Egyptian-Hittite peace treaty of c. 1259, and Ramesses II's Hittite marriage all happened over or around the corpse of Mitanni, and the general collapse of c. 1200-1177 was still two or three generations away. Put precisely: Mitanni ceased to be a great power about 130-150 years before 1177, and even its vassal remnant was erased some 75-100 years before the system itself went down — proof that the Bronze Age order could absorb the death of a member state long before it could not absorb everything at once.

CAUTIONS:
- Do not give single hard dates: every date here is approximate and chronology-dependent; Assyrian regnal dates differ by about 10 years between schemes (Adad-nirari I c. 1305-1274 vs c. 1295-1264; Shalmaneser I c. 1273-1244 vs c. 1263-1234), Hittite dates by up to 20 (Suppiluliuma I c. 1344-1322 is the common convention but contested), and one website's 'January 1273 BC' precision for the conquest of Hanigalbat is not reliable.
- Do not name Tushratta's murderer: the Shattiwaza treaty says only that a son led the conspiracy; some popular sources guess Shuttarna III, but Shuttarna III is usually identified as the son of the rival claimant Artatama II, so naming the killer would be invention.
- Do not state as fact that the Ishtar of Nineveh statue was sent to heal the dying Amenhotep III: that is the traditional reading, but Moran and others argue EA 23 ties the statue's visit to blessing the Tadukhepa marriage; safest phrasing is the verified loan itself ('with the request that she be sent back') plus a hedged 'perhaps to heal the ailing king, perhaps to bless the wedding.'
- The brutal numbers are royal propaganda, not statistics: Shalmaneser I's 14,400 blinded-in-one-eye captives and Adad-nirari I's salt-sown ruins of Irridu come from the kings' own inscriptions — say 'he boasted/claimed,' not 'he did.'
- Do not say Mitanni was 'destroyed' in the 1340s: Suppiluliuma reduced it to a vassal buffer (its Shattiwaza-line kings continued under Hittite protection); the actual erasure as a polity came only with Shalmaneser I's annexation c. 1260s-1250s — collapsing the two stages is the most common popular error.
- 'Erased roughly a century before the collapse' needs care: final annexation to 1177 is ~75-90 years (to c. 1200, only ~50-60), while loss of great-power status is ~130-150 years before — say 'well over a century before the collapse, Mitanni had ceased to be a great power; decades before it, even the rump was gone,' rather than one round number.
- The 'Great Powers Club' / 'brotherhood of kings' is a modern scholarly metaphor for the Amarna system — fine to use, but attribute it as the scholars' nickname, not an ancient term.
- Minor uncertainties not to overstate: the relationship between Shattuara I and Shattuara II is unclear (possibly unrelated dynasts); Washukkanni's location (probably Tell Fekheriye) remains unconfirmed; and the exact sequence/length of Suppiluliuma's Syrian wars (one-year vs multi-year campaigns) is debated.

## Old Copper Complex of the Great Lakes: dates, technique, and the abandonment of utilitarian copper tools

VERDICT: Safe to use with one correction: say "among the world's earliest metalworkers" and "millennia before Old World smelting" — but NOT "earlier than Old World copper working," since Anatolian native-copper working (Çayönü, ~10,000 BP) is as old or older; the decline-to-ornament story and the multi-causal explanations are well supported.

KEY FACTS:
- The oldest directly dated Old Copper Complex artifact is a copper conical projectile point from Vilas County, Wisconsin, with embedded wood radiocarbon-dated to 7690 +/- 40 BP, about 8,500 calibrated years ago (Pompeani et al. 2021, Radiocarbon, reanalyzing 53 dates).
- Pompeani's combination of artifact dates and lake-sediment evidence indicates copper working in the region emerged at least ~9,500 years ago and peaked roughly 7,000-5,000 years ago — but the ~9,500 BP start is inferred from sediment pollution proxies (elevated lead/copper 9,500-5,000 cal BP), not from a directly dated artifact.
- A separate Bayesian model of 76 radiocarbon dates (Bebber, Buchanan & Holland-Lulewicz 2022, PLoS One) puts the Old Copper tradition's start at 7520-6180 cal BP and its end at 4840-4190 cal BP, with copper use possibly originating as early as 9,000 cal BP and usage peaks around 5,500 and 3,300 cal BP — so the chronology is actively debated within a few-thousand-year band.
- Technique is consensus: they cold- and hot-hammered and annealed nearly pure native Lake Superior copper (Keweenaw Peninsula, Isle Royale, and glacial float copper); there is no evidence of smelting, casting, or ore reduction.
- Sediment geochemistry from McCargoe Cove shows copper mining on Isle Royale roughly 6,500-5,400 years ago, with the signal dropping off after ~5,400 BP (Pompeani et al. 2015, The Holocene).
- They made utilitarian tools — projectile points, knives, axes/celts, awls, fishhooks, harpoons — and by roughly 5,000-3,000 BP (timing contested between researchers) heavy utilitarian copper tools were largely abandoned in favor of stone and bone, while copper shifted to beads, bracelets, and mortuary/status objects in Late Archaic traditions like Red Ocher.
- Controlled experiments (Bebber et al. 2019, Scientific Reports) found replica stone flake knives were initially sharper, cutting with less force and energy than copper blades, while copper was more durable; combined with copper's much higher production cost, stone was arguably the rational choice — the authors call the abandonment 'exceptional' because the global stone-to-metal transition was otherwise essentially one-directional.
- Leading explanations for the decline are multi-causal and not mutually exclusive: a sustained dry period around ~5,000 years ago seen in sediment cores and other proxies (Pompeani's climate hypothesis), population growth and rising social complexity that redirected copper to ornament and display, and the functional/economic parity or superiority of stone for everyday cutting tasks.
- Old World comparison: the earliest Old World copper objects are worked native-copper beads, hooks, and awls from Cayonu Tepesi, southeast Turkey, ~8200-7500 BCE (~10,200-9,500 BP), so the Great Lakes tradition is roughly contemporary with the earliest Old World copper working — but it predates the earliest Old World copper smelting (~5000 BCE, e.g., Belovode, Serbia) by several millennia, and Bebber et al. argue it is the earliest sustained production of utilitarian copper TOOLS anywhere.
- These were mobile hunter-gatherer-fisher societies of the western Great Lakes (especially Wisconsin and Michigan's Upper Peninsula), with no agriculture, cities, or state structures at the time — the 'copper before cities' framing is accurate.

USABLE FRAMING:
Around eight and a half thousand years ago — by the most secure direct date we have — hunter-gatherers near the Great Lakes were hammering and annealing nuggets of nearly pure native copper into spearpoints, knives, and axes, and indirect evidence from lake sediments suggests the tradition may reach back nine and a half thousand years. That makes them among the very first metalworkers on Earth, working copper at roughly the same time as the earliest experiments in Anatolia, and millennia before anyone anywhere learned to smelt ore. They never smelted — this was cold metal, shaped by hammer and fire — and they never built cities around it. And then, sometime in the window between about five and three thousand years ago, they largely set the metal down: everyday copper tools faded out in favor of stone and bone, while copper itself became something else — beads, bracelets, objects for burials and status. Why is still argued: a long dry spell shows up in the sediment record around the time the decline begins, society was growing more complex and copper more symbolic, and modern experiments suggest a freshly knapped stone flake was actually sharper and far cheaper than a copper blade. Whatever the mix, archaeologists call it one of the only documented cases in human history where a people adopted metal tools — and then, for good practical reasons of their own, walked away from them.

CAUTIONS:
- Do NOT claim they were 'the world's first metalworkers' or 'earlier than the Old World': worked native-copper items from Cayonu Tepesi (Turkey) date to ~8200-7500 BCE (~10,200-9,500 BP), as old as or older than any Great Lakes date; 'among the earliest' is the defensible phrasing, and 'earliest utilitarian copper tool industry' is a specific claim by Bebber et al., not universal consensus.
- Do NOT present ~9,500 BP as a hard artifact date: the oldest directly dated artifact is ~8,500 cal BP; the earlier figure rests on lake-sediment lead/copper anomalies whose attribution to mining is an interpretation (supported, but indirect).
- Do NOT pin a single end date: Pompeani's team has the classic Old Copper Complex fading by ~5,400-5,000 BP, the 2022 Bayesian model ends it 4840-4190 cal BP, and the older literature (and Bebber 2019) keep utilitarian copper going until ~3,000 BP — present the decline as a window, c. 5,000-3,000 BP.
- Do NOT say they 'abandoned copper' outright: they abandoned heavy utilitarian copper tools; copper work continued for ornaments and small items (awls, beads, fishhooks linger), and later traditions (Red Ocher, eventually Hopewell) used copper extensively for prestige goods.
- Do NOT attribute the decline to a single cause: climate/drought, social complexity and population growth, and stone's functional-economic parity are all live hypotheses, generally presented as acting in combination; the drought link is a correlation in proxies, not a demonstrated mechanism.
- Do NOT call it a 'collapse' or imply technological failure: researchers frame it as a rational, deliberate shift ('technomic devolution' is Binford's term, used descriptively); the Bebber experiments tested knives/cutting blades specifically, so avoid generalizing 'stone was better' to every tool class.
- Avoid precise artifact counts (e.g., '20,000 artifacts') unless separately sourced; scale claims like 'thousands of copper artifacts' are safe.

## Maize intensification and the rise of Cahokia (North America's first city), and the honest version of the "larger than London" comparison

VERDICT: Largely safe to use as a striking correlation, but it must be framed as correlation-plus-plausible-enabler, NOT proven sole causation, and it needs several hedges (monumentality predates maize; Cahokia is in the Mississippi floodplain, not the Great Lakes; population/London numbers are estimate-ranges, not facts).

KEY FACTS:
- Maize is present in the Eastern Woodlands as a minor crop well before it becomes a staple: it appears in small amounts in the Middle Woodland (roughly 200 BCE-CE 500) and Late Woodland, but the timing and scale of early presence is genuinely debated, with recent direct AMS dating discrediting many older 'early maize' claims.
- Stable-carbon-isotope evidence shows maize became a significant dietary staple across much of the Eastern Woodlands around CE 900-1000; at Cahokia specifically, maize was introduced abruptly around CE 900 and its dietary share rose rapidly thereafter.
- Cahokia's 'big bang' (a term associated with archaeologist Timothy Pauketat) is dated to c. CE 1050: in the Lohmann phase (c. 1050-1100) the American Bottom population jumped from roughly 1,400-2,800 to roughly 10,200-15,300 people.
- Peak population estimates for greater Cahokia span a wide, contested range of roughly 6,000-40,000; most scholars cluster the central settlement around 10,000-20,000 at its zenith (c. 1100-1200).
- London's population around CE 1100 is estimated at roughly 15,000-18,000 (an oft-cited 1070s figure is ~18,000); London did not clearly pull ahead until its growth toward ~80,000 by c. 1300, so the 'larger than London' line works best pegged to c. 1100 and to a date specific.
- Spreading maize from its tropical Mexican origin to higher, colder latitudes required selection of short-season, cold-tolerant varieties (the Northern Flint race), an adaptation that mattered most for maize's later push into the upper Midwest, Great Lakes, and Northeast rather than for Cahokia's more southerly American Bottom location.
- Poverty Point, Louisiana (c. 1700-1100 BCE) was a massive monumental earthwork complex built by hunter-gatherers with no maize agriculture, and earlier Watson Brake (c. 3500 BCE) and later Hopewell earthworks show that large-scale monument-building in the East long predated a storable staple crop.

USABLE FRAMING:
For thousands of years the Eastern Woodlands had everything except a storable staple grain: rivers, the Great Lakes copper sources, fertile floodplains, and people capable of monumental engineering. Maize was around for centuries as a bit player, but only around CE 900-1000 does the chemistry of human bones show it becoming a true dietary staple across the region. And then, within roughly a century - about CE 1050 - the American Bottom near modern St. Louis explodes in what archaeologists literally call Cahokia's 'big bang,' from a few thousand people to well over ten thousand, the largest concentrated settlement north of Mexico and, by most reckonings, comparable to or larger than London around 1100 CE, which held perhaps fifteen to eighteen thousand. That timing is real and striking. But we should be honest that it's a correlation with a very plausible mechanism - a storable surplus enabling dense, sustained populations - not a proven single cause; religion, politics, and a favorable climate all played their part, and people in the East had been building monuments without corn for two thousand years before this.

CAUTIONS:
- Do not claim maize 'caused' Cahokia outright. The maize-then-city sequence is a strong, widely argued correlation and maize was likely a necessary enabler of dense population, but causation is interpreted, not demonstrated; religious/political dynamics, the rich American Bottom floodplain, and Medieval-Warm-Period climate are also invoked.
- Do not imply monumentality required maize: Poverty Point (c. 1700-1100 BCE, hunter-gatherers, no maize), the earlier Watson Brake, and Hopewell earthworks prove large-scale construction long predated a staple crop. What is genuinely new at Cahokia is sustained URBAN scale and density, not monument-building itself.
- Do not place Cahokia in the Great Lakes. Cahokia sits in the central Mississippi valley (American Bottom, near present St. Louis); the Great Lakes copper (Old Copper Complex) is a different region. Avoid blending 'Great Lakes copper' and 'Cahokia' into one place.
- Treat population figures as contested estimate-ranges, not facts. Peak Cahokia estimates run ~6,000-40,000; 10,000-20,000 is the common central range. State it as a range.
- The 'larger than London' line is sensitive to the date and the estimate chosen. It holds for c. 1100 (London ~15,000-18,000) but NOT for London's c. 1300 peak (~80,000); say 'around 1100' and acknowledge both numbers are estimates.
- Do not say the earliest maize date is settled - early (pre-CE 900) presence and its scale are debated, and several older 'early maize' claims have been overturned by direct dating.
- Keep the northern-latitude cold-adaptation point at high level and don't tie it to Cahokia itself; the short-growing-season adaptation (Northern Flint) was decisive for maize's later spread to the upper Midwest/Northeast, not for the more southerly Cahokia.
- Qualify 'first city' as 'first city north of Mexico' / 'in what is now the United States' to avoid ignoring Mesoamerican cities; and note a minority still debate whether Cahokia meets a strict 'city' definition.

## Jack Harlan's 1967 wild einkorn harvesting experiment at Karacadağ, southeastern Turkey

VERDICT: Mostly safe to use, with one important correction: the ~2 kg/hr (hand) and ~2.5 kg/hr (flint sickle) figures are the commonly cited rates for harvested grain-heads/spikes, not clean grain — after threshing (~46% grain) the yield was about 1 kg of clean grain per hour. The citation, the three-week conclusion, and the "abundance not desperation" framing are well attested, but the abundance interpretation is Harlan's inference, not a settled explanation of why farming began.

KEY FACTS:
- Citation confirmed: Jack R. Harlan, 'A Wild Wheat Harvest in Turkey,' Archaeology, vol. 20, no. 3 (June 1967), pp. 197-201.
- The experiment took place on the slopes of Karacadağ (Karaca Dağ), a volcanic massif in Diyarbakır province, southeastern Turkey, in dense natural stands of wild einkorn wheat.
- Reported harvesting rates: roughly 2.0 kg/hr by hand-stripping the ripe heads and closer to 2.5 kg/hr using a sickle of flint blades set in a wooden handle (commonly given as a 2.05-2.45 kg/hr range).
- About 46% of the harvested material was actual grain, so the clean-grain yield was roughly 1 kg per hour; the National Academies biographical memoir of Harlan states he gathered 'more than 2 pounds (about 0.9 kg) of clean wild einkorn grain per hour' with a stone-blade sickle.
- Harlan's published conclusion: a family harvesting for about three weeks (moving upslope as ripening progressed) could gather more grain than it could possibly consume in a year, without working very hard.
- The experiment is widely cited as evidence that wild cereal stands were abundant enough that scarcity or starvation need not have forced the transition to agriculture in this region.

USABLE FRAMING:
In 1967 the botanist Jack Harlan published a small experiment with outsized implications. On the slopes of Karacadağ in southeastern Turkey he waded into wild stands of einkorn wheat and harvested it the old way — first stripping the ripe heads by hand, then with a sickle of razor-sharp flint blades. He found he could gather grain-heads at roughly two to two-and-a-half kilograms an hour, and once that was threshed it came to something like a kilogram — a couple of pounds — of clean grain per hour. From this he reckoned that a family, following the ripening crop up the mountain over about three weeks, could store more wheat than it could eat in a year, without working especially hard. The lesson many have drawn is that the first farmers in this region were surrounded by abundance rather than driven by hunger — though exactly why people took up farming remains debated.

CAUTIONS:
- Do not call the 2 / 2.5 kg-per-hour figures 'clean grain.' Sources consistently treat these as gross harvested heads; the threshed, clean grain was roughly half that (about 1 kg/hr, or 'more than 2 lb/hr' in the biographical memoir). Conflating the two overstates the yield.
- The 'enough in three weeks to last a family a year' line is Harlan's own extrapolation from short solo trials, not a measured, multi-family or multi-season study — present it as his estimate, not a measured fact.
- Do not claim the experiment proves that abundance caused agriculture. It shows wild stands could be highly productive; the causes of farming's origins (climate change/Younger Dryas, population pressure, social and ritual factors) are still actively debated.
- I could not access the full text of the original 1967 paper, so Harlan's exact verbatim numbers and his precise labeling of each method could not be confirmed; secondary sources vary slightly (one gives pp. 199-201 rather than the standard 197-201, and some blur which rate was hand vs sickle).
- Wild stand density varies by location and year; do not generalize Karacadağ's yields to all regions or to the entire Neolithic.
- Keep the plant-science light: the harvest experiment itself does not establish Karacadağ as the place einkorn was domesticated (that is a separate genetic argument by Heun and colleagues), so avoid merging the two claims.

## The complaint tablet to Ea-nasir (UET V 81, British Museum): date, content, findspot, Dilmun copper trade, the "house full of complaints" detail, and Guinness/meme status

VERDICT: Safe to use in essentially the form stated, with two hedges: say "c. 1750 BCE" as the conventional museum date (his dated activity may fall a few decades earlier under Rim-Sin I), and soften "he kept his complaints" to "complaint letters were among the business archive found in what is very probably his house."

KEY FACTS:
- UET V 81 is a clay tablet in Akkadian cuneiform, 11.6 cm high x 5.0 cm wide x 2.6 cm thick, British Museum no. 1953,0411.71 (registered into the BM collection in 1953), conventionally dated c. 1750 BCE, from the city of Ur in southern Iraq.
- The letter is from a man named Nanni to the merchant Ea-nasir, complaining that copper ingots offered were of the wrong (inferior) grade, that his messenger was treated with contempt and told 'take it or leave it,' and that his messengers were sent back empty-handed repeatedly, through enemy territory.
- Ea-nasir was a member of the alik Tilmun ('Dilmun traders'), merchants of Ur who imported copper by sea up the Persian Gulf via Dilmun (centered on modern Bahrain, an entrepot; the copper itself ultimately came from Magan, the Oman peninsula, 600+ miles away), financed by pooled silver and goods.
- Tablets in Ea-nasir's archive can be dated to the 11th and 19th regnal years of Rim-Sin I of Larsa (reigned c. 1822-1763 BCE, middle chronology), per Leemans' 1960 study and Oppenheim's 1954 'Seafaring Merchants of Ur.'
- The tablet was excavated by Sir Leonard Woolley's joint British Museum-University of Pennsylvania expedition to Ur (1922-1934), in the 'AH' residential quarter; the tablets connected with Ea-nasir came from the house Woolley's team labeled No. 1 Old Street, identified as Ea-nasir's probable residence.
- Multiple complaint letters addressed to Ea-nasir were found in that house, not just Nanni's: other writers named in the sources include Arbituram (copper never delivered), Appa, Ilsu-ellatsu, Ili-idinnam, and Imgur-Sin, complaining of bad copper or delays; these sat within a larger archive of roughly two dozen tablets, most of it ordinary business correspondence.
- Guinness World Records lists the tablet as the 'oldest written customer complaint' (their entry dates it to 1750 BCE, ~3,767 years old, held at the British Museum).
- The meme began in February 2015 when a museum photo was posted to Reddit captioned '1750 BC problems' (44,000+ upvotes) and reposted the same day by Tumblr user tastefullyoffensive ('Babylonian era problems,' 587,000+ notes), per Know Your Meme; it has resurged repeatedly since (2018, 2021, 2023, including an xkcd reference).
- As of 2025-2027 the tablet is reported on loan from the British Museum to the Zayed National Museum in Abu Dhabi.
- The standard English translation podcasters quote is A. Leo Oppenheim's, from Letters from Mesopotamia (1967); W. F. Leemans had published a full translation in Foreign Trade in the Old Babylonian Period (1960).

USABLE FRAMING:
Around 3,800 years ago in the city of Ur, a man named Nanni had a scribe press his fury into clay: the copper merchant Ea-nasir had offered his messenger inferior-grade ingots, told him to take them or leave them, and sent Nanni's men home empty-handed — through enemy territory, no less. Ea-nasir was one of Ur's 'Dilmun traders,' merchants who shipped copper up the Persian Gulf through the island entrepot of Dilmun, modern Bahrain. The tablet was dug up by Leonard Woolley's British Museum-University of Pennsylvania expedition in the 1920s-30s, in a house the excavators labeled No. 1 Old Street — very probably Ea-nasir's own home, because the business archive found there included not just Nanni's letter but several other letters griping about his copper and his delays. Whether he kept them out of bookkeeping habit or sheer indifference, we can't know — but the image of a Bronze Age merchant sitting on a stack of his own one-star reviews is why, since a Reddit and Tumblr post in 2015, Ea-nasir has become the internet's favorite ancient businessman, and why Guinness World Records certifies Nanni's tablet as the oldest written customer complaint.

CAUTIONS:
- Don't state 1750 BCE as a precise date: it is the British Museum's conventional round figure. Dated tablets in Ea-nasir's archive belong to regnal years 11 and 19 of Rim-Sin I of Larsa (c. 1812/1804 BCE under the middle chronology), so his activity may be several decades before 1750, and all absolute Old Babylonian dates shift with the middle-vs-short chronology debate. Say 'around 1750 BCE' or 'almost 3,800 years ago.'
- Don't call Nanni a walk-in retail 'customer' without nuance: he was a party in a wholesale trading arrangement who had deposited money/goods with Ea-nasir; 'customer complaint' is the Guinness framing, not how Assyriologists describe the relationship. Also, Nanni dictated to a scribe rather than physically writing the tablet.
- Don't overclaim 'a house full of complaint letters': the No. 1 Old Street archive (roughly 26 tablets per secondary summaries) was mostly ordinary business correspondence; the complaint letters (Nanni, Arbituram, and a few others) were a subset. 'He kept his bad reviews' is meme-flavored inference — retaining incoming correspondence was normal archival practice.
- The identification of No. 1 Old Street as Ea-nasir's residence is probable but inferential (based on letters addressed to him found there); Woolley-era findspot records are not always precise, so hedge with 'identified as' or 'very probably his house.'
- Don't say Woolley excavated it 'in 1953' (an error that appears in at least one secondary source, Cultural Property News): excavation was during the 1922-1934 joint expedition; 1953 is the year the tablet was registered in the BM collection and the year UET V was published.
- Don't claim a specific year Guinness certified the record (sources confirm the record exists but the certification date was not verified), and don't claim the meme 'started on Tumblr' alone — Know Your Meme traces the February 2015 origin to Reddit with same-day Tumblr amplification.
- Dilmun nuance: Dilmun (Bahrain) was the trans-shipment hub; the copper itself was mined in Magan (Oman peninsula). 'Copper via Dilmun' is correct; 'copper from Dilmun's mines' would not be.
- Minor: if mentioning where to see it, note reports that the tablet is on loan to the Zayed National Museum, Abu Dhabi, c. 2025-2027, rather than on display in London.

## Transmission chain from Sumerian base-60 counting to the modern 60-minute hour and 360-degree circle

VERDICT: The chain is historically solid and safe to narrate, and the payoff line works if aimed at the minutes and seconds on the clock (the 24-hour part is Egyptian, not Sumerian) and if 'why 60' is presented as honestly unknown.

KEY FACTS:
- Sexagesimal-structured numeration is attested in Sumerian proto-cuneiform accounting tablets from the Uruk period (late 4th millennium BCE, c. 3200 BCE), making the base-60 principle older than writing itself in its mature form.
- The fully developed sexagesimal place-value notation — the abstract 'floating-point' system Babylonian mathematicians used — is first firmly attested in mathematical and administrative texts of the Ur III period, c. 2100–2000 BCE (e.g., the tablet YBC 1793).
- 60 is the smallest number divisible by every integer from 1 through 6 and has twelve divisors (1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 10, 12, 15, 20, 30, 60), which made fractions exceptionally easy to handle without a calculator.
- The earliest surviving explanation for base 60 — the divisibility argument — comes from Theon of Alexandria in the 4th century CE, roughly 3,000 years after the system arose, so it is a retrospective rationalization, not evidence of original intent.
- Babylonian astronomers divided the day into 12 bēru and 360 uš ('time-degrees,' each equal to 4 modern minutes), a scheme attested in the MUL.APIN astronomical compendium (compiled around 1000 BCE, oldest surviving copies 7th century BCE).
- By the late 5th century BCE, Babylonian astronomers had divided the zodiac into 12 signs of 30 parts each — 360 parts — which is the direct ancestor of the 360-degree circle.
- The earliest extant Greek text using the Babylonian 360-part division is the Anaphorikos of Hypsicles of Alexandria (2nd century BCE); Hipparchus (c. 190–120 BCE), who demonstrably used Babylonian observational data, is credited with extending the 360-degree division to circles generally.
- Ptolemy's Almagest (c. 150 CE) divided each degree sexagesimally, writing in Greek of 'first sixtieths' and 'second sixtieths' — he never used the Latin words behind 'minute' and 'second'.
- The Almagest was translated into Arabic in 827 CE and into Latin from Arabic by Gerard of Cremona in Toledo around 1175; the medieval Latin terms pars minuta prima ('first small part') and pars minuta secunda ('second small part') from this translation tradition became 'minute' and 'second,' entering English in the late 14th century.
- Islamic-era astronomers kept the system alive in between: al-Biruni around 1000 CE was already subdividing hours sexagesimally into minutes, seconds, thirds, and fourths.
- Minutes and seconds existed on astronomers' paper for well over 1,500 years before any machine could measure them: Europe's first mechanical clocks (c. 1300) showed only hours, minute hands appeared experimentally in the late 16th century (Jost Bürgi, c. 1577–80), became standard only after Huygens' pendulum clock (1656) and the anchor escapement (c. 1670s–90), and second hands became common only in the early 1700s.
- The 24-hour day is Egyptian, not Mesopotamian: by c. 1500 BCE Egyptians divided night into 12 hours via decan stars and daylight into 12 via sundials, so the modern clock face is a hybrid of Egyptian hours and Babylonian-derived minutes and seconds.

USABLE FRAMING:
The transmission chain is real and traceable at every step: Sumerian scribes were counting in sixties before 3000 BCE; Babylonian astronomers divided the day into 360 time-degrees and the zodiac into 360 parts; Greek astronomers — Hypsicles, then Hipparchus, then Ptolemy — borrowed those divisions and cut each degree into sixtieths of sixtieths; medieval translators rendering Ptolemy from Arabic into Latin around 1175 called those fractions the pars minuta prima and pars minuta secunda — the 'first small part' and 'second small part' — and those phrases are, literally, our words 'minute' and 'second.' When mechanical clocks finally became accurate enough in the 1600s to need subdivisions of the hour, the units were already sitting there, fifteen centuries old, waiting on astronomers' paper. So the payoff is honest in this form: the minutes and seconds on your clock are a fossil of Sumerian counting — though the 24 hours around them are an Egyptian fossil, from a completely different system. As for why 60: the oldest explanation we have, that 60 divides evenly by 1 through 6, was written by Theon of Alexandria three thousand years after the fact, and the truth is nobody knows — the divisibility was certainly why the system survived, but whether it was why the system began is lost.

CAUTIONS:
- Do not claim we know why the Sumerians chose 60: Theon's divisibility explanation is the earliest attested but is a 4th-century-CE guess; modern historians (MacTutor's survey, Neugebauer) find no theory — divisibility, merged base-12/base-5 counting systems, knuckle-counting, weights and measures, 360-day year — actually convincing, and Neugebauer cautioned the origins are 'not as simple, consistent, or singular in time as they are often portrayed.'
- The popular '12 knuckles × 5 fingers' finger-counting story is an unproven hypothesis frequently stated as fact — flag it as conjecture if used at all.
- Do not say 'the Babylonians divided the circle into 360 degrees' as an abstract geometry claim: they divided the day into 360 uš and the zodiac/ecliptic into 360 parts (firmly by the late 5th century BCE); generalizing 360 degrees to every circle was a Hellenistic Greek step (Hypsicles/Hipparchus, 2nd century BCE).
- Do not say 360 was chosen because 'the year has 360 days' as fact — that is Moritz Cantor's conjecture; Babylonians used a schematic 360-day administrative calendar but knew the real year was longer, and the direction of causation is unknown.
- Do not credit Ptolemy with the words 'minute' and 'second' — he wrote in Greek (prota/deutera hexekosta, 'first/second sixtieths'); the Latin terms belong to the 12th-century translation milieu (Gerard of Cremona's 1175 Toledo translation from Arabic is the standard reference point, though claiming it as the absolute first attestation of the phrase is stronger than the evidence requires — say 'medieval Latin translations of the Almagest').
- Do not credit Sumer or Babylon with the 24-hour day — that is Egyptian (decans and sundials, ~1500 BCE and earlier); the clock face is a hybrid artifact and the payoff line should target minutes/seconds, not hours.
- Do not say Sumerians or Babylonians 'measured minutes and seconds' — as units of lived time they were unmeasurable until the pendulum era (1656 onward); for ~1,500 years minutes and seconds were astronomers' calculating units only, and Babylonian time units were actually the bēru (120 min) and uš (4 min).
- Be careful with 'Sumerian arithmetic' in the payoff: the transmission runs Sumerian counting → Babylonian place-value mathematics → Greek astronomy → Arabic astronomy → Latin Europe → clockmakers, and the Islamic link (e.g., al-Biruni, the 827 CE Arabic Almagest) should not be skipped; 'a counting system first attested among the Sumerians' is the bulletproof phrasing.
- Avoid the claim that the Babylonians 'divided each hour into 60 minutes' for daily timekeeping — some popular sources assert this, but the 60-minute hour as we use it emerged from Greek astronomers applying sexagesimal fractions to the (Egyptian-derived) equinoctial hour, not from Babylonian civil practice.

## BBC Domesday Project (1986) as a digital-preservation cautionary tale vs. the 1086 Domesday Book

VERDICT: Safe to use in refined form: all three core claims are essentially true, but say the discs became "effectively inaccessible because the hardware/software went obsolete" (not that the discs physically decayed or the data was lost forever), and note the data was successfully rescued in 2002-2003.

KEY FACTS:
- The BBC Domesday Project was compiled 1984-1986 and published in November 1986 by the BBC with Acorn Computers, Philips, and Logica, to mark the 900th anniversary of William the Conqueror's 1086 Domesday survey.
- Over one million people contributed, including schoolchildren from more than 9,000 UK schools, writing about geography, history, and daily life in their local areas.
- The data was published on two adapted LaserVision laserdiscs in the LV-ROM format (about 300 MB per side), readable only on a specially modified system: a BBC Master AIV computer with a Philips VP415 'Domesday Player,' a complete setup costing roughly 5,000 pounds in 1986, which sold poorly.
- By early 2002 the discs were widely reported as effectively inaccessible because the required players and computers had become rare and obsolete; The Observer ran the story 'Digital Domesday Book lasts 15 years, not 1,000' on 3 March 2002.
- The CAMiLEON project (Creative Archiving at Michigan and Leeds: Emulating the Old on the New), a University of Leeds / University of Michigan partnership, demonstrated a working software emulator of the Domesday system at Leeds in December 2002, proving emulation as a preservation technique.
- A parallel rescue by the UK National Archives with Adrian Pearce (LongLife Data) and Andy Finney reverse-engineered the system and migrated the data and images to modern formats around 2002-2004; the BBC later republished much of the community content online as 'Domesday Reloaded' in 2011.
- The discs themselves had not physically rotted; the crisis was technological obsolescence of the playback hardware and software, and data extraction from surviving fragile equipment was described as 'just in time.'
- The original 1086 Domesday Book, written in Latin on parchment (prepared animal skin), survives at the National Archives in Kew, remains legible after more than 900 years, and was even rebound in 1986 for its ninth centenary, though the original is now rarely handled and consulted mainly via facsimile and digital copies for preservation reasons.

USABLE FRAMING:
In 1986, to mark the 900th anniversary of the Domesday Book, the BBC built a high-tech sequel: over a million Britons, many of them schoolchildren, recorded life in their communities onto cutting-edge laserdiscs that could only be read by one specially built computer system. Barely fifteen years later, that system was obsolete — the discs were fine, but almost nothing left on Earth could read them, and in 2002 it took an international rescue effort by archivists and computer scientists to emulate the dead hardware and pull the data back out, just in time. Meanwhile the original Domesday Book, ink on parchment from 1086, sits in the National Archives, still perfectly legible after nine centuries. That's the trap of modern media: the information doesn't have to decay, it just has to outlive the machines that speak its language. A clay tablet needs only eyes and sunlight; a laserdisc needs an entire vanished civilization of compatible technology. Which is why a Bronze Age scribe's shopping list can outlast a twentieth-century supercomputer's archive.

CAUTIONS:
- Do NOT say the discs physically decayed, suffered 'disc rot,' or that the data was destroyed or lost forever — the discs were intact and the data was fully rescued in 2002-2003 (CAMiLEON emulation, then a National Archives-backed migration; BBC put content online as 'Domesday Reloaded' in 2011).
- Avoid 'unreadable by 2002' as an absolute: a small number of working Philips VP415 players and BBC Master AIV systems still existed (the rescuers used them); the honest phrasing is 'effectively inaccessible' or 'on the brink of unreadability' as hardware became vanishingly rare and fragile.
- CAMiLEON (Leeds + Michigan) was one of two parallel rescue efforts; the National Archives / LongLife Data / ATSF migration effort was separate — don't credit CAMiLEON alone if you name names, or just say 'a rescue effort by archivists and computer scientists.'
- The original Domesday Book is legible but access-restricted: it can rarely be consulted in the original today (researchers use facsimiles/digital copies), so say 'still readable' or 'survives intact,' not 'anyone can walk in and read it.' Also, in 1986 it was held by the Public Record Office; 'The National Archives' as an institution dates from 2003 — fine for present tense, wrong for 1986.
- The 1086 Domesday Book is a survey of landholding, not strictly a census, and its writing may have continued slightly past 1086 (scholars debate completion c. 1086-1088); '1086' is the standard, safe date for the survey.
- The popular '15 years' line comes from the March 2002 Observer headline; it's fair to cite as contemporary press framing, but the project published in late 1986, so 'about fifteen or sixteen years' is the careful version.
- For the clay-tablet beat: cuneiform tablets from c. 2000 BCE (and earlier, back to c. 3300 BCE) genuinely survive, so '4,000 years' is safe — but note most surviving tablets were sun-dried and survive partly by luck (many baked hard only when buildings burned), so avoid implying clay was designed for permanence.

## James C. Scott's "Against the Grain" (2017): the grain-taxability thesis of early state formation and its scholarly reception

VERDICT: Safe to use if attributed as Scott's argument (not settled consensus) and if "no grain, no state" is presented as the popular slogan summarizing his thesis rather than a verbatim book quote — the verbatim line is the "visible, divisible, assessable, storable, transportable, and rationable" sentence.

KEY FACTS:
- Against the Grain: A Deep History of the Earliest States by James C. Scott was published by Yale University Press in 2017 (312 pp., ISBN 9780300182910); Scott was Sterling Professor of Political Science and Professor of Anthropology at Yale and died July 19, 2024, at age 87.
- Scott's verbatim core claim: "The key to the nexus between grains and states lies, I believe, in the fact that only the cereal grains can serve as a basis for taxation: visible, divisible, assessable, storable, transportable, and 'rationable.'" — six properties, of which the fact-check item correctly lists four.
- Scott's mechanism is legibility: cereals grow above ground and ripen at roughly the same time (a determinate harvest), so an assessor can survey, measure, and confiscate the crop in one operation, whereas tubers like cassava hide underground (and can be left in the soil, edible, for up to two more years) and most legumes ripen indeterminately and can be picked piecemeal before the tax man arrives.
- Scott's verbatim negative formulation: "History records no cassava states, no sago, yam, taro, plantain, breadfruit, or sweet potato states" — and elsewhere he writes that states require "an appropriable, measurable, dominant grain crop and a population growing it that can be easily administered and mobilized."
- The phrase "no grain, no state" is the standard shorthand used by readers and reviewers for this thesis, but multiple full-text searches of the book did not confirm it as a verbatim sentence in Against the Grain; treat it as a slogan summarizing the argument, not a quotation.
- Scott's timeline is central and often misstated: he stresses that the first small, stratified, tax-collecting, walled states appear in the Tigris-Euphrates valley only around 3100 BCE, more than four millennia AFTER the first crop domestications and sedentism — so grain farming long preceded states; states then locked populations into grain monoculture.
- Scott explicitly hedges that grain-friendly agro-ecological settings are "perhaps necessary for early state making, but not sufficient" — he is not strictly monocausal.
- Reception was split: archaeologist Barry Cunliffe called it "history as it should be written" and Steven Mithen reviewed it favorably, while Samuel Moyn found it "sparkling" but said its claims about ancestral egalitarianism were "never seriously defended."
- Specialist criticism is substantial: Assyriologist Seth Richardson (Journal of Near Eastern Studies 77/2, 2018, pp. 307-11) faulted Scott for conflating sources, anachronistic analogies, and treating the Bronze and Iron Ages as a single static period; the Cambridge Archaeological Journal ran a multi-author review symposium (vol. 29, issue 4, Nov 2019) including a piece literally titled "Against Scott's Grain"; Guy Middleton in the American Journal of Archaeology (Jan 2019) called the grain-tax idea "interesting but open for debate."
- The most-cited counterexample is the Andes: the Norte Chico/Caral complex (c. 3000 BCE) built monumental urban centers on maritime resources and cotton rather than a cereal staple, and the later Inca state taxed labor (mit'a) rather than grain — though whether Norte Chico counts as a "state" is itself contested.
- Scott openly framed the book as a synthesis and provocation by a non-specialist drawing on others' archaeology (he thanked Mesopotamia specialists, including Richardson, in the acknowledgments), building on the "legibility" framework of his Seeing Like a State (1998).

USABLE FRAMING:
If you ask why the first states rose where they did, the late Yale political scientist James C. Scott gave a famously sharp answer in his 2017 book Against the Grain: follow the tax man. Wheat and barley ripen above ground, all at once, on a schedule — you can't hide a wheat field from an assessor the way you can leave a tuber in the dirt — and grain can be measured, divided, stored in a granary, hauled on a boat, and doled out as rations. That makes it the perfect substance for accounting and appropriation, which is why, in Scott's telling, history gives us grain states but, as he put it, no cassava, sago, yam, taro, breadfruit, or sweet potato states — a thesis readers have boiled down to the slogan "no grain, no state." Two honest hedges: grain farming existed for some four thousand years before the first walled, tax-collecting states appeared in Mesopotamia around 3100 BCE, so grain made states possible rather than inevitable — Scott himself called it necessary but not sufficient. And while archaeologists love the book as a provocation, many specialists push back, pointing to places like coastal Peru where monumental centers rose without a cereal staple. It's an argument, and a powerful one — not a settled law of history.

CAUTIONS:
- Do not present "no grain, no state" as a verbatim quotation from the book; full-text checks did not locate that exact sentence — say the thesis "is often summarized as" or "boils down to" the slogan, or use the verified verbatim lines instead.
- Do not say grain (or agriculture) caused states directly or quickly: Scott's own headline point is the 4,000-plus-year gap between domestication/sedentism and the first Mesopotamian states (~3100 BCE), and he calls grain ecology necessary but not sufficient.
- Do not call the thesis scholarly consensus; archaeologists and Assyriologists treat it as a provocative synthesis — Richardson (JNES 2018) and the Cambridge Archaeological Journal symposium (2019) fault its evidence handling, anachronistic analogies, and flattening of the Bronze and Iron Ages — even while widely admiring the book.
- Beware the Andean counterexample if you generalize globally: Norte Chico/Caral (c. 3000 BCE) and the Inca labor-tax (mit'a) system are the standard objections, though Norte Chico's status as a "state" is itself debated — Scott's strong claims are scoped mainly to Mesopotamia.
- Scott was a political scientist/anthropologist synthesizing others' archaeology, not an excavator or ancient historian — attribute the argument to him personally, and note he framed the book as a provocation.
- The full property list is six items (visible, divisible, assessable, storable, transportable, rationable), not four; and "ripens on a schedule above ground" is Scott's mechanism for visibility/assessability rather than one of the listed adjectives.
- Avoid page-number citations for the key quote (editions differ; one notes source gives p. 133) — cite the book and the "Grains Make States" discussion instead.
- Related claims in the book (states relied on coerced labor and slavery, early states were epidemiologically miserable, "collapse" was often emancipation) are more contested than the tax-legibility core; don't bundle them in as equally established.

## Jesse Millek's revisionist reassessment of the Late Bronze Age destruction cascade c. 1200-1150 BCE: which destructions are real, which are misattributed, and how a narrator should frame the collapse

VERDICT: Safe to use if framed as a patchwork, not a firestorm: Ugarit, Emar, and Pylos have good evidence of violent/fiery destruction, Hattusa was evacuated before it burned, but Millek shows roughly 60% of claimed destructions c. 1200 BCE are misdated, unevidenced, or miscited — so never narrate a single uniform wave of burning or name the Sea Peoples as proven arsonists.

KEY FACTS:
- Jesse Millek's 2023 book 'Destruction and Its Impact on Ancient Societies at the End of the Bronze Age' (Lockwood Press) examined 153 claimed destructions c. 1200 BCE across the eastern Mediterranean and judged 94 of them (61%) to be 'false' — 33 misdated even against a generous 1225-1175 BCE window, 35 assumed without supporting evidence, and 26 resting on false citations not backed by the excavation reports (figures per Guy Middleton's review in Antiquity).
- Ugarit is a genuine violent destruction with the best evidence in the Levant: most of the city was burned and then permanently abandoned, and excavators recovered 32 arrowheads plus 12 other weapons in the streets, houses, and open spaces of a residential quarter, indicating fighting inside the city before the fire.
- Ugarit's fall is dated by a letter from the Egyptian official Bay (in office c. 1194-1190 BCE) to Ugarit's last king Ammurapi, placing the destruction around 1192-1185 BCE; Millek stresses that Ramesses III's inscriptions never name Ugarit among Sea Peoples conquests and the 'enemy ships' in Ugarit's final letters are never identified, even though Ugarit's scribes knew Sea Peoples group names.
- Emar on the Euphrates was genuinely destroyed and abandoned c. 1187-1175 BCE (a tablet synchronism with year 2 of Babylonian king Meli-Shipak gives c. 1187 BCE), but the burning was selective — monumental and religious buildings were torched while domestic structures were left untouched — and the assailant is unknown.
- Hattusa, per Jurgen Seeher's excavations, shows fire destruction on the royal acropolis, in the Upper and Lower City temples, and along stretches of fortification, but only after the court and residents had evacuated, removing valuables and the official archives; the capital was 'little more than a ghost town' when it burned in the early 12th century BCE, with Kaska groups from the north the usual (unproven) suspects for the final torching.
- In Syria, of the sites commonly listed as destroyed by the Sea Peoples (Ugarit, Tell Sukas, Tell Tweini, Carchemish, Kadesh/Tell Nebi Mend, Qatna, Hama, Alalakh, Emar), Millek shows only Ugarit and Emar were actually destroyed c. 1200 BCE: Qatna and Hama were destroyed in the mid-14th century, Alalakh's destruction dates to c. 1300, Tell Sukas and Tell Tweini show no real destruction layer, and Kadesh and Carchemish show unbroken continuity into the Iron Age.
- Carchemish — listed as 'cut off' in Ramesses III's Sea Peoples inscription — not only survived but prospered: its king Kuzi-Teshub assumed the title 'Great King' after the Hittite dynasty vanished, founding a Neo-Hittite line.
- The Palace of Nestor at Pylos was genuinely destroyed by a great fire c. 1180 BCE and never rebuilt; the blaze baked and preserved its Linear B tablets, and Millek classes Pylos (with Midea) as a possible warfare destruction.
- For mainland Greece, Millek finds that of 12 major sites only a handful have positive destruction evidence — Midea and Pylos possibly warfare, Tiryns warfare or arson, Gla and Dimini arson — while seven are 'unknown' and the claimed c. 1200 destruction of the palatial candidate Orchomenos has no supporting evidence.
- The earthquake explanation for Tiryns and Midea (Klaus Kilian's influential hypothesis) was quantitatively tested by the HERACLES archaeoseismology project (Hinzen, Maran et al., Bulletin of the Seismological Society of America 108, 2018), which concluded a destructive earthquake there is unlikely: most damage has equally good non-seismic explanations and some was clearly not earthquake-caused.
- In the southern Levant, Millek (Ugarit-Forschungen 49, 2018) found that roughly a third of 62 cited destruction events around the LB/Iron transition are not supported by the archaeology, and that famous 'Sea Peoples destructions' at sites such as Ashkelon and Akko lack destruction evidence; on Cyprus, Kition, Alassa, Maroni-Vournes and other sites show continuity rather than destruction.
- Millek does not deny the collapse itself: palatial administration, the international trade-and-diplomacy system, writing systems (Linear B, Ugaritic cuneiform), and the Hittite state all genuinely ended, and he accepts roughly 150 years of gradual, regionally uneven cooling and drying — his target is specifically the myth of a uniform wave of violent destruction.
- Mainstream scholarship has moved toward this view: Eric Cline's revised 1177 B.C. (2021) concedes Hattusa was likely burned by Kaska after being largely emptied and warns against tying destruction levels to a single event, and Middleton's Antiquity review calls the dominant narrative of 'the collapse c. 1200 BC' partly 'a modern myth and an artifact of scholarship.'

USABLE FRAMING:
For a narrator: the end of the Bronze Age was real — kingdoms, palaces, archives, and an entire international order genuinely vanished — but the popular image of one synchronized tidal wave of burning cities is largely a modern myth. When archaeologist Jesse Millek went back through the excavation reports, roughly six in ten of the destructions claimed for 'around 1200 BCE' dissolved: misdated by a century, assumed without evidence, or copied from citation to citation until they became fact. What survives scrutiny is a smaller, stranger patchwork: Ugarit burned after street fighting that left dozens of arrowheads where they fell, around 1190 BCE, attacked by enemies no surviving text ever names; Emar fell a few years later, its temples torched but its houses spared; Pylos's palace went up in a fire so hot it baked its own clay records. And Hattusa, the Hittite capital, wasn't stormed at its height — the king, court, and archives moved out first, and someone, perhaps the Kaska, later put a largely empty ghost town to the torch. Even the famous earthquake explanation for Tiryns and Midea has failed modern seismological testing. The honest story is collapse without a single culprit: some cities murdered, some abandoned, many simply carrying on — which is, if anything, more unsettling than a wave of barbarians.

CAUTIONS:
- Do NOT say the Sea Peoples destroyed Ugarit, Hattusa, or 'the cities of the Levant': Millek's central finding is that no ancient source accuses the Sea Peoples of destroying any specific excavated site — Ramesses III never names Ugarit, and Ugarit's 'enemies on boats' are anonymous; Sea Peoples involvement anywhere is inference, not evidence.
- Do NOT narrate a single year or single wave (e.g., 'in 1177 everything burned'): the genuine destructions are spread across roughly 1225-1175 BCE and beyond (Ugarit c. 1192-1185, Emar c. 1187-1175, Pylos c. 1180, Hattusa early 12th century), and even Cline treats 1177 as a symbol, not a date of synchronized catastrophe.
- Do NOT present the Tiryns/Mycenae/Midea earthquake (or Amos Nur's 'earthquake storm') as established: the HERACLES project found a destructive quake at Tiryns and Midea unlikely, and Millek finds no empirical support for the earthquake model — though some scholars still entertain seismic contributions, so frame it as a largely discredited but once-dominant hypothesis.
- Do NOT claim Mycenae itself was demonstrably sacked or quake-flattened c. 1200: the cause of its end-of-13th-century damage is genuinely unknown (Iakovidis noted the lack of evidence for violent sudden decline); Mycenae belongs in the 'contested/unknown' column, unlike Pylos.
- Do NOT overstate Hattusa's drama: the evacuation-before-burning sequence (Seeher) is well supported and widely accepted, but the identity of who burned it (Kaska) and whether Suppiluliuma II relocated the capital are hypotheses, and the abandonment may have unfolded over years or decades.
- Do NOT imply Millek says 'nothing happened': he affirms real destructions (Ugarit, Emar, Pylos, Midea, Tiryns in part) and a real systemic collapse of palatial economies, writing, and trade; his exact counts (153/94/61%; 62 events in the Levant) come from his own criteria and reviewers note individual site judgments can be debated.
- Be careful with Ugarit's exact date: c. 1192-1185 BCE is the defensible window (anchored on the Bay letter); the precise 1192 BCE eclipse-based date is one study, not consensus.
- An Emar tablet's reference to hostile 'hordes' (tarwu troops) is real scholarship but their identity is unknown — do not equate them with Sea Peoples or Arameans on air.
- Sites to avoid claiming as destroyed c. 1200: Qatna and Hama (destroyed mid-14th century), Alalakh (c. 1300), Carchemish and Kadesh (continuity), Tell Sukas and Tell Tweini (no destruction layer), Ashkelon and Akko (no evidence), most of Cyprus (continuity), Orchomenos (unknown/abandoned?).

## Slavery in the Bronze Age Near East: Kanesh contracts, Hammurabi's code, Pylos Linear B slaves, Egyptian/Hittite war captives, Ugarit slave sales

VERDICT: Safe to use in hedged form: items 1, 2, 4, and 5 rest on actual contracts, law collections, and royal records (read the royal numbers as propaganda); item 3 (Pylos) is solid on the do-e-ro word and the women's work-groups but their "slave" status is a strong scholarly inference, not a label on the tablets.

KEY FACTS:
- Kanesh/Kültepe (kārum Kanesh, c. 1950–1750 BCE) has yielded roughly 23,000 Old Assyrian tablets, among them slave-sale contracts in which Anatolians sold themselves, family members, or whole families—usually under debt—with documented prices ranging from about 5 shekels of silver for a slave woman (text KUG 3) up to over 100 shekels, and redemption clauses sometimes requiring double the purchase price to buy freedom.
- Slaves (wardum/amtum) at Kanesh appear in wills, marriage and divorce contracts, and household asset lists alongside other property, though the core Assur–Kanesh trade was tin and textiles, with people a marginal commodity.
- The Laws of Hammurabi (r. c. 1792–1750 BCE, middle chronology) treat slaves as property throughout: §15–16 impose death for helping or harboring a fugitive slave, §17 sets a 2-shekel reward for returning one, §278 lets a buyer return a slave like defective goods within one month if disease appears, and §282 orders the ear cut off a slave who denies his master.
- Hammurabi §117 (L.W. King trans., Avalon Project): a man who sells his wife, son, or daughter—or gives himself—for debt has them work three years in the buyer's house, 'and in the fourth year they shall be set free'; this release applied to free-born persons in debt bondage, not to chattel slaves (§118 allows a pledged slave to be sold outright with no recourse).
- Linear B do-e-ro/do-e-ra (doelos/doela, the direct ancestor of classical Greek doulos, 'slave') is attested at Pylos and Knossos, with some slaves owned by named individuals and others as 'slaves of the god' (te-o-jo do-e-ro) who could hold land leases—so the term covered a range of statuses.
- The Pylos Aa/Ab/Ad tablet series (palace destroyed by fire c. 1180 BCE) record women's work groups with their children—the Aa series alone counts 631 women, 376 girls, and 261 boys—receiving monthly subsistence rations of wheat and figs; servile low-status women are over 750 of the roughly 900 women in the Pylos tablets.
- Some Pylos women's groups carry ethnic labels pointing to the east Aegean and Anatolian coast—mi-ra-ti-ja (women of Miletus), ki-ni-di-ja (Knidos), ra-mi-ni-ja (Lemnos)—and one group of 26–28 women is called ra-wi-ja-ja, 'captives' (tablets Aa 807, Ad 686); Chadwick proposed the ethnic names reflect raiding or the slave markets where they were bought.
- The Annals of Thutmose III at Karnak record, after Megiddo (c. 1457 BCE), '340 living prisoners' and '83 hands,' and Thutmose III assigned over 1,500 captives (commonly cited as 1,588) to the estate of Amun; Amenhotep II's Memphis and Karnak stelae total 101,128 captives from his year-9 campaign, a figure scholars widely suspect of exaggeration or cumulative accounting.
- Hittite kings systematically took NAM.RA (Hittite arnuwala-, 'transportees/deportees'): Mursili II's Ten-Year Annals claim that from the Arzawa campaign he brought 15,500 deportees to the royal house while what his troops and horse-troops took was 'beyond counting,' with other passages giving 4,000 from the Seha River Land; deportees were resettled as labor for temples, crown land, and soldiers' households (Hoffner 2002 on the Maşat texts), and the Hittite Laws give arnuwala a distinct legal status.
- Akkadian legal texts from Ugarit (PRU volumes; city destroyed c. 1185 BCE) include slave sales and at least one known manumission document, with slave prices in the 14th–13th centuries running around 30 shekels of silver and up; RS 17.179 records a ruling letting the merchants of Ura seize a defaulting Ugaritic debtor and his family.
- At both Pylos and Ugarit, the destruction fires that ended the palaces baked the clay tablets and preserved them—the records of these dependent people survive only because the system that held them burned, and no text records what happened to them afterward.

USABLE FRAMING:
The tablets don't editorialize—they inventory. At Kanesh around 1900 BCE, Assyrian merchants filed slave-sale contracts next to their tin accounts, and Anatolian families sold their own children to cover debts, with buy-back clauses that could cost double the sale price. Hammurabi's laws make the logic explicit: a slave was returnable within a month like defective goods, and helping one escape was a capital crime—though a free man's wife and children sold for debt had to be released in the fourth year. At Pylos, the palace scribes counted hundreds of women and children by their rations of wheat and figs and by where they came from—Miletus, Knidos, Lemnos—and one group is simply labeled 'captives'; whether every one of them was legally a slave we can't prove, but the palace owned their labor and fed them at subsistence. Pharaohs and Hittite kings counted people as plunder by the thousand—340 prisoners at Megiddo, 15,500 deportees marched home by Mursili II, with the army's own take 'beyond counting'—numbers that are propaganda, but propaganda bragging about a real practice. And the only reason we can read any of this is that when Pylos and Ugarit burned around 1180 BCE, the fires baked the ledgers hard—preserving the names and ration lists of people whose fates, after the smoke cleared, no one bothered to record.

CAUTIONS:
- Do not say the Pylos Aa/Ab women are labeled do-e-ra/'slaves' on the tablets—they are not; their servile status is inferred from rations, palace dependence, ethnic labels, and the ra-wi-ja-ja 'captives' group, and some scholars prefer 'dependent personnel' or leave status ambiguous (captives vs. purchased vs. refugees).
- Do not claim Hammurabi §117 freed slaves after three years—it applied to free-born family members (and the debtor himself) in debt bondage; chattel slaves had no release term, and §118 expressly allows a pledged slave to be sold permanently.
- Do not present Hammurabi's stele as an enforced statutory code—Assyriologists debate whether it functioned as binding law, since surviving court records rarely cite it; safer to call it a royal law collection reflecting prevailing norms.
- Do not use Amenhotep II's 101,128 captives as a literal count—it is widely regarded as exaggerated or a cumulative/census-style tally; the Megiddo figures (340 prisoners, 83 hands) are far more modest and more credible precisely because they are unimpressive.
- Avoid the often-quoted '66,000 deportees from Arzawa' figure unless verified against a critical edition of Mursili II's Comprehensive Annals; the securely citable Ten-Year-Annals claim is 15,500 to the king's house plus an uncounted mass taken by the army—and even royal annals' numbers are self-glorifying.
- Do not equate every Old Assyrian wardum with a chattel slave—the word also covers servants, agents, and subordinates; scholars (Veenhof, Michel) stress a spectrum from debt servitude to outright sale, and the slave trade was marginal next to tin and textiles at Kanesh.
- At Ugarit, distinguish chattel slaves (attested in a modest number of sale documents) from the large class of royal dependents (bnš mlk)—Heltzer's 'serf' model for the latter is influential but contested; do not describe Ugarit's whole labor force as enslaved.
- Linear B do-e-ro covered a range of statuses: 'slaves of the god' could hold land leases, so avoid flattening all do-e-ro into plantation-style chattel slavery.
- Mesopotamian absolute dates (e.g., Hammurabi r. 1792–1750) follow the conventional middle chronology and shift under other chronologies; Isaac Mendelsohn's classic 'Slavery in the Ancient Near East' (1949) is foundational but dated—prefer recent work (Michel 2020, Olsen 2014, Hoffner 2002) for specifics.
- The 'what happened to them when the palaces burned' beat must stay honest: there is no textual evidence for the fate of the Pylos women or Ugarit's slaves after the destructions—frame it as an unanswerable silence, not a known massacre or liberation.

## The Hurrian Hymn to Nikkal (h.6) from Ugarit — oldest substantially complete notated melody

VERDICT: Safe to use in the hedged form given: c. 1400 BCE tablet, Hurrian cult song with interval-based notation widely read as lyre instructions, oldest *substantially complete* notated melody, reconstructions genuinely diverge, and modern recordings exist — but do not fuse it chronologically with Ugarit's final "enemy ships" letters, which date roughly two centuries later.

KEY FACTS:
- Around 36 fragments of Hurrian-language cult songs in Akkadian cuneiform were excavated by French archaeologists in the 1950s from the Royal Palace area of Ugarit (Ras Shamra, Syria), from a stratum conventionally dated c. 1400 BCE (some scholars say 14th–13th century BCE).
- Hymn h.6, the only substantially complete piece, was assembled from three fragments (RS 15.30 + 15.49 + 17.387) identified as one tablet by Emmanuel Laroche, who published accounts in 1955 and 1968; the tablet is held in the National Museum of Damascus.
- The text is a cult song generally interpreted as addressed to Nikkal, a goddess associated with orchards (and wife of the moon god); below the lyrics, the tablet carries Akkadian-derived terms naming string-pairs/intervals of the nine-string sammum (lyre/harp) plus number signs — the system scholars interpret as musical notation rooted in Babylonian tuning theory.
- h.6 is routinely described (including by Guinness World Records) as the oldest surviving substantially complete work of notated music, roughly a millennium older than the Seikilos epitaph (1st century CE), which remains the oldest fully complete surviving composition.
- Anne Draffkorn Kilmer (Assyriologist, UC Berkeley) produced an influential interpretation published in 1974; the piece was first performed in 1974 and released with Richard Crocker and Robert Brown on the 1976 LP 'Sounds from Silence' on a replica lyre.
- At least five competing decipherments exist — by Kilmer, Marcelle Duchesne-Guillemin, David Wulstan, Martin L. West, Richard Dumbrill, and Raoul Vitale among others — each yielding substantially different melodies; disputes include whether the interval signs mean simultaneous dyads or melodic motion, what the number signs count, and the rhythm (which is not notated at all).
- Modern listenable reconstructions include the Kilmer/Crocker 1976 recording, Richard Dumbrill's versions, Michael Levy's lyre recordings, and Malek Jandali's orchestral arrangement 'Echoes from Ugarit' (recorded 2008/2009 with the Russian Philharmonic).
- Ugarit is also the source of the famous last letters about approaching enemy ships (e.g., RS 20.238, King Ammurapi's correspondence), written shortly before the city's destruction c. 1185 BCE — the same archive city, but those letters postdate the hymn tablets by perhaps one to two centuries.

USABLE FRAMING:
From the same ruined city that gave us those desperate final letters about enemy ships, archaeologists in the 1950s pulled something gentler: clay tablets, written maybe a century or two earlier, carrying a hymn in the Hurrian language — most scholars read it as a song to Nikkal, a goddess of orchards. Beneath the words is what is widely interpreted as musical notation: names of string-pairs on a lyre, with numbers, drawing on Babylonian tuning theory. That makes it, by most reckonings, the oldest substantially complete written melody we have — over three thousand years old, a millennium older than anything from Greece. But here's the honest part: we can read the signs without being sure what they mean musically. At least five serious scholarly reconstructions exist, and they sound genuinely different from one another — the rhythm isn't notated at all. So when you listen to a modern recording of the 'oldest song in the world' — and you can, today — you're hearing an educated guess at a melody whose true sound is still locked in the clay.

CAUTIONS:
- Do not call it 'the oldest song' or 'oldest melody' flatly — it is the oldest *substantially complete* notated piece; older notation-related fragments and older music-theory texts (Old Babylonian tuning texts, c. 1800 BCE) exist, and the Seikilos epitaph (1st c. CE) is the oldest *fully complete* surviving composition.
- Do not present any single modern recording as 'the' ancient melody — Kilmer, Duchesne-Guillemin, Wulstan, West, Dumbrill, and Vitale all produced different decipherments, and whether the interval signs are dyads/chords or melodic steps is genuinely contested; rhythm is entirely modern conjecture.
- Do not state the lyrics' meaning confidently — the Ugarit dialect of Hurrian is poorly understood; the Nikkal dedication and 'goddess of orchards' reading are accepted interpretations, not certainties, and the translation of the text is fragmentary.
- Do not collapse the timeline in the narrative tie-in: the hymn tablets are conventionally dated c. 1400 BCE (or 14th–13th c.), while the 'enemy ships' letters date to Ugarit's destruction c. 1185 BCE — same city and same archives culture, but not the same generation.
- Be careful with the popular 'undelivered letter found baking in the kiln' detail — the famous 'oven tablets' story has been challenged in later scholarship (the 'kiln' may have been collapsed debris from an upper storey), so hedge that anecdote if used.
- The date 'c. 1400 BCE' itself is a convention, not a precision date — some sources place the tablets in the 13th century BCE; saying 'roughly 3,400 years old' or 'fourteenth century BCE, give or take' is the safe phrasing.
- Avoid saying the notation is 'for a lyre' as certain fact — the sammum is usually identified as a lyre (possibly harp), nine strings per the related tuning texts; phrase as 'instructions widely interpreted as string-pairings for a lyre-like instrument.'

## Caral / Norte Chico (Peru, c. 3000-1800 BCE): monumental urbanism, the "maritime foundations" subsistence debate, the absence of writing, and what it means for the "grain leads to states" thesis

VERDICT: Safe to use, but hedge the subsistence story carefully: the monumentality and contemporaneity are solid consensus; the "anchovy-plus-cotton, not grain" picture is a real, famous hypothesis (Moseley's Maritime Foundations) that is genuinely contested — and recent evidence points to NON-cereal plants, not fish alone, as the calorie base. The quipu-at-Caral claim must be flagged as reported-but-not-formally-published.

KEY FACTS:
- Caral's main monumental occupation is radiocarbon-dated to roughly 2627-2020 BCE (Shady, Haas & Creamer, Science 2001), with the wider Norte Chico florescence spanning c. 3000-1800 BCE (Late Archaic / Preceramic).
- Haas et al. (Nature 2004) dated the Norte Chico region from 95 samples in the Pativilca and Fortaleza valleys, finding large-scale settlement emerging from roughly 3200 BCE onward; more than 30 large Late Archaic sites with platform mounds and sunken circular plazas are known in the region.
- This makes Caral contemporary with Early Bronze Age Egypt's Old Kingdom pyramid age (Giza c. 2560 BCE) and Sumerian Early Dynastic cities, though Mesopotamian urbanism (Uruk) predates it by roughly a millennium — and Norte Chico itself had no metallurgy and no Bronze Age (it was pre-ceramic, stone-tool).
- Caral's central monumental zone covers about 65 hectares; the UNESCO 'Sacred City of Caral-Supe' property is about 626 hectares and was inscribed in 2009; the largest mound, the Piramide Mayor, is roughly 150 m on a side and about 18 m high (some sources cite larger figures, e.g. ~28 m).
- Michael Moseley's 1975 Maritime Foundations of Andean Civilization (MFAC) hypothesis argued the rich Humboldt-Current fishery (especially anchovy, Engraulis ringens) provided the caloric base for early complex society BEFORE intensive agriculture, with irrigated cotton grown chiefly to make fishing nets and lines.
- Haas & Creamer (PNAS 2013), using coprolites, pollen, tool residues and 126 radiocarbon dates, argued instead that maize was widely grown, intensively processed, and a primary dietary component throughout 3000-1800 BCE ('corn rules' thesis) — directly opposing the strong maritime model.
- Stable-isotope studies have since complicated BOTH positions: Pezo-Lanfranco et al. (Am. J. Biol. Anthropology 2022), analyzing 52 individuals, found C3 plants (likely tubers) supplied roughly 55-68% of calories at coastal Aspero and over 70% at inland Caral, with marine protein important but secondary (more so at Aspero); Pezo-Lanfranco & Colonese (Scientific Reports 2024) concluded plant cultivation, not fishing, fueled early complexity and that maize only became a staple (>25% of diet) around 500 BCE.
- Cotton was unambiguously a central irrigated crop and is central to all models: Beresford-Jones et al. (2017) 'refine' rather than reject MFAC, arguing cotton plant-fiber technology (bigger, cheaper nets) scaled up fishing and drove labor coordination.
- No writing system is known at Caral or anywhere in Norte Chico, and the culture was pre-ceramic (no pottery); a possible early quipu (knotted cords) was reported at Caral (publicized by Charles Mann c. 2005 and attributed to Ruth Shady) but it has not been formally published or described in the scientific literature and remains singular and unverified.

USABLE FRAMING:
Around 2600 BCE, while Egyptians were raising the Giza pyramids and Sumerian cities thrived, people on the desert coast of Peru built their own monumental world — Caral and dozens of sister sites in the Norte Chico valleys, with stepped platform mounds and sunken plazas — and they did it without pottery, without metal, and with no writing system we can read. What makes Caral such a useful thorn in the side of the tidy 'grain grows states' story is its economy. The classic hypothesis, Michael Moseley's 'Maritime Foundations,' held that this society ran not on a cereal staple but on the sea: dried anchovies and sardines for protein, and irrigated cotton grown largely to make the nets that caught them. That idea is contested and has been a live argument for fifty years — one camp (Haas and Creamer) countered that maize mattered more than thought, while the newest chemical evidence from human bones points a third way, suggesting that non-cereal plants like tubers, squash and beans actually supplied most of the calories, with seafood a key protein source rather than the calorie engine. The honest takeaway isn't 'fish built the pyramids' — it's that no single cereal grain sat at the center here, yet a complex, monument-building society still emerged. So if there's a rule, it may be less 'grain leads to states' than 'a storable, countable surplus leads to states' — whether that surplus is dried fish, stored tubers, or bales of cotton.

CAUTIONS:
- Do not state as settled fact that Caral was 'built on dried anchovies.' That is the strong form of Moseley's hypothesis and is actively contested; recent isotope work (2022, 2024) actually favors plant calories over marine calories as the dietary base.
- Do not flip to the opposite overclaim either — do not say maize/corn powered Caral. The 2013 'corn rules' interpretation is disputed by isotope studies indicating maize did not become a dietary staple until roughly 500 BCE; the early calorie base looks like C3 plants (tubers etc.), not any cereal.
- Be careful saying the quipu 'proves' record-keeping at Caral. The Caral quipu is a reported find attributed to Shady, popularized in media, but not formally published, described, or independently verified — treat it as 'possible' / 'reported,' not confirmed, and never as deciphered.
- Do not call Caral a 'Bronze Age' culture or imply metallurgy. It was a stone-tool, pre-ceramic (no pottery) society; the Bronze Age framing applies only to its Old World contemporaries.
- Avoid the romantic 'entirely peaceful, no warfare' claim that circulates in popular sources — absence of clear fortifications/weapons is suggestive but is an argument from absence, not a proven fact.
- Don't overstate precision on monument dimensions and population: figures for the Piramide Mayor vary between sources (~18 m vs ~28 m high) and firm population numbers for Caral are estimates, not measured.
- Frame 'oldest civilization in the Americas' as the widely held view, and 'contemporary with Egypt's pyramids' as accurate, but note Mesopotamian urbanism (Uruk) is older by roughly a thousand years if comparing first cities.

## Bronze Age-to-modern connections for narrative history podcast: verification and ranking of 8 candidate segments

VERDICT: All 8 check out in some form; ranked strongest-to-riskiest: (1) Plimpton 322, (2) shekel etymology, (3) Hammurabi interest caps, (4) ox-head letter A / "alphabet", (5) Ever Given, (6) tin-as-strategic-chokepoint analogy (yes, serious scholars draw it), (7) bevel-rim bowl rations (usable only with the debate stated), (8) Uluburun Central Asian tin (newest but actively contested — present as live controversy).

KEY FACTS:
- RANK 1 — Plimpton 322, an Old Babylonian clay tablet likely from Larsa c. 1822–1784 BC, tabulates numbers from 15 Pythagorean triples in sexagesimal notation, demonstrating systematic knowledge of the a²+b²=c² relationship roughly 1,100-1,300 years before Pythagoras (c. 570–495 BC); the triples reading is consensus since Neugebauer & Sachs (1945), though the tablet's purpose (likely a scribal-school teacher's aid, per Eleanor Robson) is debated.
- RANK 2 — 'Shekel' derives from Akkadian šiqlu (root šaqālu, 'to weigh'), attested as a Mesopotamian weight unit by c. 2150 BC under Naram-Sin and in the Code of Hammurabi c. 1750 BC (Mesopotamian shekel ~8.3-8.4 g of silver, 60 shekels = 1 mina); Israel revived the name for its currency on 24 February 1980, replaced by the new shekel on 1 January 1986 at 1,000:1 — a ~4,000-year-old word for weighed silver still on price tags today.
- RANK 3 — Old Babylonian standard interest rates were 20% on silver loans and 33⅓% on barley loans, and the Code of Hammurabi (c. 1754 BC, Law 88 and related provisions) capped rates at those levels, with a lender who overcharged forfeiting his principal; written, witnessed, interest-bearing loan contracts survive by the thousands, making this the deepest documented ancestry of modern credit.
- RANK 4 — Our letter A descends from a Proto-Sinaitic sign (c. 1900–1500 BC, Serabit el-Khadim / Wadi el-Hol region), adapted from an Egyptian ox-head pictogram for the Semitic word 'alp ('ox'); Phoenician aleph was rotated by the Greeks into alpha, so a modern capital A flipped 180° still shows the ox's horns, and 'alphabet' itself comes from alpha-beta, i.e., Semitic aleph ('ox') + bet ('house').
- RANK 5 — The container ship Ever Given grounded in the Suez Canal on 23 March 2021 and was freed on 29 March 2021 (six days), backing up 400+ ships and holding up an estimated $9–9.6 billion in trade per day, on a waterway that normally carries roughly 12% of global trade by volume and ~30% of container traffic — both target figures (six days, ~12%) verify.
- RANK 6 — Serious scholars do draw the tin-chokepoint analogy explicitly: Eric Cline in '1177 B.C.' (2014) compares Late Bronze Age tin dependence (sourced ~1,800 miles away in Afghanistan/Central Asia) to modern oil dependence, and the 2022 Science Advances Uluburun tin study and subsequent commentary frame Bronze Age tin scarcity as a direct parallel to semiconductor/rare-earth concentration today; tin is genuinely much scarcer than copper, with major 2nd-millennium-BC sources limited to a few regions (Central Asia, Anatolia, Western Europe incl. Cornwall).
- RANK 7 — Mesopotamian institutional workers were genuinely paid in barley, bread, and beer rations (abundantly documented in Uruk-period and Ur III administrative texts), and the crude, mass-produced bevel-rim bowl — found by the tens of thousands at 4th-millennium-BC Uruk-sphere sites — is widely interpreted as a standardized ration container; but its function (grain-ration measure vs. bread-baking mould vs. other uses) has been debated for nearly a century and remains unresolved.
- RANK 8 — A 2022 Science Advances study (Powell et al.) analyzing 105 of the Uluburun shipwreck's tin ingots (~91% of the tin cargo, ship sank c. 1320 BC) concluded about one-third came from the Mušiston deposit in Central Asia (Tajikistan/Uzbekistan border region, ~3,000 km away) and two-thirds from the Taurus Mountains of Anatolia — but a 2023 rebuttal by Berger, Kaniuth, Brügmann, and Pernicka (Frontiers in Earth Science) argues Mušiston's chemistry and lead-isotope signatures are incompatible with the ingots, so the Central Asia claim is contested, not settled.

USABLE FRAMING:
A narrator can safely say: a Babylonian scribe tabulated Pythagorean triples more than a thousand years before Pythagoras was born; the word on every Israeli price tag is a 4,000-year-old Akkadian term for weighing silver; and Hammurabi's Babylon already had interest-rate caps — 20% on silver, a third on barley — enforced by forfeiture of principal. The letter A you're reading began life as an ox's head sketched by Semitic speakers in Egypt around 1900 BC, and 'alphabet' literally means 'ox-house.' On supply chains, the analogy is not the podcast's invention: scholars like Eric Cline explicitly call Bronze Age tin the oil of its day, and when the Ever Given wedged itself across Suez for six days in March 2021, it choked a canal carrying roughly 12% of world trade — a one-ship stress test of the same chokepoint logic that governed tin. Frame the bevel-rim bowl as 'most archaeologists' best guess is a standardized ration bowl — though they've argued about it for a century,' and frame the Uluburun Central Asian tin result as a thrilling 2022 claim now under serious challenge, not a fact.

CAUTIONS:
- Do NOT present the Code of Hammurabi as enforced statutory law in the modern sense — scholars debate whether it was applied in courts or was largely a royal monument/scholarly text; safer to say it 'prescribed' or 'set down' maximum rates. Also, the interest provisions sit in a section of the Louvre stele erased in antiquity by the Elamites and restored from later manuscript copies — avoid saying you can 'read them on the stele.'
- Do NOT call the shekel 'the world's oldest currency' — it was a weight unit for ~2,000+ years before coinage existed (first coins c. 7th century BC Lydia); the honest claim is oldest continuously-resonant monetary WORD/name, revived by Israel in 1980. Shekel weights also varied (Mesopotamian ~8.4 g vs. biblical ~11 g+).
- Do NOT state the bevel-rim bowl was 'the world's first paycheck' as fact — the ration-measure interpretation is the leading hypothesis but the bread-mould and beer-distribution theories are live alternatives; the wages-in-beer-and-barley system itself is textually solid, the bowl's exact role is not.
- Do NOT say the letter A was 'simply flipped upside down' — it went through multiple rotations (Proto-Sinaitic ox head, Phoenician sideways aleph resembling a K, Greek upright alpha); the 'flip a capital A to see the horns' line is fine as a visualization, not a literal one-step history. Proto-Sinaitic dating (c. 1900 vs. c. 1700 BC) and the single-origin story are also debated; avoid claims that Hebrew specifically was 'the first alphabet.'
- Do NOT claim Plimpton 322 is 'the world's oldest trigonometry table' — the 2017 Mansfield & Wildberger trig claim got huge press but is widely rejected by historians of mathematics; stick to Pythagorean triples and say its purpose (probably teacher's reference for school problems) is debated.
- Do NOT say the Ever Given 'cost the world $9.6B a day' — that figure (Lloyd's List) is the value of trade HELD UP per day, not destroyed value; and the ~12% figure is share of global trade volume normally transiting Suez, not 12% of trade lost during the blockage.
- Do NOT present the Uluburun Central-Asia tin sourcing as settled: the 2022 Powell et al. one-third-from-Mušiston result was directly rebutted in 2023 by Berger/Kaniuth/Brügmann/Pernicka on chemical and lead-isotope grounds; present it as an active scientific dispute. Also note press releases disagree on whether Mušiston is in Uzbekistan or Tajikistan — the deposit is in Tajikistan.
- For the tin/semiconductor analogy, attribute it ('historians like Eric Cline have compared...') rather than asserting Bronze Age actors understood chokepoints the way modern strategists do; the tin-to-oil comparison is Cline's framing, and exact Bronze Age tin source percentages are themselves under active research (e.g., 2025 Antiquity paper arguing for a significant Cornwall role).
