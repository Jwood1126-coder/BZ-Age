# Draft-05 Brief — The Told-Story Contract

**Date:** July 2, 2026. The writer's feedback after sampling draft-04, translated into an
operational contract. This supersedes nothing in the draft-04 brief (those rules stand — callouts,
self-reference, temporal signposting); it ADDS the register layer draft-04 didn't reach. This is
the governing document for the draft-05 pass.

**The writer's words, compressed:** redundancies in content and speaking style; still reads AI in
many parts; transitions not smooth; should be *less audiobook, more discussion* — "someone telling
a story to friends while maintaining specific contexts"; hold attention; presume no prior
knowledge, but respect the listeners who have it — leave them "little nuggets of acknowledgement…
like how animated movies slip in adult humor"; more comparisons and context (a thousand years
means nothing until you set it against the whole run of Rome); little bits of humor and
modern-world comparisons welcome — "a stand-up comedian perspective in the wit and contrast — it
must be organic though and not set up like a bit."

---

## A. The redundancy kill

Two kinds, both live in draft-04:
1. **Content redundancy** — the same fact or image delivered twice in different clothes (the fleet
   caught the cross-part cases; the within-part cases remain). Rule: every fact gets ONE delivery;
   a second appearance must be a *callback that does new work*, or it dies.
2. **Habit redundancy** — the same sentence-shape recurring on a clock even when each instance is
   locally fine (the Voice Bible's clock test, now applied at paragraph rhythm level too: the
   setup-payoff-bow paragraph shape itself becomes a tic when every paragraph has it).

## B. Transitions — the connective tissue of a person deciding what to tell you next

The current seams are *sectional* (topic ends, white space, new topic starts — an audiobook's
chapter logic). A person telling a story to friends doesn't do that. They pivot out loud:
they finish a thought and the next one is *caused* by it — "which brings up something I skipped
earlier," "and that's the same reason why…," "now hold that, because the other thing happening
that decade…" The transition IS content: it tells the listener why this comes next.

Rules:
- Every `---` seam in the script gets read aloud as a question: *would a person talking arrive
  here from there?* If the answer is "only with a slide change," write the pivot sentence.
- The pivot must be causal or associative, never administrative ("Next, let's look at…" is
  banned; "And the reason that worked is the same reason it broke" is the register).
- Cold opens and State-of-the-World snapshots may keep hard cuts — those are earned formal
  devices. Everything else earns its seam.

## C. The register — discussion, not narration

Think: the smartest person you know, three drinks deep in a story they love, who never loses the
thread. Operational moves:
- **Contractions everywhere** speech would use them. Read-aloud test is mandatory.
- **Spoken syntax**: sentences that restart, asides that interrupt ("the wall — and I need you to
  picture an actual wall here, not a garden wall —"), the occasional fragment doing a full
  sentence's job.
- **The teller is allowed reactions**: "which is an insane sentence to get to say about the Bronze
  Age" — a person telling this story WOULD react to it. The narrator's existing reactions ("What
  gets me about Ventris…") are the model; there should be more of them, and they must be real
  reactions, not decorated transitions.
- **Check-ins, rationed**: "you see the problem" / "you know where this is going" — at most one
  or two per part, only where the listener genuinely is ahead of the story.
- **The danger**: conversational tics repeated become the new AI-tell. "So." as a paragraph
  opener is allowed maybe three times a part. Variance is still the fingerprint of a human.

## D. The two-audience design (the Pixar rule)

The base layer assumes NOTHING: every name introduced, every concept built from the ground, the
newcomer never lost. Layered on top, for the listeners who know the material: **nuggets** — nods
that pass completely unnoticed if you don't know, and land with a grin if you do. Never explained,
never flagged, costing the newcomer zero seconds.

Nugget types (build in these shapes):
1. **The unmarked allusion.** Describing the copper merchant's file of complaint letters as "kept,
   filed, and — you have to assume — reread" (those who know Ea-nasir's reputation grin; everyone
   else just hears characterization).
2. **The knowing epithet.** "Woolley, who never met a find he couldn't headline" — accurate for
   everyone, funnier if you know about the Flood telegram.
3. **The acknowledged deep-end.** One clause that admits more exists without opening it: "the
   chronology fights over this decade have ended careers — we're stepping around the crater."
   Advanced listeners hear the Thera debate; new listeners hear texture.
4. **The precision wink.** Using the *exact* term of art once, unexplained, where context carries
   it ("the tablets the field calls 'the o-ka set'") — the initiated notice you know the room.
Budget: 2–4 per part. Never at the expense of clarity. If a newcomer would stop and say "wait,
what?", it's not a nugget, it's a hole.

## E. The time-ruler (comparisons and context)

The writer's example is the standard: "a thousand years go by and the listener doesn't think much
of it" — until you say *that's the whole run of Rome, Republic through the fall of the West.*
Every large span in the series gets grounded against better-known history or lived experience.

Build a varied toolkit (do NOT reuse one anchor — draft-04 already leans on Christianity's age):
- The full Rome run (~Republic 509 BCE → 476 CE ≈ a millennium) — for thousand-year spans
- "Cleopatra lived closer to the moon landing than to the building of the pyramids" — for
  Egypt's internal depth `[VERIFY the standard formulation]`
- Shakespeare → now (~400 years); Columbus → now (~500); the Crusades (~900); Charlemagne
  (~1,200); the Vikings (~1,100) — mid-range spans
- Grandparent chains ("a hundred grandmothers back") — for felt generational time
- Institutional lifespans: "older than every university on Earth… by triple" — for durability
Rule: one time-ruler per major span, integrated in the sentence, never as a stat aside. Modern
comparisons (supply chains, dead file formats, customer service) welcome where the parallel is
real — the Domesday beat is the house standard.

## F. The wit — a comedian's eye, never a comedian's setup

What stand-up actually contributes here isn't jokes — it's the *observational reflex*: noticing
the absurd contrast sitting inside true material and saying it plainly. The humor is in the
framing, not appended to it.

- **The comedy of recognition** is the core register: ancient people behaving exactly like us
  (Nanni's complaint letter; the smugglers' route advice; the schoolboy's doodles). The laugh IS
  the thesis — profoundly different, profoundly the same.
- **Deadpan understatement** over exclamation: "He was assassinated in a palace conspiracy, which
  is a very Hittite ending" (already in P4 — this is the house wit at its best; more of this).
- **The rules:** never announced ("funnily enough" is banned); never a setup-punchline rhythm the
  listener can hear coming; never punching down at ancient people for being ancient; every laugh
  must carry information — if the line could be cut without losing a fact or a framing, cut it.
- Frequency: if a part has zero smiles, it's too solemn; if a listener can predict the next quip,
  it's a bit. Two to five genuine smiles per hour.
