# Miano Transcript Digest — "The 5 Earliest Known Cities in the World" (World of Antiquity)

**Source:** full transcript supplied by the writer, July 2, 2026. This digest is the durable
record (the raw transcript lives in chat history only). Miano is an ancient-history PhD; the
video's method is explicitly evidence-first. Extracted for series use; every item below is
"per Miano" — treat as a credible scholar's synthesis, not as independently verified.

## His definition of "city" (Childe's 1950 'Urban Revolution' traits, updated)
Five core: (1) size/density — rule of thumb ≥20 ha (some say 30–40) and ≥10,000 people;
(2) complex division of labor (full-time specialists, archaeologically visible workshops);
(3) commoner surplus paying for government (visible as state storage facilities);
(4) social classes; (5) centralized power (visible as rulers' residences) — with his honest
hedge that city ≠ state is arguable. Plus: (6) monumental architecture (with the note that
monuments PREdate cities — Neolithic examples); (7) bureaucracy — Childe said "writing,"
broadened to any formal record-keeping; (8) regular long-distance trade. He demotes (9)
practical sciences and (10) sophisticated art as non-essential.

## His five earliest known cities
1. **Tell Brak (Nagar?), NE Syria — urban by ~3800 BCE.** 130 ha; Eye Temple; city wall; paved
   streets; surplus storage; elite class; organized warfare. No writing before Uruk's; population
   density uncertain; "some archaeologists are still hesitant." His verdict: "I think Uruk will
   be toppled off its perch... Uruk is much bigger and more impressive, but it no longer looks
   like it was first."
2. **Uruk — full city by ~3700 BCE.** ≥70 ha by 4000–3800; walled; religious/administrative +
   residential + industrial sectors; ~250 ha by 3100 (with dependent hamlets), ~600 ha by 2800;
   population 20,000–40,000; largest city in the world in the Early Dynastic period. NOTE: he
   says the Eanna/Kullab monumental buildings are "often interpreted as temples but more
   recently understood as public assembly halls with associated cultic functions."
3. **Nekhen/Hierakonpolis — c. 3300 BCE** (urbanizing 3400–3200; Narmer's seat; ceremonial
   complex, hierarchy, specialized production, perimeter wall).
4. **Susa — c. 3100 BCE** (hiatus ~3350–3140 after conflict with Chogha Mish; then new
   eastern-connected elite, Proto-Elamite writing, 1,000+ tablets; by 3000 up to half the
   Susiana plain's population lived there).
5. **Jericho — c. 3000 BCE** (city only in the Early Bronze Age, after newcomers arrive end of
   4th millennium; peak 2700–2600; EBA destruction with earthquake-damaged walls).

## The exclusions (and his reasons) — directly useful to us
- **Göbekli Tepe:** monuments, yes; city, no — no known resident population, missing most traits.
- **Çatalhöyük:** "proto-city" at best — under 10,000, no public buildings, no social classes.
- **Trypillia mega-sites:** Talianki est. 15–20,000 people, Maidanets est. ~29,000, built
  4000–3500 BCE — "if we were just going by size they would have made the list and would have
  rivaled Uruk, but they lack... monumental architecture and record-keeping."
- **Eridu:** legendary first city, but archaeologically "almost reached city status in the late
  fourth millennium, had a setback"; actual urbanization ~2900. (Nippur similar.)
- **Indus:** urbanization ~2600 (Bhirrana ~3000 proposal is controversial, carbon-only,
  uncorroborated).
- **Caral:** ~2600; he counts "six parts of the world" where state societies arose independently.
- **Hamoukar:** important mid-4th-millennium town but ~15 ha then — too small; city after 3000.
