# Archetypes — Recurring Vantage Figures

The working list of archetypes the series can call back to across parts. **Not literal genealogy.** Recurring narrative threads that let the listener feel the continuity of human life across centuries while the structures around that life change.

This document is referenced by Move H (Draft a Turning Point Beat) in CLAUDE.md. When drafting a turning point, check this list for callback opportunities. Even a one-sentence callback ("the great-great-granddaughter of the merchant's wife we met in the second hour") earns its place.

## How to use this document

- **When introducing an archetype for the first time**, render them with enough specificity that they can be called back to. A name is optional; sensory specificity is mandatory.
- **When drafting a later part**, scan this list for archetypes whose "descendants" could appear in the current material.
- **Update this list** as new archetypes emerge or as existing ones are refined.
- **Cull this list** when an archetype isn't earning callbacks. Better six strong archetypes than fifteen weak ones.

## The working archetype list

### The Neolithic farmer
- **First appearance:** Part 1 opening State of the World snapshot, around 5000 BCE.
- **Sensory anchors:** smoke-darkened single-room house, packed earth floor, the constant background of grinding grain, sheep nearby, bare feet.
- **What defines them:** subsistence agriculture, no metal, village-scale world. Horizon is the edge of meaningful reality.
- **Callback opportunities:** every later snapshot is implicitly a contrast with this baseline. Explicit callback options: when bronze first reaches a village like this one (Part 1 or Part 2 transition); when an empire's tax collector reaches a village like this one (Part 3); when a long-distance trade caravan passes through (Part 4); when a refugee from a fallen city arrives in a village like this one (Part 7 or 8).

### The Sumerian barge-puller
- **First appearance:** Part 2 State of the World snapshot, approaching Uruk around 3000 BCE.
- **Sensory anchors:** rope across the shoulder, the smell of the river, the temple complex rising above the city wall and being wrongly large.
- **What defines them:** urban underclass, first or second generation off the farm, exposed to the polyglot soundscape of the first cities.
- **Callback opportunities:** the urban underclass at Akkad-period Sumer (Part 3); the same kind of figure in the Old Babylonian world (Part 4); the dockworker descendant at cosmopolitan-age Ugarit (Part 5, deliberate parallel); the worker in a city about to be sacked (Part 7).

### The Sumerian scribe
- **First appearance:** Part 2, the bored student with his exercise tablet.
- **Sensory anchors:** the clay tablet in the hand, the stylus, the school courtyard, the corporal punishment that scribal training relied on, the doodles on the back of the homework.
- **What defines them:** literacy as a learned skill in a society where almost no one else has it. The scribe is *somebody*, even if not aristocratic.
- **Callback opportunities:** the Akkadian-trained scribe under empire (Part 3); the Old Assyrian scribe writing for the merchant houses (Part 4); the multi-lingual scribe of the cosmopolitan world handling Akkadian, Hittite, Egyptian (Part 5); the scribe whose tablets get baked by the fire that destroys his palace (Part 7); the *absence* of his successors (Part 8 — the deepest archetype-callback in the series, where the archetype is called back specifically by its disappearance); the Iron Age scribe who learns the new alphabet and changes what literacy means (Part 9).

### The conquered Sumerian villager under Akkadian rule
- **First appearance:** Part 3 State of the World snapshot, around 2270 BCE.
- **Sensory anchors:** the village mostly unchanged, the Akkadian governor's official passing through the marketplace, the new language of official documents.
- **What defines them:** the experience of empire from below — your immediate life mostly the same, the larger framework you belong to suddenly enormous and alien.
- **Callback opportunities:** the same figure under Middle Bronze Age polities (Part 4); the cosmopolitan-age subject paying taxes that ultimately fund the international diplomatic system (Part 5); the late-collapse villager whose empire's tax demands have grown impossible because the empire is failing (Parts 6–7); the post-collapse highland farmer — the contrarian beat — who is no longer subject to any palace (Part 8).

### The Old Assyrian merchant's wife
- **First appearance:** Part 4 opening hook, the angry letter about the textiles, around 1800 BCE.
- **Sensory anchors:** the weaving floor, the household with husband absent in Anatolia, the children, the household slaves, the donkey-caravan letters arriving and departing.
- **What defines them:** literate (probably), commercial, transnational by virtue of her husband's trade, formidable in her own right. The first ancient woman in the series whose voice is fully accessible.
- **Callback opportunities:** the cosmopolitan-age elite woman managing estates in Late Bronze Age Greece or Anatolia (Part 5); the Mycenaean palace woman whose textiles get inventoried in Linear B (Part 5); the figure who experiences the trade network breaking (Part 7 — when her commercial world dies); the figure who has had to retreat to subsistence (Part 8). This archetype carries the *first globalization* throughline most powerfully across parts.

### The Late Bronze Age dockworker at Ugarit
- **First appearance:** Part 5 State of the World snapshot, around 1350 BCE.
- **Sensory anchors:** the harbor at dusk, copper ingots stacked on the wharves, three or four languages, lamp-lit doorways of dockside inns.
- **What defines them:** cosmopolitan urban labor at the peak of the first globalization. They know the names of the great kings; they have a place in the system even though they're at the bottom of it.
- **Callback opportunities:** the empty harbor in Part 7 — the same docks, no ships. (This is one of the most affecting callbacks available to the series.) Possibly: an Iron Age port worker in a much-reduced trade network, Part 9.

### The Mycenaean palace administrator
- **First appearance:** Part 5 — RENDERED as of draft-03 (no longer a glimpse): a body at Pylos on an ordinary morning at the peak; the day-tablets (rations for the weaving women and children, bronze by weight to the smiths, rowers, linen, perfumed oil); "the palace receives, the palace allocates, the palace counts — he is the counting."
- **Sensory anchors:** the damp palm-sized clay tablets, the stylus, the archive-room shelf, the morning's stack.
- **What defines them:** the late Bronze Age palace bureaucracy — administrative literacy as power, with two load-bearing plants attached in draft-03: (1) his tablets are unfired working documents never meant to last ("Remember the shelf" — collected in Part 7's Pylos fire); (2) his literacy has exactly one customer, the palace (collected in Part 8's cold open).
- **Callback chain as executed in draft-03:** Part 5 plant (the morning at the peak) → Part 6 chord (the same desk now writing rower requisitions — "the instrument hasn't changed at all, and what it's recording has changed completely") → Part 7 orphaning (skills with no customer; the close *withholds* the literacy loss — "The next part opens at their table") → **Part 8 cold open: the single detonation** (the grandfather who could read; the grandson who cannot), with the explicit identification back to Pylos. The literacy-as-losable-technology throughline at its emotional peak, struck once.

### The post-collapse highland farmer
- **First appearance:** Part 8 State of the World snapshot, around 1050 BCE.
- **Sensory anchors:** small stone-walled house in a hill village, terraced fields, goats, a storage pit cut into bedrock, no tax collector.
- **What defines them:** the contrarian beat in human form. By some measures a much-reduced life; by other measures — measures that matter, like whether your grain gets taken — a freer life. Render both.
- **Callback opportunities:** explicit contrast with the Mycenaean palace administrator (Part 5 → Part 8). Explicit contrast with the conquered Sumerian villager (Part 3 → Part 8 — the empire is gone now, and what does that mean from below?). Possibly forward to the Iron Age scribe (Part 9).

### The Iron Age scribe
- **First appearance:** Part 9, the closing State of the World snapshot.
- **Sensory anchors:** the alphabet — the 22 signs that can be learned in weeks, not years. The papyrus or potsherd or animal hide rather than clay. Whatever tradition this scribe serves (early Hebrew, early Phoenician, early Greek).
- **What defines them:** the democratization of literacy. The scribal caste is no longer a caste. The Iron Age scribe is what the Bronze Age scribe became after the apocalypse.
- **Callback opportunities:** explicit contrast with the Sumerian scribe (Part 2 → Part 9). The closing archetype of the series.

## Callback discipline

When using an archetype callback in a turning-point beat or State of the World snapshot, keep it brief — usually one sentence, occasionally two. The callback is a touch, not a re-introduction:

> *"And the great-great-granddaughter of the merchant's wife we met outside Assur — the one who couldn't get her husband to stop complaining about the textile quality — she is now doing the same work, but the goods she ships travel by ship across half a sea her great-great-grandmother had never seen."*

That's enough. The listener remembers; the callback lands; we return to the story.

## What this is not

- **Not literal family lineages.** The series is not pretending these are real chains of descent. They are narrative threads, recurring vantage figures whose later "descendants" are descendants in the same way that a recurring character archetype across a film series is a "descendant" — they share form, not blood.
- **Not a character list.** These are vantage figures, not protagonists. The series is not a story about any of them in particular. They are how the listener accesses the world at different moments.
- **Not exhaustive.** New archetypes may emerge as parts develop. Some may merge or be cut. Treat this list as a working set.
