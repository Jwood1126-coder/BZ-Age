# North Star Reasoning

## The formulation

> Great storytelling is the main goal. Everything else serves it.

## Why this is the right north star

The previous formulation was "so different, yet so much the same." That's a beautiful and powerful insight, but it's not the right north star. It's an **outcome** that great storytelling about ancient humans can deliver — not the goal of the storytelling itself.

The distinction matters:

- A series that aims at "so different, yet so much the same" directly becomes preachy. It announces its theme. It tells the listener what to feel.
- A series that aims at great storytelling, and lets the so-different-yet-so-same feeling emerge, becomes the kind of thing people don't forget. The insight is delivered by the experience, not declared at the listener.

Backed into. Not aimed at.

## The shift in practice

Every rule, throughline, and discipline in this repo serves storytelling. None of them is the goal.

When a rule, applied rigidly, would kill a great story beat — the rule gives way. This is Rule 0, implicit and overriding.

When a throughline doesn't have material that serves the story in a given part — it goes quiet in that part. The throughlines are obsessions the series returns to, not boxes to check.

When a contrarian-honest beat is true but boring — it's allowed to be skipped, unless skipping it would compromise honesty in a way the listener would feel.

## What this protects against

The most common failure mode for ambitious history podcasts: becoming lectures with sound effects. The host wants to teach. The listener wants a story. The host's desire to teach gradually crowds out the story until what comes out of the microphone is exposition pretending to be narrative.

We avoid this by keeping storytelling primary in the editorial DNA itself. The discipline starts at the conceptual level — what is this thing **for** — and propagates down through every choice.

## Carlin as model

Carlin is clearly fascinated by historical analysis, by structural arguments, by the intellectual puzzle of why things happened. But what comes out of his microphone is *story*. Anything that doesn't serve story gets cut, no matter how interesting it is on its own terms. Even his digressions are story moves — they earn their place by being interesting in their own right while contributing to the larger narrative arc.

We do the same.
