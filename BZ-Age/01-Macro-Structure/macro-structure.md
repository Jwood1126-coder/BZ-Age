# Macro Structure — The Nine-Part Arc

**Working estimate:** nine parts plus a coda. **This will move.** Some parts will swell as the research deepens; others will shrink or merge. Do not lock part counts or runtimes early.

The structure is **chronological**. We move forward through time. Thematic material braids through the chronology; it does not interrupt it.

## Why this division

### Part 1 (Before Bronze) exists as its own part because

The series cannot establish the throughlines — the metallurgical constraint, the agricultural calorie ceiling, the political imagination before the state, the ground-level world — without the Neolithic foundation. A series that opens with Sumer takes the cities as given. We take nothing as given.

### Parts 2 and 3 are split rather than combined because

Sargon's empire is a real analytical break, not just a chronological one. The shift from city-state to multi-ethnic empire is one of the most consequential transitions in human political history. It deserves its own narrative arc.

### Part 4 is held together rather than split because

The analytical point of the Middle Bronze Age is the interconnection — the trading networks, the diplomatic exchanges, the cultural diffusion. Splitting Mesopotamia from Egypt from Anatolia would obscure exactly what makes the period matter.

### Parts 6 and 7 are separated because

Crisis-building and collapse-itself are different dramatic structures. Part 6 is tension; Part 7 is rupture. Combining them would make the rupture less shocking because the listener would already be inside the collapse before it began.

### Part 9 holds the long memory thread because

The Bronze Age's afterlife is part of the Bronze Age's meaning. The *Iliad*, the early Hebrew Bible, the legends of fallen kingdoms — these are not separate from the period. They are how the period continued to act on history after it ended.

## Parts considered and rejected

### A standalone "View From Below" part

Originally proposed as Part 6 in earlier drafts. **Rejected because it ghettoizes the perspective.** Better as a structural discipline running through every part (Hard Rule 8).

### A combined Parts 2 and 3

Considered as a single "Early Mesopotamia" part. **Rejected because Sargon deserves his own narrative arc.**

### A combined Parts 6 and 7

Considered as a single "Crisis and Collapse" part. **Rejected because the dramatic structure of building tension and the dramatic structure of rupture are different and need to breathe separately.**

### A standalone Indus Valley part

Considered. **Rejected because the source material won't support it** — we cannot read the script. Indus material braids into Parts 2, 4, and the comparative threads instead.

---

## On part count

Honest assessment: the right number is probably between **seven and eleven**, and we genuinely won't know until the source map is built out. The nine-plus-coda working estimate is defensible but provisional.

The reason for the explicit warning against locking part counts early: every previous attempt at a long historical podcast series has run aground on overcommitting to a structure that the material would not support. Stay loose.

---

## Coda

Whether the rediscovery story (Schliemann, Champollion, Hrozný cracking Hittite in 1915, Ventris and Linear B in 1952, the Indus script still undeciphered) gets a standalone coda or folds into Part 9 depends on how much weight the rest of the series puts on the memory throughline. **Defer until Part 9 is being developed.**
