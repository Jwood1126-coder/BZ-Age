# The Ground-Level World

**Status:** Active throughline. Required content via Hard Rule 8.

## What it is

The world below the palace records. The farmers, slaves, migrants, women running households, merchants moving between worlds. Most humans who ever lived in the Bronze Age were not in palaces. The palace history is the unusual case; the ground-level world is the main story.

## Why it matters

Editorial commitment to honest history that doesn't let elites write the only record. Also: this is where great storytelling is most able to deliver the so-different-yet-so-same insight as an outcome rather than a stated goal — because nothing produces that feeling more reliably than the texture of an actual human life four thousand years ago.

## How it differs from "the invisible people"

The earlier formulation was methodological and defensive — "remember the people the records don't see." This formulation is generative: the ground-level world is not a supplement to the main story. It **is** the main story, and the palace history is the unusual angle.

## Where it advances

Every part. Required content (Hard Rule 8).

- **Heaviest** in Part 4 (merchant-wife letters) and Part 7 (collapse from inside)
- **Most archaeological** in Part 1 (the Neolithic body) — texts barely exist yet
- **Most textual** in Part 4 (Old Assyrian merchant correspondence, Mari personal letters) — where ground-level voices come through directly

## Anti-romanticization discipline

The ground-level world was hard, often brutal, often boring, sometimes joyful, sometimes terrifying, and never simple. Render the world. Don't redeem it.

A debt-slave in Sumer was a debt-slave. A woman in a Hittite village had a life shaped by constraints we'd find unbearable and also by communities and rituals and meanings we can barely reach.

## Target proportion

Roughly a quarter to a third of each part lives at ground level. More in parts where the material supports it. This is a target, not a quota.
