# The First Globalization

**Status:** Active throughline.

## What it is

The Late Bronze Age was genuinely interconnected. Egyptian gold in Mycenaean tombs. Baltic amber in Syrian palaces. The Uluburun shipwreck carrying cargo from at least seven cultures. This was not localized antiquity.

Interconnection on this scale happened, broke, and did not happen again for two thousand years.

## Why it matters

Shocks the listener out of the assumption that antiquity was provincial. Sets up the fragility throughline's biggest payoff — when this gorgeous, intricate system breaks, the listener understands viscerally what was lost.

## Where it advances

- **Foreshadowed** in Parts 2–3 (early long-distance trade in metals, lapis lazuli, obsidian)
- **Established** in Part 4 (Old Assyrian merchant network)
- **Peak** in Part 5 (the cosmopolitan age proper — the Amarna world)
- **Broken** in Part 7 (collapse)
- **Memory** in Part 8 (the post-collapse world's smaller scale; the slow rebuilding of long-distance networks at lower amplitude)

## Story moves available

- The Uluburun cargo as the iconic image of the cosmopolitan world
- The Amarna correspondence as the recognizable institution
- The harbor at Ugarit as the sensory anchor (a place we can walk through)

## Comparative reaches

- Early modern trade networks (Hanseatic League, Italian merchant houses, the spice trade)
- The Silk Road
- The modern global economy
- The contrast with periods of disconnection (early medieval Europe, post-Han China, etc.) — to show that the Bronze Age cosmopolitan moment was not normal even within "ancient" history
