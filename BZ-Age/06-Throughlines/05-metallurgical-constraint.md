# The Metallurgical Constraint

**Status:** Active throughline.

## What it is

Bronze requires copper and tin. Tin is rare. The major tin sources known to the Bronze Age were in Afghanistan, possibly Cornwall, possibly central Asia — far from the centers of civilization that needed it.

This means the Bronze Age civilizations were materially dependent on long-distance trade for the very metal that defined the era. When trade broke, metal broke. Part of why iron eventually won.

## Why it matters

The deep material basis for the fragility argument. The Bronze Age was built on a supply chain it could not control.

## Where it advances

- **Established** in Part 1 (as material reality — the technological leap, the dependency on tin)
- **Structural fact** throughout the middle parts (the trade network exists in large part to move tin and copper)
- **Payoff** in Parts 7–8 (collapse → tin supply fails → iron wins by default)

## Connection to the iron question

This throughline is what makes the iron question (Hard Rule 9 paradigm case) so analytically rich. Iron's eventual victory is not because iron is better than bronze — bronze was better for most purposes given the metallurgy of the time. Iron won because **iron ore exists almost everywhere** and tin doesn't. Local metal beat better metal when the trade network couldn't deliver the better metal anymore.

## Comparative reaches

- Modern dependencies on rare-earth elements (lithium, cobalt, rare earths from politically fragile regions)
- Petroleum dependency and the geopolitics of oil
- Semiconductor supply chains (Taiwan, ASML, the politics of TSMC)
- Wartime resource constraints (rubber in WWII, etc.)
