# Literacy as a Losable Technology

**Status:** Active throughline.

## What it is

Writing appears, spreads, becomes the backbone of administration — and then in places like Greece it vanishes for centuries.

Not just "forgotten" but tied to specific social functions that disappeared with the palaces. The Linear B script was specifically a tool for administering palace economies; when the palaces died, the script had no purpose and was abandoned. Writing wasn't lost as a general capacity; it was lost as a *function*.

## Why it matters

Civilization is not a ratchet. This is the deepest version of the fragility argument. It also produces one of the most surprising and chilling beats available to the series: the idea that an entire society can forget how to read.

## Where it advances

- **Invented** in Part 2 (as accounting, not poetry)
- **Ubiquitous** by Part 5 (cuneiform, hieroglyphs, Linear B, Ugaritic alphabet all operating simultaneously)
- **Lost** in Part 7 (palace destruction → no more Linear B; collapse of administrative literacy across regions)
- **Reinvented in different form** in Part 8 (the Phoenician alphabet democratizes literacy in ways the Bronze Age scribal castes never imagined)

## Comparative reaches

- Loss of Roman engineering knowledge (medieval rediscovery of concrete, aqueducts, road-building)
- Loss of indigenous knowledge systems under colonialism (deliberate suppression vs. systemic obsolescence)
- Contemporary anxieties about digital preservation and dead file formats (data trapped in formats nobody reads anymore)
- Lost languages where the last speakers die — partial analogy for what happened to administrative scripts that lost their function
